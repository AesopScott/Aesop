Choosing an LLM

◆ The Basics

What Is an LLM, and Why Does the Choice Matter?

An LLM — a Large Language Model — is the AI engine underneath tools like ChatGPT, Claude, and Gemini. When you type a question and get a surprisingly thoughtful answer back, an LLM is doing the work. Think of it like a car engine: the interface you see is the dashboard, but the LLM is what's under the hood making everything go.

The choice matters because different LLMs are genuinely good at different things. Some are better at writing. Some at math. Some at following careful instructions. Some at being honest about what they don't know. Some are free. Some cost money. Some run in the cloud. Some can run on your own computer.

Picking the right one isn't about finding "the best" — it's about finding the best fit for what you're trying to do. This guide will help you figure that out without needing to understand the technical details underneath — though those are available too, for the curious.

Technical Note — What Makes an LLM

LLMs are neural networks trained on large text corpora using a transformer architecture. They predict the next token in a sequence — a process that, at scale, produces remarkably coherent and contextually appropriate language. The "large" in LLM refers to parameter count, which ranges from a few billion (small/local models) to hundreds of billions or more (frontier models like GPT-4o, Claude 3.5 Sonnet, Gemini 1.5 Pro).

Parameter count is a rough proxy for capability, but it's not everything — training data quality, RLHF alignment, and fine-tuning significantly affect real-world performance. A well-tuned 7B model can outperform a poorly trained 70B model on specific tasks.

◆ The Landscape

The Major Players — Who Makes What

There are a handful of companies producing the most capable AI models right now. You don't need to know everything about each one — just enough to know which door to knock on.

Anthropic makes Claude. It's known for being careful, thorough, and good at following nuanced instructions. It tends to be the choice for writing, analysis, coding, and anything where you need a thoughtful collaborator rather than just a fast answer.

OpenAI makes ChatGPT and the GPT family of models. The most widely known AI tool in the world. GPT-4o is fast, capable, and has the largest ecosystem of third-party tools built around it.

Google makes Gemini. It has deep integration with Google's own products — Search, Docs, Gmail — and is particularly strong when you need AI that can work with large amounts of text at once.

Meta makes Llama — a family of open-source models you can download and run yourself. No subscription, no API fees. If privacy or cost is your top concern, Llama is worth knowing about.

Mistral is a smaller European company producing highly efficient open models. Excellent performance-per-dollar, popular in Europe, and a strong choice for developers who want to self-host.

Technical Note — Closed vs Open Weights

Models fall into two broad camps: closed (weights not publicly released — Claude, GPT-4o, Gemini) and open-weight (weights downloadable — Llama 3, Mistral, Qwen, Phi). "Open source" is sometimes used loosely here; truly open models include training code and data, which is rare. Most "open" models release only weights.

Open-weight models can be run locally via tools like Ollama, LM Studio, or llama.cpp. Quantized versions (e.g. Q4_K_M) reduce VRAM requirements significantly — a 7B model quantized to 4-bit runs comfortably on 8GB VRAM or even CPU-only on modern hardware, at the cost of some quality loss.

◆ What to Ask Yourself

Five Questions That Will Point You to the Right Choice

Before you pick a model, answer these five questions honestly. Your answers will do most of the work for you.

1. What am I mainly using it for? Writing? Coding? Research? Customer support? Image generation? Not all models do all things. A model great at writing essays may be mediocre at math. Knowing your primary use case narrows the field quickly.

2. How sensitive is my data? If you're typing confidential business information, medical records, or personal details into a cloud-based AI, that data leaves your computer. For sensitive use cases, a locally-run model may be the wiser choice — even if it's slightly less capable.

3. What's my budget? Free tiers exist for most major models, but they have limits. Paid plans typically cost $20/month for personal use. API access (for building apps) is priced per token — which can range from nearly free to surprisingly expensive depending on the model and volume.

4. Do I need it to connect to other tools? If you want your AI to read your emails, update a spreadsheet, search the web, or control your computer — the ecosystem around the model matters as much as the model itself. Some models have rich integration ecosystems; others are more standalone.

5. How much do I need it to "just work" vs. how much can I customize? If you want to open a browser tab and start chatting, any of the major consumer products will serve you. If you want to build something custom, train it on your own data, or run it offline, you'll need to go deeper.

Technical Note — Evaluating Models

Standard benchmarks for comparing models include MMLU (general knowledge), HumanEval (code generation), GSM8K (math reasoning), and GPQA (graduate-level science). These are useful reference points but imperfect — models can be benchmark-tuned, and real-world performance on domain-specific tasks often diverges from leaderboard rankings.

For production use, LLM-as-judge evals on your own task distribution are more reliable than general benchmarks. Tools like promptfoo, Braintrust, and Anthropic's own eval framework make this accessible. Context window size (measured in tokens) is also critical for tasks involving long documents — Gemini 1.5 Pro's 1M token context is a genuine differentiator for some workflows.

Technical Note — Context Windows & Tokenization

Context window size determines how much text a model can "see" at once. A token is roughly 0.75 words in English — so a 200K token context window can hold roughly 150,000 words, or about two average novels. For document analysis, RAG pipelines, or long multi-turn conversations, context size is often the decisive spec.

Pricing is typically per million input/output tokens. As a rough guide: Claude Sonnet costs ~$3/million input tokens; GPT-4o ~$5; Gemini 1.5 Pro ~$3.50. Local models (Ollama + Llama) cost effectively $0 beyond hardware. For high-volume production workloads, these differences compound significantly — model selection is often as much a cost decision as a quality one.

◆ Quick Decision Guide

What Should You Use? Here's a Straight Answer.

If you're still not sure after reading this far, here's the most direct guidance we can offer based on common scenarios.

◆ I'm a student or writer

Start with Claude or ChatGPT

Both have generous free tiers. Claude tends to produce more careful, nuanced writing. ChatGPT has a broader toolset including image generation.

◆ I'm a developer building an app

Claude API or OpenAI API

Both have excellent APIs and documentation. Claude is often preferred for instruction-following and structured output. OpenAI has a larger ecosystem of community tools.

◆ I work with sensitive data

Local model via Ollama + Llama

Nothing leaves your machine. Llama 3.1 8B runs on a modern laptop. Quality is somewhat lower than cloud frontier models, but the privacy guarantee is absolute.

◆ I need to process long documents

Gemini 1.5 Pro

The 1M token context window is the largest available and genuinely useful for analyzing entire codebases, legal documents, or research papers in one pass.

◆ I'm building AI automation (n8n, OpenClaw)

Claude via API

Claude's strong instruction-following and reliable structured output make it the most predictable choice for agentic workflows where precision matters.

◆ I want the best reasoning / math

DeepSeek R1 or Claude Opus

DeepSeek R1 punches above its weight on reasoning benchmarks and is open-weight. Claude Opus is the premium choice when cost is secondary to quality.

Technical Note — Fine-Tuning vs Prompting

For most use cases, prompt engineering (writing better instructions) will outperform fine-tuning as a first-line optimization. Fine-tuning is appropriate when you have 1,000+ high-quality examples of desired behavior, need consistent style/format that prompting alone can't achieve, or require significant latency/cost reduction via a smaller specialized model.

The current best practice hierarchy: prompt engineering → RAG (retrieval-augmented generation) → fine-tuning → pretraining. Most teams stop at RAG and never need fine-tuning. Services like OpenAI fine-tuning, Anthropic model distillation, and open-source tools like Axolotl or Unsloth lower the barrier significantly if you do reach that point.

◆ The Bigger Picture

Some Honest Things Nobody Tells You

The best model changes every few months. Whatever is leading the benchmarks today may not be in six months. Rather than optimizing for the absolute best right now, build familiarity with two or three models so you can switch when the landscape shifts. It will.

The interface matters as much as the model. A great model wrapped in a bad interface is frustrating. A good-enough model with excellent tooling around it — memory, integrations, automation hooks — can be far more useful day-to-day. This is why tools like n8n and OpenClaw are worth learning: they let any capable model punch above its weight.

Free is genuinely good enough for most people. The free tier of Claude, ChatGPT, or Gemini is more capable than anything that existed two years ago. If you're just getting started, don't pay anything yet — learn what you actually need first, then upgrade when you hit a real limit.

Privacy is worth thinking about. Most consumer AI products use your conversations to improve their models by default. Read the privacy policy of whatever you use. If you're discussing business strategy, legal matters, or anything sensitive, either use a paid plan with explicit data opt-out, or run a local model.

No model is always right. Every LLM hallucinates — that is, confidently states things that are false. The better models do it less often and are more likely to flag their own uncertainty. But the habit of verifying important facts from an AI response is worth developing regardless of which model you use.

Technical Note — Hallucination & Grounding

Hallucination stems from the fundamental nature of autoregressive token prediction — the model generates plausible continuations, not verified facts. Mitigation strategies include: RAG (grounding responses in retrieved documents), tool use / function calling (letting the model query live data rather than relying on parametric memory), chain-of-thought prompting (forcing explicit reasoning steps that expose errors), and citation-forcing (requiring the model to cite sources in its response).

Factuality benchmarks like TruthfulQA and SimpleQA attempt to measure this, though model developers are increasingly aware of these benchmarks and optimization pressure can inflate scores without commensurate real-world improvement. Empirically testing your specific use case remains the most reliable method.
