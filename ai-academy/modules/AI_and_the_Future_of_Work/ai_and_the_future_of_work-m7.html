The Strike as Algorithm: AI and the 2023 Hollywood Shutdowns

When writers and actors walked off the job, the central demand wasn't about money — it was about machines.

On May 2, 2023, the Writers Guild of America called its first strike in fifteen years. Nearly 11,500 screenwriters stopped working. Three months later, on July 14, SAG-AFTRA — the actors' union — joined them, producing the first simultaneous writers-and-actors strike since 1960. By the time the last contract was settled in November 2023, Hollywood had lost an estimated $6 billion in economic activity. The central issue was not residuals or minimum pay, though those mattered. The central issue was artificial intelligence.

Studios had proposed language that would allow them to use AI to generate script outlines, which human writers would then "polish" — at minimum-scale rates. SAG-AFTRA members were asked to submit to full-body digital scans in exchange for a single day's pay, with studios claiming perpetual rights to those likenesses. The unions said no. The resulting standoff forced the first comprehensive AI provisions ever negotiated into major entertainment labor contracts.

Why 2023 Was Different From Every Previous Technology Dispute

Labor has contested technology for centuries — the Luddites destroyed textile machinery in 1811; telegraphers struck against the railroad in 1883; autoworkers fought automation in the 1960s. What distinguished the 2023 Hollywood strikes was the specificity of the AI threat. Previous technologies displaced workers in adjacent fields or reduced headcount over time. Generative AI threatened to replicate the core creative output of the workers themselves — their voices, their faces, their narrative styles — and do so with existing assets the studios already owned.

The WGA's final contract, ratified on September 27, 2023, established several landmark provisions: studios must disclose to writers if AI-generated material is included in their assignments; writers cannot be required to use AI tools; any AI-generated material cannot be used to undermine a writer's credits or residuals; and human-written material used to train AI must be subject to collective bargaining. These were not aspirational statements — they were enforceable contract terms.

SAG-AFTRA's November 2023 agreement added a critical "digital replica" framework: studios must obtain individual consent for each use of a performer's AI-generated likeness, provide meaningful compensation comparable to what the performer would have earned for the actual work, and describe the intended use in sufficient detail for the performer to make an informed decision.

The Bargaining Table as Regulatory Arena

Congress had not passed comprehensive AI legislation. The EU AI Act was still finalizing. The National Labor Relations Board had not issued AI-specific guidance. Into that regulatory vacuum, collective bargaining stepped. Labor scholars at Cornell's ILR School noted that the WGA and SAG-AFTRA contracts represented the first instance in US history where AI governance was established through the NLRA's collective bargaining process rather than through legislation or agency rulemaking.

This matters beyond Hollywood. The International Alliance of Theatrical Stage Employees (IATSE), representing camera operators, lighting technicians, and editors, watched closely and began incorporating AI language into its own 2024 negotiations. The American Federation of Musicians had separately raised AI voice-cloning concerns with music labels in 2023. The pattern was becoming clear: where government regulation was absent, unions were writing the rules themselves.

Key Precedent

The WGA and SAG-AFTRA 2023 contracts are the first US labor agreements to contain legally binding AI consent, disclosure, and compensation provisions. They set the template that other guilds and unions began adapting immediately — establishing collective bargaining as a primary mechanism for AI governance in the absence of federal legislation.

Key Terms
Digital ReplicaAn AI-generated image, voice, or performance that reproduces the appearance or sound of a specific human being without their real-time participation.
Informed Consent ProvisionA contract clause requiring employers to obtain explicit, specific agreement from a worker before using their likeness or creative output to train or generate AI content.
ResidualCompensation paid to writers or performers each time their work is reused or rebroadcast; the WGA fought to ensure AI could not eliminate residual entitlements.

Algorithmic Management and the Erosion of Worker Autonomy

Before AI wrote scripts, it was already managing warehouse floors, routing delivery drivers, and evaluating nurse performance — invisibly and without appeal.

Amazon's fulfillment centers in the United States have been managed, in substantial part, by algorithmic systems since at least 2013. The "Time Off Task" (TOT) system tracks the seconds between scanner events; workers whose gaps exceed thresholds receive automated warnings. Termination letters — generated without human review — were sent automatically when TOT metrics crossed defined limits. Between 2017 and 2018, Amazon fired more than 300 workers at a single warehouse in Baltimore through this process. The workers had no avenue to appeal to a human manager before the termination was issued.

A 2022 investigation by The Information and subsequent reporting by The Atlantic documented Amazon's use of a system called "Mentor" to score delivery drivers on braking, acceleration, cornering, and distraction — including whether drivers were looking at their phones. The scores determined route assignments and, eventually, whether contract drivers retained their routes at all. Workers reported that the AI flagged innocent behaviors: a sneeze registered as distraction; a sharp brake to avoid a pedestrian lowered their score.

What Algorithmic Management Actually Does

Algorithmic management describes the use of automated data collection, AI-driven analysis, and automated decision-making to direct, evaluate, and discipline workers — often replacing the judgment of human supervisors. It is now documented across warehousing (Amazon, UPS), gig platforms (Uber, Lyft, DoorDash), healthcare (HCA Healthcare nurse staffing algorithms), content moderation (Facebook's contracted workers), and white-collar settings (call centers scoring emotional tone on calls).

A 2022 report by the Economic Policy Institute found that 78% of workers surveyed at companies with more than 100 employees reported some form of algorithmic monitoring. Cornell ILR researchers identified five core functions of algorithmic management that create distinct labor relations problems: task assignment without transparency, performance evaluation without human judgment, discipline without appeal, surveillance without limits, and pace-setting that exceeds safe thresholds.

The pace-setting dimension is particularly acute. Amazon's "rate" system — which sets the number of items a picker must process per hour — is recalibrated by algorithm based on the performance of the fastest workers, effectively creating a productivity ratchet. The rate increases until injuries rise, which is documented: Amazon's worker injury rate is nearly twice the industry average for warehousing, according to the Strategic Organizing Center's analysis of OSHA data from 2015 through 2022.

Union Responses: The Right to Disconnect from the Algorithm

The Amazon Labor Union, which won a historic election at the JFK8 warehouse on Staten Island in April 2022, identified algorithmic management as a central organizing grievance. Workers cited TOT monitoring and rate pressure as key reasons they voted to unionize. The ALU's proposed contract demands included the right to appeal automated disciplinary actions to a human manager, disclosure of the metrics and weights used in performance algorithms, and a cap on rate increases.

In Europe, the picture is more advanced. The European Parliament's Platform Work Directive, finalized in 2024, requires algorithmic transparency for platform workers: they must be told what data is collected, how it is used in decisions, and have a right to human review of automated decisions affecting their work. The UK's Information Commissioner's Office had already issued guidance in 2023 that workers have rights under GDPR to contest purely automated employment decisions.

In the United States, the NLRB General Counsel issued a 2022 memorandum (GC 22-06) concluding that certain employer surveillance and algorithmic management practices may violate Section 7 of the NLRA by chilling workers' rights to organize and engage in concerted activity — the first formal NLRB position on AI-driven workplace monitoring.

Documented Outcome

Amazon's automated termination system at its Baltimore warehouse fired over 300 workers without human review between 2017–2018. When this became public through investigative reporting in 2018, Amazon acknowledged the system and said it was revising it — but did not restore the terminated workers' jobs. The episode is now a standard case study in NLRA enforcement discussions about algorithmic management.

The UPS and UAW Agreements: Proactive AI Bargaining in Traditional Industries

Before the technology arrived in force, two major unions negotiated rules for it — creating a playbook other industries are now following.

In the summer of 2023, while Hollywood was on strike over AI, two other unions were at the bargaining table reaching agreements that contained some of the most detailed AI provisions in US labor history. The Teamsters' July 2023 contract with UPS — covering 340,000 workers and representing the largest private-sector collective bargaining agreement in American history — included provisions requiring UPS to give the Teamsters 90 days' advance notice before deploying any new technology that affects bargaining unit work, and establishing a joint labor-management technology committee with investigative powers.

Separately, the United Auto Workers' tentative agreements with Ford, GM, and Stellantis in October 2023 — reached after a six-week targeted strike — included language requiring that decisions about using AI or automation to eliminate or significantly restructure job classifications must be subject to bargaining. The UAW had successfully made the future of electric vehicle transition, and the AI-driven manufacturing processes that accompany it, a core economic issue rather than a management prerogative.

The UPS–Teamsters Technology Clause: What It Actually Says

The UPS contract ratified in August 2023 contained two interlocking AI provisions. First, a technology notice requirement: UPS must notify the Teamsters at least 90 days before implementing any new technology — including AI systems — that will affect jobs in the bargaining unit. This gives the union time to investigate, consult experts, and demand bargaining on impacts before the technology is deployed.

Second, a joint technology committee: a standing body with equal union and management representation that has the authority to review planned technology deployments, request documentation of how systems work, commission independent technical assessments, and refer disputes to grievance arbitration. This is not a consultative body — it has real procedural teeth.

UPS had already deployed AI extensively in logistics: the ORION (On-Road Integrated Optimization and Navigation) route optimization system processes 250 million address data points per day and saves UPS an estimated 100 million miles of driving annually. The Teamsters were not opposing ORION — they were ensuring that future AI deployments would be subject to joint oversight rather than unilateral implementation.

The UAW Contracts: AI, EVs, and the Future of Manufacturing Work

The UAW's 2023 contracts with the Detroit Three addressed AI indirectly but substantively through two mechanisms. First, by extending full UAW coverage to EV battery plants — facilities where AI-driven manufacturing processes are most prevalent — the union ensured that whatever work remains in those facilities falls under union protection and bargaining rights. GM's Ultium Cells battery plant in Ohio, previously organized separately with weaker terms, was brought fully into the UAW agreement.

Second, the contracts included a "successorship" clause ensuring that if any covered facility is sold or restructured — including through automation that shifts work to a new legal entity — the new owner must honor the UAW agreement. This directly addresses the scenario where a manufacturer might spin off automated processes to a non-union subsidiary to escape labor obligations.

Labor economist Lawrence Mishel, writing in the Economic Policy Institute's analysis of the 2023 auto contracts, noted that the UAW had effectively established that AI-driven restructuring of job classifications is a mandatory subject of bargaining — a principle with implications that extend far beyond the auto industry.

Strategic Insight

Both the Teamsters-UPS and UAW-Detroit Three contracts demonstrate a "proactive bargaining" model: securing procedural rights over future AI deployments (notice, joint committees, mandatory bargaining) rather than reacting to technology after it arrives. This approach forces employers to negotiate on AI before implementation, when workers have maximum leverage.

The Global Picture: AI Labor Policy from the EU to the NLRB

Different regulatory frameworks are producing different outcomes — and the gaps between them are becoming a competitive issue for both workers and employers.

In March 2024, the European Parliament passed the EU AI Act by a vote of 523 to 46 — the world's first comprehensive binding AI regulatory framework. Among its provisions: AI systems used in employment, worker management, and access to self-employment must be classified as high-risk, subject to mandatory conformity assessments, human oversight requirements, and transparency obligations. An AI system that decides whether to hire a worker, set their pay, or terminate their employment cannot simply be deployed; it must be documented, tested, and subject to human review.

Meanwhile, in Washington, the NLRB had issued two significant guidance documents (GC 22-06 in 2022 and GC 23-08 in 2023) and the White House had published its Executive Order on Safe, Secure, and Trustworthy Artificial Intelligence in October 2023. But no comprehensive federal AI labor law had passed. The result: a US worker subject to algorithmic management has fewer enforceable rights than their counterpart in Germany, France, or the Netherlands — unless they are covered by a collective bargaining agreement.

The EU AI Act's Labor Provisions in Practice

The EU AI Act's "high-risk" classification for employment AI creates a concrete compliance burden for employers. Before deploying a covered system, they must: conduct a conformity assessment documenting the system's design, training data, and risk mitigation measures; register the system in a EU-wide database; implement ongoing monitoring; and ensure that all consequential decisions involving the AI are subject to human override. Workers and worker representatives have the right to request explanations for AI-driven employment decisions.

The EU's Platform Work Directive, finalized in 2024, adds sector-specific requirements for gig economy employers: they must inform platform workers of how algorithmic systems affect their work, cannot make solely automated decisions on account deactivation or significant earnings changes without human review, and must allow worker representatives to access information about algorithmic systems. This effectively grants platform workers — historically excluded from labor protections in most EU states — rights comparable to traditional employees in AI-managed workplaces.

Germany's Works Constitution Act (Betriebsverfassungsgesetz) provides an additional layer: works councils (labor-management bodies required at German firms with 5+ employees) have a codetermination right over the introduction of technical equipment used to monitor workers' behavior or performance. This means any AI monitoring system in a German workplace requires works council approval before deployment — a requirement that has delayed or modified several major AI implementations by US multinationals operating in Germany.

The US Regulatory Gap and What Fills It

The absence of comprehensive US federal AI labor legislation means that worker protection in AI-managed workplaces depends on three incomplete mechanisms: existing NLRA rights (which require active organizing and bargaining to leverage), state-level legislation (Illinois, Maryland, and New York City have passed AI hiring audit laws; California has proposed broader protections), and collective bargaining agreements (available only to the approximately 10% of private-sector workers who are unionized).

The Biden administration's October 2023 Executive Order on AI directed OSHA to publish guidance on AI and worker safety, directed the Department of Labor to publish principles for employers using AI in employment decisions, and directed the NLRB to assess whether existing labor law adequately protects workers' rights in AI-managed workplaces. The Department of Labor published its "AI and the Future of Work" principles in April 2024, identifying eight principles including worker voice, transparency, and human oversight — but these are principles, not enforceable regulations.

The practical implication: US workers seeking AI-specific protections must either organize and bargain for them, rely on state laws where applicable, or wait for federal legislation that had not materialized as of early 2025. This creates a significant protection gap relative to EU workers and a competitive asymmetry for US employers operating in both markets.

Regulatory Comparison

A Deliveroo driver in the UK who is deactivated by an algorithm can invoke GDPR Article 22 rights to human review of the automated decision and request an explanation under the UK ICO's employment guidance. A DoorDash driver in the US who is deactivated by an algorithm has no equivalent federal right to human review, no right to explanation, and no right to reinstatement — unless their state has specific legislation or they can demonstrate an NLRA violation through the NLRB complaint process.
