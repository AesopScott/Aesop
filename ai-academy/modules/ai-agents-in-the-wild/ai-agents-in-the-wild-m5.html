Module 5 · Lesson 1

What Research Agents Actually Do

From retrieval tools to autonomous research pipelines — the anatomy of an agent that reads, reasons, and synthesises.
How does an AI agent turn a vague research question into a structured answer — and where does it break down?

When the nonprofit AI safety organisation Ought publicly released its research assistant Elicit in 2022, the tool attracted attention not because it could search academic databases — that already existed — but because it could reason about search results. Elicit would retrieve papers from Semantic Scholar, summarise each one into a structured row of columns (sample size, intervention, outcome), and then synthesise across dozens of studies. By 2024 Elicit claimed over one million registered researchers and had processed more than 200 million papers. The key design choice: the agent decides which columns to extract, asks follow-up search queries autonomously, and flags when its answer confidence is low — all without human prompting at each step.

The Research Agent Stack

A research agent is not a smarter search engine. It is a goal-directed loop: given a research question, it plans a retrieval strategy, executes searches, evaluates relevance, extracts structured information, identifies gaps, and repeats until it can synthesise a defensible answer. The difference from simple retrieval is agency over the process itself.

The stack has four functional layers. Planning converts a broad question into specific search queries and a collection strategy. Retrieval hits structured databases (Semantic Scholar, PubMed, arXiv, the web) or internal document stores. Extraction pulls the specific facts needed — not just relevance-ranked snippets. Synthesis integrates extracted facts into a coherent output and flags uncertainty.

Planning Layer

Decomposes an ambiguous goal into specific sub-queries. Elicit, for example, automatically generates related search queries based on the initial question and then decides which queries to pursue based on preliminary result quality.

Retrieval Layer

Executes searches against external sources. The agent controls which sources to query, the query formulation, and the stopping criterion — unlike a simple API call, this is adaptive.

Extraction Layer

Reads retrieved documents and pulls structured data: sample sizes, confidence intervals, dates, named entities, contradictions. This step transforms unstructured text into comparable data.

Synthesis Layer

Combines extracted facts into a coherent answer. Critical quality signals: does the agent note contradictions across sources? Does it distinguish high-confidence from low-confidence claims?

Key Vocabulary

RAGRetrieval-Augmented Generation — a pattern where a language model is given retrieved documents as context before generating an answer. Research agents typically extend RAG with multi-step retrieval and planning.
Agentic loopThe observe→plan→act→evaluate cycle that a research agent executes repeatedly until the task is complete or a stop condition is reached.
Extraction schemaA structured template the agent uses to pull consistent fields from each retrieved document — e.g., study design, population, outcome measure.
Semantic Scholar APIA public academic database used by Elicit, Consensus, and other research agents as a primary retrieval backend. Indexes ~220 million papers.
Why It Fails

Research agents fail in predictable ways: they hallucinate citations that look real but don't exist; they retrieve papers that are topically adjacent but methodologically irrelevant; they over-synthesise, collapsing contradictory findings into false consensus. Understanding the stack tells you exactly where each failure originates.

Elicit vs. Consensus: Two Design Choices

Both Elicit and Consensus (launched 2023, raised $3M seed) focus on academic research but make different architectural bets. Elicit emphasises extracting structured columns from papers, giving users a spreadsheet-like view of the literature. Consensus attempts to give a single consensus meter — a percentage indicating how many retrieved studies support a claim — trading nuance for speed.

The Consensus approach attracted criticism in 2023 from methodologists who pointed out that the meter collapses study quality: a poorly-designed study supporting a claim counts the same as a large RCT. This is a synthesis layer design decision with epistemic consequences. The meter is fast and scannable; it can also be systematically misleading.

Design Principle

The architecture of a research agent encodes assumptions about what counts as evidence. Extraction schemas, synthesis rules, and confidence indicators are not neutral — they reflect choices about epistemology. Evaluating a research agent means evaluating those choices, not just the output text.

Hallucination and Citation Fabrication

The specific failure mode that makes research agents dangerous — and the technical and design responses that reduce it.
When an AI confidently cites a paper that does not exist, what exactly went wrong — and how do researchers detect and mitigate it?

In June 2023, US District Judge P. Kevin Castel fined attorneys at the firm Levidow, Levidow & Oberman $5,000 after it emerged that a legal brief submitted in the case Mata v. Avianca contained six non-existent court cases — fabricated by ChatGPT and submitted without verification. Each citation had a plausible case name, realistic docket number, and a convincing-sounding summary. The supervising attorney told the court he had not imagined ChatGPT would "just make things up." The episode was widely covered but the underlying mechanism — confidently fluent generation of plausible-sounding false citations — is exactly the failure mode that research-oriented AI agents face at scale.

Why Citation Hallucination Happens

Language models are trained to produce plausible sequences of tokens, not to distinguish between things they "know" and things they're "guessing." When asked for a citation, the model generates a string that looks statistically like a citation — author names, journal name, year, volume, page range — drawn from patterns in training data. There is no internal lookup; there is no verification step. The model does not know it is wrong.

The rate of hallucination varies by domain density. For high-profile topics with many real papers in training data, hallucinations are less frequent but harder to spot — the fake paper resembles real ones closely. For niche topics, the model is more likely to fabricate because it has fewer real examples to draw on and more statistical pressure to fill the citation slot.

Documented Scale

A 2023 study by Walters and Wilder in Journal of the Medical Library Association tested ChatGPT (GPT-3.5) on medical literature questions and found that 47% of generated citations were hallucinated — non-existent DOIs, wrong author names, or papers attributed to the wrong journal. GPT-4 reduced the rate but did not eliminate it.

Mitigation Strategies in Production Systems

Research agents deployed at scale have implemented three classes of mitigation. Each has documented limits.

1
Retrieval-grounded generation: The agent only generates citations for documents it has actually retrieved and can display. Elicit, Perplexity, and Google's NotebookLM all use this approach. Limit: the agent can still misattribute quotes to the wrong retrieved paper, or the retrieved paper may not actually contain the claimed finding.
2
DOI and metadata verification: After generation, the system checks cited DOIs against a live database (CrossRef, Semantic Scholar). Papers that don't resolve are flagged. Limit: a paper can have a real DOI but a fabricated claim about what it says.
3
Verbatim quote grounding: The agent is constrained to quote directly from retrieved text rather than paraphrase. This makes fabrication detectable because the quote either appears in the source or it doesn't. Limit: verbatim quotes can be cherry-picked out of context.
Perplexity's Approach (2024)

Perplexity AI's research mode, launched in 2024, attempted to address hallucination by showing inline citations that link directly to the retrieved page — not the paper's homepage, but the specific URL containing the supporting text. Independent testers found that roughly 30% of Perplexity citations, while linking to real pages, were on pages that did not actually support the claimed statement. Real page, false claim: a subtler failure than a non-existent paper but still a hallucination.

The Attribution Problem

Even when grounded in real retrieved documents, research agents face attribution errors: the correct fact but the wrong source, or the correct source but the wrong fact. This matters because citation serves two functions — locating information and establishing provenance for a claim. An agent that gets the fact right but the citation wrong has broken the provenance chain even if the answer is accurate.

The practical implication for evaluating research agents: always spot-check not just whether cited papers exist, but whether they actually contain the cited claim. This is slower than a DOI check but is the only reliable test of citation quality.

Grounded generationA design pattern where LLM output is constrained to information present in retrieved context, reducing (but not eliminating) hallucination.
Attribution errorA citation failure where the correct fact is cited to the wrong source, or the correct source is cited for a claim it does not actually make.
DOI resolution checkA post-generation verification step that confirms cited DOIs map to real papers in a live database — necessary but not sufficient for citation accuracy.

Multi-Step Research Pipelines

How production research agents chain retrieval, reading, and synthesis into autonomous workflows — and the coordination problems that emerge.
When a research agent runs dozens of retrieval steps autonomously, how do errors compound across the pipeline — and what design choices contain them?

After AlphaFold 2 transformed structural biology, researchers faced a new problem: hundreds of thousands of papers citing AlphaFold structures appeared faster than any team could read. In 2023, several structural biology labs began deploying LLM-based pipeline agents to conduct systematic literature reviews across this corpus. A documented approach at the European Bioinformatics Institute used a four-stage pipeline: query generation, abstract screening, full-text extraction, and cross-paper synthesis — each stage feeding the next, with human review only at the final synthesis stage. The pipeline processed roughly 12,000 abstracts per day. The critical finding from their published evaluation: errors at the abstract screening stage propagated invisibly into synthesis, because downstream stages had no mechanism to re-question earlier decisions.

The Pipeline Architecture

Multi-step research pipelines decompose a complex research task into a sequence of simpler sub-tasks, each handled by a specialised agent or LLM call. The architecture offers scalability — thousands of documents can be processed in parallel — but introduces a new failure mode: cascading errors. A mistake at step 2 is amplified by steps 3, 4, and 5, which treat the step-2 output as ground truth.

The most common production pattern is the funnel: broad retrieval narrows through successive relevance filters to a small set of documents that receive deep extraction. Each filter introduces potential false negatives — relevant papers incorrectly excluded — that cannot be recovered downstream.

1
Query generation: The agent converts the research goal into multiple search queries. Error here means missing entire bodies of relevant work. A 2023 Stanford evaluation of automated literature review agents found agents missed ~20% of highly-cited relevant papers due to query formulation failures.
2
Abstract screening: Retrieved abstracts are classified as relevant or not. False negatives (relevant papers rejected) are unrecoverable in subsequent stages. The EBI pipeline found an 8–12% false negative rate at this stage.
3
Full-text extraction: Passed abstracts trigger full-text retrieval and structured extraction. Errors include misidentifying the study population, confusing primary and secondary outcomes, or extracting from the wrong section of the paper.
4
Cross-paper synthesis: Extracted data is aggregated. Errors from earlier stages are treated as data — a paper incorrectly extracted as showing a positive result will shift the synthesis toward a false positive conclusion.
Error Propagation Math

If each of four pipeline stages has 90% accuracy, the compounded accuracy of the full pipeline is 0.9⁴ = 65.6%. At 95% per stage: 0.95⁴ = 81.5%. Multi-step pipelines demand very high per-stage accuracy to maintain acceptable end-to-end reliability. This is why intermediate human checkpoints matter even in "autonomous" pipelines.

Real Coordination Mechanisms

Production systems have deployed several mechanisms to manage pipeline coordination. Confidence thresholding routes low-confidence decisions to human review rather than passing them downstream. Backwards tracing tags each synthesis claim with the specific extraction step that produced it, enabling audits to trace errors to their origin. Ensemble screening runs abstract screening with two independent LLM calls and only passes papers where both agree — reducing false negatives at the cost of throughput.

OpenAI Deep Research (2025)

OpenAI's Deep Research feature, released February 2025, runs a multi-step web research pipeline that generates sub-questions, searches iteratively, reads pages, and synthesises a report with inline citations. OpenAI's own evaluation reported that on GAIA benchmark tasks, the system achieved 67.4% accuracy — high for a complex multi-step benchmark, but meaning roughly one-in-three complex research tasks contains a significant error.

Gemini Deep Research (2024)

Google's Gemini Advanced Deep Research, launched December 2024, similarly runs multi-step web retrieval. Google's internal evaluation reported strong performance on factual recall but noted the system struggled with tasks requiring synthesis across contradictory sources — precisely the hardest synthesis problem in the pipeline.

Key Terms

Cascading errorA failure mode where an incorrect output at one pipeline stage is accepted as ground truth by subsequent stages, amplifying the original error.
False negative (screening)A relevant document incorrectly classified as irrelevant during the screening stage — excluded from subsequent analysis with no recovery mechanism.
Confidence thresholdingA pipeline design pattern where decisions below a confidence cutoff are flagged for human review rather than passed automatically to the next stage.
Backwards tracingA provenance mechanism that links each output claim to the specific pipeline stage and source document that produced it, enabling error audits.
The Human Checkpoint Question

Every production multi-step research pipeline must answer: at which stages does a human review the output before it propagates? The answer determines the error recovery surface. Fully automated pipelines are fast but fragile; human checkpoints slow throughput but catch compounding errors before they reach synthesis. The right balance depends on the cost of error in the specific domain — lower stakes for internal memos, higher for published meta-analyses or regulatory submissions.

Evaluating Research Agent Output

Practical frameworks for auditing what a research agent actually produced — and deciding how much to trust it.
Given a research report produced by an AI agent, what specific tests would tell you whether to trust it — and what would cause you to reject it?

In April 2023, Nature published an editorial explicitly addressing AI-generated research content. The journal did not ban AI assistants for drafting, but it required that any AI-generated content be disclosed and that authors take full responsibility for accuracy, including citations. The editorial noted that journals had already received manuscripts where AI-generated references cited non-existent papers. Crucially, Nature's stance was not "AI outputs are untrustworthy" — it was "AI outputs require the same rigorous verification applied to any research claim." This is the correct frame for evaluating research agent output: not rejection, but structured verification.

A Four-Test Evaluation Framework

Research agent output can be evaluated along four dimensions. Each test is practical — you can conduct it on any agent output in under an hour for a typical research summary.

1
Citation existence test: For each cited paper, verify the DOI resolves to a real paper via CrossRef.org or doi.org. A non-resolving DOI is an immediate failure. Time cost: ~30 seconds per citation.
2
Claim-source match test: For a random sample of specific factual claims (at least 20%), locate the cited source and verify the source actually supports that claim. Look for: claims not in the source at all; claims from the source's abstract but contradicted in the methods; claims that are technically present but taken out of context.
3
Contradiction coverage test: Search the topic for well-known conflicting studies. Does the agent's output acknowledge them? A synthesis that presents one-sided findings when contradictory evidence is well-documented has either missed the literature or suppressed disagreement — both are failures.
4
Confidence calibration test: Does the agent distinguish between well-replicated findings and preliminary single studies? Does it use appropriate hedging ("one study suggests" vs. "the evidence shows")? Overconfident language is a signal that the synthesis layer has not tracked evidence quality.
Real Benchmark: Ramirez et al. 2024

A 2024 evaluation study by Ramirez and colleagues at the University of Texas tested five research AI tools (Elicit, Consensus, Perplexity, Scite, ChatGPT with browsing) against a gold-standard systematic review on a medical topic. On the claim-source match test, Elicit had the lowest error rate (12% of claims not directly supported by cited source); ChatGPT with browsing had the highest (41%). All tools passed the citation existence test at above 90%. The contradiction coverage test showed all tools missed at least some well-documented contradictory evidence, with Consensus missing the most due to its consensus-metric design.

Tool-Specific Trust Signals

Different research agents provide different trust signals. Knowing what to look for in each tool's interface reduces verification time.

High-Trust Signals

Direct links to specific pages (not just papers). Verbatim quotes from sources. Explicit uncertainty flags ("only 2 studies found on this"). Separate confidence ratings per claim. Visible retrieval provenance — which search query produced which source.

Low-Trust Signals

Citations given only as author/year with no direct link. Confident synthesis language ("the evidence clearly shows") without citation count. Single consensus number with no distribution. No acknowledgment of contradictory findings. Inability to show what was searched.

The Verification Proportionality Principle

Not all research agent outputs require the same verification investment. The appropriate depth of verification scales with the cost of error. Internal background research for a meeting: skim-level verification, spot-check two or three claims. Published academic work: full four-test evaluation of all claims. Regulatory submission or legal brief: independent human verification of every source, treating the agent output as a first draft only.

The Mata v. Avianca case is the canonical example of applying insufficient verification to a high-stakes context. The attorneys treated the AI output as reliable without any citation existence test — the most basic check — in a domain where fabricated citations have professional and legal consequences.

Claim-source matchThe verification check that a cited source actually contains the specific claim attributed to it — as opposed to merely existing or being topically relevant.
Contradiction coverageThe degree to which a synthesis acknowledges well-documented conflicting evidence, as opposed to presenting a one-sided literature.
Confidence calibrationThe degree to which an agent's language about certainty matches the actual strength of the underlying evidence — well-replicated vs. preliminary findings.
Verification proportionalityThe principle that verification depth should scale with the cost of error in the specific use context — not all research outputs require the same scrutiny.
Practical Takeaway

Research agents are most reliably used as retrieval accelerators — finding relevant papers faster than manual search — and least reliably used as autonomous synthesisers trusted to produce publication-ready conclusions. The more human judgment enters the synthesis stage, the more the agent's strengths (speed, breadth) are preserved while its weaknesses (hallucination, overconfidence) are managed.
