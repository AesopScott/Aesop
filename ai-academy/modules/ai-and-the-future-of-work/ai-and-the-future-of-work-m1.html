AI and the Future of Work · Module 1 · Lesson 1

The Automation Research Landscape

What economists have actually measured — and why the headlines keep getting it wrong.

When researchers Carl Benedikt Frey and Michael Osborne published "The Future of Employment" that autumn, the number they attached to the United States labour force — 47 percent of jobs at high risk of computerisation — ricocheted through newsrooms from the Financial Times to Der Spiegel. What the headlines rarely mentioned was the careful methodological caveat buried in the paper: they were estimating susceptibility of occupations, not the probability that any individual worker would be displaced.

That distinction matters enormously. In the decade since, a cascade of follow-on studies — from the OECD, McKinsey Global Institute, Brookings Institution, and MIT — has sharpened, revised, and in several important cases reversed the original finding. Understanding what those studies actually say is the foundation of any serious analysis of AI and the future of work.

The Frey-Osborne Methodology and Its Critics

Frey and Osborne scored 702 U.S. occupations using a machine-learning classifier trained on nine task-level "bottlenecks" — perception, manipulation, creative intelligence, social intelligence, and five sub-categories — that they judged difficult to automate. Their model was explicitly a thought experiment about technical feasibility, not a labour-market forecast.

In 2016 the OECD's Melanie Arntz, Terry Gregory, and Ulrich Zierahn replicated the exercise at the task level rather than the occupation level, using PIAAC survey data for 21 countries. Their headline figure: 9 percent of jobs in OECD countries are at high risk. The gap between 47 percent and 9 percent is methodological: most occupations contain a mix of automatable and non-automatable tasks. A cashier does cash handling (highly automatable) but also handles complaints, calls for assistance, and reads customer cues (much less so).

A 2017 McKinsey Global Institute report introduced a third lens: instead of job-level or task-level risk, it estimated the share of work time across all occupations that could be automated using then-current technology. Their number was approximately 50 percent of work activities globally — but only about 5 percent of occupations could be fully automated. The remaining impact shows up as job transformation, not elimination.

What Historical Technology Cycles Tell Us

MIT economists Daron Acemoglu and Pascual Restrepo have produced the most granular empirical work on industrial robots since 2018. Studying U.S. commuting zones from 1990 to 2007, they found that each additional robot per 1,000 workers was associated with a reduction of 3–6 jobs and a 0.25–0.5 percent wage decline in the local labour market. Importantly, this was a period before modern AI; the effect was attributable to programmable industrial automation in manufacturing.

Yet aggregate U.S. employment grew throughout that period. This apparent paradox is explained by what economic historians call Baumol's cost disease in reverse — when productivity rises in automatable sectors, labour shifts toward service, care, and knowledge work where productivity is harder to improve. ATMs did not eliminate bank tellers: between 1995 and 2010 the number of bank teller positions in the U.S. grew, because cheaper ATM operations allowed banks to open more branches, each requiring human staff for relationship banking.

The ATM example, documented by economist James Bessen at Boston University, is one of the most-cited illustrations of what researchers call task recomposition: automation removes specific tasks from a job while preserving or expanding the remaining human role.

Key Finding

No credible peer-reviewed study predicts net job destruction from AI across the full economy in a static sense. The consensus debate is about pace of transition, distribution of gains, and which workers bear adjustment costs — not whether total employment will collapse.

The GPT-4 Wave: New Evidence Arrives

The 2023 paper by Eloundou, Manning, Mishkin, and Rock — researchers from OpenAI and the University of Pennsylvania — introduced a new concept: exposure, defined as the extent to which access to an LLM could reduce the time needed to complete a task by at least 50 percent. Analysing 1,016 occupations, they found that approximately 80 percent of the U.S. workforce is in an occupation where at least one task is LLM-exposed. More strikingly, roughly 19 percent of workers are in occupations where over 50 percent of tasks are exposed.

This is a fundamentally different pattern than previous automation waves. High exposure correlates with high wages and high education levels — software developers, lawyers, financial analysts — rather than the routine manual and cognitive tasks that drove 1990s–2000s automation research. This "reversal" is one of the defining empirical features of the current AI transition.

Bottom Line for This Module

The evidence base on AI and work is large, contested, and fast-moving. Reading it well requires distinguishing technical feasibility from economic adoption, task exposure from job elimination, and aggregate effects from distributional effects. Each of the next three lessons focuses on one of these distinctions in depth.

From Feasibility to Adoption: The Gap That Changes Everything

Technology can do something long before the economy decides it should — and that delay is not random.

In November 2016 the celebrated AI researcher Geoffrey Hinton made a prediction that became the most-quoted single sentence in AI-and-work discourse: "It's quite obvious that we should stop training radiologists now." Deep learning models had just matched radiologist performance on pneumonia detection in a Stanford study. Eight years later, the United States has more practising radiologists than it did in 2016 — and applications to radiology residency programmes hit a record high in 2023.

Hinton's forecast was technically reasonable: the performance claims were real. What it missed was everything that sits between a model performing well on a benchmark and a health system reorganising its workforce. Understanding that gap is not a footnote to automation research — it is the central variable.

Why Adoption Lags Capability

Economists identify several structural reasons why even clearly cost-effective technologies spread slowly through labour markets.

Complementary investments. The 2022 work of Erik Brynjolfsson, Daniel Rock, and Chad Syverson on the "productivity J-curve" shows that AI generates measured output gains only after firms have restructured workflows, retrained workers, and rebuilt data infrastructure — investments that take three to ten years. Their analysis of U.S. firm-level data found that AI-adopting firms show lower productivity in years one and two of adoption before eventually exceeding control-group peers.

Regulatory and liability constraints. In healthcare, finance, and law, deployment of AI decision-support tools requires FDA clearance, SEC guidance, bar association rules, or equivalent approval processes. The FDA cleared its first AI-based radiology diagnostic aid in 1995; by 2023 there were 692 cleared AI/ML-enabled devices — but hospital adoption of diagnostic AI remains patchy, partly because liability for AI-assisted misdiagnosis is legally unsettled.

Labour contract and organisational inertia. A 2020 survey by the World Economic Forum found that 43 percent of business leaders cited "existing employment contracts" as a significant barrier to AI task automation. Collective bargaining agreements, civil service rules, and professional licencing requirements all slow the rate at which technically feasible substitution becomes economically actual substitution.

The Radiology Case in Detail

Stanford's 2017 CheXNet paper reported that their 121-layer convolutional neural network outperformed the average radiologist on pneumonia detection from chest X-rays on a held-out test set. The paper was rigorous and the finding replicated. Yet a 2021 analysis by Luke Oakden-Rayner at the University of Adelaide examined the same benchmark and found that it contained significant shortcut learning artefacts: the model had learned to detect hospitals' specific imaging hardware signatures rather than anatomical pathology, and performance degraded when tested on images from different hospital systems.

This is not a scandal — it is how science works. But it illustrates why performance on a benchmark and readiness for deployment are separated by extensive validation, testing on diverse populations, integration with electronic health records, and institutional approval processes that can take half a decade.

Meanwhile, radiologists' work has been recomposed. AI pre-screening tools now route the most urgent cases to the top of radiologists' worklists — a workflow change that increases throughput without reducing headcount. The American College of Radiology reported in 2023 that AI is now used in some capacity by approximately 30 percent of U.S. radiology practices, primarily for triage and workflow optimisation rather than autonomous diagnosis.

The General Principle

Economic historians David Autor and Anna Salomons found in a 2018 cross-country analysis that technologies which appear most threatening to a specific occupation in year zero typically increase employment in that occupation over the following decade, because the productivity gains expand the market for the occupation's services. This pattern — called the productivity-demand effect — does not guarantee good outcomes for every worker, but it consistently delays and moderates displacement at the occupation level.

Where Adoption Is Actually Fast

The barriers above are not universal. Three conditions correlate with faster adoption: low regulatory burden (content moderation, back-office data processing), high task repetition (document classification, invoice processing), and low liability exposure (marketing copy, internal communication).

A 2023 McKinsey survey of 1,684 organisations across sectors found that 40 percent of respondents reported using generative AI in at least one business function — but that usage was concentrated in marketing, sales, and software engineering, with healthcare, legal, and financial services showing substantially lower rates. This uneven adoption pattern means that aggregate forecasts about AI's labour-market impact must be disaggregated by sector and task type to be useful.

Lesson Takeaway

Technical feasibility is necessary but not sufficient for labour-market displacement. The relevant research question is not "can AI do this task?" but "under what institutional, regulatory, and economic conditions will AI be deployed to do this task, at scale, faster than the workforce can adjust?" The answer varies enormously by sector.

Who Bears the Adjustment Costs?

When technology shifts the labour market, the gains and losses are not evenly distributed — and the evidence on who loses is more specific than most discussions admit.

The research that changed how economists think about trade and technology's distributional effects grew out of a puzzle. For decades, standard trade theory predicted that when cheap manufactured imports arrive in a country, displaced workers move to other sectors and wages eventually rebalance. In 2013, David Autor, David Dorn, and Gordon Hanson published "The China Syndrome" — an analysis showing that in U.S. commuting zones most exposed to Chinese import competition, wages stayed depressed and workers did not move to other sectors. Twenty years later those regions were still economically scarred.

The paper's methodological approach — studying local labour market shocks rather than national aggregates — transformed how researchers study automation. And its finding — that adjustment is slow, painful, and geographically concentrated — is the empirical foundation for taking displacement costs seriously even when aggregate employment is stable.

The Anatomy of Displacement Risk

The most granular picture of who faces displacement risk from AI comes from combining several datasets. Acemoglu and Restrepo's robot exposure data, cross-referenced with the Bureau of Labor Statistics Occupational Employment Statistics, reveals a consistent pattern: routine cognitive and routine manual tasks — data entry, basic customer service, assembly — face the highest displacement risk, and these tasks are disproportionately performed by workers without college degrees, in the bottom two quintiles of the wage distribution.

For generative AI specifically, the exposure pattern is different but the distributional concern is not absent. The Goldman Sachs Economics Research team (2023) estimated that approximately 300 million full-time-equivalent jobs globally contain tasks susceptible to generative AI substitution. They found that administrative and legal support occupations — legal secretaries, paralegals, court reporters — face the highest share of substitutable tasks while being paid significantly less than the lawyers and judges whose work the AI is often described as threatening.

This creates a bifurcation: senior lawyers may gain productivity from AI-assisted research; junior paralegals may lose positions as firms reduce entry-level headcount. The technological risk falls at the top of the occupation hierarchy in terms of task complexity, but the employment risk concentrates in the support roles at the bottom.

Geographic Concentration of Adjustment Costs

Following the Autor-Dorn-Hanson methodology, several research teams have mapped AI and robot exposure at the commuting-zone level. A 2019 Brookings Institution analysis by Mark Muro, Robert Maxim, and Jacob Whiton found that AI automation risk is not randomly distributed across U.S. geography: smaller metropolitan areas and rural commuting zones — particularly in the South and Midwest — show higher shares of at-risk occupations and lower capacity to absorb displaced workers into alternative employment.

Las Vegas, for example, ranked among the most AI-exposed metropolitan areas in their analysis, with a large share of employment in food preparation, hospitality, and transportation — occupations where AI-enabled robotics and autonomous vehicles pose genuine medium-term displacement risk. By contrast, the San Francisco Bay Area ranked among the least exposed despite having the highest concentration of AI development, because high-education knowledge work is both better-compensated and more likely to experience task recomposition than elimination.

Empirical Warning

Studies that report only aggregate or average effects of AI on employment systematically miss the most important policy-relevant question: which specific workers, in which specific places, face the highest adjustment costs, and what is the speed of that adjustment relative to labour-market reabsorption capacity? Aggregates that show small net effects can mask very large gross flows with very unequal distribution.

The Role of Firm Size and Market Power

A less-discussed distributional dimension is firm-level. Acemoglu, Lelarge, and Restrepo (2020) used French firm-level data to show that the productivity gains from robot adoption accrue primarily to large firms with market power, while smaller competitors face margin pressure and are more likely to exit or shed labour. This means that automation can simultaneously increase output, increase corporate profits, and reduce aggregate wages paid — a combination that is possible precisely when productivity gains are not competed away in labour or product markets.

For AI specifically, the capital requirements for frontier model development — estimated at $50–$500 million per training run for frontier models — mean that the technology is concentrated in a small number of very large firms. The question of how those firms' productivity gains are distributed — to shareholders, to high-skill employees, or to consumers — is ultimately a question of market structure and wage-setting power, not just technology.

The Core Distributional Insight

Technology does not determine distribution. The same automation capability can produce very different distributional outcomes depending on labour-market institutions (unions, minimum wages, social insurance), regulatory environments, and the degree of competition in product and capital markets. Evidence that aggregate employment is stable tells us almost nothing about whether the transition is equitable.

New Jobs, Emerging Roles, and the Limits of Prediction

Every major technology wave creates occupations that did not previously exist — but the evidence on how fast and for whom is more constrained than optimists typically claim.

In 2017, economists David Autor and Anna Salomons calculated that roughly 60 percent of current U.S. employment is in occupational categories that did not exist in 1940. The figure is real, frequently cited, and easily misread. It does not mean that technology will automatically generate enough new jobs to replace the ones it displaces — only that historically, it has. The mechanism, the pace, and the distribution of those new jobs are separate questions, and on all three the evidence is more sobering than the headline number suggests.

How New Occupations Emerge: The Evidence

David Autor's 2022 paper "New Frontiers: The Origins and Content of New Work, 1940–2018" traces the occupational categories that emerged decade by decade. The most consistent pattern: new occupations cluster around technology development and deployment in the decade of a technology's emergence, then around complementary services as the technology matures. The computing wave of the 1980s and 1990s first created programmer and systems analyst roles; later, it created user experience designers, IT security analysts, and data scientists.

The AI wave is already generating new occupation categories. LinkedIn's 2023 "Future of Work" report documented a 74 percent year-over-year increase in job postings mentioning "AI" or "generative AI" as a required skill between 2022 and 2023. New role titles — AI Prompt Engineer, LLM Fine-tuning Specialist, AI Ethics Reviewer, AI-Assisted Clinician Workflow Coordinator — appear in employer databases for the first time in 2022 and 2023.

But here is the constraint: the workers currently best positioned to fill these roles are software engineers, data scientists, and domain experts with graduate-level technical education — not the workers most at risk of displacement from AI automation of routine tasks. The new jobs created by AI are not, in the near term, accessible to the workers AI is displacing.

The Reskilling Gap: Measured, Not Asserted

The World Economic Forum's "Future of Jobs Report 2023" surveyed 803 companies across 27 industries and 45 economies, collectively employing 11.3 million workers. The report found that companies expect 44 percent of workers' core skills to change by 2027. But when asked about reskilling capacity, only 47 percent of respondents said they would be able to retrain workers threatened with displacement, and only 42 percent expected workers to move into new roles created by technology within the business.

The gap between skill-change expectations and retraining capacity is not new. Accenture's 2018 "Reskilling Imperative" analysis found that the average time for a displaced worker to return to pre-displacement wage levels in the U.S. was approximately six years — a figure that had barely changed since the 1990s despite decades of retraining programme investment. MIT's Work of the Future Task Force (2021) concluded that the main constraint is not workers' willingness to retrain but the absence of clear occupational pathways from at-risk roles to emerging ones.

The Pace Problem

Historical technology transitions — steam, electricity, computing — unfolded over 30–60 years. Generative AI capabilities advanced from GPT-2 (2019) to GPT-4 (2023) in four years. Whether institutional capacity for reskilling, regulatory adaptation, and social insurance can keep pace with a technology cycle that is compressing historical timelines by an order of magnitude is the central open question in the field — and it is genuinely open.

What the Optimistic and Pessimistic Scenarios Share

Optimistic forecasts — McKinsey's 2017 estimate that AI could create 20–50 million new jobs globally by 2030, or the WEF's 2020 estimate of 97 million new roles by 2025 — and pessimistic ones share a common limitation: they are extrapolations from current trends, not forecasts built on detailed micro-level behavioural models. Both assume that adoption rates, policy responses, and macroeconomic conditions will follow historical patterns. Neither builds in the possibility that AI capability growth continues to accelerate, nor that institutions respond faster or slower than observed precedent.

What the evidence allows us to say with confidence is narrower but more actionable: the workers most at risk are identifiable by task composition and geography; the reskilling gap is real and large; new AI-enabled roles are already appearing but require significant education investment to access; and the pace of transition is compressing in ways that historical analogies may not adequately capture. These four facts are the empirical foundation on which any serious analysis of AI and work must stand.

Module 1 Summary

The evidence base on AI and work is large, methodologically diverse, and rapidly evolving. The key distinctions — technical feasibility vs. economic adoption, task exposure vs. job elimination, aggregate effects vs. distributional effects, and near-term recomposition vs. long-term reskilling — frame every substantive debate in the field. The remaining modules build directly on this empirical foundation.
