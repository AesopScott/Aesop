AI Ethics & Decision-Making · Introduction

When we teach machines to choose, we reveal what we never decided about ourselves.

Ethics used to be a subject studied in universities. AI made it a subject debated in procurement meetings.

For most of human history, the hard ethical questions were academic: the trolley problem, the lifeboat, the dilemma you would face in some imaginable future. They were puzzles, not deliverables.

Then we built machines that had to actually answer them. A self-driving car has to decide, in milliseconds, who to harm in an unavoidable accident. A medical AI has to decide how to weigh a 3% chance of catching a cancer against a 30% chance of a false-positive biopsy. A hiring AI has to decide what qualified means across cultures and decades of biased training data.

These aren't academic problems anymore. They are shipping in products. This course is about the ethics of AI as it is actually being built — not the philosophy seminar, but the choices engineers, managers, policymakers, and users have to make when the right answer is contested and the deployment date is Tuesday.

AI Ethics & Decision Making · Module 1 · Lesson 1

When Machines Decide

What it means for an AI system to make a decision — and who is responsible

In 2018, Amazon scrapped an AI recruiting tool it had been quietly building for four years. The system was supposed to screen resumes automatically — ranking candidates and surfacing the best ones. It had been trained on ten years of hiring data. The problem: Amazon's tech workforce had been overwhelmingly male for ten years. The system learned the pattern. It began penalizing resumes that included the word "women's" — as in "women's chess club" — and downgrading graduates of all-women's colleges.

No one programmed the bias in. The engineers didn't intend it. The system simply did what it was designed to do: replicate the past. The decision-making had been automated. The accountability had not.

What "AI Decision-Making" Actually Means

When we say an AI "makes a decision," we mean a system produces an output — a score, a classification, a recommendation, a flag — that determines or substantially influences what happens to a person. The AI doesn't deliberate. It doesn't weigh options consciously. It applies a learned function to input data and produces a result.

This distinction matters enormously for ethics. Human decision-making is embedded in context, relationships, and accountability. We can ask a human why they decided something. We can argue with them, appeal to them, hold them responsible. Most automated decision systems offer none of this by default — they produce outputs without explanations, and the chain of responsibility for those outputs is often deliberately obscured.

High-Stakes AI Decisions Are Already Everywhere

AI systems currently influence who gets a loan, who gets a job interview, what bail amount a judge sees recommended, which social media posts get amplified, whether a health insurance claim is approved, and which students get flagged as "at risk." These are not hypothetical futures — they are operational systems making consequential determinations about real people right now.

The Accountability Gap

Automated decisions create what ethicists call an accountability gap: when a system produces a harmful outcome, responsibility is diffused across developers, deployers, and data sources in ways that make it easy for every party to claim they aren't the responsible one. The developer says they built a tool; the deployer says they used it as directed; the data provider says they just provided data. The person harmed is left with no clear avenue for recourse.

This gap is not accidental. Opacity in AI systems is often commercially useful — it prevents scrutiny, speeds adoption, and insulates organizations from accountability. Ethical AI requires actively closing this gap: building systems whose decisions can be explained, audited, and contested.

Fairness Is Not One Thing

Why the definitions of algorithmic fairness contradict each other — and what that means

In 2016, a ProPublica investigation found that COMPAS — a risk-assessment tool used in criminal sentencing across multiple U.S. states — was twice as likely to falsely flag Black defendants as high-risk compared to white defendants. The company that made COMPAS, Northpointe, disputed the finding. They published their own analysis showing the tool was fair: among defendants it scored as high-risk, Black and white defendants reoffended at the same rates.

Both analyses were mathematically correct. They were measuring different things — and both called it fairness.

The Impossibility Problem

Researchers Chouldechova and Kleinberg independently proved in 2017 what the COMPAS debate illustrated empirically: multiple common definitions of fairness are mathematically incompatible — you cannot satisfy them simultaneously when base rates differ across groups. This is not an engineering problem awaiting a better algorithm. It is a fundamental constraint.

The major competing definitions include: demographic parity (equal positive prediction rates across groups), equalized odds (equal true positive and false positive rates), calibration (equal accuracy of score predictions), and individual fairness (similar individuals treated similarly). When underlying rates differ between groups — as crime conviction rates do between populations with different histories of policing — satisfying one definition forces violation of another.

The COMPAS Disagreement in Numbers

ProPublica found Black defendants were labeled high-risk but didn't reoffend at roughly twice the rate of white defendants (false positive rate disparity). Northpointe found that among those labeled high-risk, Black and white defendants reoffended at similar rates (calibration parity). Both were right. The question "which measure of fairness applies?" is not technical — it's political and ethical, reflecting whose errors matter most.

Fairness Is a Value Choice

Choosing a fairness metric is not a neutral technical act. It is a decision about whose errors are more acceptable — and that decision reflects values, power, and whose interests are centered. A system optimized for false positive parity protects people from undeserved negative labels. A system optimized for calibration accuracy protects the predictive integrity of scores. When resources are limited, these goals conflict.

This is why "we use a fair algorithm" cannot be an ethical end-point. Any algorithm will be "fair" by some definition and unfair by others. The ethical question is: which definition was chosen, who chose it, who was consulted, and who bears the cost of the tradeoffs that were made.

The Trolley Problem Has a Server Farm

How AI encodes moral decisions — and why that encoding matters

When the MIT Media Lab published the Moral Machine experiment in 2018, they had collected 40 million moral decisions from people in 233 countries — each asked to decide, in an autonomous vehicle scenario, who should be sacrificed when an accident is unavoidable. The results revealed sharp cultural divergence: Western, educated, individualist societies prioritized saving more lives; collectivist societies weighted deference to the elderly; some clusters prioritized saving higher-status individuals.

The researchers weren't designing an autonomous vehicle ethics system. But someone is. When a company encodes a preference into a vehicle's collision algorithm, they are making a moral decision on behalf of every person who ever rides in that car.

Ethics by Design — Whether You Mean to or Not

Every AI system that makes consequential decisions encodes moral preferences — about whose interests matter more, which risks are acceptable, which errors are worse. This is true even when the designers are not thinking about ethics. Choosing an optimization objective, selecting training data, setting a threshold for action: each is a moral choice masquerading as a technical parameter.

The autonomous vehicle trolley problem is vivid but not representative. The less dramatic version happens constantly: a content recommendation algorithm optimizes for engagement and amplifies outrage; a predictive policing tool decides which neighborhoods warrant more surveillance; a medical triage algorithm assigns urgency scores that determine who waits. In each case, values are embedded in code — and rarely disclosed.

Whose Values Get Embedded?

The demographics of AI development teams are not representative of the populations affected by AI systems. When value choices are embedded without explicit deliberation, they tend to reflect the assumptions, experiences, and blind spots of those who built the system. This is not malice — it is the predictable outcome of making ethical decisions without recognizing them as such.

Consent and the Ethics of Proxy Decisions

When an AI system makes a moral choice on behalf of people who never agreed to that choice — who don't know the choice was made, and cannot opt out — it raises a consent problem. The person in an autonomous vehicle did not vote on which collision algorithm to use. The patient whose triage priority is determined by an AI did not consent to the values embedded in its scoring.

This is ethically distinct from, say, a law being enacted through democratic process. AI moral encoding happens in private, without deliberation, and is almost never disclosed to the people it affects. Meaningful AI ethics requires surfacing these choices and creating mechanisms for affected people to have input into them.

Designing for Accountability

What meaningful AI ethics actually requires — technically, organizationally, and legally

In 2019, Robert Williams was arrested in his front yard in front of his wife and daughters. Detroit police had matched his driver's license photo against a grainy surveillance image using facial recognition software — a match the software flagged with confidence. The match was wrong. Williams was held for thirty hours before detectives realized the error. He was the first known American publicly identified as wrongfully arrested based on a facial recognition false positive.

The system that produced the erroneous match had known accuracy disparities across demographic groups. The police department had no policy governing when facial recognition matches required additional human verification before an arrest. There were no guardrails. There was no accountability. There was just an algorithm and a pair of handcuffs.

What Accountability Actually Requires

Accountability in AI is not achieved by stating that humans are "in the loop." A human who rubber-stamps algorithmic outputs without meaningful ability to question them is not an accountability mechanism — they are liability laundering. Real accountability requires specific structural elements: explainability (the system can articulate why it produced an output), auditability (the system can be independently examined for errors and biases), contestability (affected people can challenge outputs and have them reviewed), and redress (harmful outcomes have defined remediation pathways).

The EU AI Act's Risk-Based Framework

The EU's AI Act (2024) classifies AI systems by risk level: unacceptable risk (banned), high risk (heavily regulated, requires human oversight and audit), limited risk (transparency obligations), minimal risk (voluntary codes). High-risk systems — including those used in law enforcement, employment, and credit scoring — must meet explainability and auditability requirements before deployment. This is the most comprehensive binding AI ethics framework currently in force.

Beyond Compliance: Ethics as Architecture

Regulatory compliance is a floor, not a ceiling. The Williams case occurred in a jurisdiction with no AI governance requirements. But even with requirements, minimum compliance does not make a system ethical — it makes it legal. Genuine AI ethics requires building accountability into system architecture from the start: designing for explainability before deployment, building audit mechanisms into the data pipeline, creating contestation processes before they are needed.

This is organizationally difficult. Ethics requirements slow development. Explainability often trades off with predictive accuracy. Auditability requires transparency that companies may resist for competitive or legal reasons. Acknowledging these tensions honestly — rather than resolving them with "ethics washing" — is itself an ethical act. The organizations doing AI ethics seriously are the ones that treat it as an engineering constraint, not a communications strategy.
