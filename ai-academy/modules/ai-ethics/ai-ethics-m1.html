AI Ethics & Decision-Making · Introduction

Every Powerful Tool Arrives Before the Rules Do

Why the question of who is responsible when machines make mistakes is the defining question of our era.

In 1888, George Eastman put a camera in ordinary hands and called it the Kodak. Within two years, legal scholars Samuel Warren and Louis Brandeis had published "The Right to Privacy" in the Harvard Law Review — the first serious attempt to articulate what it meant to be photographed without consent in a public place. The technology had arrived; the ethical framework was being invented in real time, under pressure, by people who understood neither photography nor the law well enough to get it fully right. The same gap — tool first, reckoning second — appeared with the telegraph in the 1840s, with broadcast radio in the 1920s, and with the internet in the 1990s. The pattern is not a failure of human foresight. It is a structural feature of how transformative technologies enter the world.

That same gap is now wider and faster-moving than at any prior point. In 2016, a ProPublica investigation called "Machine Bias" documented that the COMPAS recidivism algorithm — used by judges across the United States to set bail and sentence length — was flagging Black defendants as high-risk at nearly twice the rate of white defendants with equivalent histories. No single person had decided to build a racist tool. The bias emerged from training data that encoded decades of unequal policing. No one had looked. The algorithm had been deployed in courts for years before journalists ran the numbers.

This course is about how algorithmic decisions get made, who bears the consequences, and what frameworks exist — and what frameworks still need to be built — for holding both people and systems accountable. It will not make you an AI engineer. It will give you the vocabulary and the analytical habits to ask the right questions when a machine is doing something that matters to someone. That is, at this moment in history, a genuinely important thing to be able to do.

AI Ethics & Decision-Making · Module 1 · Lesson 1

When Machines Decide

Automated decisions are already shaping who gets a loan, who stays in jail, and whose résumé gets read.
Who is responsible when an algorithm gets it wrong — and how do we even know it did?

In 2014, Amazon began building an AI recruiting tool intended to automate the first pass through résumés for technical roles. Engineers trained the model on ten years of hiring decisions — a decade of résumés from people Amazon had chosen to interview or hire. The company's technical workforce during that period was overwhelmingly male. The model learned from that pattern. By 2015 it was systematically downgrading résumés that included the word "women's" — as in "women's chess club" — and penalizing graduates of all-women's colleges. Amazon discovered the problem in 2018 and quietly shut the project down, having never used the tool to make final hiring decisions. The case became one of the most cited examples in AI ethics for a specific reason: no one at Amazon intended to build a sexist tool. The bias was not a bug in the code. It was an inheritance from the data.

What Is an Algorithmic Decision System?

An algorithmic decision system (ADS) is any software that takes input data and produces an output that influences a decision affecting a person's life — whether or not a human reviews that output before it takes effect. The spectrum is wide. At one end: a spam filter quietly routing your email. At the other: a pretrial risk-assessment tool telling a judge whether to set bail at $500 or $50,000.

What these systems share is that they encode choices. Someone decided which features to include in the model. Someone decided what the training data should be. Someone decided what the target variable — the thing being predicted — should represent. Each of those decisions carries values, and those values can harm people if they go unexamined.

The term "algorithmic" is sometimes taken to imply neutrality — as if a mathematical process must be objective. This conflation is one of the most persistent and consequential misunderstandings in contemporary technology policy. Mathematics describes patterns in whatever data it is fed. If the data reflects a world shaped by historical discrimination, the model will, in the absence of explicit correction, reproduce and often amplify that discrimination.

The Accountability Gap

When a human makes a bad decision — a hiring manager, a loan officer, a judge — there are mechanisms, however imperfect, for identifying the decision-maker and holding them responsible. They can be asked to explain their reasoning. Their past decisions can be audited. Legal frameworks like the U.S. Equal Credit Opportunity Act (1974) or Title VII of the Civil Rights Act (1964) can be invoked.

Algorithmic systems complicate this. Consider the 2016 ProPublica investigation into COMPAS (Correctional Offender Management Profiling for Alternative Sanctions), a recidivism-prediction tool used in Wisconsin, Florida, and other states. Northpointe, the company that built COMPAS, refused to disclose the weights and features of its model, citing trade secrecy. Defense attorneys in State v. Loomis (Wisconsin, 2016) argued that a defendant had the right to understand why an algorithm had scored him high-risk before that score influenced his sentencing. The Wisconsin Supreme Court ruled that using the score was permissible as long as it was not the sole factor — a ruling legal scholars have criticized as setting a dangerously low bar.

The gap between the speed at which these systems deploy and the speed at which oversight catches up is not an accident. It is the same gap that appeared with every prior transformative technology. What is different now is the scale: a single algorithm can make or influence millions of consequential decisions before a single audit is conducted.

Why This Matters Now

The European Union's AI Act, passed in 2024, is the first comprehensive legal framework specifically regulating AI systems by risk tier. It categorizes tools like COMPAS-style recidivism predictors as "high-risk" and mandates transparency, human oversight, and documentation. The U.S. has no comparable federal statute as of 2025. This regulatory asymmetry shapes where AI systems get deployed and whose populations bear the cost of their errors.

Key Terms

Algorithmic Decision System (ADS)Software that uses data and statistical models to produce outputs that influence decisions affecting individuals — ranging from content ranking to criminal sentencing.
Training DataThe historical dataset used to build and calibrate a machine-learning model. Biases present in this data tend to be encoded and reproduced by the resulting system.
Proxy VariableA feature that correlates with a protected characteristic (race, gender, age) without explicitly encoding it — allowing discrimination to occur through apparently neutral inputs such as zip code or name.
Accountability GapThe structural mismatch between the speed of algorithmic deployment and the speed of legal, regulatory, or journalistic oversight.
Trade Secrecy DefenseThe legal claim that a company need not disclose a model's internal workings because doing so would reveal proprietary business information — frequently invoked to block algorithmic audits.
The Central Tension

Automated systems promise consistency, scale, and the removal of individual human prejudice from decisions. Each of those promises contains a caveat. Consistency at scale means errors at scale. And the prejudices of individual humans have a way of entering the system at the design stage — encoded in training data — and becoming invisible precisely because they are no longer attributable to any individual.

Three Modes of Algorithmic Harm

Researchers studying algorithmic harm have identified several recurring patterns. Three are especially relevant to this module:

Allocation harm occurs when a system distributes benefits or burdens unequally across groups. Amazon's recruiting tool allocated interview opportunities away from women. COMPAS allocated pre-trial detention toward Black defendants. The harm is material — someone did not get the job, or spent additional months in jail.

Representation harm occurs when a system misrepresents or demeans a group. Early image-recognition systems trained on datasets that over-represented light-skinned faces produced significantly higher error rates for darker-skinned individuals — a finding documented by MIT researcher Joy Buolamwini and published in her 2018 "Gender Shades" study. The technology was not neutral; it had been calibrated against a skewed portrait of humanity.

Procedural harm occurs when a system denies individuals the process they are owed — the right to know a decision was made, to understand its basis, or to contest it. The COMPAS case is a procedural harm as much as an allocation harm: defendants could not see, challenge, or rebut the score that influenced their sentence.

What Fairness Means — and Why There Are Several of Them

Mathematicians have proven that multiple reasonable definitions of algorithmic fairness cannot all be satisfied simultaneously.
If a tool is fair by one definition and unfair by another — which definition should win, and who decides?

In the months after ProPublica published "Machine Bias" in May 2016, Northpointe — the company behind COMPAS — responded with its own analysis. The company argued that their tool was fair because it was calibrated: among defendants who scored "high risk," roughly the same proportion of Black and white defendants actually reoffended. ProPublica's analysis used a different metric — false positive rate parity — and found that Black defendants who would not reoffend were labeled high-risk at twice the rate of white defendants in the same situation. Both analyses were mathematically correct. They were measuring different things. In 2016, researchers Chouldechova and Kleinberg et al. independently proved that these two fairness criteria — calibration and equal false positive rates — cannot both be satisfied at once when base rates of the target outcome differ between groups. The argument was not about which side was lying. It was about what "fair" means, and that question turned out not to have a single answer.

The Major Fairness Definitions

Computer scientists and statisticians have formalized several competing definitions of fairness for classification systems. Understanding them requires no advanced mathematics — only an appreciation for what each definition is trying to protect and who it prioritizes.

Demographic parity requires that a system produce positive outcomes at equal rates across demographic groups. If a loan-approval model approves 40% of white applicants, it should approve 40% of Black applicants. This definition focuses on equal outcomes in aggregate. Its limitation: groups with different base-rate creditworthiness may need to be treated differently to achieve the same approval rate, which may require ignoring legitimately predictive features.

Equalized odds (also called equal opportunity in a weaker form) requires that the system's error rates — both false positives and false negatives — be equal across groups. In criminal justice terms: defendants who would not reoffend should be wrongly labeled high-risk at the same rate regardless of race. This prioritizes equal exposure to erroneous harm. Its limitation: achieving this can require reducing the model's overall accuracy.

Calibration requires that the system's scores mean the same thing across groups — that a 70% predicted risk score corresponds to approximately 70% actual incidence in every demographic subgroup. This is the property Northpointe measured. Its limitation: if underlying base rates differ between groups, calibration is mathematically incompatible with equalized false positive rates.

Individual fairness requires that similar individuals receive similar predictions. This intuitive principle is difficult to operationalize because it requires a domain-specific definition of "similar" — which itself encodes values and can be gamed.

The Impossibility Result

Chouldechova (2017) and Kleinberg et al. (2016) proved that when the base rate of the predicted outcome differs between groups — as recidivism rates differ between demographic groups due to decades of differential policing and prosecution — no algorithm can simultaneously achieve calibration, equal false positive rates, and equal false negative rates. This is not a limitation of current AI. It is a mathematical proof. The choice of which fairness criterion to optimize is a values choice, not a technical one.

Why Base Rates Matter

The impossibility result depends on one precondition: that the base rate of the outcome being predicted differs between the groups being compared. In the criminal justice context, arrest and conviction rates differ by race — not because crime rates differ uniformly, but because policing is not uniformly distributed. Higher-policed communities produce more arrest records. Arrest records feed into training data. Predictions trained on arrest data inherit the geography of policing.

This is what researchers mean when they say that structural inequality is encoded in historical data. The data is not a neutral snapshot of behavior. It is a record of who was watched, who was caught, and who was processed through a system that was not itself race-neutral. A model trained on that data will reproduce those patterns — and it will do so with the veneer of mathematical objectivity.

The policy implication is uncomfortable: fixing the data may require acknowledging what the data represents. And acknowledging what the data represents means confronting the conditions that produced it — not just the algorithm.

Key Terms

Demographic ParityA fairness criterion requiring equal positive-outcome rates across demographic groups, regardless of group-level differences in the predicted characteristic.
Equalized OddsA fairness criterion requiring equal true positive rates and equal false positive rates across demographic groups — minimizing differential exposure to erroneous harm.
CalibrationA fairness criterion requiring that a model's predicted probabilities correspond to actual outcome frequencies across all demographic groups.
Base RateThe underlying frequency of the outcome being predicted within a given population. Differences in base rates between groups are the precondition for the fairness impossibility result.
Fairness ImpossibilityThe mathematically proven result that calibration, equal false positive rates, and equal false negative rates cannot all be simultaneously achieved when base rates differ between groups.
The Core Lesson

There is no algorithm setting that is simply "fair." Every fairness definition protects some people at the cost of exposing others to a different kind of error. Selecting a fairness criterion is a political and ethical decision that must be made by humans — preferably by humans who include those most affected by the system's errors. Treating it as a purely technical decision is itself an ethical choice, and usually not a neutral one.

Transparency, Explainability, and the Right to a Reason

Black-box systems make decisions that affect millions of people. Courts, regulators, and researchers are still debating whether anyone has a legal right to understand why.
When does "the algorithm decided" function as an explanation — and when does it function as an excuse?

In November 2019, entrepreneur David Heinemeier Hansson tweeted that Apple Card — whose credit decisions were made by a Goldman Sachs algorithm — had given him a credit limit twenty times higher than it had given his wife, despite their filing taxes jointly and his wife having a higher credit score. The tweet went viral. Steve Wozniak replied that the same had happened to him and his wife. New York's Department of Financial Services opened an investigation. Goldman Sachs' stated response was that the algorithm used individual, not household, credit histories — a technically accurate answer that did not explain the disparity and revealed essentially nothing about how the model worked. A Goldman spokesperson added: "We do not make credit decisions based on gender." The company could not actually demonstrate this, because the algorithm was opaque even to its operators. The investigation concluded in 2021 finding no fair lending violations — while simultaneously noting that Goldman lacked the documentation to fully explain the model's decision logic. An exoneration built on the absence of evidence rather than its presence.

Transparency vs. Explainability

These two terms are often used interchangeably but refer to different properties of an AI system.

Transparency refers to visibility into a system's architecture, data, training process, and decision logic — the ability to inspect what the system is and how it was built. A system is transparent if a qualified examiner can access its code, its training data, its feature set, and its objective function. Most commercial AI systems are not transparent, for competitive and legal reasons.

Explainability refers to the ability to produce a comprehensible account of a specific decision — why this applicant was denied, why this defendant was scored high-risk. Explainability does not require full transparency; there are techniques (such as LIME and SHAP, developed around 2016–2018) that can generate local explanations for individual predictions from complex models without revealing the model's full architecture.

The difference matters because different stakeholders need different things. A regulator auditing a credit system needs transparency. An individual applicant who was denied a loan needs explainability — a reason they can understand and, if necessary, contest. Providing one does not guarantee the other.

The Right to Explanation in Law

The General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR), effective in the European Union since May 2018, includes provisions in Articles 13, 14, and 22 that collectively create something approaching a right to explanation for automated decisions. Article 22 specifies that data subjects have the right not to be subject to decisions based solely on automated processing that produce significant legal or similarly significant effects — and the right to obtain human review, express their point of view, and contest the decision. Recital 71 specifies that such processing should include "suitable measures to safeguard the data subject's rights," including "the right to obtain an explanation of the decision reached after such assessment."

Legal scholars have debated intensely whether GDPR creates a true, enforceable right to explanation or merely a right to some procedural intervention. Bryce Goodman and Seth Flaxman's 2017 analysis in the European Journal of Law and Technology argued the right is meaningful. Sandra Wachter, Brent Mittelstadt, and Luciano Floridi published a rebuttal the same year arguing the right as written is far weaker and procedurally unenforceable without additional regulatory guidance.

In the United States, no federal law creates a comparable right as of 2025. The Equal Credit Opportunity Act requires lenders to provide applicants with the principal reasons for adverse credit decisions — a weak form of explainability that was designed for human decision-makers and fits awkwardly onto complex models. The Consumer Financial Protection Bureau has issued guidance urging lenders to ensure model outputs can be explained in these terms, but enforcement has been inconsistent.

The Explanation Paradox

The most accurate predictive models (deep neural networks, large ensemble methods) are frequently the least explainable. Simpler, more interpretable models (logistic regression, decision trees) are often somewhat less accurate. Requiring full explainability may mean accepting lower-performing models. This is a genuine trade-off, and different domains justify different positions on it. For a spam filter, low explainability may be acceptable. For a pretrial detention decision, it may not be — regardless of the accuracy cost.

Contesting Algorithmic Decisions

Even where an explanation is technically available, contesting an algorithmic decision presents obstacles that differ from contesting a human decision. When a loan officer denies a loan, there is a person who made a judgment call that can be interrogated, appealed, and — if discriminatory — litigated. When a model denies a loan, several complications arise:

Attribution difficulty. Which feature drove the denial? In a model with hundreds of inputs, the contribution of any single feature may be small, indirect, and mediated through complex interactions. SHAP values can approximate feature contributions, but approximate attributions are harder to contest than a human's stated reason.

The moving target problem. If the criteria for a favorable decision are known in detail, applicants may game them — not by improving the underlying characteristic, but by altering the surface features the model uses as proxies. This is sometimes called "Goodhart's Law": when a measure becomes a target, it ceases to be a good measure.

Disparate impact vs. disparate treatment. U.S. civil rights law recognizes two theories of discrimination. Disparate treatment requires showing intentional discrimination. Disparate impact requires showing a facially neutral practice has a discriminatory effect — and that it cannot be justified by business necessity. Algorithmic systems are more amenable to disparate impact claims, but courts have not uniformly accepted that statistical disparity alone is sufficient to trigger legal liability without demonstrating an alternative, less discriminatory approach.

Oversight, Accountability, and Who Is Responsible

When an algorithm harms someone, responsibility diffuses across designers, deployers, and regulators. That diffusion is not accidental — it is a structural feature that must be deliberately countered.
Can a system be responsible for harm — or does responsibility always, ultimately, belong to a person?

In May 2023, the U.S. Equal Employment Opportunity Commission (EEOC) announced a settlement with iTutorGroup, an online tutoring company, over an AI-driven applicant screening tool. The tool had been configured to automatically reject female applicants over 55 and male applicants over 60. The EEOC found that more than 200 qualified applicants had been rejected without human review. The company paid $365,000 to settle the case. The EEOC's chair, Charlotte Burrows, stated in the announcement: "Employers that use AI tools to assist in their hiring process must ensure those tools comply with federal employment laws." The case was notable not because the discrimination was subtle — it was stark — but because it established a clear regulatory position: using an algorithm does not transfer legal responsibility away from the employer. The company that configures a discriminatory tool is the responsible party, even if the tool was built by a vendor and deployed automatically.

The Responsibility Stack

Algorithmic systems are built, sold, configured, and operated by different actors. Each step creates opportunities to introduce harm and opportunities to catch it. Understanding who is responsible requires mapping this stack:

System developers (e.g., the engineers who build and train the underlying model) make foundational choices about architecture, training data, target variables, and evaluation metrics. They are responsible for what the model is capable of and what its known failure modes are.

Vendors (companies that package and sell AI tools as products) are responsible for how they represent the system's capabilities and limitations, what documentation they provide, and whether their product is fit for the purposes they market it for. The COMPAS case involves a vendor — Northpointe — that sold a product to courts while refusing to disclose how it worked.

Deploying organizations (hospitals, banks, employers, courts that purchase or license AI tools) are responsible for configuring the system appropriately, ensuring it complies with applicable law, providing human oversight, and maintaining the ability to audit and correct decisions. The EEOC's iTutorGroup settlement established this layer of responsibility clearly.

Regulators are responsible for setting standards, enforcing them, and updating them as technology evolves. Regulatory failure — whether from under-resourcing, captured agencies, or statutory gaps — is itself a form of accountability failure.

Meaningful Human Control

One response to algorithmic accountability failures is to require "human in the loop" review of consequential decisions. The limitation of this requirement, as written, is the word "meaningful." A human reviewer who rubber-stamps algorithmic recommendations at high volume — without the time, information, or institutional incentives to override them — does not constitute meaningful oversight. Researchers Danielle Citron and Frank Pasquale described this problem in 2014 as "technological due process" — the question of whether procedural requirements nominally satisfied by a human reviewer actually protect the individual affected.

The European Union's AI Act (2024) attempts to address this with its "high-risk AI" category, which requires not just human oversight but human oversight that is capable of understanding and intervening in the system's outputs. This places demands on training, documentation, and system design — not just on placing a human somewhere in the workflow.

In practice, meaningful human control requires that the reviewer: (1) understands what the model is doing, (2) has access to information the model does not have, (3) has the institutional authority and incentives to deviate from the model's recommendation, and (4) can document the basis for that deviation. Most current deployments fall short of all four conditions simultaneously.

The Automation Bias Problem

Research in cognitive psychology has consistently found that humans tend to defer to automated recommendations even when they have information suggesting the recommendation is wrong — a phenomenon called automation bias. Studies of radiologists reviewing AI-flagged medical images (Goddard et al., 2012) and pilots flying with autopilot engaged both show elevated rates of error when the automation makes a mistake and the human is in nominal review mode. Requiring human oversight is necessary but not sufficient — the conditions under which review occurs shape whether it is real or ceremonial.

Auditing and Documentation

Accountability requires the ability to reconstruct what happened and why. This means documentation. The EU AI Act and the NIST AI Risk Management Framework (published January 2023) both emphasize documentation as a foundational accountability mechanism — not as bureaucratic overhead but as the precondition for any meaningful audit.

At minimum, a documentable AI system should record: what data was used to train it and what pre-processing was applied; what the target variable was and how it was operationalized; what fairness metrics were measured during development and what values were observed; who approved deployment, under what conditions, and with what review period; and what mechanisms exist to receive, investigate, and remediate complaints about its outputs.

The gap between this standard and current practice is large. A 2021 study by researchers at MIT and Salesforce reviewed 40 deployed AI systems in high-stakes domains and found that fewer than half had any accessible documentation describing their training data or evaluation methodology. The systems without documentation were not meaningfully less deployed — they were often more widely used, because documentation requirements slow release cycles.

Key Terms

Responsibility StackThe chain of actors — developers, vendors, deploying organizations, regulators — each of whom bears partial responsibility for the design, configuration, deployment, and oversight of an algorithmic system.
Meaningful Human ControlHuman oversight of algorithmic decisions that is substantive rather than ceremonial — requiring that reviewers understand the system, have access to relevant information, and have both authority and incentive to override it.
Automation BiasThe cognitive tendency of humans to defer to automated recommendations even in the presence of contradictory information, reducing the effectiveness of nominal human-review requirements.
Algorithmic AuditA systematic examination of an AI system's inputs, outputs, and decision logic to identify errors, biases, or compliance failures — requiring documentation that many deployed systems currently lack.
EU AI Act (2024)The European Union's comprehensive AI regulation classifying AI systems by risk tier and imposing documentation, human oversight, and transparency requirements on high-risk deployments.
