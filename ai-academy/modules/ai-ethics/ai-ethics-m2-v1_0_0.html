Surveillance at Scale

How AI-powered monitoring transforms the relationship between institutions and individuals

In 2020, during the COVID-19 pandemic, several universities deployed AI proctoring software to monitor students taking remote exams. The systems used webcams to track eye movement, flag "suspicious" behavior, and analyze keystrokes. At one institution, a student with a physical disability received an automated cheating flag for moving her head in a pattern the system classified as looking away from the screen. Her exam was invalidated. She hadn't cheated. Her disability caused the movement.

The proctor was an algorithm. There was no human watching — until after the flag had already determined the outcome. The surveillance was automated. The judgment was automated. The harm was real.

What AI Surveillance Makes Possible

AI dramatically changes the scale and cost of surveillance. Before AI, monitoring people continuously required proportional human labor — watching cameras, reading logs, reviewing records. That cost served as a natural limit. AI removes the limit. A single system can monitor thousands of people simultaneously, flagging deviations from expected behavior without human attention until after consequences are triggered.

This creates qualitatively new power dynamics. Employers can monitor every keystroke of remote workers. Schools can analyze student attention via webcam. Law enforcement can run license plates against databases in real time. Insurance companies can track driving behavior via telematics. The entities deploying these systems accumulate information asymmetries that fundamentally reshape the relationship between institution and individual.

The Chilling Effect

Surveillance doesn't need to catch everyone to change behavior. When people know they are being monitored — or merely believe they might be — they self-censor, self-regulate, and conform. This chilling effect suppresses not just wrongdoing but creativity, dissent, political activity, and identity expression. The most significant harm of pervasive surveillance may not be what it catches but what it prevents from happening at all.

Consent, Context, and Contextual Integrity

Privacy theorist Helen Nissenbaum's concept of contextual integrity offers a useful framework: information flows appropriately when they match the norms of the context in which information was originally shared. A student sharing their work with a professor operates in a context with implicit norms about what the professor can do with that information. Sending it to a third-party AI surveillance vendor violates those norms — regardless of whether a buried consent checkbox was checked during enrollment.

The "you consented" defense for surveillance systems is frequently inadequate. Consent obtained as a condition of access — to a school, an employer, a platform — is not freely given. It is coerced by the power differential between the consenting party and the institution requiring consent. Ethical surveillance design must account for this asymmetry, not dismiss it.

Who Gets to Be Invisible

Facial recognition, biometric data, and the unequal distribution of surveillance

Between 2019 and 2021, researchers at MIT and the National Institute of Standards and Technology tested leading commercial facial recognition systems. The findings were consistent and striking: error rates for darker-skinned women were up to 35 percentage points higher than for lighter-skinned men. The same systems being marketed to law enforcement and used in airports, border crossings, and retail loss prevention were dramatically less accurate for the populations most likely to face adverse consequences from their errors.

The systems weren't designed to discriminate. They were trained predominantly on images of lighter-skinned faces — because that's what the training datasets contained. The technology reflected the world that produced it. The question is whether deploying it anyway is an ethical choice.

The Accuracy Disparity Problem

Facial recognition accuracy disparities are well-documented and persistent. They arise from training data imbalances, optimization for majority-group performance, and the historical underrepresentation of certain populations in technical development pipelines. The disparity matters most where the stakes are highest: law enforcement use creates serious risk of wrongful detention for people in already over-surveilled communities; border crossing deployment affects vulnerable populations with limited recourse.

The ethical question is not whether the disparity exists — it demonstrably does — but what obligations it creates. Is it acceptable to deploy a system with known accuracy disparities in high-stakes contexts? Who bears the cost of those errors? And does improving average accuracy while maintaining disparate error rates across groups constitute improvement in any meaningful ethical sense?

The "Good Enough on Average" Problem

A system that is 98% accurate on average sounds impressive. But if that 98% average is composed of 99.5% accuracy for one group and 85% accuracy for another, aggregate accuracy obscures profound inequity. In criminal justice contexts, an 85% accuracy rate means 15 wrongful outcomes per 100 decisions — for a group that is already disproportionately targeted by the systems where these tools are deployed.

Biometric Data and Irreversibility

Biometric data — facial geometry, fingerprints, iris patterns, gait signatures — raises distinct ethical concerns beyond accuracy. Unlike passwords or account numbers, biometric identifiers cannot be changed when compromised. A data breach that exposes your facial geometry exposes it permanently. This irreversibility means the ethical stakes of collecting, storing, and deploying biometric data are categorically higher than for other personal data.

Several U.S. states (Illinois, Texas, Washington) have enacted biometric privacy laws — most notably Illinois' BIPA, which requires informed consent before collection. No equivalent federal framework exists. In the EU, biometric data is classified as sensitive personal data requiring explicit consent under GDPR. The regulatory gap in most jurisdictions means biometric collection in commercial contexts often proceeds without meaningful protections.

The Prediction Problem

Predictive systems, risk scores, and the ethics of acting on futures that haven't happened

In 2017, the city of Fresno, California deployed a "threat score" system that analyzed social media posts, criminal records, and other public data to assign risk scores to individuals before police responded to calls. Officers would see a color-coded score — green, yellow, or red — for residents before making contact. The system flagged a local activist as "red" based on her social media posts criticizing police. She hadn't committed any crime. Her political speech had generated a threat score.

The scores were generated by an algorithm. The officers who saw them couldn't evaluate how they were calculated. A prediction about who someone might be had become a fact about who they were.

The Self-Fulfilling Prophecy Problem

Predictive systems in high-stakes contexts face a structural problem: the prediction influences the outcome. A neighborhood flagged as "high crime" by a predictive policing tool receives more police patrols. More patrols generate more arrests. More arrests update the model's risk assessment of the neighborhood — confirming the original prediction. The system hasn't predicted crime; it has produced crime statistics by directing enforcement toward the areas it models as risky.

This feedback loop is not a bug — it is an inherent feature of predictive systems that influence the environments they predict. It means that historical data embedded in these systems doesn't just reflect past patterns; it actively reproduces them. Communities with histories of over-policing generate data that justifies continued over-policing, regardless of actual underlying behavior.

Punishing the Unchosen

Risk scores often incorporate factors individuals cannot control: neighborhood, social associations, family criminal history. Acting punitively on the basis of these factors means punishing people for circumstances they did not choose and cannot change. This violates a foundational principle of justice: that consequences should be proportional to an individual's own acts, not to statistical correlates of group membership.

From Prediction to Presumption

Legal systems built on the presumption of innocence struggle to accommodate predictive risk scores. When a judge considers a bail recommendation generated by an algorithm, they are being asked to treat a statistical prediction about future behavior as relevant to a decision about the present. The defendant has done nothing yet. The score describes what someone statistically similar might do — not what this person will do.

This is ethically and legally fraught. The defendant cannot rebut the prediction directly — it concerns a future that hasn't happened. They often cannot access the model that generated the score or the data it used. And the score, once generated, carries an aura of objectivity that human assessment does not — making it more influential, not less, for being opaque.

Who Governs the Governors

Regulatory frameworks, corporate self-governance, and the limits of both

In 2021, Frances Haugen, a former Facebook data scientist, leaked internal documents to regulators and journalists. The documents showed that Facebook's own researchers had documented harms — amplification of misinformation, mental health effects on teenage girls, contribution to ethnic violence in Myanmar — and that the company had prioritized engagement metrics over addressing those harms. The documents revealed not a company unaware of its impacts, but one that had weighed them and continued anyway.

No external regulator had required the research. No external regulator had access to it. The company had governed itself — and the internal governance had failed the people its products affected.

The Limits of Corporate Self-Governance

The dominant approach to AI ethics through most of the 2010s was corporate self-governance: companies published principles, formed ethics boards, funded research, and made voluntary commitments. The Haugen revelations illustrated the structural limitation of this approach. Corporate ethics functions operate within a company's strategic interests. When ethical obligations conflict with growth objectives, internal ethics functions rarely have the authority, independence, or resources to prevail.

This is not unique to tech. Tobacco companies funded health research. Oil companies funded climate science. The pattern is consistent: internal research can document harm without producing the organizational change needed to address it. Self-regulation in the presence of strong financial incentives to externalize harm is predictably inadequate.

Ethics Boards Without Teeth

Several major AI companies have formed ethics review boards or responsible AI teams, then dissolved or sidelined them when their recommendations conflicted with business objectives. Google dissolved its Advanced Technology External Advisory Council within two weeks of forming it in 2019. The pattern suggests that ethics functions without organizational authority and without external accountability mechanisms are primarily reputational instruments, not governance ones.

Regulatory Approaches and Their Tradeoffs

External regulation of AI systems faces genuine tensions. Prescriptive rules can quickly become obsolete as technology changes. Technical complexity makes it difficult for regulators to evaluate compliance meaningfully. Regulatory capture — where regulated industries gain undue influence over their regulators — is a persistent risk in technically specialized domains.

Despite these challenges, regulatory frameworks are emerging. The EU AI Act (2024) establishes risk-based requirements with enforcement mechanisms. The U.S. has taken a more fragmented approach — sector-specific guidance from the FTC, FDA, and other agencies without a unified federal framework. The regulatory gap matters: voluntary principles do not constrain behavior when financial incentives point the other direction. The question is not whether external governance is perfect, but whether the alternative — unaccountable self-governance — produces better outcomes. The evidence from the past decade suggests it does not.
