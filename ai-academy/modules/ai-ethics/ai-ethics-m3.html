Module 3 · Lesson 1

Training Data & the Scraping Problem

Every large AI model learned from someone's words. Most of those people have no idea.
When does collecting publicly visible data cross the line into taking something that was never freely given?

In 2020, a startup called Clearview AI had quietly scraped more than 3 billion photographs from Facebook, Instagram, Twitter, Venmo, and millions of other websites. No user consented. No platform was asked. Clearview built a facial recognition tool and sold it to law enforcement agencies across the United States, Canada, and Australia. The photos were public — but they were posted to share moments with friends, not to train a surveillance engine. The line between "public" and "fair game" had been crossed without anyone noticing.

What Training Data Actually Is

Modern AI systems — whether generating text, images, or making predictions — learn statistical patterns from enormous datasets. GPT-4 was trained on hundreds of billions of words scraped from the public internet, books, and licensed sources. Stable Diffusion's initial training set, LAION-5B, contained 5.85 billion image-text pairs harvested from web crawls. Most creators whose work appeared in LAION were never contacted.

The fundamental question is whether "publicly accessible" means "consented to AI training." A Reddit post is visible to anyone, but its author may have intended it for a specific community. A novelist's book can be legally purchased; that doesn't mean the author consented to their prose style being absorbed into a model that can imitate them commercially.

Documented Case — Books3 Dataset

Books3, a training dataset used by multiple major AI companies, contained approximately 196,640 ebooks scraped from a shadow library called Bibliotik. In 2023, authors including Sarah Silverman, Paul Tremblay, and Mona Awad filed suit against Meta and OpenAI, arguing their copyrighted books appeared in training sets without compensation or consent. Meta's LLaMA model training data documentation listed Books3 explicitly.

The Consent Gap in Practice

There are three distinct consent failures in standard AI data collection practices:

No notice: Data subjects typically don't know their content is being collected. Common Crawl, which feeds most major language model training pipelines, indexes content from billions of websites on an automated basis — site owners can opt out via robots.txt, but most individuals who posted content on platforms they don't control have no mechanism to do so.

No choice: Even when users discover their data was used, there is rarely a meaningful opt-out. The model has already been trained; the pattern has been absorbed. Deletion requests have no technical mechanism to excise a specific person's contribution.

Scope mismatch: The purpose for which data was shared rarely matches its use in training. Medical forum posts meant for peer support have appeared in health AI training datasets. Personal blog entries written for therapeutic expression have been used to train customer service bots.

Key Terms
Informed ConsentAgreement given by an individual with full knowledge of what their data will be used for, by whom, and for how long — before collection begins.
Web ScrapingAutomated collection of data from websites, often at massive scale, using crawlers or bots. Technically legal in many jurisdictions but ethically contested when applied to personal content.
robots.txtA file website owners can post to signal which parts of their site crawlers should not index. Industry convention, not law — many AI scrapers have been documented ignoring it.
Contextual IntegrityThe principle, developed by philosopher Helen Nissenbaum, that information flows appropriately when they match the norms of the context in which data was originally shared.
The Clearview Ruling

In May 2022, the UK Information Commissioner's Office fined Clearview AI £7.5 million and ordered it to delete all data on UK residents. Italy, France, Australia, and Canada issued similar findings. The company's defense — that the photos were public — was rejected. Regulators consistently held that public accessibility does not equal consent to mass biometric processing.

Why This Matters for AI Ethics

The consent problem isn't merely legal or regulatory. It shapes what AI systems fundamentally are: artifacts built on labor, expression, and personal data that was extracted rather than contributed. When writers, artists, and ordinary internet users discover their work inside a model, the ethical question isn't only "was it legal?" but "was it right?"

As AI systems become more capable and more commercially valuable, the asymmetry widens. Billions of people contributed the raw material; the economic value accrues to a small number of companies. The module ahead examines the frameworks — legal, technical, and ethical — that have emerged to address this gap.

Facial Recognition & Biometric Surveillance

Your face is a data point you carry everywhere. Once it's in a database, it's nearly impossible to remove.
When governments and corporations can identify anyone in any crowd, what happens to the right to move anonymously through public space?

In January 2020, Robert Williams, a Black man living in Detroit, was arrested in front of his wife and daughters. Police had matched his driver's license photo to security footage using facial recognition software — a match that was wrong. Williams spent 30 hours in detention before investigators acknowledged the error. He was the first American known to be wrongfully arrested based on a facial recognition false match. He was not the last.

How Facial Recognition Systems Are Built

Facial recognition AI requires vast labeled datasets to train on. The two most widely used academic datasets — MS-Celeb-1M (Microsoft) and MegaFace (University of Washington) — together contained the faces of millions of people scraped from the internet, largely without consent. MS-Celeb-1M was quietly taken down in 2019 after journalists revealed it included faces of activists, journalists, and ordinary people who had never agreed to be in a facial recognition training corpus.

IBM's Diversity in Faces dataset was similarly withdrawn after it emerged that Flickr users whose Creative Commons photos were included had not consented to their faces being used for biometric training. Creative Commons licenses govern copying and reuse of images — they say nothing about biometric data extraction.

The Error Rate Problem

A 2019 NIST study of 189 facial recognition algorithms found that most had false positive rates 10–100 times higher for Black and Asian faces than for white male faces. The algorithms were trained predominantly on lighter-skinned faces, encoding demographic bias directly into the technology. When deployed in law enforcement, this bias has real-world consequences: people wrongly identified, detained, charged.

Where Facial Recognition Is Deployed

The technology operates far more broadly than most citizens realize. In the United States, half of all adult Americans are in at least one law enforcement facial recognition database, according to a 2016 Georgetown Law study — predominantly through driver's license photos, which people must provide to drive legally. This creates a de facto opt-out-impossible biometric database built without explicit consent to law enforcement use.

China's Skynet and Sharp Eyes surveillance networks have deployed over 600 million cameras with facial recognition capability, creating a system that can identify citizens in public spaces nationwide. The system is explicitly used for social control, tracking individuals flagged as dissidents or members of ethnic minority groups including Uyghurs in Xinjiang.

In the UK, the Metropolitan Police has run live facial recognition deployments at public events since 2019, scanning every face that walks past cameras and matching against watchlists. Civil liberties groups challenged the practice; in 2020 the Court of Appeal ruled existing deployments had been unlawful due to insufficient safeguards — but the technology continued to be used.

The "You Were in Public" Argument

Proponents of public facial recognition often argue that people who walk down public streets have no expectation of privacy. The counterargument, made by courts and ethicists alike, is that a qualitative change occurs when continuous identification becomes possible. Walking anonymously in a city — attending a protest, visiting a clinic, going to a place of worship — has historically been a meaningful form of privacy even in fully public spaces. Mass facial recognition eliminates that anonymity entirely.

Legislative Responses

San Francisco became the first major US city to ban government use of facial recognition in 2019. Boston, Portland (Oregon), and several other cities followed. Illinois's Biometric Information Privacy Act (BIPA), passed in 2008, requires written consent before biometric data can be collected — and has been the basis for billions of dollars in class action settlements against companies including Facebook (now Meta), which paid $650 million to settle claims its photo tagging feature collected facial geometry without consent from Illinois residents.

Health Data, Profiles & the Inference Problem

You didn't share your diagnosis. You shared your browsing history — and the AI inferred the rest.
If an AI can deduce your HIV status, pregnancy, or mental health condition from data you thought was innocuous, does it matter that you never disclosed it?

In 2012, journalist Charles Duhigg revealed that Target Corporation's data analytics team had built a model that could predict customer pregnancies before they were publicly announced — and in some cases before the customer had told family members. The model assigned a "pregnancy prediction score" based on changes in purchasing patterns: unscented lotion, mineral supplements, cotton balls in large quantities. Target sent targeted maternity coupons to women its algorithm identified. In one documented case, a teenage girl's pregnancy was revealed to her father through a Target mailer before she had told him herself.

What Inference Means for Consent

Traditional privacy frameworks focused on protecting information you deliberately disclosed: your medical records, your financial data, your address. The AI inference problem breaks this framework entirely. When a system can derive sensitive attributes from non-sensitive inputs, consent to share the inputs does not constitute consent to create the inferred outputs.

In 2021, researchers published a study demonstrating that a model trained on Spotify listening histories could predict listeners' age, gender, and mental health indicators including depression risk scores with statistically significant accuracy. The users had consented to Spotify collecting listening data. They had not consented to mental health profiling.

Documented Case — Facebook Emotional Contagion (2014)

Facebook conducted a psychological experiment on 689,003 users without their knowledge, manipulating their News Feed to show more positive or more negative content, then measuring whether their own posts became more positive or negative. The study was published in the Proceedings of the National Academy of Sciences. Facebook's defense was that users had consented through the platform's Data Use Policy — a document averaging 2,700 words that users must accept without negotiation to use the service. The experiment demonstrated both non-consensual psychological manipulation and the fiction of meaningful ToS consent.

Health Data Specifically

Health data receives special legal protection in many jurisdictions — HIPAA in the United States, GDPR Article 9 in the EU. But these protections apply to disclosed health information held by covered entities like hospitals and insurers. They do not, in most cases, reach data brokers, apps, or inferences.

In 2023, the Federal Trade Commission took action against data broker Outlogic (formerly X-Mode Social) for selling precise location data that could be used to track people visiting abortion clinics, addiction treatment facilities, and domestic violence shelters. The data included no medical information — it was movement data — but the inferences available from it were medically sensitive and potentially dangerous in states with new abortion restrictions.

Period tracking app Flo Health settled with the FTC in 2021 after sharing users' menstrual and fertility data with Facebook and Google despite promising users their health data would remain private. The company's SDK automatically transmitted data including when users marked themselves as pregnant or trying to conceive. Flo's 100+ million users had trusted the app with deeply personal data; that data was used to target advertising.

The Aggregation Problem

Individual data points that seem harmless in isolation can become dangerously revealing in combination. A ZIP code is not sensitive. A first name is not sensitive. The fact that someone googled "how long do miscarriages last" is not technically medical data. But the combination of name + ZIP + search history + period app data + purchase history creates a profile that can reveal pregnancy, health conditions, sexual orientation, religious beliefs, and political views — none of which the person ever "disclosed" in any meaningful sense.

Data brokers like Acxiom, LexisNexis, and Epsilon maintain profiles on hundreds of millions of people, derived from aggregating thousands of individually innocuous data streams. These profiles are sold to insurers, employers, political campaigns, and law enforcement — often without the subjects' knowledge that any profile exists at all.

The Inference Gap in Law

Current privacy law in most countries focuses on protecting data you disclosed. It has no adequate framework for the inferred data an AI creates about you from that disclosed data. Closing this gap — requiring consent not just for collection but for the inferences drawn — is one of the central challenges in contemporary AI governance. The EU's GDPR includes early language on this (Article 22, on automated decision-making), but enforcement has been inconsistent.

Module 3 · Lesson 4

Toward Meaningful Consent: Frameworks & Fixes

The consent you never gave doesn't have to be the consent you never get. Real frameworks exist — they just require political will to implement.
What would a genuine consent architecture for AI look like — and which existing models come closest to achieving it?

On May 25, 2018, the General Data Protection Regulation took effect across the European Union. For the first time, a major jurisdiction required that consent to data collection be freely given, specific, informed, and unambiguous — and that withdrawing it be as easy as giving it. Within hours of the law taking effect, Facebook and Google faced billion-euro complaints filed by privacy activist Max Schrems's organization noyb.eu, arguing that the companies' "forced consent" mechanisms — agree or leave the platform — violated the regulation's core requirement that consent be freely given.

What Genuine Consent Requires

Privacy scholars and regulators have converged on several criteria that distinguish meaningful consent from the checkbox formalities that currently dominate:

Granularity: Consent must be purpose-specific. Agreeing to use a service cannot constitute agreement to have your data used for AI training, sold to brokers, or used to build behavioral profiles. Each distinct use requires separate consent.

Comprehensibility: A 50,000-word terms of service document that takes 76 minutes to read does not enable informed consent. Several jurisdictions now require "plain language" summaries; the EU's GDPR requires "clear and plain language" that is "easily accessible."

Non-coercive: Consent obtained by threatening loss of a core service — your email, your job's communication platform, your child's school system — is not freely given. The GDPR explicitly holds that consent is not valid when there is a "clear imbalance" between data subject and controller.

Revocable with effect: Meaningful consent can be withdrawn, and withdrawal must have practical consequences — not just stopping future collection, but creating mechanisms to address past collection where feasible.

Technical Approaches to Consent

Beyond legal frameworks, technical mechanisms have emerged to give individuals more control:

Opt-out registries for AI training: Following outcry from artists, Adobe's Firefly generative AI model was trained exclusively on licensed and public domain content. Stability AI introduced an opt-out mechanism for Stable Diffusion training data. OpenAI created a form allowing website owners to request exclusion from future training crawls. Critics note these are opt-out rather than opt-in systems, and that past models cannot be retroactively corrected.

Data provenance and watermarking: Efforts like C2PA (Coalition for Content Provenance and Authenticity) aim to attach verified metadata to content indicating its origin and any consent status. This doesn't solve past scraping but could create the infrastructure for consent-aware training in future models.

Differential privacy: A mathematical technique that adds carefully calibrated noise to training data, making it impossible to extract individual records from the trained model while preserving statistical utility. Apple and Google have deployed differential privacy in some production systems. It doesn't replace consent but reduces some harms from non-consensual collection.

The "Right to Erasure" Gap

GDPR Article 17 grants EU residents the "right to be forgotten" — the right to demand deletion of their personal data. But AI models that have been trained on that data cannot be surgically edited to remove a person's contribution. The model's weights encode statistical patterns, not discrete records. "Machine unlearning" — techniques to excise specific training data's influence from a trained model — is an active research area, but no production-ready solution exists for large language models. The right exists on paper; the technical capability does not yet match it.

What Progress Looks Like

In 2023, the Authors Guild and a coalition of writers including Jodi Picoult, Scott Turow, and George R.R. Martin filed a class action lawsuit against OpenAI. The lawsuit was not only about past training but sought to establish that future AI training on copyrighted material requires consent and compensation. Several publishers including Axel Springer, Associated Press, and the Financial Times signed licensing agreements with AI companies — establishing a commercial precedent for compensated consent.

The emerging model — compensation for past use combined with opt-in consent architecture for future training — represents a more sustainable equilibrium than the current extraction-first approach. Whether it will prevail depends on regulatory pressure, litigation outcomes, and whether consumers develop enough awareness to demand it.

The Core Ethical Obligation

Across all the cases in this module — Clearview's face scrapes, Target's pregnancy predictions, Facebook's emotional manipulation, Books3's copyright violations — a single principle emerges: the power to collect and process data creates a corresponding obligation to obtain genuine consent. The sophistication of the technology does not diminish this obligation. If anything, the scale at which AI operates makes it more urgent.
