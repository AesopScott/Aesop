AI Ethics & Decision Making · Module 3 · Lesson 1

The Consent You Never Gave

Data collection, inference, and the gap between what you share and what companies know

In 2012, the statistician Andrew Pole built a pregnancy prediction model for Target. Using purchasing patterns — unscented lotion, certain vitamins, cotton balls, specific combinations of products — the model could identify which customers were likely pregnant and estimate their due date. Target began sending pregnancy-related coupons to these customers before they had announced pregnancies to anyone.

One father discovered his teenage daughter was pregnant because Target mailed baby product promotions to his home. His daughter hadn't told him. Target had inferred the pregnancy from her shopping behavior — data she had shared willingly, in a completely different context, for a completely different purpose. She had consented to a loyalty program. She had not consented to a pregnancy disclosure.

The Inference Gap

Modern data collection creates what researchers call an inference gap: the distance between the information a person knowingly provides and the information that can be derived from it. People share location data; companies infer religious practices, political affiliations, and health conditions from where they go. People share purchase histories; companies infer pregnancy, financial stress, relationship status. People share social connections; companies infer sexuality, mental health, immigration status.

This gap fundamentally undermines the concept of informed consent. When a person agrees to share data, they cannot meaningfully consent to all the inferences that data will enable — because those inferences are not disclosed, are not fully known even to the collecting company at the time of collection, and often concern attributes far more sensitive than the data itself.

Sensitive Inferences From Non-Sensitive Data

Researchers have demonstrated that typing patterns on smartphones can infer emotional states and depression risk. Facial expressions captured by retail cameras can infer political orientation. Music listening histories correlate with personality disorders. In each case, people share data in one context with one expectation — and companies derive sensitive inferences they never agreed to disclose. The inference is more personal than the data. The consent covers only the data.

The Business Model of Inference

Inference is not incidental to the data economy — it is the product. Companies collect behavioral data at scale specifically because it enables prediction and targeting at a granularity that people would never consent to if asked directly. The entire architecture of surveillance capitalism, as theorist Shoshana Zuboff termed it, depends on the gap between what people think they are sharing and what companies actually extract.

This creates a structural problem for consent-based privacy frameworks. GDPR and similar regulations require consent to data collection — but consent to raw behavioral data implicitly consents to whatever inferences that data enables, because the inferences are never disclosed as a separate consent item. Meaningful privacy protection requires addressing inference, not just collection.

Manipulation at Scale

Personalization, persuasion architecture, and the ethics of systems designed to change behavior

In 2014, Facebook conducted an experiment on 689,000 users without their knowledge. The company adjusted News Feed algorithms to show some users more positive content and others more negative content, then measured whether users' own posts became more positive or negative in response. They found emotional contagion worked — people's emotional expression shifted based on what they were shown. The experiment was published in a peer-reviewed journal in 2014.

Facebook users had agreed to the platform's data policy, which mentioned research. But none of the 689,000 participants knew they were in an experiment. None had consented to having their emotional state deliberately manipulated. The experiment ended. The emotional manipulation infrastructure it studied remained operational.

Persuasion Architecture

Modern AI-powered platforms are not neutral information conduits — they are persuasion architectures: systems optimized to produce behavioral outcomes in users. Variable reward schedules (the unpredictable timing of likes and notifications) exploit the same psychological mechanisms as slot machines. Infinite scroll eliminates natural stopping points. Autoplay removes the decision to continue. Each feature is designed to extend engagement by working around, rather than with, deliberate user choice.

The ethical problem is not that these systems influence behavior — all designed environments do. It is that they influence behavior by exploiting psychological vulnerabilities rather than appealing to informed preferences, they do so at scale without disclosure, and the behavioral outcomes they optimize for (engagement) are not aligned with user wellbeing. The user wants to check in on friends; the system wants to maximize time spent regardless of whether that time is enjoyable or beneficial.

The Engagement-Wellbeing Misalignment

Internal Facebook research (leaked in 2021) found that Instagram use was associated with negative body image, anxiety, and depression in teenage girls — and that the company was aware of this. Engagement-optimized systems can produce high engagement and significant harm simultaneously. Users who feel compelled to return are not the same as users who benefit from returning. The metric the system optimizes does not capture the outcome the user cares about.

Microtargeted Persuasion

Beyond platform design, AI enables microtargeted persuasion: delivering individually tailored messages to exploit specific psychological profiles at scale. Cambridge Analytica claimed to have developed psychographic profiles of most American voters, enabling political messaging tailored to individual personality vulnerabilities. Whether the specific claims were accurate, the underlying capability — using behavioral data to identify psychological leverage points and target persuasion accordingly — is real and widely deployed in political and commercial contexts.

This is ethically distinct from conventional advertising. Mass advertising exposes everyone to the same message; targeted audiences can collectively see and critique it. Microtargeted persuasion is invisible — each person receives messages calibrated to their specific vulnerabilities, and no one outside the targeting system sees the full picture of what is being said to whom. The manipulation is personalized and unauditable.

Content Moderation's Impossible Task

Scale, speed, context, and why automated content decisions keep getting it wrong

In 2017, Facebook's automated moderation system removed a photograph of the Pulitzer Prize-winning image "Napalm Girl" — Nick Ut's 1972 photo of children fleeing a napalm attack in Vietnam. The system classified it as containing nudity. The photo is one of the most significant historical images of the twentieth century. Norwegian Prime Minister Erna Solberg posted it; it was removed. She posted it again with commentary; it was removed again. Facebook eventually restored it — after the story became an international news event.

The system had done exactly what it was designed to do: flag and remove images containing unclothed minors. Context — historical significance, journalistic purpose, decades of cultural recognition — was not in the training data. The algorithm was fast. It was consistent. It was wrong.

Why Context Is Not Compressible

Content moderation at platform scale — billions of posts per day — is practically impossible to do well with human review alone. Automated systems can process volume that humans cannot. The problem is that most genuinely difficult moderation decisions turn on context: the same image, the same words, the same video can be hate speech or counter-speech, abuse documentation or abuse promotion, historical record or harmful content — depending on who posted it, why, for what audience, in what surrounding conversation.

AI systems trained on labeled examples learn surface patterns. They learn that certain words correlate with policy violations. They don't learn — and cannot easily learn — that the same word used by a community member in reclamation is categorically different from the same word used as an attack. Context requires interpretation, and interpretation requires understanding that current AI systems do not reliably possess.

The Over-Removal Problem

Automated moderation systems face two types of error with asymmetric visibility: false positives (removing content that should stay) and false negatives (leaving up content that should be removed). Platforms face public pressure primarily for false negatives — harmful content that wasn't caught. False positives — legitimate speech that was wrongly removed — are largely invisible because the people whose content was removed rarely have the platform or resources to make it a news story. This asymmetry biases automated systems toward over-removal.

The Global Standard Problem

Platforms applying consistent global moderation policies across different cultural, legal, and political contexts produce predictable failures. Speech that is legally protected in one jurisdiction is criminal in another. Images that are culturally normative in one context are taboo in another. Political speech that is legitimate dissent in one country is classified as extremism in another — often with pressure from governments that have interests in suppressing it.

Facebook's role in the Rohingya genocide in Myanmar is the most documented case: the platform's automated hate speech detection, trained primarily on English-language content, failed to catch hate speech in Burmese — a language with far fewer resources in training datasets. The UN Fact-Finding Mission cited Facebook as having played a "determining role" in inciting violence. The same automated system that over-removed historical war photography was simultaneously under-removing content calling for ethnic violence in a language it couldn't parse.

The Long Game

AI ethics across time — path dependency, lock-in, and decisions that compound

In 1936, Alan Turing published a theoretical paper describing a universal computing machine. He included the mathematical proof but chose not to patent it. In 2017, researchers at DeepMind published AlphaGo Zero — an AI that taught itself to play Go with no human knowledge input, by playing millions of games against itself. DeepMind patented the underlying methods.

Between those two dates, a chain of decisions about what to publish vs. patent, what to share vs. keep proprietary, who to fund, where to locate, and what to optimize for shaped the entire architecture of modern AI. Turing's choice to publish created a commons. Each subsequent decision about openness or closure compounded — making some futures more reachable and others progressively less so. Ethics isn't only about individual decisions. It's about what those decisions make possible and impossible over time.

Path Dependency and Lock-In

AI development is characterized by path dependency: early decisions constrain later choices, not through explicit design but through the accumulation of infrastructure, investment, expertise, and expectations that make alternative paths progressively harder to take. The dominance of English in large language model training, the concentration of compute in a small number of companies, the dependence of much AI research on benchmark datasets with particular properties — each reflects early decisions that now constrain the field's direction.

Lock-in is the extreme form: a path becomes effectively irreversible. If AI development concentrates sufficient economic and political power in a small number of actors before governance frameworks are in place to constrain that concentration, the actors who would need to be constrained become capable of preventing the constraints from being imposed. This is not hypothetical — it describes the trajectory of social media regulation, where platform power grew faster than regulatory capacity.

The Concentration Problem

The compute, data, and talent required to develop frontier AI systems are now concentrated in a small number of companies — primarily in the United States and China. This concentration has compounded: large models require large compute, which only well-capitalized actors can afford, which produces models that attract the talent and data that make the next generation possible. The gap between frontier AI actors and everyone else grows with each generation. Decisions made now about openness, access, and governance will determine whether this concentration is reversible.

Ethics Across Generations

Some AI ethics questions concern immediate individual harms: a wrongful arrest, a discriminatory loan denial, a manipulative design pattern. Others concern structural conditions that compound over time and affect people who are not yet part of the conversation: what data infrastructure will future AI systems be built on, what power structures will they embed, what kinds of futures will they make easier or harder to reach?

This longer time horizon is where the stakes are highest and the discourse is most contested. Disagreements about AI risk, AI alignment, and AI governance are substantially disagreements about who has standing to make decisions that bind future generations, and what obligations present decision-makers have to futures they won't live in. These are not purely technical questions. They are questions about power, democracy, and the ethics of compounding decisions — and they are being answered right now, by a small number of actors, without broad deliberation.
