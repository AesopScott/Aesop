Module 4 · Lesson 1

When the Algorithm Fires You

Automated HR tools now make consequential decisions about people's livelihoods — often without meaningful human review.
Who is actually responsible when an AI system denies someone a job, a loan, or a livelihood?

In 2021, the UK Information Commissioner's Office investigated Uber's use of automated facial recognition to verify drivers. The system disproportionately failed to verify drivers with darker skin tones, resulting in account deactivations with no meaningful human appeal process. Drivers who depended on Uber as their primary income suddenly found themselves locked out — by an algorithm, with no person to call.

Uber was ordered to allow human review. But the weeks lost represented real income those drivers never recovered.

The Rise of Algorithmic Employment Decisions

Hiring, scheduling, performance scoring, and termination are increasingly handled by automated systems. Amazon's warehouse management software tracks "time off task" down to the minute and can trigger automated warnings or termination recommendations without a manager's direct involvement. In 2019, reporting by The Verge confirmed that Amazon's system had fired hundreds of workers at a single fulfillment center in a year — largely through automated processes.

HireVue, used by major employers including Unilever and Goldman Sachs, uses AI to score video interviews — analyzing facial expressions, word choice, and tone to rank candidates. The Illinois Artificial Intelligence Video Interview Act (2019) was the first U.S. law requiring companies to disclose when AI is assessing video interviews, obtain consent, and explain what characteristics the AI evaluates. It passed specifically because legislators recognized that applicants had no idea an algorithm was judging their microexpressions.

The Accountability Gap

When a human manager fires someone, there is a face, a decision trail, and a legal framework. When an algorithm terminates employment, the employer often claims the system made the decision — and the vendor claims they only provided software. Neither fully owns the outcome. This is the accountability gap that defines automated professional harm.

Proxy Discrimination in Scoring Systems

Automated employment systems rarely use protected characteristics directly. Instead, they use proxy variables — features that correlate with race, gender, or disability without naming them. A résumé screener trained on historical hires may penalize gaps in employment (disproportionately affecting women who took parental leave). A voice analysis system may score accents as low-confidence indicators. A "culture fit" model trained on a predominantly white workforce may systematically disadvantage minority applicants.

In 2018, Reuters reported that Amazon scrapped an internal AI recruiting tool after discovering it penalized résumés that included the word "women's" — as in "women's chess club" — and downgraded graduates of all-women's colleges. The system had learned from a decade of predominantly male hiring data and simply reproduced the pattern. Amazon dissolved the team working on the tool.

Proxy Variable A feature used in a model that correlates with a protected characteristic without being that characteristic itself. Commonly used (often unintentionally) to reproduce discriminatory outcomes while appearing facially neutral.
Disparate Impact A legal doctrine under U.S. Title VII holding that a practice can be unlawfully discriminatory if it has a disproportionate adverse effect on a protected class, even without discriminatory intent.
The Duty to Intervene

Professional ethics frameworks — whether for doctors, lawyers, engineers, or HR professionals — share a common obligation: practitioners cannot hide behind tools. A physician cannot blame a diagnostic algorithm for a missed cancer. An attorney cannot claim an AI contract reviewer caused legal malpractice. A hiring manager cannot fully delegate the fairness of a selection decision to a vendor's black box.

The EU AI Act (2024) classifies employment-related AI systems as high-risk — meaning they require human oversight, transparency about how decisions are made, and the right to appeal. The obligation is structural: not just an ethical recommendation but a legal requirement for organizations operating in the EU.

Core Principle

Automation does not dissolve accountability. It relocates it. The professionals who choose, deploy, configure, and monitor AI systems inherit the ethical consequences of what those systems do — especially to people who cannot see or challenge the decisions being made about them.

Lesson 2 examines AI in healthcare — where the professional's dilemma takes on life-or-death stakes, and documented failures have already caused patient harm.

Module 4 · Lesson 2

The Diagnostic Black Box

AI tools are entering clinical practice faster than the evidence base to support them — and faster than clinicians' ability to evaluate their claims.
When an AI system recommends a treatment — and the recommendation is wrong — who bears the ethical and legal weight?

Between 2017 and 2018, IBM's Watson for Oncology — marketed to hospitals worldwide as an AI that could recommend cancer treatments — was found by internal IBM documents (obtained by STAT News) to have recommended treatments that oncologists at Memorial Sloan Kettering called "unsafe and incorrect." Watson had been trained on a relatively small set of hypothetical cases, not real patient outcomes. Hospitals in India, South Korea, and the United States had deployed it for actual patient care. MD Anderson Cancer Center spent $62 million on a Watson project before canceling it in 2017 after it failed to achieve its clinical goals.

The Clinician's Dilemma

When a physician uses a clinical decision support AI, they face a structurally new problem: automation bias. Research published in JAMA and other peer-reviewed journals documents that clinicians tend to over-rely on algorithmic recommendations, even when those recommendations conflict with their own judgment and available evidence. The very features that make AI tools appealing — speed, apparent comprehensiveness, confidence — also make them dangerous when wrong.

In 2023, a study in Nature Medicine found that a widely used commercial sepsis prediction algorithm (Epic's Sepsis Prediction Model) missed more than two-thirds of sepsis cases in real hospital deployments — despite strong performance claims in vendor marketing materials. At University of Michigan and other institutions, clinical teams had structured workflows around the alert, meaning missed alerts led to delayed responses.

Automation Bias in Clinical Settings

Studies at the University of Cambridge and in aviation research established that humans consistently over-trust automated recommendations — even when warned not to. In medicine, this creates a documented harm pathway: AI recommendation → clinician deference → patient injury. The clinician believes they are using a tool. They may actually be delegating judgment.

Algorithmic Bias in Healthcare Triage

In 2019, a landmark study published in Science by Obermeyer et al. documented that a commercial healthcare algorithm used by major U.S. health systems to identify patients for high-risk care management was systematically biased against Black patients. The algorithm used healthcare cost as a proxy for healthcare need — but Black patients, facing systemic barriers to care access, generated lower costs than equally sick white patients. The result: Black patients were assigned lower risk scores and enrolled in intensive care management programs at significantly lower rates than equally ill white patients. The algorithm affected approximately 200 million patients in the U.S. annually.

The vendor, Optum (a subsidiary of UnitedHealth Group), subsequently worked with the researchers to reduce the measured bias. But the case became a canonical example of how proxy variables — used without bias auditing — can systematically harm marginalized populations at massive scale.

Automation Bias The tendency to over-rely on algorithmic or automated recommendations, reducing independent critical evaluation. Particularly dangerous in high-stakes professional contexts where the AI tool carries an aura of authority.
Clinical Decision Support (CDS) AI or rule-based systems integrated into clinical workflows to assist with diagnosis, treatment planning, or risk stratification. Regulated as medical devices by the FDA when they meet certain criteria.
Professional Responsibility Cannot Be Outsourced

The American Medical Association, the Royal College of Physicians, and the WHO have all issued guidance affirming a consistent principle: AI tools in clinical settings are decision support — the clinician retains final responsibility. This is not merely a legal position. It reflects the ethical architecture of professional medicine: a license to practice carries a duty of judgment, not just a duty to follow protocols.

In practice, this requires clinicians to understand enough about any AI tool they use to know when to override it. It requires institutional systems that make override possible and non-punitive. And it requires ongoing audit of AI tool performance in actual patient populations — not just the validation cohorts used in regulatory submissions.

The Watson Lesson

IBM's Watson for Oncology was not a failure of data science alone. It was a failure of professional scrutiny at every institutional level — vendors who overstated capabilities, hospital administrators who purchased without adequate clinical evaluation, and clinicians who used tools without validated evidence of real-world performance. The ethical obligations distributed across all of them.

Module 4 · Lesson 3

Predictive Policing and the Weight of a Score

Criminal justice AI assigns risk scores that shape arrests, bail decisions, and sentencing — often with no meaningful transparency to defendants or their counsel.
When a risk score determines whether someone goes home or to jail, what does fairness require of the professionals who deploy it?

In 2016, ProPublica published an analysis of COMPAS (Correctional Offender Management Profiling for Alternative Sanctions), a risk-scoring tool used by courts in Florida and many other states to assess the likelihood of reoffending. ProPublica's analysis found that COMPAS incorrectly flagged Black defendants as future criminals at roughly twice the rate of white defendants — and incorrectly labeled white defendants as low risk at higher rates. One defendant, Brisha Borden, had been arrested for riding a stolen bicycle and scored high-risk. A 41-year-old man with a lengthy record of violent offenses scored low-risk. Both predictions proved wrong.

The Architecture of Algorithmic Justice

COMPAS is not unique. Risk assessment instruments — now including AI-powered versions — are used across the U.S. criminal justice system for pretrial detention decisions, sentencing recommendations, parole determinations, and police resource allocation. The ShotSpotter acoustic gunshot detection system, deployed in cities including Chicago and Oakland, uses AI to detect and locate gunshots — and has been shown by audits in Chicago to produce a high rate of false positives, sending police to locations where no gun crime occurred.

PredPol (now Geolitica), a predictive policing software used by departments in Los Angeles, Santa Cruz, and elsewhere, generated "hot spot" predictions that civil liberties researchers at RAND Corporation and the ACLU argued amounted to feedback loops — increased police presence in flagged areas generated more arrests, which fed back into the training data, which reinforced the same predictions. Santa Cruz became the first U.S. city to ban predictive policing software in 2020.

The Northpointe Defense

The maker of COMPAS (Northpointe, now Equivant) argued that the tool was fair by a different statistical measure: its scores were equally predictive across racial groups. This is technically true — and perfectly compatible with the ProPublica finding that it produced racially disparate false positive rates. The case became a canonical demonstration that multiple definitions of fairness can be mathematically incompatible, and that choosing a definition is itself a value judgment — not a technical one.

Opacity as a Rights Violation

In 2016, Wisconsin's Supreme Court heard State v. Loomis — a case in which a defendant challenged the use of a COMPAS score in his sentencing. He argued he had a due process right to examine how the score was calculated. The court upheld the sentencing while acknowledging the concern — but noted that the algorithm's proprietary nature meant even the court could not fully examine it. The defendant, his counsel, and the sentencing judge were all making consequential decisions about a human being's liberty based partly on a score no one could interrogate.

In Europe, the GDPR's Article 22 provides a "right not to be subject to solely automated decision-making" in significant legal decisions — with a right to human review and explanation. No equivalent federal protection exists in the United States for criminal justice AI.

Recidivism Risk Score An algorithmic output estimating the probability that a convicted or accused person will reoffend. Used in bail, sentencing, and parole decisions in many U.S. jurisdictions.
Feedback Loop A system dynamic where an algorithm's outputs (e.g., police deployment) affect the data environment (e.g., arrests in flagged areas) that the algorithm is later trained on — amplifying initial biases over time.
The Professional's Position

Judges, prosecutors, public defenders, and law enforcement officers who work with criminal justice AI face an acute version of the professional dilemma: they are bound by institutional systems that present algorithmic outputs as inputs to their decisions, while retaining personal professional duties — a judge's duty to individualized sentencing, a defense attorney's duty to challenge the basis of evidence, a prosecutor's duty to pursue justice rather than merely conviction.

The Wisconsin Supreme Court's Loomis decision was criticized by legal scholars precisely because it allowed professional obligations to be partially discharged through deference to an opaque tool. Public defenders in many jurisdictions now specifically challenge algorithmic risk scores as part of standard case preparation — arguing that if the algorithm cannot be interrogated, it cannot be used as evidence without violating defendants' constitutional rights.

Incompatible Fairness Definitions

The COMPAS controversy made a critical point that applies broadly: fairness is not a single technical property. Calibration (equal predictive accuracy across groups) and equal false positive rates are mutually incompatible when base rates differ across groups. Choosing which definition of fairness to optimize is a moral and political choice — not one algorithms make. Professionals who deploy these systems must own that choice.

Module 4 · Lesson 4

The Whistleblower's Calculus

When internal AI ethics concerns are dismissed or suppressed, some employees face a defining professional choice: stay silent, or risk everything.
At what point does professional complicity become professional misconduct — and what frameworks exist to protect those who refuse to stay silent?

In December 2020, Google fired Timnit Gebru, a prominent AI ethics researcher and co-lead of its Ethical AI team, after she objected to the retraction of a research paper she had co-authored on harms in large language models. Gebru said she was told to either retract her name from the paper or resign — and when she sought clarification, Google terminated her. Her colleague Margaret Mitchell was fired two months later, also following internal disputes about AI safety research and how Google handled Gebru's departure.

The incident prompted public debate across the AI research community about whether large technology companies could meaningfully self-regulate on AI ethics when doing so might conflict with commercial interests.

Internal Ethics Suppression: A Pattern

Timnit Gebru's case is not isolated. Frances Haugen, a former Facebook product manager, testified before the U.S. Senate in October 2021 that Facebook had internal research showing its recommendation algorithms were amplifying harmful content and contributing to teen mental health harms — and that the company had suppressed or deprioritized this research when it conflicted with engagement growth goals. Haugen had preserved internal documents under whistleblower protections before leaving and provided them to the SEC and to journalists.

At OpenAI in May 2024, a cluster of safety researchers resigned, including Jan Leike (then head of alignment), who stated publicly that "safety culture and processes have taken a back seat to shiny products." Several departing employees had signed non-disclosure and non-disparagement agreements that they argued prevented them from speaking fully about their safety concerns. A letter signed by current and former employees called for AI companies to commit to non-retaliation against employees who raised safety concerns with regulators.

The NDA Problem

Non-disclosure and non-disparagement agreements are standard in technology employment. But when applied to safety concerns about AI systems, they create a direct tension with professional ethics obligations. Engineers, researchers, and product managers who believe a system poses risks to the public may be contractually bound to silence. In 2024, the SEC clarified that NDAs cannot prevent employees from reporting concerns to federal regulators — but the chilling effect persists.

Legal Protections for AI Whistleblowers

The legal landscape for AI-specific whistleblowing is still forming. General whistleblower protections in the U.S. — under Sarbanes-Oxley for publicly traded companies, the SEC whistleblower program, and state-level protections — cover some AI concerns. The EU AI Act (2024) includes explicit whistleblower protections for employees who report AI Act violations to national market surveillance authorities. The UK's Public Interest Disclosure Act provides some protection for workers who make "qualifying disclosures" in the public interest.

But gaps remain significant. A researcher at a private AI lab with no publicly traded parent company may have minimal legal protection for raising internal safety concerns. A contractor rather than a full employee may have even less. The mismatch between the pace of AI deployment and the development of adequate legal frameworks for professional dissent is itself an ethical issue.

Duty of Candor A professional obligation — formally codified for lawyers, physicians, engineers, and auditors — to disclose material facts, especially when concealment would cause harm. Applies regardless of employer instructions or contractual restrictions.
Responsible Disclosure A framework originating in cybersecurity, now applied to AI harms: bring concerns internally first, give the organization a defined period to respond, then escalate externally if the concern is not addressed. Balances insider access with public accountability.
What Professional Ethics Actually Requires

Engineering ethics codes — including the IEEE Code of Ethics and the ACM Code of Ethics and Professional Conduct — are explicit: engineers have a duty to prioritize public safety over organizational loyalty when the two conflict. The National Society of Professional Engineers' canon states that engineers shall "hold paramount the safety, health, and welfare of the public." These are not aspirational suggestions. They are the conditions of professional legitimacy.

For AI professionals who are not formally licensed engineers, these obligations are less legally enforced but no less ethically real. The emerging field of AI ethics has increasingly focused on whether existing professional frameworks are adequate — or whether AI development requires new institutional structures: independent safety boards, mandatory incident reporting, licensing regimes, or external audit requirements analogous to financial auditing.

The Gebru-Mitchell Precedent

The termination of Timnit Gebru and Margaret Mitchell at Google established a visible data point: prominent, credentialed researchers could be removed for raising ethics concerns that conflicted with business interests. The AI community's response — widespread public criticism, a letter signed by thousands of researchers, and increased scrutiny of tech company ethics teams — demonstrated that professional community pressure is itself a mechanism of accountability, even when institutional ones fail.

This module has examined four domains — employment, healthcare, criminal justice, and whistleblowing — where AI creates specific professional dilemmas. The module test will ask you to apply these frameworks across all four areas.
