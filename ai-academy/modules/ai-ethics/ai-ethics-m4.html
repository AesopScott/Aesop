AI Ethics & Decision Making · Module 4 · Lesson 1

The Professional's Dilemma

What engineers, designers, and researchers owe — to users, to the public, and to themselves

In 2020, Timnit Gebru — then co-lead of Google's Ethical AI team — was fired after refusing to retract a research paper she had co-authored. The paper examined risks in large language models, including environmental costs and dangers of training on internet text that encodes historical biases. Google said the paper needed more work. Gebru said she was fired for raising concerns leadership didn't want raised. Hundreds of Google employees signed a letter of protest. The paper was later published elsewhere and widely cited.

The incident crystallized a question every AI practitioner eventually faces: What do you do when the organization you work for is building something you believe is causing harm?

Professional Responsibility in Technology

Most professions with significant public impact — medicine, law, engineering, accounting — have developed explicit codes of professional ethics that create obligations beyond employer loyalty. Civil engineers have professional liability for designs that endanger the public. Lawyers have duties to courts that override client instructions. Doctors have duties to patients that override hospital administrator preferences.

Software and AI development has been notably resistant to this framing. The industry has generally treated practitioners as employees with standard employment obligations, not as professionals with public duties. The consequences are visible: individual engineers routinely build systems they know to be harmful because they are told to, or because raising concerns is professionally costly, or because the harms are diffuse and affect people far from the development team.

The "Just Following Orders" Problem

The most common ethical failure in technology is not malice — it is compliance. Engineers build systems they have misgivings about because challenging them is hard, because their concerns were noted and overridden, because the decision was made above their level. This diffusion of moral responsibility across an organization is not a defense; it is a structural feature of how harm gets built. Professional ethics frameworks exist precisely to give individuals something to stand on when organizations press them toward harmful outcomes.

Whistleblowing: Costs, Protections, and Obligations

When internal dissent fails, whistleblowing — disclosing organizational wrongdoing to external parties — becomes an ethical option and sometimes an obligation. The costs are real and often severe: retaliation, termination, legal action, reputational damage, blacklisting in an industry. Existing legal protections for technology whistleblowers in the U.S. are weak compared to other sectors and vary significantly by context.

The ethical question is not simply "is it legal to blow the whistle?" but "at what point does the harm being caused obligate disclosure despite the costs?" This calculation involves the severity and breadth of harm, whether internal channels have genuinely been exhausted, whether disclosure would actually prevent the harm, and who bears the cost of silence vs. the cost of speaking. These are real ethical questions with no clean universal answers — they require judgment, not rule-following.

Power, Concentration, and AI

How AI development concentrates power — and why that is itself an ethical problem

By 2024, the five largest AI models in commercial deployment were owned by four companies: Google, Microsoft (via OpenAI), Meta, and Anthropic. Training a frontier model required hundreds of millions to billions of dollars in compute — accessible to no university, no government outside a few wealthy nations, and no startup without major corporate backing. The infrastructure that shapes how hundreds of millions of people access information, generate content, and make decisions was controlled by a handful of organizations in one geographic cluster.

This wasn't a conspiracy. It was the predictable result of unconstrained scaling economics. But the ethical question remains: who should control infrastructure this consequential?

Why Power Concentration Is an Ethical Problem

Democratic societies have long recognized that concentrated private power over critical infrastructure requires governance. Utilities, telecommunications, financial systems, and media are regulated — not because their owners are evil, but because unaccountable control over systems everyone depends on creates structural risks regardless of the intentions of those in control. The same logic applies to AI infrastructure.

AI power concentration creates specific risks beyond what applied to earlier information infrastructure. The systems being built can affect what people believe (through content generation and recommendation), determine who gets resources (through automated decision systems), and shape the capabilities of other powerful actors (through access to AI tools for surveillance, persuasion, and automation). Control over AI infrastructure is, in a meaningful sense, control over the conditions under which democratic society functions.

The Open vs. Closed Debate

One axis of the power concentration debate is whether frontier AI models should be open-source (weights publicly released) or closed (API-only access). Open release distributes power but also makes it harder to prevent misuse by bad actors. Closed systems concentrate control but allow safety interventions. Neither position is obviously correct — both involve genuine tradeoffs between access, safety, and accountability. The debate is an ethical one dressed in technical clothing.

Global Asymmetry and Who Benefits

AI development is not globally distributed. The majority of frontier AI capability is concentrated in the United States and China, with significant but secondary capacity in Europe and the UK. Most of the world — including the populations most likely to be affected by AI-powered decision systems — have almost no role in shaping AI development, no access to frontier models, and limited ability to develop domestic capacity to govern AI systems applied in their jurisdictions.

This asymmetry has concrete ethical implications. Training data includes text from across the world, extracted without compensation or consent. The productivity gains from AI deployment accrue primarily to economies that already have technological advantages. The risks and harms of AI deployment — biased decision systems, surveillance technology, disinformation — land globally, including in communities with the least capacity to respond. The ethics of who benefits and who bears costs is not separable from the ethics of how AI is built.

Organizational Ethics and Culture

Why individual ethics isn't enough — and what ethical AI organizations actually look like

In 2022, a former Facebook data scientist named Frances Haugen provided thousands of internal documents to regulators and journalists. The documents showed that Facebook's own research had identified that Instagram was harmful to teenage girls' mental health, that its algorithms amplified political outrage and misinformation, and that the company had consistently prioritized engagement metrics over interventions its own researchers recommended. None of this required malicious employees. It required an organization structured to optimize for engagement revenue — and an internal culture where safety research results that threatened that optimization had predictable outcomes.

The ethical failures were organizational before they were individual.

How Organizations Shape Ethical Outcomes

Individual ethical intentions are necessary but not sufficient for ethical organizational outcomes. An organization's structure — what gets measured, what gets rewarded, who has authority to stop a product launch, what happens to people who raise concerns — determines ethical outcomes more reliably than the personal values of its members. A company that measures success by engagement metrics and compensates leaders on that basis will produce engagement-maximizing decisions even if every individual employee prefers different outcomes.

Ethical AI organizations share several structural features: ethics review with actual authority (not advisory committees whose recommendations can be overridden), incentive alignment (compensation and promotion that includes safety and ethics outcomes, not only product metrics), psychological safety (where raising concerns is career-neutral or career-positive), and red lines (categories of harm that are hard-coded as non-negotiable rather than subject to cost-benefit calculation).

The Ethics Team Without Power Problem

Many large technology companies have created ethics, safety, or responsible AI teams in response to public pressure. The critical question is not whether such a team exists but whether it has meaningful authority: Can it stop a product launch? Can it require design changes? Are its recommendations binding or advisory? An ethics team that can flag concerns but cannot compel action is a reputational buffer, not a governance mechanism. Its existence can make genuine accountability harder by providing the appearance of oversight without its substance.

The Role of External Pressure

Internal organizational ethics is shaped by external pressure — from regulators, from users, from civil society organizations, from employees, and from the market. The Facebook documents became public because Frances Haugen made them public. The changes that followed — congressional hearings, regulatory attention, policy updates — came from external pressure, not internal reform.

This is not an argument for cynicism about internal ethics efforts; it is a realistic account of how organizational change happens. Internal ethics advocates are more effective when they can credibly invoke external accountability. Regulators are more effective when they have access to internal documents. Civil society organizations are more effective when they can document harm. The ecosystem of external accountability is not an alternative to internal ethics — it is what gives internal ethics advocates leverage.

AI Ethics & Decision Making · Module 4 · Lesson 4

The Question of Building at All

When is not building something the ethical choice — and what that question demands of practitioners

In March 2023, an open letter signed by thousands of AI researchers and technology figures called for a six-month pause on training AI systems more powerful than GPT-4. The signatories included prominent researchers and executives. The letter was criticized by others in the field who argued that pauses would advantage less safety-conscious actors, that the risks cited were speculative, and that the letter's actual effect was more about public perception than technical reality. No pause occurred.

The letter's impact was limited. But the question it raised was not: At some capability threshold, does the ethical analysis of building AI change — and what does a practitioner owe when they believe they've crossed it?

When "Build It and Fix It Later" Fails

The dominant ethos of technology development — move fast, ship early, iterate based on real-world feedback — assumes that harms are detectable, reversible, and correctable through iteration. This assumption fails for a class of AI harms that are diffuse (affecting many people in ways that are hard to attribute), irreversible (once a harmful system has shaped social structures or individual lives, rolling it back changes little), or catastrophic (a narrow category but one that the most capable AI systems increasingly enters).

For most AI applications, "build and fix" remains a reasonable approach because the harms are bounded and manageable. For some — systems that affect democratic processes, systems with significant dual-use potential, systems that interact with other powerful systems in ways that are hard to predict — the ethical burden of "fix it later" is higher than the field has generally acknowledged. Recognizing which category a given system falls into is itself an ethical obligation of practitioners.

The Dual-Use Problem

Many AI capabilities are genuinely dual-use: useful for beneficial applications and dangerous for harmful ones in ways that are hard to separate. Facial recognition can identify missing children and enable authoritarian surveillance. Language models can assist writers and generate targeted disinformation. Autonomous systems can improve logistics and enable lethal autonomous weapons. The existence of beneficial uses does not automatically justify building the technology — it creates an obligation to honestly evaluate whether the beneficial applications require the dangerous capability, or whether they can be achieved more safely.

What Practitioners Owe at the Frontier

People building the most capable AI systems in the world occupy an unusual position: they have more knowledge than almost anyone about what they are building, they have chosen to build it, and they have more ability than almost anyone to shape how it is built. This combination of knowledge, agency, and capacity creates ethical obligations that are different in kind from those of most workers.

This does not mean AI practitioners should unilaterally stop all development based on personal risk assessments — individual judgment is not a sufficient basis for decisions with this scope. It means they have obligations to be honest about what they know and don't know, to push for governance structures commensurate with the risks they're creating, to raise concerns through available channels and escalate when those channels fail, and to take seriously — rather than dismiss — the possibility that "we don't know how to build this safely yet" is sometimes the correct conclusion. Intellectual honesty about uncertainty is itself an ethical act.
