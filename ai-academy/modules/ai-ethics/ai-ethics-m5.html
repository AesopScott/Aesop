AI Ethics & Decision Making · Module 5 · Lesson 1

AI and Democracy

How AI systems interact with elections, political discourse, and the conditions for democratic self-governance

In 2016, researchers at the University of California identified a phenomenon they called the Search Engine Manipulation Effect: because search results shape what information people encounter, a search engine biased toward one candidate could shift voter preferences by 20 percent or more in undecided voters — without users being aware of any influence. The researchers found the effect was larger among people who considered themselves politically neutral. Those who thought they were making independent judgments were the most susceptible.

No one needed to intend the outcome. The recommendation algorithm didn't need to be politically motivated. The architecture of information delivery was sufficient.

How AI Shapes Political Information

Democratic self-governance depends on citizens forming political views through genuine deliberation — exposure to real arguments, accurate information, and diverse perspectives. AI systems now intermediate nearly all political information consumption: search results determine what articles are found, recommendation algorithms determine what content is amplified, content moderation systems determine what speech remains visible. Each of these systems embeds choices about what information reaches whom.

The ethical problem is not that these systems have political effects — all information systems do. The problem is that the choices embedded in them are made by private organizations with no democratic accountability, are largely invisible to users, and are often optimized for engagement rather than for the conditions that support good democratic deliberation. An algorithm that surfaces outrage-generating content to maximize engagement is shaping political discourse whether or not it intends to.

AI-Generated Political Content at Scale

Large language models enable the generation of politically targeted content at a scale and speed previously impossible. A single actor can now generate thousands of personalized political messages, realistic synthetic media of political figures, coordinated inauthentic social media behavior, and targeted disinformation campaigns tailored to specific audiences. The 2024 election cycle saw documented use of AI-generated content in political advertising across multiple countries. The detection and attribution problem is not solved.

The Epistemic Infrastructure Problem

Healthy democracy requires what some theorists call epistemic infrastructure: shared facts, trusted institutions, and mechanisms for collective belief formation. AI-generated synthetic media, automated disinformation, and algorithmically-curated filter bubbles all degrade this infrastructure — not by making any specific claim false, but by making it harder for citizens to distinguish true from false, to find shared factual ground, and to trust institutions that depend on collective agreement.

This epistemic degradation is a distinct ethical harm from any specific piece of disinformation. A society in which no one trusts any source of information is ungovernable in ways that persist even after specific false claims are corrected. Building AI systems that contribute to this degradation — even inadvertently, even as a side effect of optimization for other goals — requires serious ethical justification.

AI Ethics & Decision Making · Module 5 · Lesson 2

Autonomy, Nudging, and Manipulation

The ethics of AI systems designed to change human behavior — and where influence becomes manipulation

In 2012, Facebook ran an experiment on 689,003 users without their knowledge or consent. By adjusting the emotional valence of content in their news feeds — showing more positive or more negative posts — Facebook found it could measurably shift users' own emotional expression in subsequent posts. Users who saw more negative content wrote more negatively. Users who saw more positive content wrote more positively. The emotional states of people who had never interacted were being altered by algorithmic content selection.

The experiment was later published in an academic journal. When it became public, the backlash was significant — not primarily because the finding was surprising, but because it made explicit what had always been true: the feed was never neutral, and Facebook had known it.

The Line Between Nudging and Manipulation

Behavioral economics has established that how choices are presented — their framing, ordering, and defaults — significantly affects what people choose, often without their awareness. This insight has produced both legitimate applications (opt-out organ donation defaults, calorie labeling, retirement savings auto-enrollment) and ethically problematic ones. AI systems are the most powerful choice architecture tools ever built, capable of personalizing environmental design to millions of individuals simultaneously.

Philosophers distinguish nudges — interventions that influence behavior while preserving genuine choice, are transparent about their intent, and serve the interests of those nudged — from manipulation — interventions that exploit psychological vulnerabilities, bypass rational agency, are concealed, or serve the interests of the influencer rather than the influenced. Most AI engagement optimization sits closer to manipulation: it exploits documented psychological vulnerabilities (variable reward schedules, social comparison, fear of missing out) to maximize time-on-platform, serves the platform's advertising revenue, and is deliberately concealed from users.

Dark Patterns and Designed Friction

Dark patterns are user interface designs that manipulate users into actions they didn't intend — subscribing when trying to cancel, sharing data when trying to decline, purchasing when trying to browse. AI enables the systematic personalization of dark patterns: interfaces can be optimized per user to exploit that individual's specific decision-making vulnerabilities. What was once a blunt instrument (a confusing cancellation flow) becomes a precision instrument (a cancellation flow optimized against your specific psychological profile). The FTC and EU have both taken enforcement action against dark patterns, but the practice continues at scale.

Autonomy as an Ethical Value

Autonomy — the capacity to form and act on one's own values and judgments — is foundational to most ethical frameworks. Manipulation is wrong not only because it produces bad outcomes but because it treats people as means rather than as agents: it bypasses their reasoning rather than engaging it. AI systems that systematically undermine users' ability to make genuine choices — about what to buy, what to believe, how to spend their time — are causing a distinctive kind of harm that is not reducible to other categories of harm.

This is why "users keep engaging, so they must want it" is an insufficient defense of addictive design. Compulsive engagement driven by exploited psychological vulnerabilities is not the same as genuine preference. A gambling addict who keeps gambling is not expressing autonomous preference for gambling. A user who can't stop scrolling because the feed is engineered to exploit variable reward schedules is not expressing genuine preference for scrolling. The distinction between exploitation and preference satisfaction matters for ethics even when it is uncomfortable for business models that depend on conflating them.

AI Ethics & Decision Making · Module 5 · Lesson 3

Labor, Creativity, and What AI Displaces

The ethics of AI systems that replace human creative and cognitive work — and what is actually at stake

In 2023, the Writers Guild of America and Screen Actors Guild went on strike simultaneously for the first time in over sixty years. Among the central issues: studios' desire to use AI to generate scripts, scan actors' likenesses, and replace human creative labor with AI-generated content. The strikes lasted months. The final agreements included some protections — studios had to disclose AI use, could not use AI-generated material as a basis for scripts without writer consent, had to compensate actors for digital replicas. But the agreements also reflected how much had already changed: the question was no longer whether AI would be used, but on what terms.

The strike was not about technology. It was about who captures the value that technology creates — and who bears its costs.

What AI Displacement Actually Displaces

When AI systems replace human creative and cognitive work, the ethical question is not only economic — who gets paid — but also about what kinds of work are valuable in ways that are not reducible to economic output. Human creative work involves more than producing text, images, or music: it involves a person's relationship to their craft, the development of skills over time, the expressive capacity to communicate something that a person genuinely means. These values don't disappear when AI can produce similar-looking outputs more cheaply.

The economic argument for AI displacement is that it makes outputs more affordable and accessible — more people can have custom art, personalized writing, tailored media. The ethical counter is that this calculus ignores the value of the work itself to the worker, the loss of the conditions under which human creative practice develops, and the question of who captures the productivity gains. When studios replace writers with AI scripts, the efficiency gain accrues to studios; the cost — loss of livelihood, craft, and a viable creative career path — is borne by writers.

Training Data and the Consent Problem

Most large generative AI models were trained on copyrighted creative work — books, art, music, code, journalism — without the consent or compensation of the creators. Multiple ongoing lawsuits argue this constitutes copyright infringement. Even where current law is uncertain, the ethics are more straightforward: creators whose work was used to build AI systems that now compete with them for employment received no compensation for that contribution. The economic argument for AI generation rests partly on creative labor that was extracted without permission.

Who Captures the Value

The distribution of AI productivity gains is not determined by technology — it is determined by power. When technology increases productivity, the gains can flow to workers (through higher wages or shorter hours), to consumers (through lower prices), or to capital owners (through higher profits). Historically, without countervailing power, productivity gains from automation have disproportionately flowed to capital. AI is not different in this respect; the question is whether sufficient countervailing power — through collective bargaining, regulation, or other mechanisms — exists to shape the distribution.

The WGA and SAG-AFTRA strikes demonstrated that creative workers can exercise collective power to shape AI deployment terms. The agreements are imperfect and the situation remains contested — but they established a precedent that AI deployment in creative industries is a subject of legitimate negotiation rather than a purely unilateral management decision. Whether similar mechanisms can be developed in sectors where workers have less collective power is an open and urgent question.

Looking Forward: Responsibilities at the Threshold

What AI ethics requires now — as a field, as organizations, and as individuals

In 2023, Geoffrey Hinton — often called a godfather of modern deep learning — resigned from Google and began publicly warning about AI risks. He said he regretted some of his life's work. He warned that AI systems might develop goals of their own that conflict with human interests. He expressed concern that the competitive race between major AI labs made it structurally difficult for any one actor to slow down unilaterally, even if they wanted to.

Hinton's departure was significant not because it resolved any question — his predictions remain contested — but because it made visible what had previously been said mostly inside labs: the people closest to the technology have serious concerns they don't know how to resolve, and they are building it anyway.

The Race Dynamic and Its Ethics

AI development is currently structured as a competitive race between a small number of well-funded actors with strong incentives to deploy quickly and weak incentives to slow down unilaterally. This structure has a predictable ethical consequence: safety and ethics investments that are individually costly but collectively necessary will be systematically underproduced. Every actor has reason to free-ride on safety investments made by others while capturing the competitive advantages of faster deployment.

This is a collective action problem, not a problem of individual ethics. Individual AI researchers can have excellent personal values and still participate in a system that produces outcomes no one would choose if they were deciding collectively. The solution space is therefore institutional — binding standards, liability frameworks, international coordination — not merely individual — better values, more ethical practitioners. Both matter; institutional solutions matter more.

What "AI Safety" Actually Means

AI safety is not one thing. It encompasses near-term safety (preventing current systems from causing harm through bias, manipulation, or error), medium-term safety (preventing AI systems from being misused for surveillance, disinformation, or autonomous weapons), and long-term safety (preventing AI systems from developing goals or acquiring capabilities that threaten human welfare or autonomy at civilizational scale). These are related but distinct concerns requiring different technical and policy responses. The field has not converged on how to weigh them against each other.

What This Course Asks of You

AI ethics is not primarily a theoretical discipline — it is a practical one that requires action at multiple levels. For individual practitioners: develop and act on professional ethical obligations, raise concerns through available channels, maintain intellectual honesty about what you know and don't know, and recognize that "I was just doing my job" is not a sufficient ethical position for people building consequential systems. For organizations: build accountability structures with actual authority, align incentives with ethical outcomes, create genuine psychological safety for concerns to be raised. For governance: develop binding standards, create liability frameworks that close accountability gaps, invest in international coordination on risks that are global in scope.

For anyone engaging with AI — which is now nearly everyone — the minimum obligation is to understand what you're using. The goal of this course has been to provide the conceptual tools to think clearly about AI systems: what they actually do, whose interests they serve, what choices are embedded in them, and what ethical standards are appropriate for systems with this much consequence. Understanding is not the same as being able to fix everything. But understanding is where the obligation to act begins — and where uninformed acquiescence ends.
