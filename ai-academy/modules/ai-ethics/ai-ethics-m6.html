AI in Healthcare

Clinical decision support, diagnostic AI, and the ethics of machines in medicine

In 2023, a study published in JAMA found that an AI diagnostic tool for detecting diabetic retinopathy performed significantly worse on images taken in lower-resource clinical settings — worse lighting, older equipment, less standardized image capture — than it had in the controlled conditions of its validation study. The patients in those lower-resource settings were disproportionately from communities of color and rural populations. The tool had been approved and deployed. The disparity was discovered after deployment, through a research study that the deploying health systems had not commissioned.

The tool wasn't biased by design. Its validation hadn't been fraudulent. Its deployment context had simply never been its testing context — and the people who paid for that mismatch were the ones who could least afford to.

What Clinical AI Actually Does

AI systems in healthcare span a wide range of functions: diagnostic support (flagging abnormalities in medical images, predicting disease risk from clinical data), clinical decision support (recommending treatment options, alerting to drug interactions), administrative automation (prior authorization, billing, scheduling), and operational optimization (resource allocation, staffing). These categories have very different ethical profiles — administrative automation raises different concerns than AI-assisted diagnosis of cancer.

The highest-stakes category — clinical decision support that influences diagnosis or treatment — raises distinct ethical issues. When an AI system recommends a course of treatment, the question of how much that recommendation should influence the clinician's decision is not purely technical. Automation bias — the documented tendency for humans to over-rely on algorithmic outputs even when their own judgment would be more accurate — means that a clinician presented with an AI recommendation does not simply receive information; they receive information with an implicit authority that can displace clinical judgment.

The Validation-Deployment Gap

FDA clearance for medical AI devices typically requires demonstrating performance on a validation dataset. The validation dataset is almost never fully representative of the deployment environment — different equipment, patient populations, clinical workflows, and image acquisition standards all affect performance. A tool validated on data from academic medical centers may perform substantially worse in community hospitals or rural clinics. This gap is well-documented and systematically underaddressed. Post-market surveillance requirements for AI medical devices remain weaker than for most other medical interventions.

Consent and the Patient's Right to Know

Informed consent in medicine requires that patients understand what is being done to them and have meaningful opportunity to decline. When AI systems influence clinical decisions — flagging a scan, recommending a medication, predicting a risk score — patients are frequently not informed that AI played a role. The ethical question is not simply whether patients should be told AI was used (though many argue they should) but whether patients have the right to request human-only review, to know the performance characteristics of the AI tool applied to them, and to contest AI-influenced decisions.

No consistent regulatory or professional standard currently answers these questions. Several jurisdictions have proposed requiring disclosure of AI use in clinical settings; none have enacted comprehensive requirements. The gap between what informed consent requires in principle and what healthcare AI practice provides is significant — and the patients who have the least power to demand disclosure are typically the patients most at risk from AI performance disparities.

AI in Criminal Justice

Predictive systems, risk scores, and the ethics of algorithmic judgment in law

By 2016, risk assessment instruments were being used in pretrial detention decisions, sentencing, parole, and probation across dozens of U.S. jurisdictions. COMPAS, PSA, LSI-R — different tools, similar structure: input demographic and behavioral data, output a risk score, use the score to inform decisions about people's liberty. Courts in several states had ruled their use constitutional. The tools had never been required to demonstrate that they actually reduced recidivism. They had been required only to predict it — and prediction is not the same as prevention.

The systems were not designed to make people safer. They were designed to predict who would be dangerous. Whether those predictions changed anything — or just changed who bore the burden — was a question no one had answered.

The Prediction vs. Prevention Problem

Risk assessment tools in criminal justice are sold and regulated as predictive tools: they estimate the probability that a person will commit a future offense. They are not required to demonstrate that their use actually prevents crime — that detaining high-scoring individuals leads to fewer offenses than alternatives would. This distinction matters enormously for ethics. A tool that accurately predicts recidivism but whose use doesn't reduce it is not serving public safety — it is serving administrative convenience, at the cost of the liberty of people labeled high-risk.

The prediction-prevention gap is compounded by base rate realities. Even a highly accurate predictive tool applied to a population where most people do not reoffend will generate large numbers of false positives — people labeled high-risk who would not have reoffended. Those false positives pay the cost of detention or supervision without having done anything. In a system that already has documented racial disparities in policing and prosecution, prediction tools trained on that system's historical outputs inherit and encode those disparities.

The Right to Contest

In most U.S. jurisdictions using algorithmic risk scores, defendants have no right to see the algorithm's inputs for their specific case, to challenge the accuracy of those inputs, to access the validation data for the tool, or to have a human independently verify the score. The Eric Loomis case (Wisconsin Supreme Court, 2017) held this constitutional on the grounds that judges were not required to follow the score — even though research consistently shows judges are significantly influenced by the scores they are told not to treat as determinative. Paper rights that are reliably circumvented by documented cognitive effects are not real rights.

Recidivism Prediction and Self-Fulfilling Prophecy

A person labeled high-risk is more likely to be detained pretrial, receive a harsher sentence, and face more intensive supervision post-release. More intensive supervision means more opportunities for technical violations — violations that result in incarceration independent of new criminal conduct. A system that labels someone high-risk and then monitors them intensively will generate more violations from that person than it would from someone labeled low-risk receiving minimal supervision. The risk score influences the conditions that then appear to confirm the risk score. This is not prediction; it is construction.

The ethical implications are significant. If risk scores are partially self-fulfilling — if they generate the outcomes they predict partly by shaping the conditions under which people live — then they are not neutral measurement instruments. They are interventions with effects, and those effects should be evaluated and governed as interventions, not as passive observations.

AI in Education

Personalization, surveillance, and the ethics of AI systems that govern how children learn

During the COVID-19 pandemic, remote proctoring software was deployed at scale across universities worldwide. The systems used AI to monitor students during online exams: flagging eye movements, detecting background noise, analyzing keystroke patterns, requiring students to scan their rooms with webcams, and in some cases flagging dark skin tones as "no face detected" due to lighting model failures. Students reported significant anxiety from the surveillance. Faculty reported concerns about whether the systems detected cheating or just detected difference — non-native speakers, students with ADHD, students in poor lighting. Some institutions abandoned the tools during deployment after student protests.

The surveillance was sold as security. What it produced, for many students, was something closer to a panopticon exam experience — monitored, suspected, never quite trusted.

Personalization and Its Limits

Adaptive learning systems — AI that adjusts educational content and pacing based on student performance — represent one of the most genuinely promising applications of AI in any domain. When they work well, they can identify gaps in understanding that standardized instruction misses, provide more practice where students struggle, and move faster where mastery exists. When they work badly, they can lock students into remedial tracks based on early performance, optimize for tested content at the expense of deeper understanding, and produce engagement metrics that look good while learning is hollow.

The ethical concerns with educational AI are not primarily about the technology's potential — they are about governance, data, and power. Educational data — learning records, behavioral profiles, engagement patterns — is among the most sensitive personal data that exists. It is collected on minors, often without their meaningful consent, and is increasingly used beyond educational contexts for things students and parents did not agree to. The institutions that collect this data often have weak security and weaker accountability for how it is used.

Early AI Tracking and Its Consequences

Adaptive learning systems that categorize students as "advanced," "grade-level," or "remedial" in early grades are making predictions that have documented long-term effects. Students tracked into lower-level courses in elementary or middle school are substantially less likely to reach higher-level coursework in high school. If an AI system makes these tracking decisions based on limited data — early performance, which correlates strongly with socioeconomic background and school quality — it is encoding systemic inequality into educational trajectories while appearing to provide objective assessment.

Surveillance, Trust, and the Learning Environment

Learning requires psychological safety — the willingness to attempt things you might get wrong, to ask questions that reveal ignorance, to take intellectual risks. Pervasive surveillance in educational environments is documented to undermine this safety. Students who know they are monitored restrict their behavior, including the kinds of exploratory behavior that learning depends on. The proctoring software controversy illustrated this at scale: the surveillance designed to prevent cheating may have prevented learning in the same motion.

There is also a power asymmetry that is specific to education: students, especially minors, have extremely limited ability to decline surveillance that their schools require, to access the data collected about them, or to contest interpretations of their behavioral data. FERPA in the U.S. provides some protection, but its coverage of AI-generated behavioral profiles is untested, and its enforcement has historically been weak. The combination of sensitive data, limited consent capacity, and weak accountability creates serious ethical obligations for educational AI developers and deployers.

AI in Financial Services

Credit, insurance, and the ethics of algorithmic decisions that determine economic access

In 2019, Apple and Goldman Sachs launched the Apple Card. Shortly after launch, a software developer named David Heinemeier Hansson posted on Twitter that his Apple Card credit limit was twenty times higher than his wife's — despite her having a higher credit score. The story went viral. Others reported the same pattern. The New York Department of Financial Services opened an investigation. Goldman Sachs said its algorithm did not use gender. An investigation found the algorithm had used variables — address history, credit utilization patterns — that correlate with gender in ways the company said it hadn't known and hadn't tested for. The investigation found no intentional discrimination, but identified the failure to test for disparate impact before deployment.

The discrimination wasn't intended. But the failure to look for it was a choice.

Disparate Impact and the Obligation to Test

U.S. fair lending law prohibits both disparate treatment (intentionally treating protected classes differently) and disparate impact (practices that are facially neutral but disproportionately harm protected classes without business justification). The Apple Card investigation highlighted a gap that is widespread in financial AI: the obligation to test for disparate impact before deployment, not only after complaints arise.

Testing for disparate impact in AI credit models requires knowing what variables correlate with protected attributes — which requires understanding the structural conditions that create those correlations. A lender that doesn't test for these correlations isn't neutral; it is choosing not to know. Given that disparate impact is legally prohibited and the test is feasible, "we didn't know" is not an adequate defense — it is an admission that the lender chose not to look.

Insurance AI and the Use of Proxies

Insurance pricing AI has developed an extensive vocabulary of proxy variables that correlate with protected characteristics without explicitly using them. Credit scores are used in auto and homeowners insurance pricing across most U.S. states — even though credit score correlates with race and income in ways that have nothing to do with driving risk or property loss risk. Education level, occupation, and ZIP code have all been used in insurance pricing. Each is presented as predictive; each encodes characteristics that are legally protected. The regulatory landscape varies significantly by state; federal protection is limited.

Explainability in Financial Decisions and the ECOA Obligation

The Equal Credit Opportunity Act requires lenders to provide "specific reasons" for adverse credit actions. This right predates AI but its application to AI credit models is contested. A neural network that produces a credit decision based on hundreds of weighted variables cannot easily produce the kind of specific reason the regulation envisions — "your debt-to-income ratio exceeds our threshold" — because its decision emerges from the interaction of variables in ways that resist simple explanation.

Financial regulators have increasingly focused on this gap. The CFPB has stated that ECOA's adverse action notice requirement applies to AI credit decisions, and that "complex algorithm" is not a valid reason for failing to provide specific explanations. The technical challenge of generating ECOA-compliant explanations from opaque models is real — but it is a constraint that should shape model choice (toward more explainable architectures), not a justification for deploying inexplicable models in violation of legal rights.
