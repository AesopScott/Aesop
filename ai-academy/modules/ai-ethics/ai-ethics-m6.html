Module 6 · Lesson 1

Diagnostic AI & Algorithmic Bias

When the training data excludes you, the algorithm may too.
Why did a widely deployed clinical algorithm systematically under-treat Black patients — and what does that reveal about AI in medicine?

Researchers at the University of Michigan published a study in the New England Journal of Medicine in December 2020 documenting a disturbing pattern: pulse oximeters — the small clip devices that measure blood oxygen saturation — were nearly three times more likely to miss dangerously low oxygen levels in Black patients than in white patients. The devices had been calibrated predominantly on lighter-skinned subjects, and the discrepancy had persisted, unexamined, for decades.

When this same flawed pulse-ox data was fed into clinical AI systems — including algorithms used by hospitals to triage COVID-19 patients in 2020 — the bias was not corrected. It was amplified.

The Landmark Obermeyer Study

In October 2019, Ziad Obermeyer and colleagues published in Science what became one of the most cited papers on racial bias in clinical AI. They analyzed a commercial risk-stratification algorithm used by major U.S. health systems to identify patients who needed additional care management. The algorithm was built by Optum, a subsidiary of UnitedHealth Group, and was used on roughly 200 million people per year.

The core finding: the algorithm used healthcare cost as a proxy for health need. Because systemic inequities mean Black patients historically incur lower costs than equally sick white patients — due to reduced access, distrust of the system, and economic barriers — the algorithm interpreted lower past spending as lower current need. Black patients had to be substantially sicker than white patients to receive the same algorithm score and the same level of care.

Obermeyer estimated that correcting the bias would nearly double the percentage of Black patients receiving high-risk care management from 17.7% to 46.5%.

Key Finding

The Optum algorithm was not built with racist intent. It was built by optimizing for a measurable proxy — cost — that happened to encode decades of systemic health inequity. This is a canonical example of proxy bias: choosing a measurable stand-in variable that correlates with the outcome of interest but also correlates with protected characteristics in ways that produce discriminatory results.

Why Healthcare AI Is Particularly High-Stakes

Healthcare AI decisions differ from most other AI domains in at least three critical ways. First, stakes are irreversible — a missed cancer diagnosis or a delayed sepsis alert can cost a life. Second, feedback loops are slow — a clinician may not know for months or years whether an algorithm's recommendation was correct. Third, populations are heterogeneous in ways that training datasets often fail to capture — genetic differences, socioeconomic differences, geographic differences, and differences in care-seeking behavior all interact.

The FDA began requiring algorithmic bias assessments for AI-based medical devices in its 2021 action plan for AI/ML-based software as a medical device (SaMD). But the vast majority of hospital algorithms deployed before 2021 — and many deployed after — were never subjected to such scrutiny.

Key Terms

Disparate ImpactWhen a facially neutral policy or algorithm produces systematically worse outcomes for members of a protected group, regardless of intent.
Calibration BiasWhen a model's predicted probabilities are systematically miscalibrated for specific demographic subgroups, causing the model to be overconfident or underconfident for those groups.

What Happened After Obermeyer

Optum, upon reviewing the findings, acknowledged the bias and worked with Obermeyer's team to redesign the algorithm. The revised model used direct health measures — number of active chronic conditions, lab values — rather than cost as the primary predictor. This is notable: the response was a technical fix, not a policy debate. But critics noted that without mandatory auditing requirements, thousands of similar algorithms remained unexamined in hospitals across the country.

The episode prompted the National Academy of Medicine to issue a 2022 report calling for mandatory bias audits of all clinical decision support tools, and contributed to Congressional hearings on health AI equity in 2023.

Core Principle

Bias in healthcare AI is not primarily a problem of bad actors. It is a problem of invisible assumptions — about whose data is representative, whose outcomes matter, and which proxies are acceptable stand-ins for which realities. Identifying those assumptions requires both technical auditing and genuine inclusion of affected communities in system design.

AI in Radiology & Pathology

Computer vision that reads scans has saved lives — and missed diagnoses too.
How has AI-assisted radiology performed in real clinical deployments, and where have the documented failures emerged?

In April 2018, the FDA granted De Novo authorization to IDx-DR — a clinical AI system developed by IDx Technologies — making it the first AI diagnostic device permitted to operate without a clinician reviewing the output. The system analyzed retinal photographs to detect diabetic retinopathy. In its pivotal trial of 900 patients, IDx-DR achieved 87.2% sensitivity and 90.7% specificity. For the first time in U.S. history, an AI was authorized to make a clinical recommendation autonomously.

Documented Successes: Skin Cancer and Lung Nodules

In January 2017, a landmark study published in Nature by Esteva et al. from Stanford demonstrated that a deep convolutional neural network trained on 129,450 clinical images could classify skin lesions — including melanoma — at a level comparable to 21 board-certified dermatologists. The CNN achieved an area under the ROC curve of 0.96 for detecting keratinocyte carcinomas and 0.91 for melanomas.

In lung cancer screening, the NLST (National Lung Screening Trial) showed CT screening reduced mortality by 20%. Google Health's 2019 Nature Medicine study trained a deep learning model on 42,290 lung CT scans. The model outperformed six radiologists on cancer detection, reducing false positives by 11% and false negatives by 5% compared to the radiologists' average. For scans without a prior comparison scan — a common real-world scenario — the model performed particularly well.

Real Case — Diagnostic Failure

In 2019, Epic deployed an AI-powered sepsis prediction algorithm called the Epic Sepsis Model (ESM) across hundreds of U.S. hospitals. A 2021 independent study in JAMA Internal Medicine by Wong et al. evaluated the ESM on 27,697 patients at a University of Michigan hospital. The model had an AUROC of only 0.74 — substantially below the 0.76–0.83 range Epic had reported internally — and generated 18 false alerts for every true positive catch in high-risk patients. Clinicians began ignoring the alerts. The study authors warned that widespread desensitization to AI alerts could create new patient safety risks.

The Distribution Shift Problem

Most radiology AI systems are trained on curated datasets from academic medical centers — typically large, well-resourced institutions with high-quality imaging equipment, standardized protocols, and relatively affluent patient populations. When these models are deployed in community hospitals, rural clinics, or lower-income settings, they frequently encounter distribution shift — scans acquired with different machines, protocols, or patient populations than the training data.

A 2020 study in Radiology: Artificial Intelligence examined a pneumonia detection model trained on data from one hospital system and applied it to data from another. Model performance dropped dramatically — AUC fell from 0.931 to 0.736 — simply because the new hospital used a different standard for what constituted a "positive" training label. The model had learned the idiosyncrasies of one radiologist group, not the underlying pathology.

This problem is not merely academic. In the UK, NHS England deployed an AI tool for chest X-ray triage from Qure.ai. A 2022 independent evaluation found performance varied substantially across NHS trusts, with some sites showing strong results and others showing performance only marginally better than chance.

FDA's Evolving Framework

By 2023, the FDA had authorized over 690 AI/ML-enabled medical devices, with radiology AI accounting for roughly 75% of authorizations. The FDA's 2021 AI/ML Action Plan introduced the concept of Predetermined Change Control Plans (PCCPs) — allowing manufacturers to describe in advance how their algorithms would be updated post-market without requiring a new premarket submission. This created flexibility but also new accountability gaps.

Critics, including a 2023 JAMA editorial by Shah and colleagues, argued that the FDA's current framework lacks mandatory post-market surveillance requirements for AI performance degradation, leaving hospitals to discover failures through adverse events rather than proactive monitoring.

The Validation Gap

Most AI radiology tools are authorized based on single-site retrospective studies. Real-world multi-site prospective validation is rare. The gap between controlled validation and messy deployment is where most real clinical AI failures occur — not because the models are fundamentally flawed, but because the conditions of their certification don't match the conditions of their use.

Consent, Privacy & Health Data Governance

Patient records trained the algorithms — but did patients ever agree to that?
When hospitals use patient data to train commercial AI systems, what ethical obligations do they have — and what has happened when those obligations were ignored?

In 2015, Google's DeepMind struck a partnership with the Royal Free NHS Trust in London to develop an acute kidney injury (AKI) detection app called Streams. The agreement gave DeepMind access to 1.6 million patient records — including HIV status, abortion history, and mental health data — covering every patient treated at the Royal Free over five years. Patients were not informed. No individual consent was obtained.

In July 2017, the UK's Information Commissioner's Office (ICO) ruled that the Royal Free had violated the Data Protection Act. The partnership had assumed that clinical care justification — improving AKI detection — was sufficient legal basis for transferring five years of historical records including data entirely unrelated to AKI. It was not.

The Scope and Significance of the DeepMind Ruling

The ICO's 2017 enforcement notice against the Royal Free NHS Trust was the first major regulatory ruling on health AI data partnerships in the UK. The ruling found that patients whose records were shared had a "reasonable expectation" that their data would be used for their direct care — not transferred to a third party, including a technology company, for algorithm development.

The problem was not that AKI detection is a bad goal. It was that the breadth of the data transfer far exceeded what was necessary for the stated purpose. The 1.6 million records included patients who had no history of kidney problems. Their HIV status, mental health diagnoses, and abortion records were transferred anyway, because the data pipeline was built for convenience rather than data minimization.

DeepMind subsequently published a detailed account of lessons learned and revised its data governance practices. Google Health (which absorbed DeepMind Health in 2019) has faced continued scrutiny over its health data partnerships, including the Project Nightingale controversy in 2019 — a partnership with Ascension health system to process records of 50 million Americans, again without individual patient notification.

Project Nightingale — 2019

In November 2019, the Wall Street Journal reported that Google and Ascension had struck a deal allowing Google employees to access detailed medical records of approximately 50 million Americans across 21 states under a HIPAA Business Associate Agreement. The arrangement was technically legal under HIPAA's "treatment, payment, or operations" exception — but patients were not informed, and Ascension employees were not notified until the project was already underway. The episode prompted investigations by HHS Office for Civil Rights and bipartisan Congressional scrutiny.

HIPAA and Its Limits in the AI Era

HIPAA (Health Insurance Portability and Accountability Act, 1996) was designed for a world of paper records and faxed referrals. Its core privacy protections apply to covered entities — hospitals, insurers, providers — and their business associates. But the law contains significant gaps for the AI era.

First, HIPAA does not require individual notification when data is shared with a business associate for "healthcare operations" — a broad category that courts and HHS have interpreted expansively. Second, de-identified data under HIPAA's Safe Harbor standard (removing 18 specified identifiers) is not subject to HIPAA at all — but researchers have repeatedly demonstrated that such de-identified records can be re-identified with external data sources. A 2019 study found that 99.98% of Americans could be re-identified from a dataset with just 15 demographic attributes.

Third, HIPAA does not cover health data generated outside the clinical encounter — wearable data, symptom tracker apps, genetic testing services like 23andMe — leaving vast amounts of sensitive health information entirely unprotected by federal law.

Emerging Frameworks: The EU and State-Level Responses

The EU's GDPR (2018) provides substantially stronger protections for health data than HIPAA, classifying it as a "special category" requiring explicit consent for processing and prohibiting secondary use without fresh authorization. The EU AI Act (2024) further classifies AI systems used in healthcare as high-risk, requiring conformity assessments, transparency documentation, and human oversight mechanisms before deployment.

At the U.S. state level, Washington's My Health MY Data Act (2023) became the most comprehensive state health privacy law in the country, requiring explicit opt-in consent for collection of consumer health data outside HIPAA — including wearable data — and creating a private right of action for violations.

Data MinimizationThe principle that only data strictly necessary for a specified purpose should be collected and retained — a core GDPR requirement often violated in early health AI partnerships.
Business Associate Agreement (BAA)HIPAA contract mechanism allowing covered entities to share patient data with vendors for healthcare operations without individual patient consent — a provision widely used (and debated) in health AI partnerships.
Re-identification RiskThe probability that a nominally de-identified dataset can be linked back to individual identities using external reference data — consistently higher than de-identification standards assume.
Secondary UseUsing patient data collected for direct clinical care for a different purpose — such as algorithm training — without explicit patient authorization for that new purpose.
The Consent Architecture Problem

Most current health AI data governance relies on broad institutional permissions rather than meaningful individual consent. Patients sign blanket consent forms at hospital admission that few read and fewer understand. Building health AI on this foundation creates a legitimacy deficit — systems trained on patient data without genuine patient awareness or agreement — that erodes trust precisely when AI could do the most good.

Module 6 · Lesson 4

Clinical Decision-Making & Human Oversight

Automation bias, accountability gaps, and the irreplaceable role of clinical judgment.
When an AI recommends a treatment and the clinician follows it — and the patient is harmed — who is responsible?

In 2023, the American Medical Association surveyed physicians about their use of clinical AI tools. Sixty-two percent said they had encountered situations where an AI recommendation conflicted with their clinical judgment. Of those, 38% reported following the AI recommendation despite their reservations — citing documentation pressure, time constraints, and concern that deviating from an AI recommendation might expose them to liability if the patient's outcome was poor.

This is the automation bias paradox in healthcare: physicians defer to AI not because they trust it, but because the accountability architecture rewards deference.

The IBM Watson for Oncology Failure

IBM Watson for Oncology was one of the most publicized clinical AI systems of the 2010s. IBM claimed Watson could recommend cancer treatments by ingesting medical literature and matching cases to evidence-based protocols. By 2018, the system had been licensed by over 230 hospitals in 13 countries, including prominent institutions in India, South Korea, and Thailand.

In July 2018, internal IBM documents reviewed by STAT News revealed a troubling pattern. At Manipal Comprehensive Cancer Center in India — one of the system's largest deployments — physicians had identified 96 instances of Watson recommending treatments that were unsafe or incorrect. In one documented case, Watson recommended a chemotherapy combination for a patient with severe bleeding that physicians said would have posed unacceptable hemorrhage risk.

The root problem was that Watson for Oncology had been trained primarily on hypothetical cases curated by a small group of Memorial Sloan Kettering oncologists — not on actual patient outcomes data. The training data reflected the opinions of a handful of U.S. cancer specialists, and those opinions were encoded as ground truth. When cases deviated from that template — as they frequently do in South Asian patient populations with different genetic profiles and available drug formularies — Watson's recommendations were unreliable.

IBM quietly wound down Watson for Oncology by 2021. The program cost IBM an estimated $62 billion in write-downs across its Watson Health portfolio.

Automation Bias in Medicine

A 2018 study by Goddard et al. in PLOS ONE found that clinicians shown an AI diagnosis became significantly less likely to question it even when presented with contradictory clinical evidence — a phenomenon called automation bias. The effect was strongest when the AI was presented as highly accurate, and weakest when clinicians were explicitly reminded that the AI could be wrong. This has direct implications for how clinical AI tools should be designed: displaying confidence scores and known failure modes may be as important as improving accuracy.

The Accountability Gap

When an AI system contributes to a harmful clinical outcome, current legal frameworks struggle to assign clear responsibility. The physician who followed the recommendation may claim reliance on a certified tool. The hospital that deployed it may point to the vendor's authorization. The FDA may note that its clearance was based on submitted evidence. The vendor may argue that the hospital misconfigured the system.

This many-hands problem — where harm is distributed across many actors, none of whom feel individually culpable — is a distinctive feature of AI-mediated errors. A 2022 legal analysis in the Journal of Law and the Biosciences found that no U.S. court had yet held an AI medical device manufacturer liable for a clinical outcome, largely because establishing causation in complex AI decision chains is extraordinarily difficult.

The EU AI Act addresses this partially by requiring that high-risk healthcare AI maintain meaningful human oversight — a human decision-maker must remain "in the loop" with genuine ability to override — and by placing liability on deployers who fail to implement appropriate oversight mechanisms.

Designing for Human Oversight

Research from the field of human-computer interaction and clinical informatics has identified several design principles that reduce automation bias and maintain meaningful clinician agency. These include uncertainty quantification — displaying the model's confidence and known failure modes rather than a single recommendation; counterfactual explanations — showing what would have had to be different for the recommendation to change; and friction design — requiring clinicians to actively confirm AI recommendations rather than accepting them by default.

A 2021 study at University of Chicago Medicine evaluated two versions of an AI sepsis alert: one that simply flagged high-risk patients, and one that required nurses to document either agreement with or a reason for overriding the alert. The second version reduced alert fatigue and improved response rates — not because the AI was more accurate, but because the interaction design preserved clinical ownership of the decision.

The Irreducible Role of Clinical Judgment

Healthcare AI at its best is a tool that augments the pattern-recognition capacity of clinicians — not a substitute for the relational, contextual, and ethical judgment that clinical practice requires. Watson for Oncology failed not merely because its training data was narrow, but because it was designed and marketed as a replacement for expertise rather than an extension of it. The cases that matter most in medicine are precisely the ones that don't fit the template — and those are the cases where human judgment remains irreplaceable.
