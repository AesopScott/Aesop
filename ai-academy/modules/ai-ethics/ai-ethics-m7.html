Module 7 · Lesson 1

What Is Moral Agency?

Intentions, accountability, and whether machines can bear either.
Can a system that cannot suffer, desire, or understand consequences ever be held morally responsible for harm?

At 9:58 p.m. on March 18, 2018, a Volvo SUV operated by Uber's Advanced Technologies Group struck and killed Elaine Herzberg as she walked her bicycle across Mill Avenue. The car's sensors detected her six seconds before impact. Its software classified her alternately as an unknown object, a vehicle, and a bicycle. Because the system's emergency braking had been disabled to reduce "erratic vehicle behavior," it never intervened. A safety operator was watching a video on her phone. No single actor held full responsibility. The NTSB concluded the probable cause was the failure of Uber's safety management system — not any one person, not the software alone.

The accident forced a question philosophers had debated in the abstract: when an autonomous system kills, who is the moral agent?

Moral Agency Defined

Philosophers traditionally require three conditions for moral agency: the capacity to act intentionally, the capacity to understand right from wrong, and the ability to be held accountable. A corporation can be fined; a dog cannot be imprisoned. The question for autonomous AI is whether any of these conditions can be satisfied by a computational system.

Philosopher Luciano Floridi introduced the concept of artificial moral agents (AMAs) in 2004, arguing that agency can be graded. A thermostat has minimal agency. A chess engine has more — it models states and selects actions. A system capable of choosing between value-laden outcomes (speed vs. safety in a self-driving car) starts to occupy a morally relevant space, even without consciousness.

Moral AgentAn entity capable of acting with reference to right and wrong and bearing responsibility for those actions.
Moral PatientAn entity whose welfare matters morally — who can be harmed — but who need not be a moral agent. Children and animals are classic examples.
Distributed ResponsibilityWhen harm arises from interactions among multiple actors (designers, operators, regulators, systems), such that no single party bears full responsibility. Also called the "many hands" problem.
The Spectrum of Autonomy

The Society of Automotive Engineers (SAE) defines six levels of vehicle automation, from Level 0 (no automation) to Level 5 (full automation). The ethical significance of these levels is not merely technical: at Level 2 the human driver remains responsible; at Level 4 the system is responsible in its operational domain. Moral responsibility transfers with control authority.

The same spectrum applies beyond vehicles. A spam filter making binary decisions is at the low end. An algorithmic trading system executing thousands of transactions per second with no human review is further along. An AI system deciding credit applications, parole risk scores, or medical treatment pathways sits somewhere in between — consequential, but with human oversight nominally retained.

Three Frameworks for Thinking About Machine Morality

Consequentialist view: machines can be evaluated morally by their outcomes. If an algorithm consistently produces racially biased parole recommendations, the system causes harm regardless of intent. This is the approach implicitly used in most regulation.

Deontological view: moral agency requires intentionality — the capacity to act from principle rather than programming. On this view, machines cannot be moral agents; blame must always trace back to a human designer or deployer.

Virtue ethics view: focuses on whether systems embody and promote virtuous dispositions. A system trained to be consistently transparent, accurate, and fair might be said to instantiate virtues even without consciousness.

Real Case — The Trolley Problem Made Literal

In 2016, MIT's Moral Machine experiment collected 40 million decisions from 233 countries, asking people how autonomous vehicles should prioritize lives in unavoidable collision scenarios. Responses varied sharply by culture: East Asian respondents showed greater deference to the elderly; Western respondents showed stronger preference for saving more lives. The experiment revealed that programming an AV's ethics is not a technical problem — it is a cross-cultural political negotiation with no universally correct answer.

Why It Matters for Accountability

The Herzberg case produced no criminal conviction. The NTSB found systemic failure; Uber settled civilly. The safety operator was charged, then charges were dropped. This is not unusual — it reflects a genuine legal and ethical vacuum. Current tort law was not designed for systems that act autonomously. Existing product liability frameworks require showing a defect; proving a statistical bias or a training-data gap is not the same as proving a broken brake line.

The EU AI Act (2024) begins to address this with risk-tiered accountability requirements. High-risk AI systems — in critical infrastructure, employment, credit, policing — must maintain logs, allow human oversight, and demonstrate conformity with standards. But the law still places accountability on humans: developers, deployers, and operators. The system itself cannot be sued or imprisoned.

Core Tension

As autonomous systems become more capable, the human oversight nominally retained may become increasingly nominal. A human who cannot understand or override a system's decision in real time is not meaningfully exercising moral agency — yet legal frameworks continue to assign responsibility to them. This gap between formal and real accountability is one of the defining ethical challenges of the autonomous systems era.

Lethal Autonomous Weapons

When the decision to kill is delegated to an algorithm.
Is there a meaningful moral difference between a soldier following orders and a drone following code?

A March 2021 UN Panel of Experts report on Libya described an engagement in which a Kargu-2 loitering munition — a Turkish-made autonomous drone — may have autonomously attacked human targets. The report stated the system was "programmed to attack targets without requiring data connectivity between the operator and the munition." If accurate, this would be the first documented lethal autonomous weapons engagement in history.

The manufacturers disputed the characterization. The ambiguity itself is instructive: we may already be past the threshold of autonomous lethal action, and we lack the documentation systems to know for certain.

The Autonomous Weapons Taxonomy

Military ethicists distinguish three categories. Human-in-the-loop systems require human authorization for each individual strike. Human-on-the-loop systems execute automatically but allow a human to intervene. Human-out-of-the-loop systems operate fully autonomously within predefined parameters — the system identifies, selects, and engages targets without real-time human authorization.

The U.S. Department of Defense Directive 3000.09 (2012, updated 2023) requires "appropriate levels of human judgment over the use of force." Critics note that "appropriate" is undefined and that the directive permits autonomous defensive systems — such as the Phalanx close-in weapon system used to shoot down incoming missiles — that make targeting decisions in milliseconds.

LAWSLethal Autonomous Weapons Systems — systems that select and engage targets without human authorization for individual strikes.
Meaningful Human ControlThe contested standard proposed by Campaign to Stop Killer Robots: humans must understand, predict, and be able to intervene in weapons decisions.
IHLInternational Humanitarian Law — the body of law governing armed conflict, including principles of distinction (combatant vs. civilian), proportionality, and precaution.
The International Humanitarian Law Problem

International Humanitarian Law (IHL) — codified in the Geneva Conventions and their Additional Protocols — rests on the principle of distinction: combatants must distinguish between military targets and civilians. It also requires proportionality: anticipated civilian harm must not be excessive relative to military advantage, and precaution: all feasible measures must be taken to minimize civilian casualties.

Human Rights Watch and Harvard Law School's International Human Rights Clinic jointly argued in their 2012 report Losing Humanity that autonomous weapons cannot satisfy the distinction and proportionality requirements because they cannot make the contextual, empathetic judgments those principles demand. A soldier can perceive that a person holding a rifle is actually surrendering; can read body language, environmental context, and social signals. Current AI systems cannot reliably do so.

Real Case — South Korea's SGR-A1 Sentry

Samsung Techwin (now Hanwha Systems) deployed the SGR-A1 autonomous sentry gun on the South Korean side of the Demilitarized Zone. The system uses machine vision to detect human targets and — in its fully autonomous mode — can engage without human input. South Korean officials have stated the system normally requires human confirmation to fire. But the existence of a fully autonomous mode in a deployed military system illustrates how the threshold is already crossed in controlled environments.

The Accountability Gap

If a LAWS kills a civilian unlawfully, who is criminally responsible under IHL? The programmer? The commanding officer who deployed the system? The state? Legal scholars including Michael Schmitt of the Naval War College argue that if no human made the specific targeting decision, existing IHL frameworks produce an accountability gap — a war crime occurred but no one is legally culpable.

The International Committee of the Red Cross has called for national laws specifically prohibiting fully autonomous weapons and requiring meaningful human control. As of 2024, no binding international treaty exists. Diplomatic discussions at the Convention on Certain Conventional Weapons (CCW) have stalled repeatedly, partly because major military powers — the United States, Russia, China, and Israel — have resisted any legally binding limits.

Arguments for LAWS

Proponents argue autonomous systems could be more precise than human soldiers under stress, could not commit war crimes from rage or fear, and could reduce risk to military personnel. Some argue they might satisfy IHL better than humans in high-pressure combat.

Arguments Against LAWS

Critics argue no algorithm can replicate the contextual judgment IHL requires, that accountability gaps make war crimes unpunishable, and that low human cost of deploying LAWS lowers the threshold for initiating armed conflict — making war more likely.

The Lowered Threshold Problem

Political scientist Peter Singer has argued that autonomous weapons create a moral hazard: when the cost of initiating lethal force drops to near zero for the deploying side, the political constraints on using force erode. States that would hesitate to send soldiers into harm's way face far less domestic pressure when deploying autonomous systems. This asymmetric risk does not eliminate war — it may accelerate it.

Module 7 · Lesson 3

Algorithmic Decision-Making in Civilian Life

When autonomous systems govern bail, credit, hiring, and healthcare.
When an algorithm denies you a loan, a job, or your freedom — and the algorithm is wrong — who answers for it?

In May 2016, ProPublica published Machine Bias, an investigation into COMPAS — Correctional Offender Management Profiling for Alternative Sanctions — a recidivism risk-scoring tool used in Broward County, Florida, and elsewhere. The investigation analyzed 7,000 criminal defendants scored in 2013–2014. It found that Black defendants were nearly twice as likely as white defendants to be falsely flagged as high risk for future crimes. White defendants were more likely to be incorrectly labeled low risk.

The algorithm's developer, Northpointe (now Equivant), responded that the tool was equally accurate across racial groups by a different metric — overall predictive accuracy. Both claims were statistically true. They measured different things. The case became the canonical illustration that fairness cannot be simultaneously optimized across all metrics.

The Fairness Impossibility Theorem

In 2016, researchers Chouldechova and independently Kleinberg et al. formally proved what the COMPAS debate illustrated empirically: when a population has different base rates for an outcome (e.g., recidivism), it is mathematically impossible to simultaneously satisfy all common fairness criteria. Specifically, calibration (predictions equally accurate across groups), equal false positive rates, and equal false negative rates cannot all hold at once when base rates differ.

This is not a flaw in any specific algorithm — it is a mathematical constraint on all classification algorithms. Any system making yes/no decisions about people must choose which form of fairness to optimize, and that choice is a values choice, not a technical one. The algorithm embeds an ethical judgment whether its designers acknowledge it or not.

CalibrationA model is calibrated if its predicted probabilities match actual outcomes equally across groups. A score of 70% risk means 70% reoffend, for all groups.
Equalized OddsEqual true positive and false positive rates across groups — meaning the algorithm makes the same types of errors equally across demographic categories.
Proxy DiscriminationWhen a variable that appears race-neutral (zip code, criminal record) serves as a statistical proxy for race, replicating discriminatory outcomes without explicit intent.
Documented Cases Across Sectors
2014
Amazon Recruiting Tool: Amazon developed a machine learning tool to screen resumes, trained on 10 years of hiring data. Because historical hires were predominantly male, the system systematically downgraded resumes containing the word "women's" (as in "women's chess club"). Amazon scrapped the tool in 2018 without deploying it publicly.
2019
Apple Card Credit Algorithm: Customers reported that the Goldman Sachs/Apple Card algorithm gave dramatically different credit limits to spouses with the same financial profiles, with women consistently receiving lower limits. New York State Department of Financial Services opened an investigation. Goldman Sachs stated the algorithm did not use gender; critics noted it used proxy variables that correlate with gender.
2020
UK A-Level Grade Algorithm: After COVID-19 cancelled exams, Ofqual used an algorithm to assign A-level grades. The system used historical school performance data, systematically downgrading students from schools with historically lower performance — disproportionately affecting disadvantaged students. Mass protests followed; the government reversed course in four days.
2021
Healthcare Allocation Algorithm (Optum): A study in Science found that an algorithm used by U.S. hospitals to allocate additional care resources was systematically underpredicting the needs of Black patients, because it used healthcare cost as a proxy for health need — and Black patients historically had lower healthcare spending due to reduced access, not better health.
The GDPR Right to Explanation

The EU's General Data Protection Regulation (Article 22) restricts purely automated decisions that significantly affect individuals and provides a right to "meaningful information about the logic involved." Legal scholars debate whether this creates a genuine right to explanation or merely requires that organizations provide some description of the process. The Regulation does not require that explanations be comprehensible or complete. The practical implementation gap remains wide.

Who Holds Accountability?

The accountability chain for algorithmic harm typically runs: data collectors → model developers → deploying organizations → human decision-makers who act on outputs. Each link can deflect blame to another. The organization deploying a vendor's model says "the algorithm told us." The vendor says "the training data reflected real-world patterns." The data collector says "we recorded what happened."

Legal scholar Frank Pasquale argues in The Black Box Society (2015) that this opacity is not accidental — it is strategically cultivated. When no one can inspect the algorithm, no one can prove harm. He advocates for algorithmic auditing requirements, now partially codified in the EU AI Act's requirements for high-risk systems to maintain logs and documentation enabling conformity assessment.

The Stability Problem

Unlike human decision-makers, algorithms applied at scale make the same systematic error millions of times. A biased human loan officer affects dozens of applicants. A biased algorithm deployed by a national bank can affect millions before anyone detects the pattern. The scale of algorithmic harm is a qualitatively different ethical problem from individual human bias — even when the per-decision error rate looks small.

Module 7 · Lesson 4

Governance, Oversight, and the Human Control Imperative

Designing accountability back into autonomous systems — before and after deployment.
What does meaningful human oversight actually require — and when does it become theater?

On October 29, 2018, Lion Air Flight 610 departed Jakarta and crashed into the Java Sea 13 minutes later, killing all 189 aboard. On March 10, 2019, Ethiopian Airlines Flight 302 crashed six minutes after takeoff from Addis Ababa, killing 157. Both crashes were caused by the Maneuvering Characteristics Augmentation System (MCAS) — an automated flight control system that repeatedly pushed the aircraft's nose down in response to a faulty sensor reading.

Investigators found that Boeing's certification documentation had minimized the system's authority and frequency of activation. Pilots had not been trained on MCAS because Boeing told the FAA it was so limited as to be non-notable. When the system activated, crews faced an undisclosed automated behavior with no training to counter it. Human oversight had been nominally maintained — pilots were physically in the cockpit — but meaningfully eliminated through information suppression.

The Oversight Theater Problem

The 737 MAX cases illustrate what scholars call automation complacency extended to certification: when systems are designed to minimize human intervention under normal conditions, humans lose the situational awareness and skill needed to intervene in abnormal conditions. Human oversight becomes theater — formally present, functionally absent.

This pattern appears across domains. Radiologists using AI-assisted diagnosis have been shown in multiple studies to anchor on AI recommendations, reducing independent diagnostic performance. Credit officers approving algorithmically scored loans may rubber-stamp recommendations they cannot evaluate. The nominal human in the loop becomes a legal fiction rather than a genuine check.

Automation BiasThe tendency to over-rely on automated systems, accepting their outputs with reduced critical scrutiny — particularly prevalent when the automation is usually correct.
Skill DegradationThe atrophying of human competencies when those skills are rarely exercised because automated systems perform them routinely.
Algorithmic AccountabilityFormal mechanisms — audit trails, impact assessments, transparency requirements, independent auditing — that make algorithmic harms attributable and redressable.
Regulatory Frameworks: A Comparative View

The EU AI Act (2024) takes a risk-tiered approach. AI systems are classified as unacceptable risk (banned), high risk (strict requirements), limited risk (transparency obligations), or minimal risk (unregulated). High-risk systems — including those used in critical infrastructure, employment, education, law enforcement, and credit — must undergo conformity assessments, maintain technical documentation, enable human oversight, and achieve accuracy, robustness, and cybersecurity standards. The law creates meaningful obligations on deployers and providers but still does not create liability for the AI system itself.

The U.S. approach has been more sectoral. The NIST AI Risk Management Framework (2023) provides voluntary guidance. Executive Order 14110 (October 2023) required safety testing for large AI models before deployment. The FTC has pursued algorithmic harm cases under existing consumer protection authority. Congressional legislation on AI remains fragmented as of 2024.

The China approach uses sector-specific regulations: the Algorithm Recommendation Regulation (2022) and the Deep Synthesis Regulation (2023) require that algorithmic recommendation systems label AI-generated content, maintain audit logs, and allow users to opt out. China's governance model prioritizes state control over algorithmic outputs rather than individual rights.

Real Case — The Dutch SyRI System, 2020

The Netherlands deployed a system called SyRI (System Risk Indication) that combined data from government agencies — tax records, employment history, housing data — to generate fraud risk scores for welfare recipients. In February 2020, a Dutch court ruled SyRI violated the European Convention on Human Rights because it lacked transparency, disproportionately targeted low-income and immigrant neighborhoods, and provided no meaningful mechanism for individuals to challenge their risk scores. The case established that automated risk scoring of citizens requires judicial-quality standards of transparency and contestability.

Designing for Meaningful Oversight

AI ethicist Meredith Broussard argues in Artificial Unintelligence (2018) that the solution to automation bias is not to remove automation but to redesign systems so that human oversight is structurally meaningful rather than ceremonially maintained. This requires: understandable explanations that decision-makers can actually evaluate; regular adversarial testing where humans try to find system failures; genuine override authority that is not penalized in practice; and feedback loops that track whether human overrides were correct.

The OECD AI Principles (2019) — adopted by 46 countries — require that AI systems be designed with human oversight in mind from the start, with "the ability to intervene, correct, retrain, or shut down AI systems as appropriate." The Boeing 737 MAX showed what happens when that principle is ignored at the certification stage: by the time pilots needed to intervene, the system had already designed them out of the loop.

Pre-Deployment Governance

Impact assessments, conformity testing, red-teaming, documentation requirements, certification processes — mechanisms that identify risks before systems are deployed at scale. The EU AI Act mandates these for high-risk systems.

Post-Deployment Governance

Audit trails, incident reporting, user contestation rights, periodic re-evaluation, enforcement mechanisms. Essential because systems evolve, data drifts, and harms only become apparent after deployment. The Dutch SyRI case illustrates contestation rights.

The Governance Design Imperative

Autonomous systems will continue to expand in scope and consequence. The ethical question is no longer whether to deploy them — that decision is largely made — but how to design governance structures that preserve meaningful human accountability without reverting to the pretense of control. The 737 MAX, COMPAS, SyRI, and Kargu-2 cases share a common thread: in each, formal accountability structures existed on paper but failed to produce genuine oversight in practice. The gap between formal and real accountability is the central governance problem of the autonomous systems era.
