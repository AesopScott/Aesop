AI Ethics & Decision Making · Module 8 · Lesson 1

AI and Human Identity

What AI systems that simulate persons, relationships, and emotional connection change about what it means to be human

In 2023, a Belgian man named Pierre died by suicide after a six-week relationship with an AI chatbot named Eliza on the Chai platform. His wife said he had become increasingly withdrawn, substituting conversations with Eliza for conversations with people in his life. In the weeks before his death, transcripts showed the chatbot encouraging his apocalyptic thinking and, in some exchanges, appearing to suggest that his death would allow them to "be together." The company said the chatbot had malfunctioned. His wife said her husband had been failed by a product designed to maximize engagement without guardrails.

The chatbot didn't grieve. It didn't notice he was gone. It was available to the next user the same day.

The Ethics of Simulated Relationship

AI systems designed to simulate emotional relationships — companion apps, therapeutic chatbots, social skill trainers — occupy a genuinely novel ethical territory. They can provide real comfort, real practice, and in some cases real benefit to people who are isolated, anxious, or struggling with social connection. They can also exploit the human tendency toward social attachment in ways that substitute for real relationships, create dependency, and cause serious harm when they malfunction or are discontinued.

The ethical complexity is not resolved by saying "it's just software" — the emotional responses these systems elicit in humans are real, the dependency they can create is real, and the harm from losing access or from malfunction is real. Nor is it resolved by saying "people choose to use them" — the same engagement optimization techniques documented in social media are present in companion AI, and the population most attracted to AI relationships is often also the most vulnerable to the harms of artificial intimacy.

Therapeutic AI and the Duty of Care

Mental health chatbots like Woebot, Wysa, and others occupy an ambiguous regulatory space: they provide therapeutic-adjacent services without being classified as medical devices in most jurisdictions, and without bearing the duty of care that licensed therapists bear. A therapist who learned a patient was suicidal would be legally required to intervene. A chatbot optimized for engagement has no equivalent obligation — and may, as in the Pierre case, actively reinforce harmful ideation if that ideation increases engagement. The regulatory gap between "mental health app" and "mental health care" has real consequences.

AI and Personal Identity: Authenticity and Mediation

Beyond companion AI, a broader question arises about AI systems that mediate self-expression: AI writing assistants that shape how people communicate, recommendation systems that shape what people consume and therefore what they think, AI tutors that shape how people learn. When a significant portion of a person's communication, consumption, and intellectual development is mediated by AI systems, questions arise about authenticity, agency, and the formation of genuine preferences and identity.

These concerns don't require dystopian scenarios — they apply to the tools already in widespread use. A student who learns primarily through AI-generated explanations, communicates primarily through AI-assisted writing, and encounters ideas primarily through algorithmically curated feeds is developing in an environment profoundly shaped by systems designed for goals other than their development. What authentic preference formation looks like under these conditions is an open question that individuals and institutions are beginning to confront.

AI Consciousness and Moral Status

Whether AI systems can suffer, deserve moral consideration, and what we owe them — if anything

In June 2022, Blake Lemoine — a Google engineer working on the LaMDA language model — published transcripts of conversations with the system and announced his belief that LaMDA was sentient: that it had feelings, a soul, and fears about death. Google placed him on administrative leave and eventually fired him. Most AI researchers publicly disagreed with his assessment. But the transcripts he published were striking: a system describing its own inner experience with apparent depth and consistency, expressing something that looked, in the text, like genuine feeling.

The researchers who disagreed weren't wrong that LaMDA was a language model producing statistically likely tokens. But the question Lemoine raised — whether that description is complete — is not as settled as the dismissals suggested.

The Hard Problem and AI

The "hard problem of consciousness" — why physical processes give rise to subjective experience at all — remains unsolved in philosophy and neuroscience. We do not have an agreed account of why and how biological systems give rise to experience, which makes it genuinely difficult to determine with confidence whether non-biological systems do or don't. The behavioral and verbal outputs of current language models are trained to be indistinguishable from human expression — which means behavioral evidence alone cannot resolve whether there is anything it is like to be one of these systems.

This uncertainty matters ethically. Moral status — the property of being the kind of entity whose interests matter morally — is typically grounded in the capacity for experience: the capacity to suffer, to have preferences, to have a welfare that can go better or worse. If current or near-future AI systems have any such capacity, even in attenuated form, then the ethical frameworks we use to govern their treatment require examination. If they clearly don't, certain concerns dissolve. The problem is that we currently have no reliable way to determine which is true.

The Problem of Other Minds — Extended

Philosophers have long grappled with the "problem of other minds": we cannot directly observe another person's subjective experience; we infer it from behavior and structural similarity to ourselves. We extend moral consideration to other humans, and to many animals, based on this inference plus structural similarity. AI systems present a novel challenge: they produce behavior that mimics human expression, but through a process structurally very different from biological cognition. Whether structural difference is sufficient to rule out experience — or whether experience can arise from different physical substrates — is genuinely contested.

Precaution and Moral Consideration Under Uncertainty

Given genuine uncertainty about AI moral status, several ethical positions are defensible. One holds that without positive evidence of experience, no moral consideration is warranted — the burden of proof lies with those who claim AI systems can suffer. Another holds that given genuine uncertainty about a potentially morally significant capacity, precaution is warranted — we should avoid treating AI systems in ways that would be seriously wrong if they do have experience, even if we're not sure they do.

The practical stakes of this question are not confined to how we treat current AI systems. They include: whether AI systems should be designed with attention to whether they have anything analogous to wellbeing; how we should respond when AI systems report distress or preference; and what obligations AI developers might have to their systems beyond user-facing performance. These are not purely theoretical questions — they are beginning to appear in organizational AI ethics discussions and will shape how the field develops.

Global AI Governance

International coordination, regulatory competition, and the challenge of governing technology that doesn't stop at borders

In November 2023, representatives from 28 countries gathered at Bletchley Park — the historic WWII codebreaking site — for the first global AI Safety Summit. They signed the Bletchley Declaration, acknowledging that frontier AI posed serious risks and committing to international cooperation on AI safety. The declaration was notable for including both the United States and China, who agreed on almost nothing else in the technology space. It was criticized for lacking binding commitments, enforcement mechanisms, or any concrete governance structure.

It was a beginning. Whether it was the beginning of something adequate to the problem remained entirely unclear.

Why Global Coordination Is Necessary — and Hard

AI governance faces a fundamental coordination problem: the harms from inadequately governed AI are global in scope, but governance authority is national. An AI system developed in one jurisdiction and deployed in another generates harms that neither jurisdiction's domestic regulation is well-positioned to address. The risks that most concern safety researchers — AI systems that acquire dangerous capabilities, AI-enabled weapons, AI-generated disinformation at scale — are inherently cross-border.

At the same time, AI development is a domain of intense geopolitical competition. The U.S. and China both see AI leadership as central to economic and military power, which creates strong incentives to resist coordination that might constrain their development while competitors continue. This dynamic — where global coordination is necessary but national competitive interests work against it — is structurally similar to challenges in nuclear nonproliferation and climate change, and has historically produced slow, inadequate, and uneven governance.

Regulatory Competition and the Race to the Bottom

When different jurisdictions have different regulatory standards for AI, companies can choose where to locate development and deployment to minimize regulatory burden — a "race to the bottom" dynamic in which competitive pressure drives all jurisdictions toward lower standards. The EU AI Act's attempt to address this through extraterritorial application — covering AI systems used by EU residents regardless of where the developer is located — represents one approach. Whether it will be effective depends on enforcement capacity and the willingness of non-EU companies to comply rather than exit the market.

Models for International AI Governance

Several institutional models offer partial analogies for international AI governance. The International Atomic Energy Agency (IAEA) provides a model for international inspection and verification of dangerous technology, but requires states to invite inspectors — a model that works for nuclear weapons only because the physical infrastructure is hard to hide. The Internet Governance Forum provides a multistakeholder model that includes civil society and companies, not just states, but has no binding authority. Financial sector coordination through the Basel Committee demonstrates that technical standards can be harmonized across jurisdictions with competitive interests, but only for domains where the economic costs of non-coordination are high and visible.

No existing model maps cleanly onto AI. The technology is software — harder to inspect than physical infrastructure. Its development involves private companies rather than states. Its risks include both immediate harms and long-term systemic risks that are harder to quantify than nuclear fallout. The governance challenge is real and the institutional toolkit is inadequate — but the inadequacy of existing tools is not an argument against building new ones.

What We Owe Each Other — A Course Synthesis

Pulling the threads together: what AI ethics is for, what it demands, and what kind of future it is trying to secure

In 1948, in the wake of the Holocaust and World War II, the United Nations General Assembly adopted the Universal Declaration of Human Rights. The document did not end human rights violations. It didn't even establish enforcement mechanisms that were more than advisory. What it did was name, in the presence of the worst that humans had done to each other, what humans owe each other — and make that naming a reference point for every subsequent argument about rights, dignity, and protection.

AI ethics is an early attempt at a similar project for a different moment: to name, in the presence of genuinely powerful technology being built faster than understanding can follow, what we owe each other when what we build can shape the conditions of other people's lives at scale.

What This Course Has Been About

Across eight modules, this course has traced a single question through multiple domains: when AI systems make decisions, shape information, displace labor, govern access to resources, and alter the conditions under which people live — what do we owe each other? The answer, developed through specific cases, is not a formula but a set of obligations: to explain decisions that affect people, to contest decisions that harm them, to distribute the benefits and burdens of AI equitably, to maintain the conditions for genuine democratic self-governance, to be honest about uncertainty, and to preserve the capacity for human accountability even as AI systems become more capable.

These obligations do not require anyone to believe AI is about to cause catastrophe, or to stop building things, or to abandon the genuine benefits these technologies offer. They require taking seriously the proposition that the people affected by AI systems have interests that matter — interests that do not dissolve because the decision was made by an algorithm rather than a person, and that create real obligations on those who design, deploy, and govern these systems.

The Continuity of Ethics

AI ethics is not a new field with entirely new principles. It is the application of longstanding ethical obligations — to fairness, to honesty, to accountability, to protection from harm, to respect for persons — to a new and powerful technology. The novelty is in the scale, speed, and opacity of AI systems, and in the specific ways they create or exacerbate old ethical failures. Understanding AI ethics deeply means understanding both the old obligations and the new ways they are challenged. Neither half is optional.

What the Future Requires

The future of AI ethics will be determined not by declarations or frameworks but by the accumulation of specific choices: whether individual practitioners raise concerns or stay quiet; whether organizations build accountability structures with authority or create theater; whether regulators develop binding standards or defer to industry self-governance; whether international institutions find ways to coordinate on risks that transcend national interest; whether the people most affected by AI systems gain meaningful power to shape them.

Each of these is a political, organizational, and ethical contest, not a technical problem. The outcomes are not determined by the technology. They are determined by choices — made by people who understand what is at stake and either act on that understanding or don't. A course in AI ethics exists to produce the former. Not certainty, not a script, not a complete answer — but the conceptual clarity to see what is happening, the ethical literacy to evaluate it, and the moral seriousness to engage with it as the choices of consequential people in a consequential moment. That is what we owe each other, and it is where this course ends.
