Get Hired Using AI · Introduction

The Hiring Process Has Already Changed — Without Telling You

Why the rules that got people hired in 2019 are quietly working against them today.

In 1876, Western Union turned down Alexander Graham Bell's offer to sell them the telephone patent for $100,000, concluding internally that "this device is inherently of no value to us." Within a decade, every serious business in America was rewiring its offices around the technology Western Union had dismissed. The people who adapted first — who learned to speak clearly into a receiver, who grasped that correspondence could now be instantaneous — moved ahead. Those who kept writing letters as their primary tool were not destroyed, but they were consistently slower, and in competitive markets, consistently slower is consistently losing.

Something structurally similar is happening in hiring right now. Since roughly 2018, large employers began adopting Applicant Tracking Systems layered with AI screening — tools like HireVue's video-interview analysis, Pymetrics' game-based neuroscience assessments, and resume parsers built on natural-language processing. By 2023, a SHRM survey found that 79% of employers were using AI somewhere in their hiring pipeline. Candidates who understood how those systems work — what they reward, what they silently discard — were advancing through processes that baffled peers with equal or superior qualifications.

This course is a practical map of that landscape. It will not promise that AI is a magic shortcut, and it will not pretend the tools are neutral — they are not. What it will do is show you, specifically, how recruiters' AI systems make decisions, how you can use AI tools on your own side of the table to compete intelligently, and where the genuine human judgment still lives in the process. By the end you will know more about how hiring actually works in 2024 than most people doing the hiring.

The Invisible Screener: How ATS and AI Filter Candidates Before Any Human Looks

Most rejections happen in a system, not a conversation. Understanding the system is the first move.
What actually happens to your résumé in the 0.4 seconds before a recruiter ever sees it?

In October 2018, Reuters reported that Amazon had quietly scrapped an internal AI recruiting tool it had been developing since 2014. The system, trained on ten years of successful Amazon hires, had learned to penalize résumés that included the word "women's" — as in "women's chess club" — and downgraded graduates of all-female colleges. Amazon's engineers tried to correct the bias but ultimately concluded they could not guarantee the system was neutral. They shut it down. The tool had been making hiring recommendations for years before anyone realized what patterns it had absorbed.

Amazon's case became famous precisely because it was an extraordinary company failing at something ordinary employers were doing everywhere, with far less scrutiny. What it revealed was not a flaw unique to one tech giant but a structural truth about AI screening: these systems learn from historical hiring outcomes, and historical hiring outcomes encode historical biases. The candidate who understands this is already ahead of the one who simply submits a résumé and waits.

Today, the tools have evolved considerably. Modern ATS platforms — Workday, Greenhouse, Lever, iCIMS — do not merely keyword-match; they parse semantic meaning, infer seniority from job title sequences, and score candidates on rubrics recruiters configure in advance. The rubric is the secret you need to reverse-engineer.

What an ATS Actually Does

An Applicant Tracking System is, at its foundation, a database with an intake form. When you submit a résumé, the ATS parser converts your document into structured data fields: name, contact, education, job titles, dates, skills. This parsing step is where formatting disasters begin. A résumé built in a complex Word template with text boxes, columns, and embedded graphics may parse as near-empty, because the parser cannot extract text from non-linear layouts.

Once parsed, the system applies whatever scoring logic the recruiter or HR team has configured. In a basic setup, this might be simple keyword matching against a required-skills list. In more sophisticated deployments — particularly at companies using Workday's Talent Optimization or SAP SuccessFactors — the scoring layer uses machine learning models that have been trained on that company's own historical hire data. Your score is not a measure of your absolute quality; it is a measure of how much you resemble people this company has hired and kept before.

Practical implication: a résumé that performed brilliantly for a mid-size healthcare startup in 2021 may score poorly when submitted to a large financial services firm in 2024 — not because your experience is weaker, but because the target model is different. Every application is a targeting exercise, not a broadcasting exercise.

Documented Case

In 2019, HireVue's video-interview AI was being used by over 100 companies including Unilever and Goldman Sachs. Unilever reported in its own case study that the tool reduced time-to-hire by 75% and increased diversity of candidates reaching final rounds. HireVue's system analyzed facial movements, word choice, and vocal tone. In 2021, under pressure from the Electronic Privacy Information Center (EPIC), HireVue discontinued facial-analysis scoring — but continued audio and linguistic analysis. Candidates who knew this distinction could prepare differently than those who assumed "AI interview" meant only keyword detection.

The Three Screening Layers

Most enterprise hiring pipelines today use three distinct AI-assisted layers before a human recruiter makes a substantive judgment call.

Layer 1 — Résumé Parsing and Scoring: As described above. Happens within seconds of submission. The output is typically a score or a pass/fail flag that determines whether you enter the recruiter's review queue at all. Jobscan's 2023 analysis of 20,000 job applications found that 70% of résumés were eliminated before human review.

Layer 2 — Asynchronous Video or Assessment Screening: Tools like HireVue, Spark Hire, and Pymetrics ask candidates to complete recorded video interviews or gamified assessments before any live conversation. These are analyzed for language patterns, response structure, cognitive traits, and in some systems, emotional indicators. They are designed to replace the initial phone screen and handle high-volume filtering at scale.

Layer 3 — LinkedIn and Social Signal Enrichment: Many ATS platforms integrate with LinkedIn's Recruiter product, automatically pulling your public profile data and cross-referencing it against your submitted résumé. Discrepancies — different job titles, unexplained gaps, missing credentials — are flagged automatically. Some platforms also use third-party data enrichment services to add public signals you may not have submitted yourself.

Why This Matters for You

Understanding the three-layer structure means you know exactly which part of your application is speaking at each stage. Your résumé speaks in Layer 1. Your recorded performance speaks in Layer 2. Your public professional presence speaks in Layer 3. Optimizing only your résumé while ignoring the other two layers is like preparing only your opening statement for a trial that has three separate hearings.

The critical insight from Lesson 1 is this: the hiring process you are navigating is not a human process with some digital tools added. For most large employers, it is a digital process that occasionally escalates to human judgment. Your strategy must be built for the system as it actually exists, not the system as it was described to you in 2015.

How Recruiters Use AI on Their Side of the Table

LinkedIn Recruiter, sourcing algorithms, and the signals that make you findable before you even apply.
If AI is helping recruiters find candidates proactively, what does your public professional presence need to look like?

In January 2023, a software engineer named Gergely Orosz — known for his newsletter The Pragmatic Engineer — published a detailed account of how LinkedIn's recruiter-facing AI features had changed headhunting. He described how LinkedIn's "Likelihood to Move" score, a machine-learning signal that LinkedIn began rolling out to enterprise recruiters around 2019, was being used by recruiters at major tech firms to prioritize outreach. The system predicted which passive candidates were statistically likely to be open to new opportunities based on behavioral signals: profile update frequency, content engagement, InMail response history, and LinkedIn activity changes.

What made this consequential was not just that LinkedIn had such a signal — it was that most candidates had no idea it existed. People who had not updated their profiles in two years, who never engaged with content, who had ignored previous InMails, were effectively invisible to recruiter AI regardless of their qualifications. Meanwhile, candidates who had recently added certifications, who posted or commented occasionally, and who had responded positively to past outreach were being surfaced repeatedly to active hiring teams.

The recruiter's AI is not looking for the best candidate in the world. It is looking for the best candidate it can find, from the signals it has access to. Your job is to be in those signals.

LinkedIn Recruiter and AI-Powered Sourcing

LinkedIn Recruiter, the enterprise product used by the majority of large company talent acquisition teams, is not a simple search engine. As of 2023, it incorporates several AI layers that go far beyond keyword search. The platform's Recommended Matches feature uses collaborative filtering — the same logic behind streaming service recommendations — to surface candidates who resemble people recently hired for similar roles. If a company just hired five product managers who all had MBA degrees and experience at Series B startups, the algorithm will surface more candidates who fit that profile, whether or not those candidates match on explicit search terms.

The Spotlight features — "Open to Work," "Actively Applying," "Growing Skills" — are not just labels. They are data inputs that LinkedIn's algorithm uses to adjust your visibility in recruiter searches. Candidates who signal active job-seeking behavior appear higher in recruiter search results for matching roles. This was documented in a 2022 LinkedIn Engineering Blog post describing how activity signals were incorporated into the talent search ranking model.

Beyond LinkedIn, companies are using AI sourcing tools including SeekOut, Hiretual (now HireEZ), and Entelo that aggregate public data from GitHub, patents, publications, conference speaker records, and social media to build candidate profiles that go far beyond what individuals have explicitly shared on job boards.

Documented Case

In 2021, Unilever described its AI-powered recruiting in detail to the Harvard Business Review. The company used Pymetrics gamified assessments to evaluate traits like risk tolerance, attention, and emotional regulation. Candidates who matched the neurological profiles of high-performing Unilever employees advanced to the next stage. Notably, Unilever reported that its AI-screened cohort was more diverse than its historically recruiter-selected cohort — because the AI evaluated behavioral traits rather than the educational pedigree signals human recruiters had historically overweighted. The system still had limitations, but it illustrates how recruiter AI tools are redefining which signals matter.

The Passive Candidate Market

Recruiter-side AI has made passive candidate sourcing dramatically more efficient. A LinkedIn 2022 Global Talent Trends report found that 73% of professionals considered themselves "passive candidates" — not actively job searching but open to the right opportunity. Historically, reaching these people required expensive headhunter relationships. Now, AI sourcing tools can identify and rank tens of thousands of passive candidates within a given skill set in minutes.

This creates an important strategic point: optimizing for inbound recruiter interest is now a legitimate parallel track to active job searching. The person who makes themselves findable and signals the right behavioral and professional cues to AI sourcing tools may receive high-quality opportunities without ever submitting a single cold application.

What You Can Control

Your LinkedIn profile headline, skills section, About text, recent activity, and profile completeness score all feed into recruiter-side AI ranking. You do not control LinkedIn's algorithm, but you control every data point it reads about you. The practical sessions in this course will address each of these levers specifically.

Bias, Gaps, and Legal Risk: What Recruiters' AI Actually Can't Do

Every AI screening system has documented failure modes. Knowing them protects you and makes you a more credible candidate.
Where do AI screening tools produce genuinely unreliable results — and what does that mean for how you present yourself?

In November 2019, a group of researchers at MIT Media Lab and the University of Toronto published a study examining HireVue's video interview AI. They found that the system's performance scores correlated with background objects visible in the video — candidates interviewed in front of bookshelves scored differently than candidates in front of blank walls, regardless of their verbal responses. HireVue disputed the methodology, but the study highlighted a core technical vulnerability: AI systems trained on video data can pick up spurious visual correlates of "success" that have nothing to do with job performance.

This was not an isolated research curiosity. In 2021, the Washington, D.C. government became the first jurisdiction in the United States to pass legislation — the Stop Discrimination by Algorithms Act — specifically regulating algorithmic employment decisions. New York City followed with Local Law 144 in 2023, requiring employers using AI hiring tools to conduct annual bias audits and notify candidates. Illinois passed the Artificial Intelligence Video Interview Act in 2019, requiring employers to inform candidates when AI analyzes their video interviews and obtain consent.

The regulatory picture is moving fast, which means companies using these tools are increasingly nervous about them. That nervousness is information you can use.

Documented Failure Modes of Hiring AI

Understanding where these systems break down matters for two reasons: it tells you where your application may be unfairly disadvantaged through no fault of your own, and it tells you which concerns savvy recruiters share — concerns you can address proactively.

1. Socioeconomic proxy bias. AI tools that score on education, employer prestige, or zip code data embed socioeconomic class distinctions into their recommendations. A 2019 study in the journal Management Science found that algorithm-based résumé screening systems consistently scored candidates from elite universities higher than equally qualified candidates from less prestigious schools, and that this gap was larger in algorithmic screening than in human review.

2. Racial and gender bias via language patterns. Natural language processing models trained on historical corporate communications encode the communication styles of historically dominant workplace demographics. A 2020 study by the AI Now Institute found that NLP-based résumé screening tools scored communication styles associated with Black and Latinx candidates lower on "professionalism" metrics — not because of explicit discrimination but because their training data defined professionalism implicitly.

3. Disability and neurodiverse candidate disadvantage. Asynchronous video interview AI systems that analyze speech rhythm, eye contact, and facial expression systematically disadvantage candidates with conditions including autism spectrum disorder, ADHD, Tourette's syndrome, and anxiety disorders. HireVue acknowledged this risk in its own technical documentation as a known limitation.

4. Employment gap penalization. Most ATS parsers and scoring models treat gaps in employment chronology as negative signals, regardless of cause. A 2022 LinkedIn survey found that 62% of hiring managers said they had passed over candidates solely because of employment gaps — a pattern that AI systems encode and amplify, disproportionately affecting women who took parental leave and caregivers.

New York City Local Law 144 (2023)

Effective July 5, 2023, NYC Local Law 144 requires any employer or employment agency using an "automated employment decision tool" in hiring or promotion to conduct an annual independent bias audit, publish a summary of those results publicly, and notify candidates that such a tool is being used. This is the most comprehensive AI hiring regulation in the United States as of 2024. Candidates in NYC can now request disclosure of which AI tools assessed them.

What This Means Strategically

The existence of these failure modes and the regulatory response to them creates several practical implications for candidates.

You can address gaps directly. Because AI systems penalize chronological gaps but humans respond to context, your cover letter and LinkedIn summary are the places to frame any gap proactively: caregiving, health, continuing education, freelance work, or deliberate career pivot. ATS systems may flag the gap, but a human recruiter who reads your framing can override that flag.

You can request disclosure. In New York City, Chicago, and other jurisdictions with AI hiring regulations, you have legal rights to know when an AI tool is evaluating you and to request bias audit summaries. Exercising these rights is legitimate and increasingly normalized.

You can recognize when an assessment is poorly designed. A well-calibrated candidate who understands that background objects in video interviews can influence AI scores can control their environment. A candidate who doesn't know this cannot.

The Honest Limit

Understanding AI hiring bias does not eliminate its effect on you. If a system is scoring your résumé against a biased model, awareness alone doesn't fix the score. What it does is help you target employers who have invested in bias mitigation, understand when a rejection may be systemic rather than personal, and use the human touchpoints in the process — cover letters, LinkedIn messages, referrals — as the channels where nuanced human judgment can override the algorithmic flag.

The goal of this lesson is not to make you paranoid about AI hiring tools but to make you an informed participant in a process that affects you significantly. The candidate who understands the system's documented weaknesses is better positioned than the one who treats it as a black box. More human judgment still exists in hiring than the technology coverage suggests — the skill is knowing exactly where it lives and directing your best material there.

Where Human Judgment Still Lives — and How to Reach It

AI handles volume. Humans still make the offers. The skill is navigating from one to the other.
After all the filtering, where does irreplaceable human judgment re-enter the hiring process — and how do you deliberately place yourself in front of it?

In 2022, a former Google recruiter named Nolan Church — who had also served as Chief People Officer at Carta — gave a widely circulated interview to CNBC in which he described how the actual final hiring decisions at technology companies are made. Church noted that by the time a candidate reaches a final-round interview at a serious company, the AI screening and recruiter filtering have already produced a pool of candidates who are all roughly qualified. At that stage, he said, the decision comes down almost entirely to interpersonal dynamics, cultural fit signals, and the gut-level conviction of the hiring manager — factors that no AI system currently measures reliably.

Church's observation matches what researchers have found. A 2021 study published in the Journal of Applied Psychology tracked hiring decisions at eighteen companies using AI screening and found that AI explained 34% of variance in who advanced past initial screening but less than 8% of variance in who ultimately received an offer. The final decision remained substantially human. The AI is a filter, not a decider.

Understanding where the human judgment lives — and which human — is the strategic complement to understanding the AI layer. You need both to compete effectively.

The Stages Where Humans Still Decide

In a well-documented enterprise hiring process, human judgment is decisive at four specific points that AI tools do not reliably replace.

1. The Recruiter's Read of the Cover Letter. ATS systems can parse and score cover letters for keywords, but most recruiters who actually read them report that they are evaluating something different: whether the candidate has done serious research on the company, whether they can communicate a coherent professional narrative, and whether they say something specific enough to be interesting. A generic AI-generated cover letter that passes ATS keyword scoring but reads as formulaic to a human is a missed opportunity at the first human touchpoint.

2. The Recruiter Phone Screen. The initial recruiter call — typically 20–30 minutes — is still conducted by a human in the vast majority of hiring processes. Its purpose is to verify basic facts, assess communication quality, and determine whether the candidate is serious about the role. Preparation for this call requires understanding the company's current business priorities, recent news, and the recruiter's own perspective — none of which AI screening handles.

3. The Hiring Manager Interview. This is where the largest single human judgment occurs. Research by Lori Foster Thompson at North Carolina State (published 2020) found that hiring managers' final recommendations in structured interviews are driven by a combination of demonstrated competence signals, interpersonal warmth, and perceived cultural alignment — with perceived cultural alignment having a disproportionate weight in close-call decisions.

4. The Internal Advocate. A referral from a current employee can bypass or significantly downweight the AI screening layer entirely at many companies. LinkedIn's own data from 2021 showed that referred candidates were four times more likely to be hired than non-referred applicants, and that referral pathways reduced time-to-hire by 55%. This is not an AI signal — it is a human trust signal that the system treats as a strong positive override.

Documented Case

In 2020, McKinsey & Company published research on talent acquisition transformation across 50 large companies. They found that organizations which relied most heavily on AI screening without maintaining strong human judgment at the offer stage experienced 23% higher first-year attrition than organizations that used AI purely for initial filtering and maintained deliberate human judgment at later stages. This suggests that over-reliance on AI selection signals — even accurate ones — misses the interpersonal and motivational factors that predict retention.

Reaching Human Judgment Deliberately

Given that human judgment is decisive at specific points, there are concrete tactics for placing yourself in front of it intentionally rather than waiting for the algorithm to deliver you there.

LinkedIn warm outreach before applying. A brief, specific message to the hiring manager or a current team member, sent before you submit an application, creates a human awareness signal before your résumé hits the ATS. Even if they don't respond, the name familiarity can influence how a recruiter treats your application when it surfaces. If they do respond, you have bypassed the AI layer entirely for the initial conversation.

Employee referral programs. Most large employers have formal referral programs. Identifying a current employee in your network — even a second-degree LinkedIn connection — and asking for an introduction is not a cold ask; it is a normal use of professional networking that the employer has actively encouraged through the referral program's existence.

Demonstrating research in cover letters. Human recruiters and hiring managers consistently report that the most effective cover letters demonstrate specific knowledge of the company's current situation — a product launch, a strategic challenge, a publicly stated goal — rather than generic enthusiasm. This specificity cannot be faked efficiently and signals to a human reader that the candidate is genuinely interested, not mass-applying.

Module 1 Synthesis

You now have the structural picture of how AI operates in hiring on both sides of the table. Recruiters' AI filters volume and sources passively. Your application must be optimized to pass those filters and score well against rubrics you can partially decode. But AI does not make offers. The humans who do respond to specificity, to preparation, and to interpersonal quality signals that no algorithm currently captures reliably. Your strategy must be built for both layers simultaneously — not one or the other.

Recruiter Phone ScreenThe initial live human conversation in a hiring process, typically 20–30 minutes, used to verify basic facts and assess communication quality. The first point where a human is forming a direct judgment about you as a person.
