Module 6 · Lesson 1

Researching Companies and Roles Before the Interview

Most candidates scan the About page. AI lets you build a genuine intelligence file in twenty minutes.
What would you ask if you already knew everything about the company — and how do you get there?

In 2022, Glassdoor published analysis of 500,000 interview reviews showing that the single most common candidate failure point was arriving with only surface knowledge of the company. Interviewers consistently noted phrases like "your website says you value innovation" as evidence of shallow preparation. The candidates who stood out were those who referenced recent earnings calls, product launches, or strategic pivots — information freely available but rarely synthesized.

Why Standard Research Fails

Reading a company's LinkedIn page and skimming their homepage gives you the same information every other candidate has. Interviewers at companies receiving hundreds of applications can identify boilerplate preparation instantly. What they remember — and what moves candidates forward — is evidence of genuine curiosity and analytical thinking.

The challenge is time. A thorough research job on one company — annual reports, press releases, Glassdoor reviews, industry news, competitor landscape, leadership backgrounds, recent product changes — could easily take four to six hours per application. AI compresses that dramatically when used correctly.

Building an Intelligence File With AI

Think of AI as a research partner who reads fast, synthesizes well, and asks clarifying questions. Your job is to give it specific inputs and ask for structured outputs. The goal is a company intelligence file you can review the morning of your interview.

The file should answer five questions: What does this company actually do and how do they make money? What are their stated strategic priorities right now? What pressures or risks are they facing? How does this specific role connect to those priorities? What can I learn about the people interviewing me?

Prompt Template — Company Intelligence Briefing I have an interview at [Company Name] for a [Job Title] role. Please help me build a research briefing. I'll paste in the job description and any recent news I've found. Then ask me clarifying questions and help me produce a one-page intelligence summary covering: business model, strategic priorities, recent challenges, how this role fits the strategy, and 3 specific questions I should ask at the end of the interview.

Once you have AI generating this framework, you feed it the raw material: the job description, a recent press release, a paragraph from their annual report, a notable Glassdoor review about culture. AI synthesizes across those sources into a coherent picture you could not easily build alone.

Real Pattern — Amazon Leadership Principles

Amazon publicly publishes its 16 Leadership Principles. Candidates who succeed in Amazon interviews systematically prepare stories for each principle using the STAR format (Situation, Task, Action, Result). AI can map a job description's key requirements to specific Leadership Principles and help you identify which of your experiences fits each one. This is the kind of targeted prep that separates prepared candidates from the rest.

Researching the Interviewers

LinkedIn is the primary source. AI cannot browse the web in real time, but you can paste a person's LinkedIn summary or bio into a prompt and ask: "Based on this person's background, what topics are they likely to care about, and what questions might they ask me?" You can also ask AI to help you identify shared professional ground — industry experience, previous employers, academic background — that you can reference naturally in the interview.

This is not manipulation. It is the same research any professional does before an important meeting. The difference is using AI to synthesize it faster and draw connections you might miss.

Key Principle

AI is most powerful for interview research when you give it specific raw material — not just a company name. Paste in the job description. Paste in paragraphs from their annual report. Paste in a recent press release. The quality of the intelligence file is directly proportional to the quality of inputs you provide.

What to Do With the File

Print or write out your intelligence file the night before. Review it that morning. The goal is not to memorize talking points but to internalize enough context that you can respond naturally to unexpected questions. When an interviewer asks "what do you know about where we're headed strategically?" you want to have a genuine, specific, recent answer — not a recitation of their mission statement.

Your three prepared questions at the end of the interview should reference something specific from your research. "I noticed you recently launched [product]. I'm curious how this role interfaces with that initiative." This signals you did real work. It also tends to generate better conversations that help you evaluate whether you actually want the job.

Module 6 · Lesson 2

Generating and Practicing Common Interview Questions

Every interview contains predictable questions. AI helps you answer them better than you thought you could.
How do you prepare for the questions you cannot predict — by practicing the ones you can?

In 2019, Google's People Operations team published research confirming what hiring managers had informally observed for years: structured interviews — those using consistent, predetermined questions — predicted job performance twice as reliably as unstructured ones. The implication for candidates is that interviewers, especially at large companies, are working from a question bank. The categories are predictable even when the exact wording is not.

The Six Predictable Categories

Most interview questions, regardless of company or industry, fall into six categories: background and motivation ("tell me about yourself," "why this role"), behavioral competency ("tell me about a time when…"), situational judgment ("what would you do if…"), role-specific technical, culture fit and values, and candidate questions for the interviewer. AI can generate realistic examples in all six categories once you give it the job description.

The value is not in knowing exactly what will be asked. It is in activating your relevant memories and experiences before the interview so that when a question comes, you are drawing from practiced material rather than improvising under pressure.

Prompt Template — Question Generation I'm interviewing for [Job Title] at [Company Type/Industry]. Here is the job description: [paste]. Please generate 20 realistic interview questions across these categories: (1) background and motivation, (2) behavioral competency, (3) situational judgment, (4) role-specific technical, (5) culture and values. Weight toward behavioral and situational since those are most common.
The STAR Format and Why AI Improves It

STAR stands for Situation, Task, Action, Result. It is the standard framework for behavioral answers because it forces structure — you describe a real scenario, explain what needed to be done, walk through what you specifically did, and quantify the outcome. The problem most candidates have is not knowing the framework; it is identifying good stories and shaping them into tight STAR answers.

AI excels at this. You describe a professional experience in rough, unpolished terms — "I once had a situation where a project was falling apart and I basically had to take over" — and ask AI to help you structure it as a STAR answer, identify what competency it demonstrates, and tighten the language. You can then refine iteratively until the answer sounds natural and specific.

Prompt Template — STAR Story Builder I need to build STAR-format answers for behavioral interview questions. Here is a rough description of a professional experience: [describe in plain language]. Help me structure this as a STAR answer (Situation, Task, Action, Result), identify which behavioral competencies it demonstrates (e.g., leadership, problem-solving, collaboration), and suggest how I could strengthen the Result section with more specificity.

Prepare five to seven strong STAR stories covering different competencies. Most behavioral questions can be answered with one of those stories, adapted slightly. A story about leading a cross-functional project can answer questions about leadership, conflict resolution, stakeholder management, and handling ambiguity — depending on which part of the story you emphasize.

Mock Interview Mode

After generating questions, switch AI into mock interview mode. Ask it to ask you questions one at a time, wait for your answer, then give specific feedback on structure, specificity, and whether your answer actually addressed what was asked. This is practice with stakes — the feedback is direct and immediate, unlike rehearsing alone in a mirror.

Avoiding the "Canned Answer" Problem

The risk of extensive preparation is sounding rehearsed. Interviewers notice when answers feel scripted — there is no natural variation in phrasing, no genuine recall of detail, no spontaneous energy. The solution is to prepare the structure and key details of your stories, not the exact wording.

Ask AI: "Give me three different ways to open this STAR answer so I don't sound like I'm reciting a script." The variation helps you internalize the story as a real memory rather than a performance. The goal is to arrive at the interview with well-prepared stories that you can tell freshly, not recite mechanically.

The Laszlo Bock Standard

Laszlo Bock, former SVP of People Operations at Google, wrote in his 2015 book Work Rules! that the best behavioral answers are specific, recent, and told in first person with clear ownership. AI can help you audit your answers against all three criteria: Is this specific enough? Is the situation recent and relevant? Does the answer make clear what you did versus what the team did?

Module 6 · Lesson 3

Running AI Mock Interviews and Analyzing Feedback

Practice under simulated pressure. The feedback you cannot get from friends.
What if the most honest interview feedback you ever received came from a machine?

In 2021, HireVue — the AI-powered video interview platform used by Unilever, Goldman Sachs, and Delta Air Lines among others — processed more than two million candidate interviews annually. Unilever's HR team reported in a widely-cited case study that using AI-assisted initial screening reduced time-to-hire by 75% and increased diversity in candidates advancing to final rounds by 16%. Candidates who performed best in those AI-screened interviews had one thing in common: they had practiced answering out loud, with feedback, before the actual interview.

Setting Up a Real Mock Interview

A mock interview with AI is only useful if you treat it like the real thing. That means typing out full answers (not bullet points), resisting the urge to edit before sending, and committing to the scenario. The cognitive friction of articulating a complete answer under simulated pressure is what builds the recall pathways you need on the actual day.

Set up the session with a specific prompt. Give the AI the job description, the company name, the interviewer's likely seniority level, and your target role. Ask it to conduct a structured 45-minute interview covering background, three to five behavioral questions, two situational questions, and a closing. Tell it to wait for your full answer before proceeding and to give direct, specific feedback after each one.

Prompt Template — Mock Interview Setup I want you to conduct a realistic mock interview for the following role: [paste job description]. Assume you are a senior [hiring manager title] at [company type]. Ask me questions one at a time. After each answer, give me direct feedback on: (1) structure and clarity, (2) specificity and evidence, (3) whether I actually answered what was asked, (4) one thing I should change. Then ask the next question. Begin with "Tell me about yourself."
What Good Feedback Looks Like

The most useful AI feedback on interview answers identifies specific problems: "Your answer described what the team did but not what you specifically contributed." "You named the result but gave no numbers — what was the actual outcome?" "You answered a question about conflict resolution by describing a project success — those are different competencies." This is the kind of feedback that rarely comes from supportive friends or family doing practice interviews.

Ask AI to rate each answer on specificity (1–5), structure (1–5), and relevance to the question (1–5). Request that it identifies one competency your answer demonstrated strongly and one it failed to demonstrate. Over several sessions, these scores show you which answer types need the most work.

Weak Answer Patterns AI Catches
  • Using "we" when "I" is required
  • Vague results ("the project went well")
  • Answering a different question
  • Missing the Task component of STAR
  • Answers over 3 minutes (too long)
  • Answers under 45 seconds (too short)
Strong Answer Patterns to Build
  • Specific numbers in Results
  • Clear "I" ownership of Actions
  • Concrete timeline and context
  • Named stakeholders and stakes
  • Natural conclusion / lesson learned
  • 1.5–2.5 minute delivery window
Iterating on Weak Answers

After AI flags a weak answer, ask it to help you rebuild it. "My answer about managing conflict was too vague — help me rework it with more specific detail and a clearer result." Then practice it again. This iterative loop — answer, feedback, rebuild, re-answer — is significantly more effective than one long practice session because each cycle targets a specific weakness.

Three focused sessions of 30 minutes, each targeting a different answer category, will improve performance more than a single two-hour marathon. Use the feedback from session one to prepare for session two. Keep a running note of the feedback patterns — if "specificity" appears in six of ten answers, that is your primary preparation target.

The Tonal Register Problem

AI can also catch tone. Ask it to evaluate whether your answer sounds confident or uncertain, whether you hedge too much ("I think maybe the result was…"), and whether your language matches the seniority level of the role. "Sounds like entry-level framing for a senior role" is exactly the feedback that changes how you carry yourself in the actual interview.

Module 6 · Lesson 4

Handling Difficult Questions, Salary Negotiation, and Post-Interview Follow-Up

The interview does not end when you leave the room. AI helps you navigate the hard questions and the follow-through.
What do you say when the question has no good answer — and how do you use the days after the interview to keep advancing?

In 2023, LinkedIn's Global Talent Trends report found that salary and compensation transparency had become the top factor affecting candidate decisions at offer stage. Simultaneously, a 2022 Payscale survey found that only 37% of workers always negotiate their salary when offered a new job — with the most common reason for not negotiating being fear of discomfort or damaging the relationship. AI cannot eliminate that discomfort, but it can build the script, the data, and the confidence to enter the conversation prepared.

Difficult Question Categories and How to Prepare

Three types of questions consistently trip up candidates: questions about weaknesses and failures, questions about gaps in employment or experience, and questions about leaving previous employers. Each requires a different strategy, but all share a common principle: the goal is not to avoid the difficulty but to demonstrate self-awareness and forward orientation.

  • 1
    Weakness questions — "What's your greatest weakness?" Ask AI to help you identify a real, credible limitation and frame it with a genuine development narrative. The classic "I work too hard" answer fails because it is not believable. A specific skill gap with a concrete story of how you're addressing it is credible and compelling.
  • 2
    Gap or experience questions — "I notice a gap in your resume / you haven't worked in this industry before." AI can help you build a bridge narrative: what you did during the gap, what transferable skills your background offers, and why this specific gap or background mismatch is actually an asset in this role.
  • 3
    Departure questions — "Why did you leave your last job?" AI can help you craft honest, neutral, forward-focused answers that do not disparage previous employers (which always reflects poorly) while also not sounding evasive.
    • Prompt Template — Difficult Question Prep I need to prepare answers to these difficult interview questions: [list them]. For each, help me craft an honest, credible answer that demonstrates self-awareness and forward focus. Here is the relevant context about my background: [brief description]. Flag if any answer sounds defensive or evasive.
    Salary Negotiation With AI Research

    Negotiating salary requires three things: a number anchored in real market data, a clear articulation of your specific value, and a strategy for the conversation. AI helps with all three. For market data, you bring sources — Glassdoor, Levels.fyi (for tech), LinkedIn Salary, Bureau of Labor Statistics — and ask AI to synthesize a defensible salary range for your role, level, and geography. AI cannot browse in real time, but it can analyze data you paste in and help you build the case.

    For value articulation, AI helps you structure a "negotiation brief" — a one-paragraph summary of why you specifically warrant the top of the range. This is not a document you hand to the employer; it is internal preparation so you can speak confidently without rambling when the moment comes.

    Prompt Template — Negotiation Brief I've received an offer of [amount] for [role]. Based on the following market data I've collected [paste Glassdoor/Levels data], help me: (1) assess whether the offer is at, above, or below market for my level and location, (2) draft a one-paragraph value statement I can use to anchor my counteroffer, (3) write a script for the actual phone call that is direct, professional, and does not apologize for negotiating.
    The Research Basis for Negotiating

    A 2021 Jobvite survey found that 74% of hiring managers have room to negotiate the initial offer, and that in 80% of cases where candidates negotiated, the employer did not rescind the offer. The risk of negotiating professionally and with data is significantly lower than most candidates assume. AI's role is to ensure you enter that conversation with a specific, data-backed number rather than a vague hope for "a bit more."

    Post-Interview Follow-Up

    Most candidates send a generic thank-you email. The ones who advance send a strategic one. The difference is specificity: referencing something specific from the conversation, restating your interest in terms of what you learned in the interview, and addressing any concern that was raised (or that you sensed) during the conversation.

    AI can help you write this email in under ten minutes. The input: notes you took immediately after the interview — what was discussed, any moment where the interviewer seemed uncertain about your fit, a topic that generated genuine energy. The output: a brief, professional, non-sycophantic follow-up that reinforces your candidacy rather than just saying thank you.

    Prompt Template — Post-Interview Email I just finished an interview for [role] at [company]. Here are my notes: [describe key topics discussed, any moment of hesitation from the interviewer, something that genuinely excited me]. Help me write a follow-up email that: (1) is under 150 words, (2) references one specific thing from the conversation, (3) addresses any concern I sensed without being defensive, (4) does not open with "Thank you so much for your time."
    The Module Principle

    Every stage of interview preparation — research, question practice, mock interviews, difficult questions, negotiation, follow-up — benefits from AI not because it replaces your thinking but because it extends your preparation time without extending your clock. The candidate who shows up best prepared is rarely the most talented one in the pool. They are the one who prepared most systematically — and AI makes systematic preparation accessible.
