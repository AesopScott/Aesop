automation, labor market power, and what the evidence says</p> </div> <div class="story-scene" data-label="STORY"> <p class="story-text">Elena had been a paralegal for eleven years. Her firm adopted an AI document review system. Within six months, what had taken her team four days to complete — reviewing contract documents for risk clauses — took the AI four hours.</p> <p class="story-text">Elena still had a job. But her role had changed: less document review, more supervising AI outputs, catching errors, handling edge cases the system couldn't recognize. She was paid the same. The firm billed fewer hours. The efficiency gain went to the clients — and to the partners. <em>The job hadn't disappeared. The more accurate picture: AI is primarily automating <strong>specific tasks within jobs</strong>, not eliminating jobs wholesale. A paralegal who spent 60% of their time on document review now spends that time differently. The job persists; its content transforms.</p> <p>This distinction matters for policy and for individuals. Task automation is a different challenge than structural displacement. It requires different responses — retraining for changed roles, managing the transition, negotiating what new job content is worth — rather than simply finding new employment.</p> <div class="callout"> <p class="callout-label">Which Tasks Are Most Vulnerable?</p> <p>Research consistently finds that AI most readily automates tasks that are routine, rules-based, and data-intensive — regardless of whether those tasks appear in "high-skill" or "low-skill" jobs. Radiologists reading scans, lawyers reviewing documents, and data entry clerks all have high-automation-risk tasks. The division is not by job prestige but by task structure.</p> </div> </div> </div> <div class="section-divider"></div> <div class="lesson-section"> <div class="section-heading">The Labor Power Shift</div> <div class="section-body"> <p>Beyond task content, AI deployment consistently shifts <strong>labor market power toward employers</strong>. When an employer can automate a significant portion of a role, they gain negotiating leverage: the implicit threat of automation raises the cost to workers of demanding higher wages or better conditions. Workers whose tasks are partially automatable face this leverage whether or not their employer currently deploys AI.</p> <p>This dynamic appears across sectors — not just in manufacturing, where automation has been visible for decades, but in knowledge work, creative work, and care work. The shift in power is often more consequential than the direct displacement of jobs.</p> </div> </div> <div class="page-nav"> <button class="pnav-btn" onclick="goPage('p-q1')">Quiz →</button> <button class="pnav-btn primary" onclick="goPage('p-lab1')">Lab 1 →</button> </div> </div> <!-- QUIZ 1 --> <div class="page" id="p-q1"> <div class="quiz-hero"> <h1>Lesson 1 Quiz</h1> <div class="tagline">2 questions — free, untracked, retake anytime.</div> </div> <div class="quiz-box" id="q1-l1"> <div class="quiz-q">The most accurate description of AI's primary labor market effect is:</div> <div class="quiz-opts"> <button class="quiz-opt" onclick="answer(this,'wrong','q1-l1')">Eliminating entire job categories rapidly across the economy</button> <button class="quiz-opt" onclick="answer(this,'wrong','q1-l1')">Creating more jobs than it eliminates in every sector</button> <button class="quiz-opt" onclick="answer(this,'correct','q1-l1')">Automating specific tasks within jobs, transforming job content rather than eliminating positions outright</button> <button class="quiz-opt" onclick="answer(this,'wrong','q1-l1')">Primarily affecting low-skill jobs while protecting knowledge workers</button> </div> <div class="quiz-feedback right">✓ Correct! New jobs created by AI development are concentrated in: AI research and engineering, data annotation and quality assurance, AI product management, and the infrastructure supporting AI deployment. These jobs require skills, credentials, and often geographic mobility that displaced workers often lack.</p> <p>The transition period between displacement and reabsorption is real, painful, and unequally distributed. Older workers, workers in geographically isolated communities, and workers without portable credentials face the longest and hardest transitions. AI-enabled service automation will affect specific metro areas differently from others, based on their economic mix.</p> <p>Geographic concentration means local economies — tax bases, small businesses, community institutions — face concentrated pressure even when national employment statistics remain stable. A town that loses 1,400 jobs and gains 200 is not a statistical rounding error; it is a community in genuine distress, with cascading effects on schools, healthcare, and social fabric.</p> <div class="gold-callout"> <p class="callout-label">The Policy Gap</p> <p>Most labor market policy was designed for a world of relatively slow structural change. The mismatch between transition speed and support infrastructure is itself a governance problem.</p> </div> </div> </div> <div class="page-nav"> <button class="pnav-btn" onclick="goPage('p-lab2')">← Lab 2</button> <button class="pnav-btn primary" onclick="goPage('p-q3')">Quiz →</button> </div> </div> <!-- QUIZ 3 --> <div class="page" id="p-q3"> <div class="quiz-hero"> <h1>Lesson 3 Quiz</h1> <div class="tagline">2 questions — free, untracked, retake anytime.</div> </div> <div class="quiz-box" id="q1-l3"> <div class="quiz-q">The historical claim that "technology creates new jobs" is problematic as a response to AI displacement because:</div> <div class="quiz-opts"> <button class="quiz-opt" onclick="answer(this,'wrong','q1-l3')">It is historically false — technology has never created net new employment</button> <button class="quiz-opt" onclick="answer(this,'correct','q1-l3')">It is accurate in aggregate but chronically unhelpful to individuals — new jobs require skills, credentials, and geographic mobility that displaced workers often lack</button> <button class="quiz-opt" onclick="answer(this,'wrong','q1-l3')">It applies to manufacturing automation but not AI-driven task automation</button> <button class="quiz-opt" onclick="answer(this,'wrong','q1-l3')">It ignores the role of government retraining programs in managing transitions</button> </div> <div class="quiz-feedback right">✓ Correct! Traditional labor law gives workers rights regarding wages, hours, safety, and collective bargaining — but was not designed for a world where the supervisor is an algorithm.
