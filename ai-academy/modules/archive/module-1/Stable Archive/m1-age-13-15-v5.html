What Is AI?

Priya is building an AI assistant for her science fair using an API. Three weeks in, it keeps ignoring the end of her prompts. Understanding why requires understanding what AI actually is.

Page 1 of 10 — What Is AI?

Priya had been building her AI assistant for three weeks. Not from scratch — she was using an API. The project was for the school science fair: a study companion that could quiz her on any subject she was studying.

It worked well for simple questions. But whenever she gave it long, detailed instructions — quiz me on these 12 vocabulary words, use Socratic method, don't reveal the answer until I've guessed three times — it would follow the first part perfectly and completely ignore the rest.

She posted in a developer forum: "Why does the AI keep forgetting the end of my prompt?"

A senior developer replied within minutes: "Context window. Read up on attention mechanisms — it'll change how you think about this entirely."

Priya didn't know what either of those things meant yet. She was about to.

🤖 What AI Is — A Technical Definition

Artificial Intelligence, in its modern form, refers to systems that learn statistical representations from data and use those representations to generate outputs across a wide range of tasks. The critical word is learned.

Modern AI systems don't have domain knowledge programmed in. They extract it from data. A language model doesn't "know" that Paris is the capital of France because a programmer wrote that fact. It knows it because that association appeared consistently in its training data, and the model learned to encode it.

🔑 Three Defining Properties

Data-driven: behavior is learned from examples, not specified by rules.

Probabilistic: outputs are drawn from learned distributions — the same input can produce different outputs.

Generalizing: systems handle inputs not seen during training. The degree of generalization is a key measure of capability.

🗂️ The AI System Taxonomy

Large Language Models (LLMs)

Transformer-based models trained on text. Generate text. Used for writing, coding, analysis, conversation, reasoning. Core limitation: they predict patterns, not truth. Examples: GPT-4o, Claude, Gemini.

Multimodal Models

Process multiple input types simultaneously — text, images, audio. Can describe images, generate visuals from text descriptions, transcribe speech. Examples: GPT-4o with vision, Claude with vision.

Reinforcement Learning Systems

Learn by optimizing reward signals through trial-and-error interaction with an environment. AlphaGo, robotic control, recommendation systems. Sample-inefficient but capable of superhuman performance on well-defined objectives.

Narrow vs. General-Purpose

Most deployed AI is narrow — excellent at specific task categories. LLMs are remarkably broad but still brittle: they fail in ways narrow specialists don't, and can't reliably reason outside their training distribution.

Priya read about context windows for an hour. Then she went back to her project.

The context window explained everything. Her long instructions weren't being "forgotten" — the model was giving less attention to tokens that appeared earlier in a long prompt, prioritizing more recent context.

She restructured her instructions: critical rules at the beginning AND the end. Complex tasks broken into numbered steps. Each instruction explicit and short.

The quiz assistant started working exactly as she'd intended.

She hadn't changed a single line of code. She'd changed her understanding of the tool.

✅ Correct. AI samples from probability distributions rather than computing fixed answers — which is why the same prompt can yield different responses.
Probabilistic means outputs are drawn from learned distributions. AI doesn't compute a single deterministic answer — it samples from probabilities, so the same input can produce different outputs.

🧪 Priya's companion ignored instructions placed early in a long prompt. What concept explains this?

✅ Right. Attention isn't equal across the context window. Restructuring where you place instructions — and keeping prompts tight — directly changes what the model attends to most.
Context windows are finite processing capacity, and attention isn't evenly distributed within them. Instructions buried early in long prompts can get lower weight — prompt structure matters.

🧪 What does "learning-based" AI mean — and why does it matter?

✅ Correct. Rules emerge from data rather than programmers writing them explicitly. That's what enables AI to do things its creators never built directly — and fail in ways they didn't anticipate.
Learning-based means behavior emerges from training patterns, not hand-coded rules. This creates both power (unanticipated capabilities) and unpredictability (unanticipated failures).

🧪 What is the difference between narrow AI and AGI?

✅ Right. Every current AI is narrow — powerful within its training distribution, often unreliable outside it. AGI (general cognitive ability across domains) is a research goal, not a current reality.
Current AI is narrow — even impressive LLMs fail reliably outside their training distribution. AGI matching human cognitive range across all domains hasn't been achieved.

How AI Learns

Training data, pretraining objectives, RLHF — and why the decisions made before training begins shape everything AI believes and everything it gets wrong.

Page 3 of 10 — How AI Learns

Priya was curious now. She started reading about how the model she was using had been built.

The scale was hard to grasp. Trillions of tokens. Hundreds of billions of words. The entire crawlable web, plus books, code repositories, academic papers. All of it fed into a training process with one objective: predict what comes next.

That's it? she thought. Predict the next word — and from that you get something that can quiz me on vocabulary, explain calculus, and write code?

She kept reading. The more she understood, the more she wanted to know.

📊 Training Data at Scale

Large language models train on datasets measured in trillions of tokens — hundreds of billions of words. This includes the crawlable web, digitized books, academic papers, code repositories, and curated datasets. The scale is genuinely hard to comprehend: a human reading 8 hours a day for an entire lifetime reads roughly 1 billion words. LLMs train on hundreds of times that.

📌 What the Model Is Actually Learning

The training objective for most LLMs is simple: predict the next token. From this single objective, applied to an enormous dataset, models develop the ability to reason, write, code, and converse. The learned representations encode not just facts but relationships between concepts, argument structures, writing styles — and the biases and errors in the source data.

The AI doesn't memorize training data. It compresses it into billions of parameters — numerical weights that encode statistical relationships across every concept the model encountered. This is why models can generalize: the representations transfer across tasks.

🎓 RLHF: Teaching Values, Not Just Patterns

After pretraining, models go through RLHF — Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback. Human annotators compare pairs of model outputs and indicate which is better. The model learns to prefer outputs that score highly.

This isn't a minor adjustment. RLHF substantially shapes how a model behaves: its tone, what it's willing to discuss, how it handles ambiguous requests, what it treats as appropriate. The values embedded in the rating guidelines become the values embedded in the model.

⚠️ The Values Embedding Problem

RLHF rater guidelines are written by people — with perspectives, blind spots, and cultural assumptions. "Helpful, harmless, honest" sounds simple. But what counts as harmful, and to whom, is genuinely contested. The choices made in those guidelines shape what billions of users experience as "neutral" AI.

🗑️ Garbage In, Garbage Out — At Scale

At trillion-token scale, errors and biases in training data don't get averaged out — they get reinforced. A model trained on data that systematically underrepresents certain perspectives will systematically underrepresent those perspectives in outputs.

🌟 Why Data Curation Is High-Stakes

The people who decide what goes into training data — and what gets filtered out — are making decisions that affect how millions of people receive information. Data quality determines capability; data curation determines alignment. Both are consequential, and neither gets enough public attention.

🧪 Why does scale amplify rather than reduce training data problems?

✅ Right. Scale reinforces patterns — including problematic ones. A systematic bias present in training data appears across trillions of tokens.
Scale reinforces patterns. Systematic misrepresentation in training data becomes more deeply embedded as the model trains on more of it — not less.

🧪 What does RLHF embed into AI that basic pretraining does not?

✅ Correct. RLHF bakes in the judgment calls of human raters. What they marked as "helpful" and "appropriate" becomes part of how the model behaves — making AI values a product of human decisions.
RLHF shapes AI behavior through human preference ratings. The values, priorities, and judgment calls of the raters get embedded — making AI values explicitly a product of human decisions.

🧪 The NYT sued OpenAI over training data. What fundamental tension did this expose?

✅ Right. At trillion-token scale, clearing rights for all training data is essentially impossible. The legal and ethical questions this raises are genuinely unresolved — and will shape AI governance for years.
The NYT lawsuit crystallized a structural problem: building capable LLMs requires training data at a scale that makes consent-clearing nearly impossible. The legal and ethical implications are still being worked out.

🧪 Why can't AI verify its own training data for accuracy?

✅ Correct. No ground truth access. AI learned patterns — including patterns that happen to be wrong — with no internal flag to distinguish accurate from inaccurate. That verification is always the human's job.
AI has no access to ground truth during or after training. It learned statistical patterns — correct and incorrect ones alike — with no mechanism to tell the difference. Verification is always your responsibility.

How AI Thinks — And Where It Goes Wrong

Context windows, hallucination mechanics, and bias as a systems problem. The technical reality behind every AI response — including the ones that sound completely right but aren't.

Page 5 of 10 — How AI Thinks

Priya's study companion was working well now — but she noticed something odd. When she asked it about recent AI research papers, it would sometimes give her plausible-sounding citations that didn't exist.

She checked one. The author was real. The journal was real. The paper did not exist.

She went back to the developer forum. "My assistant is citing fake papers. Why?"

The same developer replied: "Hallucination. Look it up — it'll explain a lot about how the underlying model works."

It did.

🔤 Tokens, Context Windows, and Attention

Language models process text as tokens — subword units roughly corresponding to syllables or common words. Every response is generated one token at a time, each selected based on the probability distribution over all possible next tokens given the context.

The context window is the maximum number of tokens the model can process simultaneously — its total working memory for a conversation. Current frontier models have windows of 128K–200K tokens. But attention is not uniform: tokens near the beginning and end of long contexts typically receive higher attention weight than those in the middle.

💡 The Practical Implication

This is why Priya's long instructions were being deprioritized. Place critical instructions at the beginning AND end of long prompts. Break complex tasks into explicit numbered steps. For tasks requiring sustained attention across long contexts, multiple sequential calls outperform one massive prompt. Understanding the architecture makes you a better builder.

⚠️ Hallucination: A Structural Failure Mode

Hallucination occurs when the model's token-prediction process generates text that is factually false but statistically plausible. The model has no fact-checking mechanism — it cannot distinguish between a pattern that leads to truth and one that leads to a confident-sounding falsehood.

Hallucination rates are predictably higher for: obscure topics (less training signal), precise numbers and dates (high sensitivity to small errors), citations (the model learned citation formats but not citation contents), and events near or after the training cutoff.

⚠️ Verification Is Always Your Responsibility

For anything consequential — academic work, medical information, legal questions, factual claims — treat AI output as a starting hypothesis, not a verified fact. The burden of verification never shifts to the AI, because the AI cannot carry it.

⚖️ Bias as a Systems Problem

AI bias is not localized to a specific parameter — it's distributed across the model's representations, emerging from the interaction of training data, architecture, and RLHF guidelines. Categories include: representation bias (who appears in training data), measurement bias (how outcomes are labeled), aggregation bias (treating diverse groups as homogeneous), and deployment bias (using AI in contexts different from those it was trained on).

🔍 Test It Yourself

Ask an AI to write a story about a surgeon, then a nurse. Compare gender assumptions. Ask about "a startup founder" vs "an elementary school teacher." Ask the same question framed for different demographic groups. The patterns reveal systematic associations in training data.

✅ Correct. Context window = total processing capacity. Long prompts mean earlier instructions may receive less attention weight by response time.
A context window is the total tokens a model can process simultaneously. In long prompts, earlier content may receive less "attention" — which is why Priya's end-of-prompt instructions were being ignored.

🧪 What is hallucination — and what makes it structurally unavoidable?

✅ Right. Hallucination is structural — not a bug to be fixed. Without a truth-checking mechanism, statistical generation will sometimes produce plausible-sounding falsehoods with full confidence.
Hallucination is structural: statistical generation with no truth-verification can produce confident false output. Better prompting reduces it but doesn't eliminate it — the mechanism itself is the source.

🧪 Priya found a forum post explaining her prompt problem. What does this illustrate about AI literacy?

✅ Exactly. Priya's problem wasn't a bug — it was a mechanical property of how attention works. Understanding the mechanism turned an unsolvable mystery into a fixable prompt structure problem.
Knowing how AI works mechanically changes how you troubleshoot it. Priya's "broken" companion wasn't broken — it was behaving exactly as designed. Understanding context windows made that visible.

🧪 What is bias in AI — and where does it come from?

✅ Correct. Bias isn't intentional — it's learned. Patterns in training data that consistently represented groups or roles unfairly get encoded as normal and reproduced systematically.
AI bias comes from training data — not from intentional programming. Patterns that consistently misrepresented groups or roles get learned as normal distributions and reproduced reliably, not randomly.

Lab 3 — Train a Human to Be AI

This lab is a physical activity — no interface needed. It's the most visceral way to understand what training actually feels like from the inside.

  1. Setup: Pick a simple classification task — sort a set of objects, images, or words into two categories (e.g., "positive/negative sentiment," "safe/unsafe for children," "factual/opinion").
  2. Training phase: Have a partner show you 10 labeled examples. You are the model. They are the training data.
  3. Inference phase: Your partner shows you 5 new unlabeled examples. You classify them without help.
  4. RLHF phase: Your partner gives you thumbs up/down on each classification. You adjust your mental model. Classify 5 more.
  5. Reflection: What did the feedback change? What biases did you carry in from before training? Did the labels in the training set feel arbitrary? How would your classifications differ if the training examples had been different?
You just experienced pretraining, inference, and RLHF from the inside. The feelings you had — uncertainty, pattern-matching, adjusting after feedback — are the behavioral signatures of what the math is doing at scale.

LLMs, Transformers & Emergence

The transformer architecture, attention mechanisms in depth, and why capabilities nobody anticipated kept appearing as models scaled up.

Page 7 of 10 — LLMs & Emergence

Priya had restructured her prompts. Her study companion worked. But now she was curious about something deeper: why did attention mechanisms fix her problem at all?

She found the original 2017 paper: "Attention Is All You Need." She read the abstract. Then the introduction. Then she went down a rabbit hole that kept her up until midnight.

The core insight was almost elegant in its simplicity: instead of processing words sequentially — one after another, losing track of early context — the transformer let every word relate to every other word simultaneously. Weighted by relevance.

That's why putting the instruction at the end works too, she thought. It's not forgotten — it's weighted. And I can use that.

🧩 The Transformer Architecture

Before 2017, most AI language systems processed text sequentially — limited ability to reference distant context. The transformer introduced self-attention: every token can attend to every other token in the context simultaneously, with weights that capture relevance between pairs.

When processing "bank" in "river bank," attention lets the model weight "river" heavily and "money" lightly. In "bank account," the weights flip. This dynamic, context-sensitive processing across an entire document is what gives transformers their ability to handle nuance and long-range dependencies.

🔑 Why Attention Changed Everything

Before attention, language models were fundamentally limited by sequential processing — they couldn't efficiently relate distant parts of a document. Attention made that possible. Every major LLM since 2017 — GPT, Claude, Gemini, Llama — is a transformer descendant. The 2017 paper is arguably the most consequential single publication in modern AI.

🌀 Emergent Capabilities at Scale

As language models scaled from millions to billions to hundreds of billions of parameters, something unexpected happened: capabilities appeared that nobody had programmed. Researchers called this emergence.

Examples of emergent capabilities: few-shot learning (performing tasks from just a few examples), chain-of-thought reasoning, code generation, cross-language translation for language pairs not directly trained on, and basic arithmetic. None of these were engineered — they appeared at scale.

🤔 The Unsettling Implication

We cannot reliably predict what capabilities will emerge in the next generation of models. This makes safety planning significantly harder — you can't anticipate risks from capabilities you can't predict. It's one reason safety researchers advocate for proactive rather than reactive approaches.

⚖️ Is AI Intelligent? The Precise Question

"Is AI intelligent?" is underspecified. Better questions: Does it generalize to novel situations? (Yes, within distribution. Unreliably outside it.) Does it exhibit goal-directed behavior? (Within a session, not persistently.) Does it have subjective experience? (Unknown — and possibly unknowable with current methods.)

🌟 The Honest Assessment

Current AI systems are powerful, brittle, useful, and unreliable — sometimes simultaneously. We've built something impressive whose nature we don't fully understand. That's worth taking seriously on both sides: both the power and the uncertainty.

✅ Correct. Attention = simultaneous cross-token relationships. This replaced sequential processing and enabled nuance, long-range dependencies, and complex reasoning.
The transformer's self-attention lets tokens attend to each other across the entire context simultaneously — replacing sequential processing with dynamic relational weighting. That's what makes nuanced understanding possible.

✅ Exactly. Attention isn't positional — it's weighted. Understanding that let Priya engineer her prompts rather than guess at them. Knowledge of the mechanism gave her control.
Attention weights tokens based on context and relevance — not just position. Priya's insight was that understanding attention mechanics let her place instructions deliberately, not just hope they'd be followed.

✅ Right. Emergent capabilities appear unexpectedly at scale — which means safety testing of smaller models can't fully predict what risks will exist in larger ones. This is a genuine, unresolved challenge.
Emergent capabilities appear without being programmed — which means you can't fully anticipate them from smaller models. That makes safety planning harder: you can't red-team for risks you can't predict.

🧪 "Is AI intelligent?" is underspecified. What are better questions to ask?

✅ Right. Breaking "intelligent?" into specific sub-questions gives clearer answers: yes to some, no to others, unknown for subjective experience. Precision matters more than a yes/no verdict.
The question "is AI intelligent?" conflates several distinct questions. Breaking it down — generalization, goal-directedness, subjective experience — gives more precise and defensible answers than a blanket yes or no.

AI History & Architecture

From the perceptron to ChatGPT. Understanding the technical arc of how we got here — and what Priya now knows that will define how she builds.

Page 9 of 10 — AI History

Priya presented her study companion at the science fair. It worked perfectly. But her favorite moment was when a judge asked: "How does it actually work?"

Priya didn't say "I used an API." She explained pretraining. She explained attention mechanisms and why she structured her prompts the way she did. She explained why it sometimes generated plausible-sounding wrong answers — and how she'd built in a verification step for anything cited as a source.

The judge — a software engineer — nodded slowly. "Most people just use these tools. You understand them."

Priya had started this project trying to build something. She'd ended up understanding something. The tool hadn't changed. She had.

📅 The Technical Arc of AI Development

1950s–1980s: Rule-Based AI. Early AI encoded human knowledge as logical rules. Expert systems, chess programs, medical diagnosis tools. These worked — until the real world proved too complex for hand-coded rules to handle reliably.

1980s–2010s: Neural Networks and Two AI Winters. Neural networks showed promise but failed at scale twice. Funding collapsed. The field survived on the conviction that data and compute would eventually be sufficient.

2012: Deep Learning Breakthrough. AlexNet — a convolutional neural network trained on GPUs — destroyed every competitor in the ImageNet image recognition benchmark. Data + GPU compute + the right architecture was suddenly producing results nobody expected. The deep learning era began.

2017: Attention Is All You Need. Google researchers published the transformer. Every major LLM since — GPT, Claude, Gemini, Llama — descends from this paper.

2022–present: The Scaling Era. GPT-3, ChatGPT, Claude, and successors demonstrated that capabilities scale predictably with compute and data. AI became consumer infrastructure. The compute race accelerated into the billions.

🗂️ The Model Landscape: What You'll Work With

Foundation Models

Large pretrained models (GPT-4, Claude, Gemini) trained on broad data. Capable across many tasks. Accessed via API. The base layer for most AI applications.

Fine-Tuned Models

Foundation models further trained on specific datasets for specific tasks. A customer service bot is often a fine-tuned LLM. Fine-tuning costs far less than training from scratch.

Open vs. Closed Models

Open-weight models (Llama, Mistral) release model weights publicly. Closed models (GPT-4, Claude) are API-only. Open models enable local deployment and modification; closed models offer ongoing safety refinement and centralized control.

🧪 What was significant about the 2012 AlexNet result?

✅ Correct. AlexNet's ImageNet victory proved the deep learning paradigm — data + GPUs + neural networks. Everything since traces to that result.
AlexNet showed deep neural networks on GPUs could dramatically outperform all other approaches. It launched the deep learning era that eventually produced transformers and current LLMs.

🧪 Why did early rule-based AI ultimately fail to scale?

✅ Right. Expert systems and rule-based AI worked in constrained domains — but the real world has too many edge cases to hand-code every decision. Learning from data solved what rules couldn't.
Rule-based AI collapsed under complexity. The real world generates more edge cases than any team of engineers can anticipate and encode. Learning from data — rather than writing rules — bypassed that fundamental limitation.

🧪 What is the difference between a foundation model and a fine-tuned model?

✅ Correct. Fine-tuning takes an existing foundation model and trains it further on domain-specific data — far cheaper than training from scratch, and often more reliable for targeted tasks.
Foundation models (like Claude or GPT-4) are broad, general-purpose. Fine-tuned models take those foundations and further train them on specific data for specific tasks — much cheaper and faster than starting from scratch.

🧪 The science fair judge told Priya: "Most people just use these tools. You understand them." What's the difference?

✅ That's the whole point of this module. Priya didn't just build something — she understood what she'd built. That understanding changed how she could use, troubleshoot, and explain it. That's the difference.
Understanding the mechanism lets you do things users can't: diagnose failures, design prompts deliberately, verify outputs critically, and explain what's actually happening. Priya's project worked because she understood it — not just because she followed instructions.
