What Is AI? Really?

Alex's class debated whether AI "actually thinks." Half said yes. Half said no. The teacher said they were both partly right — and that understanding why is the most important question in technology right now.

Page 1 of 4 — What Is AI, Really?

Alex raised their hand first: "AI just follows instructions — it's not actually thinking."

From across the room, Jordan shot back: "But it writes poetry and solves math problems. That's thinking."

The teacher smiled. "What if I told you the answer depends entirely on what you mean by 'thinking'? And that figuring out what you mean — precisely — is actually the hard part?"

Alex wrote that down. It was the first time a question about computers had felt genuinely philosophical.

🤖 AI as Pattern Finding, Not Magic

Artificial intelligence is a computer system that has learned to recognize patterns in data and use those patterns to make predictions or decisions. The key word is learned — AI systems aren't programmed with explicit rules like "if this, then that." Instead, they're exposed to enormous amounts of examples and they figure out the patterns themselves.

Think about how you learned to recognize a dog. Nobody gave you a checklist: four legs, fur, tail, barks. You saw thousands of dogs over your lifetime — in person, in books, in photos — and your brain extracted the pattern. AI does something mathematically similar, but at a scale that's hard to imagine: millions or billions of examples, processed at incredible speed.

🔑 The Recipe Analogy

A traditional computer program is like a recipe. Every step is written out explicitly by a programmer: "if the input is X, output Y." The computer follows instructions exactly.

An AI system is more like a chef who learned by eating. Nobody wrote down all the rules — the chef tasted thousands of dishes and developed an intuition for what works. The "knowledge" is in the experience, not in written instructions.

🧠 Brain-Inspired, But Not a Brain

Modern AI systems — especially the neural networks that power tools like ChatGPT and Claude — were inspired by how biological brains work. Just as your brain has billions of neurons connected by synapses, artificial neural networks have layers of mathematical "nodes" connected by adjustable numbers called weights.

When a neural network trains on data, it adjusts those weights millions of times until its predictions match the examples it's learning from. The end result is a system that can perform impressively on the tasks it trained for — and sometimes generalizes to new situations surprisingly well.

⚠️ Important Distinction

Inspired by brains is not the same as working like a brain. Neural networks don't have consciousness, emotions, goals, or genuine understanding. They find patterns in numbers. The outputs can look remarkably human — but the process underneath is fundamentally mathematical, not biological.

🌟 So Does AI Think?

It depends what you mean. If "thinking" means processing information and producing useful outputs — yes, in a sense. If "thinking" means having awareness, understanding, intentions, or genuine comprehension — no, not in any way we currently understand. Both Alex and Jordan in the story were partly right. The question is genuinely open at the philosophical level, and that's what makes it interesting.

How AI Learns — Training Data

Where does an AI's knowledge actually come from? Not from a teacher, not from being programmed with facts — from data. Enormous amounts of data. And the quality of that data determines almost everything about how the AI behaves.

Page 2 of 4 — How AI Learns

📚 Training Data: The Foundation of Everything

When an AI system learns, it learns from a training dataset — a collection of examples that the system processes over and over, adjusting its internal weights until it can correctly handle the examples it's seen. The quality and content of this dataset is arguably the single most important factor in determining what the AI can and can't do.

For large language models like Claude and ChatGPT, the training dataset consists of an enormous amount of text: websites, books, scientific papers, code, conversations, and more. We're talking about trillions of words — more text than any human could read in thousands of lifetimes. The AI learns the patterns of language, knowledge, and reasoning from this corpus.

🔑 Garbage In, Garbage Out

This principle is one of the oldest in computing — and it applies to AI with extra force. If the training data contains errors, the AI learns those errors. If it contains biases — for example, if certain groups of people are consistently portrayed in a narrow or negative way — the AI absorbs those biases. If the training data mostly covers certain topics and ignores others, the AI will be much better at those topics than at the ignored ones.

⚗️ What Data Shapes AI Behavior

The training data doesn't just determine what facts an AI knows — it shapes how the AI writes, what it considers normal, what kinds of answers it tends to give, and even what it might refuse to do. An AI trained on formal academic writing will produce different outputs than one trained on casual internet text, even if they've seen similar facts.

This is why researchers and companies working on AI spend enormous amounts of time and effort on data curation — selecting, cleaning, and filtering the training data to remove harmful content, correct errors, and ensure a diverse and representative sample. It's one of the most important and least glamorous parts of building AI systems.

🌟 The Diversity Question

If training data is mostly from one language, one culture, or one perspective, the AI will reflect that perspective. This raises important questions: Who decides what goes into training data? Whose voices are represented? What kinds of content get filtered out — and by whose standards? These aren't just technical questions. They're questions about power and representation that society is actively wrestling with.

🧪 Why is the diversity of training data so important for AI fairness?

✅ Correct. An AI is only as fair and capable as the data it learned from. If the training data over-represents certain cultures, languages, or viewpoints — and under-represents others — the AI will be systematically better at serving the over-represented groups. This is a genuine fairness and equity concern, not just a technical detail.
If training data mostly reflects one perspective, the AI learns that perspective as its baseline for "normal." Groups or contexts not well-represented in the data will tend to get worse, less accurate, or even unfair outputs. Diversity in data is a prerequisite for AI that works fairly across different people and contexts.

Large Language Models — Predicting the Next Word

At their core, tools like ChatGPT and Claude are doing something surprisingly simple: predicting what word comes next. Over and over again, billions of times. It turns out that doing this well enough, on enough data, produces something that looks remarkably like intelligence.

Page 3 of 4 — LLMs & Prediction

Alex stared at the autocomplete suggestions on their phone. As they typed "I'm really hungry and I want to eat..." the phone suggested "pizza," then "something good," then "dinner."

"Wait," Alex thought. "Is that... basically what ChatGPT is? Autocomplete?"

Not quite — but closer to the truth than most people realize. The difference is scale. Your phone's autocomplete learns from your personal typing history. A large language model learns from the entire internet. The mechanism is similar. The result is... something else entirely.

🔤 Tokens, Not Words

Large language models don't actually work with whole words — they work with tokens, which are chunks of text that can be whole words, parts of words, punctuation marks, or spaces. The word "unbelievable" might be split into "un," "believ," and "able." The word "cat" is likely a single token.

At every step, the model looks at all the tokens it has seen so far and predicts the probability of each possible next token. It picks from those probabilities (not always the most likely — a bit of randomness keeps the output from being predictably boring), adds the new token to the sequence, and repeats. This is how every response you've ever gotten from an AI language model was generated: one token at a time, left to right, until the model decides it's done.

🔑 Why Context Window Matters

A model can only "see" a limited amount of text at once — this is called its context window. Think of it like working memory: the model can only pay attention to what's currently in the window. Longer context windows let the model handle longer conversations and documents without "forgetting" what was said earlier. Modern AI systems have context windows of hundreds of thousands of tokens — equivalent to several long novels worth of text.

🤔 If It's Just Prediction, Why Is It So Good?

This is the genuinely surprising part. Predicting the next token well — on enough data — turns out to require learning an enormous amount about the world. To predict what word follows "the capital of France is," the model needs to have encoded geographical knowledge. To predict what follows "the function returns," it needs to understand programming logic. To predict what follows "she felt a wave of," it needs to understand human emotions.

The training objective is simple. The knowledge required to accomplish it well is almost everything humans have written down. That's why large language models seem so broadly capable — they've been forced to internalize an enormous amount of structured knowledge just to get good at next-token prediction.

⚠️ But It Can Still Get Things Badly Wrong

Predicting what sounds right and knowing what is correct are different things. When a language model doesn't have good training data on a topic, it will still produce a confident-sounding prediction — which may be completely wrong. This is called hallucination: the model generates fluent, plausible-sounding text that is factually incorrect. It happens because the model is optimized to predict likely text, not to verify facts.

AI Tools You Might Know

ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini, voice assistants, image generators, recommendation algorithms — they all use AI, but they're doing different things. Understanding how they differ helps you use each one more effectively.

Page 4 of 4 — AI Tools You Know

🛠️ The Landscape of AI Tools

Large Language Models (ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini): These are conversation and text generation tools built on the transformer architecture we covered in the last lesson. They're designed for text-in, text-out tasks: answering questions, writing, coding, analysis, summarization. Their strengths and weaknesses come directly from how they were trained.

Voice Assistants (Siri, Alexa, Google Assistant): These combine several AI components — speech recognition to convert your voice to text, a language model to understand your request, and text-to-speech to respond. The "intelligence" you're interacting with is a combination of specialized systems working together, not a single unified AI.

Image AI (Midjourney, DALL-E, Stable Diffusion): These use a completely different architecture — diffusion models — that work by learning to reverse a process of adding noise to images. They're trained on enormous datasets of image-text pairs, which is why they can generate images from text descriptions. And why they sometimes reflect the biases present in their training images.

🔑 Recommendation Algorithms

Your YouTube recommendations, TikTok feed, Spotify playlist suggestions, and Netflix suggestions are all AI — but a different kind than language models. These systems are trained to predict what you'll engage with next based on your behavior history and the behavior of millions of similar users. They're extremely good at their objective. The question worth asking is: whose interests does that objective actually serve?

⚖️ How These Tools Differ

Even among language models, meaningful differences exist. Claude is developed by Anthropic with an explicit focus on safety and careful behavior. ChatGPT (GPT-4) is from OpenAI, optimized heavily for helpfulness and instruction-following. Gemini is from Google, with deep integration into Google's knowledge infrastructure. Llama from Meta is open-source — downloadable and runnable on your own computer.

These differences aren't just marketing. They reflect genuine differences in training data, fine-tuning decisions, what behaviors were rewarded during RLHF, and what the developers considered most important. Learning to notice these differences — and thinking about why they exist — is a meaningful form of AI literacy.

🌟 The Right Question to Ask

For any AI tool: What was it trained to do, and whose interests does that objective serve? A recommendation algorithm trained to maximize engagement is serving the platform's advertising business, not necessarily your wellbeing. A language model trained to be "helpful" reflects the definition of helpful held by its developers. These aren't reasons not to use these tools — they're reasons to use them with awareness.
