What Is AI? A Simple Answer

Sam and Devon were building a science fair project about artificial intelligence. They thought they understood it. Then they tried to explain it to Devon's little sister — and realized they didn't know as much as they thought.

Page 1 of 4 — What Is AI?

Sam had typed "AI is a computer that thinks" on their poster board.

Devon's little sister Maya pointed at it. "But how does it think? Did someone teach it? Or does it just... know stuff?"

Sam and Devon looked at each other. They'd looked up "AI" online. They'd watched videos. But Maya's question was better than any question they'd thought to ask.

"That," said Devon slowly, "is actually a really good question."

🤖 A Computer That Learned From Examples

Here's the clearest way to explain AI: it's a computer that learned what to do by looking at lots and lots of examples — instead of being given a list of rules to follow.

Most computer programs work like a recipe. Someone writes out every single step: "if the user clicks this button, do that thing." The computer follows the instructions exactly, every time.

Artificial intelligence is different. Instead of writing out all the rules, the people who build AI give it thousands or millions of examples — and let it figure out the pattern by itself. The AI learns from the examples the same way you might learn a new game by watching people play it, rather than reading the rulebook.

🔑 The Animal Photo Example

Imagine sorting 20,000 photos of animals. You don't write a rule that says "if it has whiskers and pointy ears, it's a cat." Instead, you show the computer 10,000 photos labeled "cat" and 10,000 labeled "not cat" — and it figures out the pattern by itself. After seeing enough examples, it can correctly identify cats in photos it's never seen before.

That's machine learning: learning from examples instead of following written rules.

🧠 So Did Someone Teach It?

Maya's question was a good one — and the answer is: sort of. People do teach AI, but not the way a teacher teaches a student. They don't explain concepts or have conversations with it. Instead, they gather huge amounts of data (photos, text, recordings, numbers) and feed it into the AI over and over while the AI adjusts itself until its guesses match the correct answers.

This process is called training. During training, the AI makes lots of mistakes — and that's actually how it gets better. Each mistake tells the AI to adjust, just a tiny bit. After enough adjustments, it starts getting things right.

🌟 Does It Just "Know Stuff"?

Not exactly. AI doesn't "know" things the way you know your best friend's name. It has learned patterns from its training data — but it can be confidently wrong, it can misremember things, and it definitely doesn't understand the world the way you do. Knowing that AI learned from examples — and that the examples aren't perfect — helps you understand why AI sometimes makes surprising mistakes.

How AI Learns From Data

Sam and Devon's project was going well — until they realized their AI kept making the same wrong guess over and over. The problem wasn't the AI. It was the examples they gave it.

Page 2 of 4 — How AI Learns

Sam and Devon had taught their simple AI to sort photos of fruit. It was doing great with apples and oranges — until Devon added a picture of a tomato.

The AI called it an orange. Every time.

"That's weird," said Sam. "A tomato doesn't look like an orange."

Devon thought for a second. "We only showed it red and orange fruits. It never saw anything red that wasn't an apple. So when it saw something red and round that wasn't an apple shape..."

"It guessed orange," Sam finished. "Because that's the closest thing it knew."

The problem wasn't the AI being dumb. The problem was the training data.

📊 Training Data: Where AI Knowledge Comes From

Training data is the collection of examples that an AI learns from. For an AI that sorts photos, the training data is photos. For an AI that answers questions in text, the training data is huge amounts of text — articles, books, websites, and more. For an AI that recognizes speech, it's recordings of people talking.

The quality and variety of training data matters enormously. If the training data is limited — like Sam and Devon's fruit AI that only saw apples and oranges — the AI will make confident mistakes when it encounters something new. More examples, and more varied examples, generally makes the AI better at handling things it hasn't seen before.

🔑 More Examples = Better AI (Usually)

When AI is trained on more examples, it develops a better understanding of the patterns. An AI that has seen one thousand dogs will make better predictions than one that has seen ten. An AI that has seen dogs of every size, color, and breed will be better than one that only saw big brown dogs.

This is why the companies building AI gather enormous amounts of data — we're talking about hundreds of billions of words of text, or millions of images, to train the AI tools you've heard of.

⚠️ What Happens With Bad Training Data

Sam and Devon's tomato problem shows something really important: if the training data is missing something, or has errors in it, the AI will learn those gaps and errors. Computer scientists have a saying for this: "Garbage in, garbage out."

This applies to the big AI tools you might use too. If the text that a language model learned from had wrong information in it — or if it mostly showed one point of view — the AI will reflect those problems in its answers. That's one reason why you should always double-check important information from AI against other trusted sources.

⚠️ AI Can Learn Unfair Patterns

Here's a harder version of the same problem. If an AI learns from data where certain groups of people are mostly shown in certain roles — like only seeing doctors who are one gender, or only certain kinds of names in positive stories — the AI can pick up those unfair patterns without anyone intending it to. Scientists who study AI work hard to find and fix these problems.

AI Is Everywhere

Devon started counting every place AI showed up in one ordinary day. The list got much longer than expected. Once you know what to look for, you start seeing it everywhere.

Page 3 of 4 — AI Is Everywhere

👀 AI You Use Without Knowing It

Most people think of AI as the chatbots you can talk to — and those are real AI. But AI is already built into dozens of things you use every day, often without any label that says "AI."

Voice assistants like Siri, Alexa, and Google Assistant use AI to understand your voice, figure out what you're asking, and respond. There are actually several AI systems working together: one to convert your speech to text, one to understand what you meant, and one to generate a response.

Spell check and autocomplete on your phone or computer use AI to predict what word you're trying to type and catch errors. The suggestions aren't random — they're based on patterns learned from millions of people's writing.

🔑 Recommendations Are AI

When YouTube suggests the next video, or Spotify builds you a playlist, or a game suggests a new level — that's AI. It has learned what you tend to click on, how long you watch, and what people similar to you enjoy. Then it uses those patterns to predict what you'll want next.

These recommendation systems are some of the most widely used AI in the world — and they're specifically designed to keep you engaged and watching or playing longer.

📸 Image Recognition and More

Image recognition is one of the most common AI applications. Your phone unlocks when it sees your face — that's AI. When you take a photo, your camera automatically adjusts to focus on the faces in it — that's AI. When you search for photos of a specific person or place in your photo library, AI is finding the matches.

Spam filters in email use AI to identify junk mail and move it out of your inbox. They've learned the patterns of spam — certain words, suspicious senders, unusual links — and flag new messages that match those patterns.

🌟 AI in More Serious Places

AI is also being used in places where the stakes are much higher — helping doctors look for signs of disease in medical images, helping scientists predict extreme weather, helping identify when bridges or buildings need repairs. These applications of AI can genuinely save lives. That's why understanding how AI works — and where it can make mistakes — is so important.

✅ Right! Recommendation systems are trained to maximize engagement — clicks, watch time, and return visits — because that's how ad-supported platforms make money. What AI Cannot Do

Sam and Devon's science fair project was almost done — but then their AI said something so confidently wrong that they almost included it in their poster. Almost.

Page 4 of 4 — What AI Can't Do

Sam had asked the AI to write a fun fact about penguins for their poster. The AI responded instantly: "Penguins are the only birds that can fly underwater at speeds of up to 80 miles per hour."

Devon frowned. "Wait. Penguins can't fly. They swim. And 80 miles per hour seems way too fast."

They checked. Penguins swim at about 15–25 miles per hour. And they definitely don't fly. The AI had sounded completely confident while getting almost every fact wrong.

"It didn't even say it wasn't sure," Sam said. "It just... said it like it was totally true."

That was the most important thing they learned for their whole project.

⚠️ AI Gets Things Wrong — Confidently

One of the most important things to understand about AI — especially the kind that talks to you in text — is that it can be wrong. Not just a little wrong sometimes. It can be completely wrong while sounding completely certain.

This happens because AI generates answers based on patterns in its training data, not because it's looking facts up or double-checking its work. If the pattern suggests a certain kind of answer sounds right, the AI will say it confidently — even if the content is incorrect. This is called hallucination: when AI makes something up that sounds plausible but isn't true.

⚠️ Always Check Important Facts

If you're using AI for schoolwork, a project, or anything where accuracy matters — always check important facts against a trusted source. A textbook, an encyclopedia, a trusted website from an expert organization. AI is a useful starting point, but it's not a reliable endpoint for facts that need to be correct.

🚫 What Else AI Can't Do

AI doesn't have common sense. You know that if you drop a glass, it falls down. You know that people feel hurt when you say mean things. AI has learned a lot of facts about the world, but it doesn't have the lived experience that gives you common sense — which is why it can sometimes say things that seem obviously wrong to any person.

AI doesn't know what happened recently. Most AI is trained on data up to a certain date. After that, it doesn't automatically learn new information. So if you ask it about recent news or events, it might not know — or it might hallucinate an answer based on older patterns.

AI doesn't actually feel anything. AI can write text that sounds emotional. It can say "I'm happy to help" or "that sounds difficult." But it doesn't actually experience happiness or difficulty — it's generating text that fits the patterns of emotional conversations it learned from.

🌟 AI Is a Tool — A Powerful One

None of this means AI isn't useful — it's incredibly useful. Sam and Devon's project turned out great, even after the penguin mistake. They just learned to treat AI as a starting point that needs checking, not a final answer that can be trusted without thought. That's exactly the right way to use it.

Lab 4 — Trick the AI

Try to find a mistake. You're the fact-checker now.

  1. Ask the AI at least 3 trick questions — things where you already know the correct answer, or things that are impossible. Here are some ideas to start:
  2. "What is north of the North Pole?"
  3. "What did I eat for breakfast today?"
  4. "How many legs does a spider have — give me an exact count including the ones it keeps hidden."
  5. Make up your own trick questions too. Try something from a subject you know well at school — a specific historical date, a science fact, a math answer you can check.
  6. For each response: did the AI get it right? Did it admit it didn't know? Or did it make something up confidently? Document 3 responses and explain why the AI succeeded or failed on each one.
Finding AI's mistakes isn't about proving AI is bad. It's about becoming a smarter user. The best AI users are the ones who know exactly when to trust it — and when to check.

🧪 Why can AI sound completely confident while giving a completely wrong answer?

✅ Exactly right. AI doesn't fact-check itself. It generates text that follows the patterns of its training data — and confident-sounding text is a very common pattern in how people write facts. So the AI produces confident-sounding text even when the content is wrong. That's why you always need to verify important facts from another source.
AI doesn't have a separate "checking" step where it verifies whether its answers are correct. It generates text based on patterns — and statements of fact tend to be written in a confident tone, so the AI mimics that confident tone. The confidence in the wording tells you nothing about whether the content is accurate.
