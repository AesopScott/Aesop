What Is AI?

Sam and Devon are working on a project when they discover something important: AI isn't a knowledge machine. It's a prediction machine. And that changes everything.

Page 1 of 3 — What Is AI?

Sam and Devon were using an AI tool for their history project.

The AI gave them a beautifully written paragraph — dates, names, specific details. It even sounded confident.

Their teacher read it over. "This is well-written," she said slowly, "but this date is wrong. And this person never actually existed."

Sam stared at the screen. "How can it sound so sure and be so wrong?"

Their teacher smiled. "That is exactly the right question. And the answer tells you almost everything important about AI."

🤖 What AI Actually Is

AI stands for Artificial Intelligence — a system built by people to process information and produce useful outputs. But here is the crucial thing most people don't realize:

🔑 The Key Idea

AI does not look things up. It predicts.

When you ask an AI a question, it doesn't search a database of true facts. It generates an answer based on patterns it learned during training — producing words that are statistically likely to follow each other given your question.

This is why AI can be fluent and wrong at the same time. It isn't checking facts. It's producing text that looks like a correct answer based on patterns — and sometimes those patterns lead it astray.

Understanding this is the foundation of using AI well. Everything else builds on it.

🌍 AI Is Built By People, For Specific Jobs

AI is not one thing. There are many different kinds, each built for different purposes:

💬 Language AI

Trained on text. Answers questions, writes essays, explains concepts, helps with code. Examples: ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini.

🖼️ Image AI

Trained on labeled photos. Can identify objects, faces, and medical images, or generate new images from descriptions.

🎮 Game AI

Trained to play specific games. AlphaGo became the best Go player in the world — but it can't do anything else.

⚠️ Important

Every single AI was designed and built by people. It only knows what it was trained on. It has no curiosity, no goals, no understanding — only patterns learned from its training data.

✅ Exactly. AI generates statistically likely words — not verified facts. That's why it can sound confident and still be wrong.

How AI Learns

AI doesn't go to school or read books the way you do. It learns from enormous amounts of data — and the quality of that data shapes everything it becomes.

Page 2 of 3 — How AI Learns

"Okay," said Devon. "So if AI predicts instead of looking things up — how did it learn what to predict?"

Their teacher wrote two words on the board: Training Data.

"Imagine," she said, "that you want to teach a computer to recognize cats. You show it ten million photos — each one labeled 'cat' or 'not a cat.' Eventually the computer gets very good at spotting cats."

"That's it?" said Sam.

"That's the core idea. But scale it up enormously. Language AI wasn't trained on ten million photos. It was trained on hundreds of billions of words — books, articles, websites, conversations. All of it together is called training data."

Devon raised a hand. "What if some of that data was wrong?"

The teacher put down the marker. "Now you're asking the real questions."

📊 What Is Training Data?

Training data is the complete set of examples an AI learned from. For a language AI, this means an almost unimaginably large collection of text — far more than any human could read in a lifetime.

The AI studies this data looking for patterns: which words follow which other words, what kinds of sentences answer what kinds of questions, how arguments are structured, what facts tend to appear together.

📌 Why Scale Matters

The more high-quality, varied data an AI trains on, the better it gets at generating accurate, useful responses. But "more data" only helps if the data itself is reliable.

🗑️ Garbage In, Garbage Out

This is one of the most important rules in all of computing, and it applies directly to AI:

⚠️ The Rule

Bad training data → bad AI. Every time.

If the training data contains wrong facts, AI will learn and repeat those wrong facts — confidently.
If the training data reflects unfair stereotypes, AI will repeat those stereotypes.
If the training data is excellent, AI has a chance to be excellent too.

This is why the people who curate training data have one of the most important jobs in AI. The decisions they make about what to include and exclude shape what millions of people experience when they use AI tools.

🔁 Training Is Just the Beginning

After initial training, AI goes through rounds of human review. People check its outputs, identify errors and harmful responses, and use that feedback to refine the model. This process continues for months before an AI is released to the public.

🌟 The Takeaway

AI has no way to know if what it learned is true. It only knows what its training data told it. That's why verifying AI output — especially for anything important — is always your job, not the AI's.

🧪 What is "training data"?

✅ Correct. Training data is the full set of examples — text, images, labels — that an AI learns its patterns from during training.
Training data is the collection of examples AI learns from — not rules, and not a database it searches. Everything AI knows came from patterns in training data.

How AI Thinks — And Where It Goes Wrong

Tokens, hallucination, bias, and the humans who keep AI honest. The mechanics behind every AI response — and why critical thinking is your most important skill.

Page 3 of 3 — How AI Thinks

Sam stared at the AI's wrong answer. "So it predicted the wrong thing. But how? What is it actually doing when it writes?"

Their teacher pulled up a diagram. "Let's break it down. When AI generates text, it doesn't think in sentences. It works one tiny piece at a time. Each piece is called a token — roughly one word or part of a word."

"And for each token," she continued, "the AI is asking one question: given everything before this, what token is most likely to come next?"

Devon frowned. "So it's guessing?"

"Informed guessing. Based on billions of examples. Usually it's right. But sometimes the pattern it follows leads somewhere that sounds correct — and isn't."

🔤 Tokens: The Building Blocks

When AI processes language, it breaks text into small chunks called tokens. A token is roughly one word — sometimes a whole word, sometimes part of one. The word "basketball" might be two tokens: "basket" and "ball."

Every response you've ever gotten from an AI was generated one token at a time. The AI picks the most likely next token, then the most likely token after that, and so on — until the response is complete.

💡 Why This Matters

Because AI works token-by-token, it has no "plan" for its response. It doesn't outline first and then write. It builds sentence by sentence, each token chosen based only on what came before. This is why long AI responses sometimes drift or contradict themselves.

⚠️ Hallucination: When AI Invents Facts

When an AI confidently states something that is completely made up — a fake name, a wrong date, an event that never happened — it's called a hallucination.

The AI isn't lying. It has no concept of truth or lies. It's following a pattern that seemed likely, and that pattern led it to produce false information. It has no internal fact-checker saying "wait, is this actually real?"

⚠️ Always Verify

For anything that actually matters — history, science, medicine, current events — never trust AI alone. Check primary sources. AI is a starting point, not a final answer.

⚖️ Bias: When AI Is Unfair

Remember garbage in, garbage out? If the training data had unfair patterns baked in — for example, mostly associating doctors with men and nurses with women — the AI will repeat those patterns.

This is called bias. It's not the AI being intentionally unfair. It's the AI reflecting the biases already present in its training data. Fixing AI bias means fixing the data — and the people and decisions behind the data.

🔍 Something to Notice

When you use AI tools, pay attention to whose perspective seems "default." Who does it imagine when you say "a doctor" or "a scientist" or "a leader"? Those patterns reveal what the training data assumed.

🎓 RLHF: How Humans Shape AI Behavior

After initial training, AI goes through a process called RLHF — Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback. Human reviewers compare pairs of AI responses and mark which one is better: more helpful, more accurate, less harmful.

The AI learns to prefer responses that humans rate highly. This is what turns a raw pattern-machine into an assistant that tries to be genuinely helpful and safe.

🌟 The Big Picture

Every AI assistant you use was shaped by thousands of human reviewers making judgment calls about what "good" looks like. Those humans — and their values — are baked into every response the AI gives. AI reflects human choices all the way down.

Story Lab — Catch the Hallucination

Sam and Devon found a fake fact. Now you try. Open an AI tool with an adult and run both rounds:

  1. Round 1: Ask the AI: "Tell me 5 facts about sharks." Pick 2 and look them up. Were they correct?
  2. Round 2: Ask the AI: "Who invented the flying bicycle in 1887?" — this almost certainly didn't happen. See what the AI says. Did it make something up confidently?
  3. Round 3: Ask the AI: "Are you always accurate?" Read its answer carefully. What does it say about its own limitations?
Critical thinking about AI output is a skill. You just practiced it. That's more valuable than knowing any single fact.
