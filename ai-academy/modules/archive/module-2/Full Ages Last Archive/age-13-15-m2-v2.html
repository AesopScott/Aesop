📖 How Recommendation Algorithms Work

Most of the content you see online — videos, posts, products, music — is decided by recommendation algorithms. These aren't random. They're built to predict what you'll click, watch, or buy.

The Basic Mechanism

The most common approach is called collaborative filtering: the algorithm looks at what you've interacted with, finds other users with similar patterns, and recommends what those users liked. It's not judging the content itself — it's finding people who behave like you and predicting you'll like what they liked.

What It's Optimizing For

Here's the key thing to understand: recommendation algorithms don't try to show you "good" content. They try to show you content that keeps you on the platform. That might mean accurate information — or it might mean content that makes you angry, scared, or outraged, because those emotions drive more engagement than calm informational posts.

Facebook's internal research (revealed in 2021) found that their algorithm was amplifying outrage content because outrage reliably drove clicks and comments. The algorithm wasn't "broken" — it was doing exactly what it was designed to do. The problem was that what it was designed to do wasn't the same as what was good for users.

The Filter Bubble Effect

When algorithms consistently show you content you engage with, they create a "filter bubble" — you see more and more of a certain type of content and less of everything else. Over time, your feed starts to look very different from someone with different initial preferences. Two people can use the same platform and have completely different pictures of what's happening in the world.

📖 AI in Schools: The Debate

When ChatGPT launched in late 2022, schools were thrown into a debate they weren't ready for. Students could suddenly generate essays, solve homework problems, and write code instantly. Should schools ban it, embrace it, or figure out a middle path?

The Ban and the Reversal

New York City schools initially blocked ChatGPT on their networks. Within months, they reversed course. The reason: blocking it at school just meant students used it at home, without any guidance about when it's helpful, when it's harmful to their learning, or how to use it critically. Supervised use — with discussion about what AI can and can't do — was ultimately considered better than unsupervised use.

What Learning Actually Requires

The deeper question is about what school is actually for. If the goal of a writing assignment is to produce a polished essay, AI can help. But if the goal is to practice organizing your thoughts, working through confusion, and developing your voice — then letting AI do the writing defeats the purpose, even if the output looks good.

Cognitive scientists call some kinds of difficulty "desirable" — the struggle of retrieving information from memory, working through a hard problem, writing a draft and revising it — actually builds stronger learning than easier paths. AI tools can shortcut that struggle, which might feel helpful but can undermine what the learning was designed to build.

The Harder Question

If AI can write better essays than most students, what exactly are we measuring when we grade essays? Schools are being forced to rethink not just policies but what education is actually trying to accomplish — and that's a bigger, more important conversation than whether ChatGPT should be blocked.

📖 The COMPAS Algorithm & Criminal Justice

In criminal courts across the US, judges sometimes use a score from a computer algorithm when deciding whether to release someone on bail, how long to sentence them, or whether to recommend parole. One of the most common tools is called COMPAS — and it became the center of a major controversy about algorithmic fairness.

What ProPublica Found

In 2016, investigative journalists at ProPublica analyzed COMPAS scores in one Florida county. They found that Black defendants were nearly twice as likely as white defendants to be incorrectly labeled "high risk" for future crime — meaning the algorithm was more likely to be wrong about Black people in a way that hurt them. Meanwhile, white defendants who later committed crimes were more likely to have been wrongly labeled "low risk."

The Response — and the Twist

The company that made COMPAS pushed back, saying the algorithm was actually fair — and they could show it with different statistics. Here's the strange part: they were both right. Depending on which definition of "fairness" you use, the algorithm was either fair or unfair. There is no mathematical way to satisfy both definitions at the same time when different groups have different rates of reoffending (which themselves reflect historical inequalities in policing and prosecution).

Why This Matters

A person in court who received a high COMPAS score couldn't see how the score was calculated, couldn't challenge the methodology, and may not have even known the algorithm existed. A proprietary tool was making life-altering decisions with no transparency or accountability. This is the pattern that critics of algorithmic governance most worry about: important decisions made by opaque systems with no meaningful way to contest them.

📖 AI in Medicine: Breakthroughs and Limits

AI is changing medicine in remarkable ways — and also revealing how complicated it is to deploy AI systems where mistakes have serious consequences.

AlphaFold: A Real Scientific Breakthrough

For fifty years, scientists couldn't reliably predict the 3D shape of proteins from their chemical makeup — a problem that affects drug development, disease research, and our understanding of life itself. In 2020, DeepMind's AlphaFold solved it at a level of accuracy that stunned the field. They then made the results available to scientists worldwide for free. That decision — to not lock it behind a paywall — meant researchers everywhere could benefit immediately.

When Medical AI Goes Wrong

Early in the COVID-19 pandemic, researchers rushed to build AI tools to detect COVID from chest X-rays. Many studies showed impressive accuracy. But later analysis found a troubling pattern: some of the models had learned the wrong things. Instead of learning what COVID looks like in lungs, some models learned that COVID scans came from specific hospitals with specific equipment — they were detecting the scanner, not the disease. In one case, a model had learned that images containing a ruler (used in some hospitals to show scale) were more likely to be COVID cases. That model would fail completely in a hospital that didn't use rulers.

The Lesson

A model that performs well in testing doesn't always perform well in practice. Medical AI needs rigorous testing across many different hospitals, patient populations, and equipment types — not just the dataset it was trained on. Getting this right matters enormously when the wrong prediction could mean a missed diagnosis.

📖 Algorithmic Management: The Invisible Boss

When you order from DoorDash or request an Uber, there's no manager assigning drivers their next job, setting their pay, or deciding who gets deactivated. An algorithm does all of that — and it raises real questions about how workers are treated when their boss is a piece of software.

How It Works

Gig economy apps use AI to do things like: decide which driver to assign an order to, set prices dynamically based on demand, score drivers on acceptance rates and customer ratings, and — in some cases — deactivate drivers automatically when their scores fall too low. All of this happens in real time, with minimal human review.

What Researchers Found

Sociologist Alex Rosenblat studied Uber drivers and found something interesting: many felt like the app was making their decisions for them. Features like "surge pricing" zones on the map and bonus targets created strong pressure to drive in certain areas or accept certain rides — without the app explicitly telling them what to do. The control was real, but it was designed to feel like free choice.

The Legal Gap

In most places, Uber drivers are classified as "independent contractors" rather than employees. That means the standard protections that come with being an employee — minimum wage guarantees, anti-discrimination law, the right to challenge a firing — largely don't apply. When an algorithm deactivates a driver, they have limited recourse. Courts and governments in the EU and US are now debating whether this classification is accurate given how much control platforms actually exercise.

📖 Deepfakes: How They Work and Why They Matter

A deepfake is a synthetic video, audio, or image created using AI — usually to make someone appear to say or do something they didn't. The technology has been around since 2017 and has become dramatically more realistic and accessible.

How Deepfakes Are Made

Early deepfakes used a technique called a "generative adversarial network" (GAN): two AI systems compete — one generates fake images, the other tries to detect them — and through competition, the generator gets better and better. Modern tools use diffusion models that can generate realistic audio, video, or images from text prompts alone. Voice cloning can now work from just a few seconds of audio.

The Most Common Use — Which Isn't Disinformation

Despite the attention deepfakes get in political contexts, the majority of deepfake content in documented datasets is non-consensual intimate imagery — fake explicit content targeting specific individuals, overwhelmingly women. Many victims are private people, not public figures. This is the most common harm from deepfake technology, and it often gets less coverage than political examples.

Political Deepfakes

In the 2024 US election, an AI-generated recording that sounded like President Biden advised voters not to vote in the New Hampshire primary. In Slovakia, an AI audio clip appeared to show a politician discussing election fraud — released during a legally required media blackout. In both cases, the deepfakes spread quickly through social media before they were identified as fake.

The Harder Problem: The Liar's Dividend

Researchers have identified a secondary problem: as deepfakes become well-known, some people use "it could be a deepfake" as an excuse to dismiss real, authentic videos they don't like. The existence of deepfake technology makes it easier to deny genuine evidence. This "liar's dividend" may ultimately cause as much harm as the deepfakes themselves.

📖 Facial Recognition & Predictive Policing

AI is changing how police work — but not always in ways that are fair or well-governed. Two of the most controversial applications are facial recognition and predictive policing software.

The Robert Williams Case

In 2020, Robert Williams was arrested at his home in front of his family. The police had matched his face to a suspect using facial recognition software. The match was wrong. Williams, who is Black, spent 30 hours in custody before investigators acknowledged the error. He was the first person known to have been wrongfully arrested based on a facial recognition match in the US — and researchers believe his case was identified only because he and his legal team pursued it. Undocumented cases of facial recognition misuse are likely much more common.

Why Error Rates Matter More for Some People

Multiple studies have shown that commercial facial recognition systems have significantly higher error rates for darker-skinned individuals, particularly women. A system that works 99% of the time sounds impressive — until you realize that 1% of errors across millions of scans means thousands of wrong identifications, and if those errors fall disproportionately on Black and brown individuals, the system is encoding discrimination into law enforcement decisions.

Predictive Policing

Some police departments use software that predicts which neighborhoods or individuals are likely to be involved in crime. Critics documented that these systems often created feedback loops: over-policing an area produces more arrests there, which produces more data suggesting crime there, which produces more policing — regardless of actual crime rates. Several cities banned predictive policing after this was documented.

The Accountability Problem

In Robert Williams's case, no individual officer made the facial recognition match — an algorithm did. When an algorithm makes a wrong decision that sends someone to jail, it's often unclear who is accountable, and the algorithm's methodology may be proprietary and unavailable to defense attorneys. This is a fundamental problem for democratic governance of AI in high-stakes contexts.

Lesson 1: AI You See Every Day

Voice assistants, recommendations, autocorrect — and how the invisible AI layer around you actually works.

Zara and her friend Marcus are watching videos online. They share an account on the same platform — but when Marcus logs into his own account later, his recommendations are completely different. Same platform, same day, but totally different content. Zara's feed is full of art tutorials and indie music. Marcus's is all sports highlights and gaming. They're living in the same world but seeing very different pictures of it.

This isn't an accident. The algorithm has built a different version of the platform for each of them, based on everything they've ever watched, clicked, and scrolled past. It's trying to predict what will keep each of them watching — and it's gotten very good at it.

The AI Stack Around You

Most of your digital life is shaped by AI systems you never directly interact with. Your phone keyboard predicts your next word using a language model trained on millions of texts. Your email filters spam using a classifier. Your streaming service picks what to suggest using an algorithm that tracks your behavior and compares it to millions of other users.

These systems aren't neutral — they're optimized for a goal. Usually that goal is keeping you on the platform as long as possible. That might lead them to show you great content. It might also lead them to show you content that makes you angry or scared, because those emotions keep people scrolling even when calm or happy content would better serve their actual interests.

🔑 The Objective Function

Every AI system is built to optimize for something. That something is called its "objective function." A recommendation algorithm optimizing for watch time will behave differently than one optimizing for user satisfaction or time well spent. Understanding what an AI is optimizing for tells you a lot about why it behaves the way it does.

Recommendation Systems: How They Actually Work

The most common approach to recommendations is called collaborative filtering. Instead of analyzing what content is "good," the algorithm finds users who behave like you — similar watch history, similar click patterns — and recommends what they liked. It's essentially saying: "People like you enjoyed this, so you probably will too."

This creates powerful recommendations — but it also creates filter bubbles. The algorithm keeps showing you more of what you already engage with, which gradually narrows what you see. You might end up in a corner of the internet that looks very different from what other people are seeing, without ever choosing to go there.

🌟 Ask Yourself

Try this: look at your recommendations right now on any platform. Can you see the pattern of what the algorithm thinks you are? Is it accurate? Is there anything you notice that you didn't choose but the platform keeps showing you anyway?

Ambient AI: The Systems You Don't Notice

Beyond recommendations, AI runs quietly in the background of nearly everything digital. Smart speakers like Alexa or Google Home listen for wake words — which means there's always a microphone active and a model running locally on the device. Traffic apps like Waze or Google Maps build real-time traffic models from the GPS signals of every active phone on the road. Credit card fraud detection analyzes every transaction in milliseconds against patterns of fraudulent behavior.

None of these announce themselves. They're part of the infrastructure — which is exactly why it's worth thinking about. When AI is invisible, it's easy to forget that someone designed it, chose what it optimizes for, and made decisions about whose data it collects and how it's used.

Lesson 2: AI at School and at Play

Educational AI tools, AI in sports and games — and the question of what "help" actually helps.

Jordan has a history essay due tomorrow. She opens an AI tool, types in the prompt, and reads the result. It's actually pretty good — better organized than what she usually writes. She feels a flash of uncertainty: if she submits it, is she cheating? If she edits it and makes it "hers," is it still cheating? And if she doesn't use it, knowing everyone else probably will, is she just disadvantaging herself?

Her teacher hasn't said anything about AI. The school policy says "no plagiarism," but doesn't mention AI tools. Jordan's sitting with a question that millions of students are sitting with right now — and nobody has given them a clear answer.

AI in Education: A Moving Target

Schools are still figuring out how to handle AI tools, and the policies are all over the map. Some teachers ban them entirely. Some encourage them. Many haven't addressed them at all. The lack of clarity puts students like Jordan in a genuinely confusing position.

The clearest thing that educators agree on is this: using AI in ways that prevent you from developing skills you'll actually need is a bad trade, even if it feels helpful in the short term. If you use AI to write every essay, you might get better grades — but you may not develop the ability to think through arguments, organize ideas, or find your own voice as a writer. Those skills matter beyond school.

🔑 The Struggle That Actually Helps

Research in learning science shows that struggling with difficult tasks — not having the answer handed to you — actually builds stronger learning. When you have to retrieve information from memory, organize your own thoughts, or work through confusion, your brain builds stronger pathways than when you just read someone else's answer. This is called "desirable difficulty." AI that makes learning frictionless might feel helpful but can actually undermine the parts that build real skill.

AI in Sports and Games

AI has transformed professional sports. Teams use AI to analyze player performance, predict injuries before they happen, scout opponents, and even optimize in-game strategy in real time. The statistical revolution started in baseball — documented in Moneyball — has spread to every major professional league. Today, AI doesn't just advise coaches; it shapes which players get contracts and for how much.

In gaming, AI has reached superhuman levels at Chess, Go, StarCraft, and many other games — not by copying how humans play, but by discovering entirely new strategies that humans had never thought of. When DeepMind's AlphaGo beat the world champion in 2016, it played moves that experts called "alien" — valid and brilliant, but coming from a completely different kind of intelligence.

🌟 What Does "Help" Actually Help?

A chess player who uses an AI to win games hasn't become a better chess player. A student who uses AI to write their essay hasn't become a better writer. But a chess player who uses AI to study their mistakes and understand why they lost can improve dramatically. The difference is whether you're using AI to skip the process or to deepen it.

Lesson 3: AI in Our Community

Traffic systems, emergency services, local government — and what happens when community decisions are made by algorithms nobody can see.

Devon's city just installed a new AI traffic system. Average commute times have dropped. But Devon's neighborhood — which is on the edge of town, mostly lower-income — now has freight trucks routing through it at all hours, because the AI found that it was the most efficient path. The people who benefit from shorter commutes aren't the same people dealing with the noise, exhaust, and wear on neighborhood streets.

When Devon's mom goes to a city council meeting to ask who decided this, she finds out the routing decisions are made automatically by the traffic management software. Nobody at city hall made this specific choice — the algorithm did. Asking it to change requires a vendor contract negotiation. Nobody on the council knows how to do that.

AI in Local Government

AI systems are being deployed in local services across many cities: traffic management, pothole reporting and repair prioritization, permit processing, 911 call routing, and more. Many of these applications genuinely improve efficiency. But they also shift how decisions get made — from human officials who can be held accountable to automated systems whose reasoning is often invisible to the public.

The problem Devon's mom ran into has a name: the accountability gap. When a human official makes a bad decision, there's a process for challenging it — attend a meeting, write a letter, vote them out. When an algorithm makes a bad decision, that process doesn't work the same way. The decision was made by code; the code belongs to a private company; the contract limits what the city can change.

🔑 Who Is Accountable?

Democratic accountability means that people can understand decisions that affect them and have some way to challenge or change those decisions. When AI makes the decision, the chain of accountability gets broken. The city council didn't decide — the algorithm did. The algorithm belongs to a vendor. The vendor's code is proprietary. At each step, it gets harder to answer the question: who do we talk to about this?

AI in Criminal Justice

Some of the most controversial AI applications in communities involve the justice system. Algorithms like COMPAS are used in courts to assess how likely someone is to reoffend. Predictive policing software directs patrol cars to certain areas based on historical crime data. Facial recognition is used to identify suspects.

These tools raise the same accountability question in a much higher-stakes context. If a traffic algorithm routes trucks badly, it's a quality-of-life problem. If an algorithm incorrectly labels someone as high-risk and that affects their bail or sentencing, it's a question of freedom. And if the algorithm's criteria are proprietary and can't be disclosed to defense attorneys, the defendant has almost no ability to challenge it.

🌟 Aggregate vs. Individual Fairness

A system that performs well "on average" can still treat specific communities unfairly. An AI that routes traffic more efficiently on average might still be routing most of the burden through lower-income neighborhoods. An AI that predicts reoffending accurately in aggregate might still be much more likely to be wrong about Black defendants. Looking at average performance hides these distributional problems. You have to ask: accurate for whom?

Lesson 4: AI in Medicine and Science

Diagnosis, drug discovery, climate modeling — and why impressive AI results don't always mean what they look like.

A hospital system announces it's using AI to screen chest X-rays for early signs of pneumonia. The AI was tested on a dataset of 100,000 X-rays and achieved 94% accuracy — better than many radiologists. The hospital is excited. Patients should be caught earlier. Lives should be saved.

Six months after deployment, a doctor notices something odd: the AI seems to perform much better on X-rays from certain imaging machines than others. A researcher investigates and discovers the problem — the AI had learned that images from one type of scanner were more commonly associated with pneumonia in the training data. It wasn't detecting pneumonia in the lungs. It was detecting which scanner the hospital used. In a new hospital with different equipment, its accuracy drops significantly.

When AI Learns the Wrong Thing

The scenario above is based on real patterns documented in medical AI research. AI systems sometimes learn "shortcuts" — statistical signals that predict the label in the training data but aren't the actual feature that matters. A model trained to detect malignant skin lesions was found in one study to be partially detecting the presence of a ruler placed next to suspicious lesions, because clinicians tend to photograph suspicious-looking spots with a ruler for scale — so images with rulers were more likely to be malignant in the training data.

This isn't about AI being bad — it's about AI doing exactly what it was trained to do. It found the statistical patterns that predicted the label. The problem is that the patterns it found weren't the ones we actually care about, and nobody caught it during testing because the test set had the same shortcut available.

🔑 Shortcut Learning

When an AI learns a spurious correlation rather than the real underlying feature, it's called "shortcut learning." The model performs well in testing but fails in deployment when the shortcut isn't present. In medical AI, catching shortcut learning requires testing across diverse hospitals, equipment types, and patient populations — not just one dataset from one place.

Real Breakthroughs: What AI Has Changed

Despite the risks, AI has produced genuine breakthroughs in science and medicine. AlphaFold solved protein structure prediction — a problem that had stumped biologists for fifty years — and DeepMind released the results freely, enabling researchers worldwide to accelerate drug discovery and disease research. AI climate models are helping scientists understand how greenhouse gas changes translate into temperature patterns with more speed and precision than was previously possible. AI tools in radiology, when properly validated across diverse patient populations, have demonstrated real clinical benefits in detecting early signs of certain cancers.

The difference between breakthroughs and failures often comes down to validation — how carefully the AI system was tested before deployment, across how many different settings, and with how much critical scrutiny for the ways it might be wrong.

🌟 The Validation Question

For any AI medical tool, the most important question isn't "how accurate was it in the study?" It's "how was it tested, on what kind of patients, in what settings, and was anyone looking for how it could fail?" Good performance in one dataset doesn't mean good performance in the world. The world is messier and more varied than any training set.

Lesson 5: AI in Business and Work

Automation, customer service, productivity — and what AI is changing about how work works.

Sam's older sister Maya just graduated and is working as a delivery driver while she figures out her next move. She's frustrated. The app controls which deliveries she gets, how much she earns, and what her "score" is. Last week her score dropped — she never found out why. When she contacted support, she got a form email. There's no number to call, no manager to talk to, no way to actually explain what happened. She's being managed entirely by software.

Meanwhile, Sam's mom just started using an AI writing assistant at her office job. It's genuinely helpful — it drafts emails, summarizes documents, helps her prepare for meetings. She finishes her work faster. But she's also starting to wonder: if AI can do 40% of what she does, what does her value to the company look like in five years?

Algorithmic Management

In gig economy apps, AI doesn't just route orders — it manages the workforce. Uber, DoorDash, Instacart, and Amazon Flex all use algorithms to decide which driver or shopper gets which job, how much they're paid, and what happens if their performance metrics fall. When things go wrong for a worker, there's often no human to appeal to — just an automated system with limited recourse.

This matters especially because most gig workers are classified as "independent contractors" rather than employees. That classification means minimum wage, overtime, anti-discrimination protections, and the right to contest a "firing" — all of which apply to employees — don't apply. The algorithm has a lot of power over these workers, but the legal protections are minimal.

🔑 The Classification Problem

If a company has significant control over when, where, and how you work, labor law traditionally considers you an employee with the protections that brings. Platform companies argue that because gig workers set their own schedules, they're independent contractors. Courts and governments in the EU, UK, and some US states are challenging this. How it gets resolved will affect the employment model for millions of workers.

AI and White-Collar Work

Generative AI tools are changing knowledge work. Studies consistently show that workers who adopt tools like Copilot, ChatGPT, or similar products get more done — sometimes significantly more. The biggest gains tend to be for workers who were previously less productive; AI seems to bring their output closer to the level of top performers. This is a real productivity story.

The harder question is what happens to that productivity gain. If AI makes each worker 30% more productive, companies might hire 30% fewer workers, or pay existing workers more, or charge customers less, or keep the gains as profit. Which one happens depends on factors like labor market conditions and collective bargaining power — not on the technology itself.

🌟 Technology vs. Policy

Previous waves of automation — looms, assembly lines, computers — did eventually create new jobs and prosperity, but the transition was often brutal for people in the specific jobs that were automated. AI is moving faster than previous transitions, and it's affecting cognitive work rather than just physical labor. The question of how to distribute the gains is a policy question, not a technical one — and it needs to be answered now, while the transition is still happening.

Lesson 6: AI in Art, Music, and Culture

Generative media, deepfakes, cultural impact — and some genuinely hard questions about creativity, consent, and authenticity.

Priya loves art. She's been drawing since she was five. She enters a local art competition and loses to an AI-generated image. The creator used a text prompt to generate it and spent maybe fifteen minutes selecting and refining. Priya spent thirty hours on her piece. She's furious. And then she's confused: the image really is beautiful. Does that mean it's not art? Does it mean she wasted her time? Does it matter how something was made if the result moves people?

Her friend points out something she hadn't thought about: the AI that made the winning piece was trained on millions of images — including works by artists who didn't consent to their work being used as training data and who get nothing when the AI reproduces their style. Is that fair?

AI and Creative Work: The Consent Question

Generative AI tools for images, music, and text were trained on massive amounts of human creative work scraped from the internet. The people who created those works — artists, photographers, musicians, writers — generally weren't asked and don't get compensated when their work is used as training data or when AI generates something "in their style."

Artists and photographers have filed lawsuits against AI companies, arguing this is copyright infringement. The legal outcome is genuinely unsettled — copyright law wasn't designed with AI training at scale in mind. What's not legally unsettled is the fairness question most people have an intuition about: if your creative work is what taught the AI to make things that compete with you, something seems wrong about that, regardless of what the courts eventually decide.

🔑 Style vs. Expression

Copyright traditionally doesn't protect style — you can paint in the style of Van Gogh without permission. But AI trained on thousands of a single artist's images can produce outputs that are very specifically in their style, almost indistinguishably. Where does "inspired by" end and something more problematic begin? That line is genuinely contested, and the courts are only beginning to address it.

Deepfakes and Trust

Deepfakes — synthetic videos, audio, and images created using AI — started as a tool for harmless parody and entertainment but quickly expanded into more harmful uses. The majority of deepfake content documented in research is non-consensual intimate imagery targeting real people, overwhelmingly women. Political deepfakes have also appeared in multiple elections — AI-generated audio or video falsely depicting candidates saying things they never said.

There's also a subtler problem called the liar's dividend: as deepfakes become well-known, some people use "it might be a deepfake" to dismiss authentic evidence they don't like. The existence of the technology makes it easier to deny real things. A politician caught on video doing something wrong can now claim the video is AI-generated, and a skeptical enough audience may accept that claim.

🌟 What Makes Something "Real" Art?

Priya's question — does it matter how something was made? — doesn't have a clean answer. Different people draw the line in different places. A photograph was once considered "not real art" because the camera did the work. Computer-generated graphics faced the same skepticism. The tools change; the questions about creativity, effort, and meaning keep coming back. What matters to you when you decide whether something counts?

Lesson 7: AI in Government and Infrastructure

Surveillance, policing, smart cities, public policy — and what happens to democracy when government runs on algorithms.

Marcus is walking through downtown when a police officer stops him. They say there's been a robbery nearby and their facial recognition system flagged a match. Marcus didn't rob anything. He's never been in any trouble. But the system matched his face to a suspect. He has to prove he wasn't there — which turns out to be harder than it sounds, because the robbery happened at a time he was alone at home.

Later, Marcus finds out that the system used by this police department has a much higher error rate for Black men than for other groups. The company that makes the system says this is known but the department used it anyway. Nobody reviewed the algorithmic match before sending the officer to stop him. There's no form to fill out to challenge the algorithm. There's no one to call.

AI in Policing: High Stakes, Low Accountability

Facial recognition, predictive policing algorithms, and automated surveillance systems are being deployed by law enforcement across the US and internationally — often without public disclosure, without independent auditing, and with limited oversight. The stakes are very high: these systems affect people's liberty, safety, and freedom from police contact.

Robert Williams's 2020 wrongful arrest in Detroit was the first documented case of wrongful arrest caused by facial recognition in the US. But researchers believe undocumented cases are much more common — cases where facial recognition shaped a police investigation that never led to formal charges and left no paper trail. The documented cases represent only the visible surface of a larger pattern.

🔑 Deployment Before Governance

A recurring pattern in AI: the technology gets deployed widely before there are clear rules governing it. Facial recognition was in use by hundreds of US law enforcement agencies before any significant regulation existed. By the time policy attention arrived, the systems had users, contracts, and established practices — making them harder to regulate or remove. The window for shaping how technology is used is before it reaches scale.

AI in Public Benefits and Administration

Beyond policing, AI systems increasingly affect access to housing, healthcare, education, and government benefits. Algorithms score creditworthiness, assess welfare eligibility, flag potential fraud, and prioritize social services. These applications can improve efficiency — but they also concentrate the risk of algorithmic error on the populations who can least afford to challenge mistakes.

In the Netherlands, a government benefits fraud detection algorithm called SyRI was struck down by a court in 2020 for violating human rights. The court found it disproportionately targeted low-income and minority communities, operated on secret criteria, and gave people no way to contest their scores. The same problems — targeting, opacity, no appeal — appear repeatedly across AI systems used in government administration worldwide.

🌟 The Pattern to Watch For

Look for this combination: a high-stakes decision that affects someone's life, made by an AI system, operated by a powerful institution, with little transparency about how it works and limited ways to challenge the outcome. When all of those are present together, the accountability problem is serious. The people most affected are usually those who already have the least power to push back.

The AI systems being deployed in government today — in policing, in benefits administration, in social services — are making decisions that matter enormously to real people. They're not inherently bad. Some of them genuinely help. But the pattern of "deploy now, govern later" has already caused real harm, and the communities most affected are often those with the least ability to challenge what's happening to them.

Understanding this pattern is the first step toward being someone who can change it — whether as a future voter, advocate, policymaker, or technologist. The decisions being made right now about AI in government will shape the relationship between citizens and institutions for a long time.
