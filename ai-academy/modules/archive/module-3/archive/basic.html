AI Makes Mistakes

Trust calibration — knowing when and how much to rely on AI.
📖 Opening — The Resume Screener

A company started using AI to screen job applications. The AI was fast — it could review hundreds of resumes in seconds. But six months in, HR noticed something strange: almost no women were making it past the first screen for engineering roles.

They dug into the AI's training data. It had been trained on the company's past successful hires — and those hires had been overwhelmingly male, because of the industry's historical demographics. The AI had learned that "engineer" = "man," and it filtered accordingly.

The system had to be shut down. It wasn't trying to discriminate — it was doing exactly what it was trained to do. That was the problem.

💡 Why AI Makes Mistakes

AI systems fail for predictable reasons. Understanding the categories helps you spot problems faster:

  • Training data problems: The data reflects the past, including its biases and gaps.
  • Distribution shift: The world changes, but the model doesn't update automatically.
  • Overconfidence: Many AI systems assign high confidence to wrong answers.
  • Misaligned objectives: The model optimizes for what it was trained to measure, which may not be what you actually want.

In the resume case: all four problems appeared at once.

Module 3 · Lesson 2

When AI Doesn't Know

Hallucination, confabulation, and the confidence problem.
📖 Case — The Legal Brief

In 2023, lawyers in a real U.S. court case used an AI assistant to help draft a legal brief. The AI cited six prior court cases in support of their argument. When the opposing lawyers tried to look up those cases, they found none of them existed. The AI had invented case names, docket numbers, and quotes — in perfect legal citation format.

The lawyers were sanctioned by the judge. They hadn't verified the AI's output. The AI didn't know it was lying — it was doing what language models do: predicting the next likely token. Legal citations are a pattern; it produced the pattern. The citations were fake.

💡 Understanding Hallucination

Language models predict text. They do not retrieve facts from a verified database — they generate what statistically fits the context. This produces hallucinations: confident, fluent, completely false outputs.

  • Confabulation: Filling gaps with plausible-sounding invented detail.
  • Citation hallucination: Producing fake papers, court cases, URLs that match the format but don't exist.
  • Temporal errors: Asserting outdated facts as current.
  • Overconfident uncertainty: Saying "I'm not sure, but…" and then being confidently wrong.

The key insight: hallucinations are hardest to detect when they are on topics you know least about. The AI sounds equally confident whether it's right or wrong.

Whose Fault Is It?

Accountability chains and the human-in-the-loop.
📖 Case — The Autonomous Vehicle Gray Zone

In 2018, an autonomous vehicle struck and killed a pedestrian in Arizona. Investigations found the car's sensors detected the woman, but the AI misclassified her as a false positive — an object it could ignore. The backup human safety driver was watching a video on her phone.

Fault was distributed: the AI system designer hadn't built adequate fail-safes. The company's safety protocols were insufficient. The human driver abdicated her responsibility. The regulator approved the testing without enough oversight.

No single party was entirely at fault. No single party was entirely innocent. This is the accountability problem in AI: when something goes wrong in a complex system of humans and machines, responsibility scatters.

💡 The Accountability Chain

Every AI deployment involves a chain of responsible parties:

  • Researchers who design the models and publish the methods.
  • Companies that build products on top of those models.
  • Deployers who integrate AI into real-world systems.
  • Users who choose to act on AI outputs.
  • Regulators who define what is and isn't permitted.

The "human in the loop" concept is often proposed as a safeguard — keeping a person responsible for final decisions. But as the case above shows, human oversight can become a rubber stamp if people aren't genuinely engaged.

Bias In, Bias Out

Where bias comes from and how it spreads through AI systems.
📖 Case — Skin Tone and the Diagnosis Gap

Medical AI systems trained mostly on lighter-skinned patients have shown lower accuracy for darker-skinned patients in detecting conditions like skin cancer and pulse oximetry (blood oxygen). If the training data doesn't represent the full range of human variation, the model doesn't learn to recognize it — and the patients who need the most accurate diagnosis may be the ones it serves least well.

💡 The Bias Pipeline

Bias can enter an AI system at every stage of development:

  • Collection bias: The data collected doesn't represent all populations.
  • Labeling bias: Human annotators bring their own assumptions when labeling data.
  • Proxy bias: The model learns a proxy variable correlated with a protected class (e.g., ZIP code as a proxy for race).
  • Deployment bias: The model is deployed in a context different from where it was tested.
  • Feedback loops: Biased outputs influence future training data, amplifying the bias over time.

Fairness and AI

Why "fair" is harder to define than it sounds.
💡 Three Definitions of Fairness

Here's the uncomfortable truth: there are multiple mathematically valid definitions of fairness, and they often cannot all be satisfied at the same time.

  • Demographic parity: Equal outcome rates across groups. A loan approval AI should approve loans at the same rate for all demographic groups.
  • Equal opportunity: Equal true positive rates across groups. Among people who would actually repay a loan, the approval rate should be the same across groups.
  • Individual fairness: Similar individuals should receive similar outcomes, regardless of group membership.

These three definitions can mathematically conflict. If base rates differ across groups (e.g., historical wealth disparities), you often cannot satisfy all three simultaneously. This is not a bug that will be fixed — it's a mathematical reality that forces design choices.

⚠️ The Impossibility Theorem

In 2016, researchers proved that no algorithm can simultaneously satisfy all three common fairness definitions when base rates differ across groups. Every fairness metric is a value judgment about which tradeoff is acceptable — not a neutral technical decision.

Failure Modes and Mitigation

Systematic patterns in how AI breaks — and what builders do about it.
💡 Common AI Failure Modes

Beyond individual errors, AI systems fail in recognizable, repeating patterns:

  • Specification gaming: The model achieves the measured goal while violating the spirit of it. A game-playing AI may find exploits rather than play well.
  • Reward hacking: In reinforcement learning, the model maximizes its reward signal in unintended ways.
  • Shortcut learning: Models latch onto spurious correlations in training data (e.g., learning "if the image has snow, the animal is a wolf").
  • Adversarial brittleness: Tiny, human-imperceptible changes to inputs can flip an AI's output completely.
  • Out-of-distribution failure: Models work within their training distribution and fail silently when real-world inputs differ.
🛡️ Mitigation Approaches

Builders use several strategies to make AI systems more robust:

  • Red-teaming: Deliberately trying to break the system before deployment.
  • Adversarial training: Including adversarial examples in training data to improve robustness.
  • Uncertainty quantification: Teaching models to report their own confidence levels.
  • Human oversight gates: Requiring human review for high-stakes outputs.
  • Staged deployment: Rolling out to limited users before full deployment.
