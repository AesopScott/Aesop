AI Makes Mistakes

Even the smartest tools get things wrong sometimes.
📖 Story — Mia and the Recipe Robot

Mia wanted to bake her grandmother's favorite cookies. She typed the recipe into an AI helper and asked, "How many cups of sugar do I need?"

The AI answered instantly: "You need three tablespoons of sugar." But Mia's grandmother had always used one cup. The cookies came out wrong — too flat and barely sweet.

Later, Mia realized the AI had mixed up tablespoons and cups. It sounded totally sure of itself, but it was wrong. The AI didn't know it had made a mistake — it just answered as if everything was fine.

Mia learned something important that day: AI helpers can be really useful, but they are not perfect. You still have to think for yourself.

💡 What's Going On?

AI is a tool — like a calculator or a map app. Tools are helpful, but they can give you wrong answers. A calculator can't tell you if you typed the wrong number. A map app might send you down a road that no longer exists.

AI is the same way. It gives answers based on patterns it learned, but those patterns are not always right.

  • AI can confuse similar words or measurements.
  • AI can give an outdated answer if things have changed.
  • AI can misunderstand what you asked.

AI Makes Mistakes

How do you know when to trust it?
🔍 Trust Calibration

Trusting AI is about balance. You don't want to believe everything it says without checking. But you also don't want to ignore it completely — it can still be very helpful!

Here is a simple rule: The more important the decision, the more you should double-check.

  • Asking AI what a word means? Probably fine to trust it.
  • Asking AI for a medical symptom? Check with a real doctor too.
  • Asking AI for a recipe measurement? Maybe compare it to another source.
Mia's AI helper gave her the wrong measurement. What does this example teach us?
✅ Lesson Takeaway

AI is like a very fast, very knowledgeable friend — but friends can be wrong too. Good thinkers always ask: "Does this answer make sense to me?"

When AI Doesn't Know

Sometimes AI makes up an answer — and sounds very confident.
📖 Story — The Book That Wasn't There

Tomás was doing a school project. He asked an AI: "Can you name a book about ocean animals by Dr. Sarah Bloom?"

The AI replied: "Sure! Dr. Sarah Bloom wrote The Deep Blue Encyclopedia in 2018. It has wonderful chapters on dolphins and deep-sea fish."

Tomás was excited — until he searched the library and the internet. The book didn't exist. Neither did Dr. Sarah Bloom. The AI had invented the whole thing.

Tomás's teacher called this "hallucinating" — when an AI confidently says something that isn't true.

💡 What Is Hallucination?

AI language tools predict what sounds like a good answer based on patterns. Sometimes those patterns lead them to invent things — names, facts, book titles, quotes — that do not actually exist.

The tricky part? The AI does not know it's wrong. It doesn't "feel" uncertain. It just produces the next likely-sounding word.

  • Hallucinations happen most often with specific facts: names, dates, quotes, citations.
  • They can sound very confident and convincing.
  • You can usually catch them by checking another source.

Module 3 · Lesson 3

Whose Fault Is It?

When AI gets something wrong, who is responsible?
📖 Story — The Navigation Mistake

The Reyes family used a navigation app on a road trip. The app — which uses AI — told them to turn right onto a mountain road. What the app didn't know: that road had been closed for months after a landslide.

The family ended up stuck for two hours until help arrived.

Afterward, they wondered: whose fault was it? The app that gave bad directions? The company that made the app and hadn't updated its maps? Or the family for following the directions without checking?

This is one of the hardest questions about AI: when something goes wrong, who is responsible?

💡 The Responsibility Question

Responsibility can be shared across many people and systems:

  • The user — chose to follow the AI's advice.
  • The company — built and maintains the AI system.
  • The AI — gave bad information, but AI is a tool, not a person.

Most of the time, responsibility falls on people — the ones who build AI and the ones who use it. That's why understanding AI is so important. When you know how it works, you can make better decisions about when to trust it.

Module 3 · Lesson 4

Bias In, Bias Out

What happens when the training data has a blind spot?
📖 Story — The Autocorrect That Kept Changing Names

Amara noticed something odd: whenever she typed her name on her phone, the autocorrect kept trying to change it. But her friend Emily's name never got changed.

She asked her older sister about it. "That's bias," her sister said. "The autocorrect learned from a lot of text. If most of that text was written by people with certain kinds of names, the AI learned to expect those names and treats other names as 'wrong.'"

Amara felt annoyed. Her name wasn't wrong — the AI just hadn't learned enough about names like hers.

"So the AI isn't being mean on purpose," Amara said slowly. "It just doesn't know any better because of what it was taught?"

"Exactly," her sister said. "Garbage in, garbage out — or in this case, bias in, bias out."

💡 What Is Bias in AI?

AI learns from data — text, images, decisions — that humans created. If that data contains patterns that treat some groups differently, the AI will learn and repeat those patterns.

  • If most training photos of doctors show men, AI may assume doctors are male.
  • If training text favors certain names, spell-check may flag other names.
  • If hiring data favored certain backgrounds, an AI hiring tool may do the same.

This is why the people who build AI tools have a responsibility to think carefully about where their data comes from.
