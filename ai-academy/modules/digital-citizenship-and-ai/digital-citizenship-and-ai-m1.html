Who Are You Online With AI? · Introduction

Your identity has a digital shadow — and AI just made it permanent.

Everything you post, share, or say online is now raw material for systems that never forget.

In October 2022, a thirteen-year-old in New Jersey posted a short video on TikTok mocking a classmate's haircut. She sent it only to her close friends — maybe eight people. Within 48 hours it had been screenshotted, shared to a group chat of 200 students, and clipped into a longer "drama compilation" that reached thousands. The girl in the video never consented to any of it. Neither did the poster, really. By the time adults got involved, the original video had been deleted, but copies existed in at least a dozen places, some indexed by Google. No one planned any of this. There was no villain. The system just worked the way systems work.

That incident happened before AI image tools, AI voice cloners, and AI-generated text became widely available to anyone with a phone. Now those tools exist, and they work on twelve-year-olds the same way they work on celebrities. A photo can be aged, altered, or placed in a context that never happened. A voice can be cloned from a ten-second clip. A writing style can be mimicked well enough to fool a teacher or a parent. The rules of the game changed faster than most adults had time to notice — which means a lot of people your age are navigating this with zero guidance from people who actually understand it.

This course is about giving you that understanding. Not rules to follow blindly, but a real mental model for what is happening when you post, when you share, when you let an AI app access your photos, and when someone else does all of those things involving you without asking. Four lessons. Each one opens with something that actually happened. By the end, you will be able to look at any situation involving your identity and AI and know exactly which questions to ask. That is worth more than any list of dos and don'ts.

Who Are You Online With AI? · Lesson 1 of 4

The Post Nobody Meant to Go Viral

How ordinary moments become permanent records — and why that changes everything about how you present yourself online.
If you never intended something to spread, are you still responsible for what it becomes?

On a Saturday afternoon in March 2012, a seventeen-year-old named Rehtaeh Parsons attended a small house party in Cole Harbour, Nova Scotia. What happened there — and what happened in the months that followed — would become one of the most discussed cases involving digital sharing and youth identity in Canadian history. A photograph taken at the party was shared over text message. Then it spread. By Monday morning, students across multiple schools had seen it. Rehtaeh, who said she had been assaulted, now faced a second ordeal: classmates sending the image to each other on their phones, people laughing at her in hallways, messages calling her names. The photograph moved through social networks the way a rumor moves through a cafeteria, except a rumor fades. The photograph did not fade. It was copied, re-sent, downloaded. Rehtaeh died by suicide in April 2013. She was seventeen.

This is an extreme case. Most of what happens with digital sharing is far less severe. But the mechanism — the way an image or post or video moves beyond the control of the person it involves — is the same mechanism operating every day, in every school, in every group chat. What Rehtaeh's case made undeniable is that the rules of spreading content do not care about intent. The first person who forwarded that image may have thought it was just gossip. By the thousandth forward, the original moment of sharing had been replaced by something enormous and unstoppable. Nobody planned it. The system just amplified what it was given.

That was 2012. The content was a photograph taken on a phone. By 2024, AI tools can take a single photo and generate dozens of variations, place a face into different scenes, or animate a still image. The amplification engine got much bigger. The content that feeds it got much easier to create. Everything else about the mechanism stayed exactly the same.

Section 1 — What "Going Viral" Actually Is

Most people think "going viral" is about fame or popularity. It is really about a specific feature of how digital networks are built. When you share something online, you are not handing someone a physical object that only they can hold. You are making a copy. The person you share it with can make another copy. Each copy can generate more copies. There is no original that gets "used up." There is no distance the copy has to travel. A message sent at midnight can reach ten thousand people before breakfast the next day.

Researchers who study how information spreads online call this property scalability — meaning the thing being shared can scale up to any size without friction. A rumor whispered between two people is not scalable. It takes time, requires presence, and changes slightly with each telling. A screenshot is perfectly scalable: identical, instant, unlimited.

Scalability The ability of information to be copied and spread to an unlimited number of people without degrading or requiring effort — a core feature of digital content that does not exist in the same way for physical objects or spoken words.

The second feature is persistence. Physical things decay. Spoken words vanish when the sound wave dies. Digital files don't behave that way. Once something has been saved — even once, by anyone — it can survive indefinitely. Even when platforms delete content, cached versions, screenshots, and backups exist in places neither the original poster nor the platform controls. Snapchat's "disappearing" photos generated a cottage industry of third-party apps designed specifically to save them before they vanished. "Delete" on a social platform means "remove from view here." It does not mean "erase from the world."

Persistence The tendency of digital content to survive indefinitely, regardless of deletion attempts, because copies can be made and stored anywhere by anyone who has seen it.

Scalability plus persistence is a combination that has no real equivalent in human history before the internet. Understanding this combination — not just knowing the words but really feeling the implication — is the first thing that separates people who navigate digital life well from people who get blindsided by it.

Section 2 — The Context Collapse Problem

In November 2013, a communications researcher named danah boyd published a book called It's Complicated: The Social Lives of Networked Teens. She had spent eight years interviewing teenagers across the United States about how they used social media, and she identified something she called context collapse. The concept is simple but profound: when you post something online, you are technically sending it to a single place, but that place is seen by people from completely different social contexts simultaneously.

Think about how you talk differently in different situations. The way you joke with your best friends is different from how you talk to a teacher. The stories you tell at a family dinner are different from what you say in a group chat. Those differences are not fake — they are normal. Every person you know also does this. It is called audience management and you have been doing it your whole life without thinking about it.

Context collapse When content created for one specific audience (close friends, for example) is seen by multiple different audiences at the same time, collapsing the social context it was meant for — often with unintended consequences.

Social media platforms collapse all those different audiences into one. When you post to Instagram, your closest friends see it, but so do acquaintances, older relatives, people you barely know from school, and anyone those people might forward it to. You wrote for one audience; it landed in front of a dozen. The post that makes your friends laugh might horrify your grandmother. The joke that works in one friend group lands completely differently in another.

AI makes this worse in a specific way: AI tools can now search across platforms, pull together posts from different contexts, and create a combined profile of a person. Something you posted at age ten and something you posted at fourteen can be stitched together into a narrative that was never the one you were trying to tell. You cannot un-collapse a context once it has collapsed. You can only think carefully before collapsing it.

The Ethical Tension

Here is a question with no clean answer: If someone posts something publicly — even if they were only thinking about a small audience — does anyone have the right to share it further? The post was technically public. But the poster clearly did not intend "public" to mean "everyone." Who decides what counts as the intended audience? Is there such a thing as a moral obligation not to share something, even if sharing it is technically allowed?

Section 3 — What AI Added to This Equation

In August 2023, a set of AI-generated nude images of female students at Westfield High School in New Jersey circulated among students via Snapchat. The images were fake — created using an AI "nudify" tool that had been downloaded from a readily available website — but the faces were real. They were taken from the students' social media accounts. Fourteen-year-olds. The images spread through the school's social networks before anyone adult knew what was happening. Investigators were eventually able to identify the students who created them, and charges were filed. But the images had already been seen by dozens of students, and several of the girls depicted were aware that their classmates had seen them.

This is not an isolated incident. By 2024, similar cases had been reported in schools in Spain, Australia, South Korea, the United Kingdom, and across the United States. The tool used — AI image generation applied to a real person's face — had gone from something requiring technical expertise to something a middle schooler could do in under five minutes using a smartphone.

What AI added to the equation of scalability and persistence is generativity: the ability to create new content involving real people who never participated in creating it. Before AI, spreading harmful content required that harmful content exist first. Someone had to take a real photo. Now, a real photo of someone at the beach can become the raw material for content that person never consented to and never existed. The barrier between "what actually happened" and "what can be made to appear to have happened" collapsed almost entirely in the span of about two years.

Generativity (in AI context) The ability of AI tools to create new content — images, audio, video, text — using a real person's likeness or voice as input, without that person's knowledge or consent.

You now understand something that most adults in positions of authority over these situations — school principals, police officers, even many lawyers — are still catching up to. Scalability, persistence, and generativity together describe an environment where your digital identity can be taken, altered, and circulated faster than you can respond. Knowing the names of those three forces means you can see clearly what is actually happening when something goes wrong — and think clearly about what, if anything, can be done.

You can now see what most people miss

Most people, when something goes viral, say "that shouldn't have happened" — and leave it there. You can now name exactly why it happened: scalable copying, persistent storage, and potentially AI-generated content, all operating on a moment that was never meant to travel. That analysis is the foundation of every smart decision in this domain.

Section 4 — Your Digital Identity Is Not Just What You Post

In 2016, a journalism professor at Syracuse University named Jeff Hancock led a study on how people form impressions of others online. He found something counterintuitive: people trusted composite pictures of someone assembled from many small data points — posts, likes, follower lists, location tags — more than they trusted self-descriptions the person wrote themselves. In other words, the digital trail you leave without thinking about it is considered more "true" than the story you deliberately tell.

Your digital identity is not just your profile picture and bio. It is also: photos you are tagged in that you did not choose, comments you left on other people's posts three years ago, the name of every group chat you joined, who you follow and who follows you, what you searched for (if that data leaked), the time stamps of your activity (which reveal your sleep schedule), and increasingly, the content AI systems generate about you by analyzing all of the above together.

This matters because identity theft in the traditional sense — someone stealing your passwords — is only one kind of digital identity problem. The newer problem is identity misrepresentation: someone assembling your real data points into a false narrative. Or someone using AI to generate content that slots into that digital trail as if it were real. Both of those things are happening to real people right now. Both are made possible by the combination of scalability, persistence, and generativity.

There is one thing worth sitting with here, with no resolution offered: you cannot fully control your digital identity. Other people post about you. Platforms keep data you have no access to. AI systems draw inferences from your behavior that you never consented to share. The question of who owns your digital identity — legally, ethically, practically — is genuinely unresolved. Lawmakers in the European Union passed the General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR) in 2018 specifically to try to address some of this, and it is still not settled. You are living inside an open question that civilization has not answered yet.

Who Are You Online With AI? · Lesson 2 of 4

The Algorithm Knows You Better Than Your Friends Do

How recommendation systems build a model of who you are — and what that model is used for.
If a system built from your behavior predicts things about you that are accurate — did you consent to revealing those things?

In February 2012, a statistician named Andrew Pole — employed by Target's marketing analytics team in Minneapolis — explained to a reporter from the New York Times something his team had discovered about purchasing behavior. By analyzing the basket of items a shopper bought together, Target's models could predict with reasonable accuracy whether a female customer was pregnant — and roughly how far along she was. The model was built entirely from shopping data: specific combinations of unscented lotion, cotton balls, supplements, and particular food items clustered together in a way that correlated strongly with the second trimester.

The story became famous because of one incident. A father in Minneapolis received a Target mailer at his house addressed to his teenage daughter, containing coupons for baby clothes and cribs. He went to the store and complained to the manager that Target was "sending her coupons for baby clothes, and she isn't pregnant." The manager apologized, assuming an error. A few days later, the manager called the father to follow up — and the father apologized instead. His daughter was, in fact, pregnant. Target's algorithm had known before her father did. The system was not reading her diary. It was reading her shopping cart. The inference it drew was more personal than almost anything in her diary.

That was a retail recommendation system, built in 2010, predicting pregnancy from grocery data. The AI systems analyzing your behavior today — on TikTok, Instagram, YouTube, Google, and dozens of apps — are orders of magnitude more sophisticated. They have more data, faster processors, and years more refinement. And the things they are inferring about you are not just whether you are pregnant. They are inferring your political leanings, emotional state, sexual orientation, financial stress, and social relationships — often more accurately than you would describe yourself.

Section 1 — What a Recommendation Algorithm Actually Does

Every major platform you use has a recommendation system. TikTok's is called the "For You Page" algorithm. YouTube's is its recommendation engine. Spotify's is what powers Discover Weekly. These systems do not work the way most people imagine. They are not simply showing you more of what you have liked. They are building a behavioral model of you — a mathematical representation of your preferences, moods, attention patterns, and social connections — and using that model to predict what you will engage with next.

The inputs to that model are far more granular than "liked" or "didn't like." TikTok's algorithm, according to a leaked internal document reported by The Wall Street Journal in 2021, weights these signals heavily: the exact percentage of a video you watched before scrolling away, how many times you rewatched a clip, whether you paused on a frame, whether you searched for a term related to the video, and whether you shared it or sent it to someone specific. None of those things require you to press a button. Your hesitation is data. Your rewatch is data. The two seconds you spent on a video before scrolling is data.

Behavioral model A mathematical profile of a person constructed from their actions — scrolls, pauses, rewatches, searches, taps — used to predict what they will do next and what content will keep them engaged.

In 2021, the Wall Street Journal created several test TikTok accounts and let them run automated watching sessions. An account that paused slightly longer on sad content was, within about 36 minutes, being fed a near-continuous stream of content about depression and self-harm. The algorithm did not "decide" to show that content because it was harmful. It showed it because the behavioral signal — slightly longer watch time — indicated engagement. The system optimizes for engagement, not for your wellbeing. Those are not the same thing.

Section 2 — The Inference Problem

In 2013, researchers at the University of Cambridge published a study in the Proceedings of the National Academy of Sciences. They analyzed the Facebook likes of 58,000 volunteers and demonstrated that they could predict, with significant accuracy, whether someone was gay or straight (88%), Democrat or Republican (85%), Christian or Muslim (82%), and whether their parents had divorced (60%). The only data used was their Facebook likes — not their profile information, not their posts, not their messages. Just what they had clicked "Like" on.

This is the inference problem: when you use a platform, you are not just handing over the data you consciously share. You are handing over a behavioral signal from which far more can be inferred than you intended to share. The gap between "what I posted" and "what the system knows about me" is enormous — and mostly invisible.

Inference A conclusion a system draws about you from behavioral data — not something you told it, but something it calculated from patterns in what you did.

For teenagers specifically, this has a complication: your identity is still forming. At twelve or thirteen, you might not know your political leanings, sexual orientation, or long-term values. But the algorithm does not wait for you to figure yourself out. It is already building a model based on your current behavior, and that model feeds into what content you see — which then shapes your behavior — which then refines the model. This is a loop, and you are inside it.

The Ethical Tension

If an algorithm infers that a thirteen-year-old is struggling with depression — based purely on their scrolling behavior — and then shows them more content related to depression because that content gets higher engagement, who is responsible for that? The company that built the algorithm? The engineers who chose "engagement" as the metric? The regulators who didn't set rules? The parents who gave the child the phone? There is no clean answer. Every party bears some fraction of this. The question is what fraction.

Section 3 — What Your Data Is Actually Used For

In April 2018, Facebook CEO Mark Zuckerberg testified before the U.S. Senate and House of Representatives following the Cambridge Analytica scandal. A political consulting firm called Cambridge Analytica had obtained data on approximately 87 million Facebook users — not through a hack, but through a quiz app that collected data legally under Facebook's terms of service at the time — and used it to build psychological profiles of American voters. Those profiles were then used to target political advertisements designed to influence behavior.

The important detail is not Cambridge Analytica specifically. It is the mechanism: behavioral data collected for one purpose (recommending content, selling ads) can be repurposed for entirely different goals (political influence, insurance risk assessment, job screening). This is called data repurposing, and it is standard practice in the industry. When you agree to a platform's terms of service, you are typically granting them the right to share your data with third parties — a category that can include anyone from advertisers to political campaigns to data brokers who sell it further.

Data repurposing Using data collected for one purpose to serve a completely different purpose, often without the original user knowing or having consented to the new use.

In 2022, researchers at the Norwegian Consumer Council tested how data from period-tracking apps (used by millions of people to track menstrual cycles) was being shared. They found that several major apps were sending intimate health data — including cycle dates, mood logs, and sexual activity entries — to Facebook and other advertising companies, typically buried in terms of service that almost nobody read. Health data shared with a period-tracking app had become advertising data. The same mechanism applies to fitness apps, mental health apps, and educational apps — including many used in schools.

Knowing this does not mean you should delete every app. It means you should read "free" as "you are paying with data," and understand what that data is likely to be used for. That is not paranoia. That is basic literacy for the world you are in right now.

Knowing This Changes How You Read Every Headline About AI

When you see a news story about "AI personalization" or "smart recommendations," you can now ask the questions that most adults skip: What behavioral data feeds this model? What is being inferred beyond what I shared? Who else has access to that inference? And what else could it be used for? Those four questions cut through almost every AI data story you will encounter.

Section 4 — Can You Opt Out?

After the GDPR took effect in Europe in May 2018, companies operating there were required to give users meaningful control over their data — including the "right to be forgotten," meaning you can request that a company delete the data it holds on you. In 2021, China passed its own data protection law, the PIPL, with similar requirements. In the United States, only California had a comparable law (the CCPA, passed in 2018). Most American teenagers have no legal right to demand deletion of data that platforms hold about them.

But legal rights are not the only lever. The behavioral model a platform builds about you is not permanent — it reflects your recent behavior most heavily. The TikTok algorithm you feed differently will show you different things within about a week. Researchers call this "algorithmic gardening": deliberately engaging with content you want to see more of, and refusing to engage with content you want to stop seeing, as a way of steering the model. It works. Not perfectly, and not forever — the model keeps updating — but it is a form of agency in a system that tries to deny you agency.

There is also the question of what to do at an institutional level. In 2023, the U.S. Senate introduced the KOSA Act (Kids Online Safety Act), which would require platforms to provide minors with stronger default privacy settings and reduce their ability to be profiled for targeted advertising. As of this writing, the bill had not passed. Whether it should pass — and what the right balance between platform freedom, business interests, and user protection actually is — is a live policy debate. People your age will be the ones voting on politicians who make those decisions in the next decade. Understanding the technical reality behind the debate is what lets you form a real opinion rather than a reactive one.

Who Are You Online With AI? · Lesson 3 of 4

Deepfakes, Fake Accounts, and the Identity You Didn't Create

When someone else builds a digital version of you — what can you do, and who is responsible?
If an AI-generated version of you says something you never said and people believe it — what has actually been taken from you?

In January 2024, a series of sexually explicit AI-generated images appeared on X (formerly Twitter). The images depicted Taylor Swift. None of the images were real. All of them were generated using AI image synthesis tools that had been available to the general public for less than two years. Within 17 hours of first appearing, the images had been viewed an estimated 47 million times. X removed them after public outcry, but by then they had been downloaded, re-uploaded on other platforms, and shared in private group chats across multiple countries. One X account that posted the images had 900,000 followers before being suspended.

What made this case notable beyond the scale was the response it triggered. Within weeks, U.S. Senators Joe Manchin and Josh Hawley introduced bipartisan legislation specifically criminalizing the creation and distribution of non-consensual AI-generated intimate images. Several states passed their own laws in the months that followed. The incident was widely reported as a "turning point" in AI regulation discussions — not because the images involved a celebrity, but because it made undeniable that the technology existed, worked, required no expertise, and could be used against anyone. Taylor Swift has more resources to respond to something like this than virtually any other person on earth. That she couldn't prevent or fully contain it was the signal lawmakers were watching.

By the time that legislation was being drafted, similar images had already been created and circulated involving students at schools in New Jersey, Spain, South Korea, and dozens of other locations. In the New Jersey case from 2023, the students depicted were fourteen. The legislation being drafted referenced both celebrity cases and school cases. The age of the people involved was not a limiting factor for the technology. It was only a limiting factor — barely — for the social consequences.

Section 1 — What a Deepfake Actually Is

The word "deepfake" was coined in 2017 by a Reddit user who used early AI tools to swap celebrity faces into existing videos. The name comes from "deep learning" — the type of AI architecture used — combined with "fake." In 2017, creating a convincing deepfake video required significant computing power and days of processing. By 2023, it required a smartphone and about thirty seconds of source video.

There are several distinct types of synthetic media involving real people's identities:

Face swap An AI technique that maps one person's face onto another person's body in an existing video or image, often indistinguishable from the real thing at casual viewing.
Voice clone An AI model trained on audio recordings of a specific person's voice, which can then generate new speech in that voice saying anything — including things the person never said.
Text-style clone An AI model trained on a person's written content — posts, messages, essays — that can generate new text in their voice and style.

In January 2023, a 15-year-old student at a school in suburban Chicago recorded her science teacher's voice during a normal class session, fed the recording into a voice cloning tool, and generated a fake audio clip of the teacher making racist statements. She shared it on social media as if it were real. The teacher was put on administrative leave while an investigation was conducted. The clip was confirmed fake after audio forensics. The student faced expulsion proceedings. None of this required technical skill. The voice clone tool was free, available online, and required about three minutes of source audio to function.

That case illustrates something important: the harm from synthetic media is not only to the person depicted. It disrupts institutions, triggers investigations, and erodes the basic trustworthiness of audio and video as evidence. When anyone can generate a convincing fake of anyone saying anything, the value of recorded evidence collapses across all contexts — in schools, in courts, in journalism, in politics.

Section 2 — Fake Accounts and Identity Impersonation

In 2022, Meta (which operates Facebook and Instagram) reported that it had removed 1.4 billion fake accounts in a single quarter. Not 1.4 billion total — in three months. The overwhelming majority were not the work of sophisticated state actors or organized criminals. They were accounts created by individuals for ordinary social manipulation: following yourself to inflate follower counts, spreading rumors as an anonymous persona, or — increasingly — impersonating a real person to damage their reputation.

Identity impersonation online takes several forms. At the low end: creating an account with someone's name and photo to post embarrassing things. At the higher end: constructing an elaborate fake persona using AI-generated profile pictures (so the account looks real), an AI-written posting history, and the real person's actual details — school, friend group, interests — gathered from their genuine profiles.

Sock puppet account A fake online identity created to manipulate public opinion, spread rumors, or impersonate a real person — named after the way a puppet hides the hand controlling it.

In 2023, researchers at Stanford's Internet Observatory documented cases where high school students had created AI-generated fake accounts impersonating classmates, using AI-written posts designed to make the real person appear to hold views they didn't hold. In one documented case, a fake account constructed around a real student's identity posted fabricated screenshots of "her" conversations that were designed to look like they came from her actual phone. The posts spread through the school's social network before anyone flagged them as fake. By the time the real student's friends understood what had happened, opinions had shifted. Some of those opinions never fully shifted back.

The Ethical Tension

Online anonymity has historically been a tool for people who face real danger if they speak under their real name: political dissidents, abuse survivors, whistleblowers. Complete elimination of anonymous accounts would silence those voices. But anonymous accounts are also the primary mechanism for coordinated impersonation and harassment. There is no solution that protects everyone. Every policy choice protects some people and harms others. What is the right trade-off? And who should be making that decision?

Section 3 — Legal Reality and Its Limits

Most countries have some form of law against impersonation, defamation (making false statements that harm a real person's reputation), and non-consensual intimate image distribution. The United States, as of 2024, had enacted non-consensual intimate image laws in 48 states, with two states still in the process. Federal law under the SHIELD Act (Stopping Harmful Image Exploitation and Limiting Distribution Act), passed in 2022, also covered this at the national level.

Here is where legal protection runs into practical reality: laws require identification of the perpetrator. When the person who created a deepfake or a fake account is anonymous, identifying them requires forensic investigation, platform cooperation, and often a court order demanding that platforms reveal user data. That process takes months. The content spreads in hours. Even when the perpetrator is identified and charged, the content that harmed someone's reputation or caused emotional distress has already done its work. The legal system can impose consequences after the fact. It cannot rewind the harm.

In 2024, the European Union's AI Act came into force. Among its provisions: AI-generated content must be labeled as such when it depicts real people. The implementation of that requirement — how labels would appear, how they would be enforced, what counts as "realistic" AI depiction — was still being worked out. In the United States, a similar labeling bill was introduced in the Senate but had not passed as of late 2024.

The practical implication is this: right now, the legal system is approximately two to three years behind the technology. During that gap, the primary protection against synthetic media involving your identity is: people who know you well enough to spot fakes, platforms with fast takedown policies, and your own awareness of what is out there about you. None of those are reliable. All of them are better than nothing.

You can now see what most people miss

When most people hear about a deepfake, they think "celebrity problem." You now understand the full chain: the technology that makes it possible, the legal gap that makes it hard to address quickly, the institutional harms beyond the personal harm to the individual, and the policy debate that is actively unfolding. That full picture is what gives you the ability to have an informed opinion — not just a reaction.

Section 4 — What Can You Actually Do?

The question of what a 12 or 14-year-old can realistically do about deepfakes and fake accounts has an honest answer: not much, retroactively. But there are forward-looking actions that reduce exposure and increase response speed if something happens.

First: reduce source material. AI tools need training material to work with. A deepfake using your face requires images of your face from multiple angles and lighting conditions. Keeping your image library limited — especially on public or semi-public accounts — reduces the quality of output any tool can generate. This is not a perfect protection, but a face swap built from three photos is detectably worse than one built from three hundred.

Second: maintain a clear public record. If there is a reliable, unambiguous record of who you are and what you actually say — through genuine public posts, a real digital presence — it becomes easier to contrast against fake content. People who have no digital presence at all are paradoxically easier to impersonate, because there is no baseline to compare against.

Third: know your platforms' takedown mechanisms. Every major platform has a process for reporting impersonation and non-consensual intimate images. In the United States, the National Center for Missing and Exploited Children operates a CyberTipline specifically for minors. The Cyber Civil Rights Initiative maintains a crisis helpline (844-878-2274) specifically for non-consensual image cases. Knowing these exist before you need them is vastly better than trying to find them in the middle of a crisis.

Fourth, and most important: tell someone immediately. Every piece of research on how these situations resolve suggests that speed of adult involvement — a parent, a school counselor, a trusted adult — dramatically improves outcomes. The instinct to handle it alone, or to feel shame and stay silent, is understandable and almost always makes things worse.

Who Are You Online With AI? · Lesson 4 of 4

The Digital Self You Are Building Right Now

Your online behavior today creates the data set that future AI systems will analyze. What kind of record are you building?
If everything you do online is stored somewhere — are you building the identity you want, or one that is being built for you?

In 2015, the Internet Archive — a nonprofit organization that has been systematically archiving web pages since 1996 — opened a public collection of approximately 450 billion archived web pages, including a substantial portion of early MySpace content from 2003 to 2010. MySpace was the dominant social network before Facebook; at its peak in 2008, it had more than 100 million registered users, most of them teenagers. When MySpace lost users to Facebook and eventually collapsed, most people assumed their old posts, photos, and profiles had simply vanished. They had not. Crawlers from the Internet Archive, the Library of Congress, and academic institutions had saved significant portions of the content. Researchers studying social behavior, marketers analyzing consumer patterns, and curious individuals Googling old acquaintances could now find posts written by people when they were 15 — posts those people had assumed were gone for a decade.

In 2019, a reporter at BuzzFeed News named Ryan Broderick wrote about what he called "digital haunting" — the experience of encountering your own teenage online self in archived content you had forgotten existed. He interviewed adults in their late twenties and thirties who had discovered their old MySpace posts and found themselves confronted with opinions, photos, and conversations that felt foreign, embarrassing, or worse. None of them had intended those posts to last. Most had been posted with the implicit assumption that they were ephemeral — that social networks were like conversations, not like libraries.

The generation entering social media today faces a version of this problem with one crucial difference: AI systems are now actively indexing, analyzing, and drawing inferences from this archived content at scale. It is not just that an embarrassing post might be found by a future employer doing a Google search. It is that AI systems trained on public social media data have already incorporated those posts into models that may influence what content you see, how your creditworthiness is assessed, or whether you are flagged for additional scrutiny in contexts you cannot predict. The archive is not passive. It is being read, right now, by systems that will make decisions about you.

Section 1 — The Long Record

In 2018, a 27-year-old named Kevin Hart was announced as the host of the 2019 Academy Awards. Within 24 hours, journalists and social media users had surfaced a series of tweets he had posted in 2009 and 2011 containing homophobic jokes. Hart apologized and stepped down from the hosting role. The tweets were ten years old. The people who found them were not conducting a sophisticated investigation — they ran a basic Twitter search limited by date range. The entire process took about four hours.

This incident is cited frequently as an example of "cancel culture," but that framing misses the more structural point: digital content is easily searchable across time. A post you make at twelve is as findable in a search as a post you make at twenty-two, unless the platform has deleted it and the archives have not captured it — which, as we established, is not guaranteed. The question "would I be comfortable if anyone could read this ten years from now?" is not overcautious. It is factually accurate as a description of how the system works.

Temporal persistence The quality of digital content to remain accessible and searchable across years or decades, regardless of the platform's current status, due to archiving, caching, and copying.

For teenagers, this has a specific complication. Your brain at twelve is not the same brain you will have at twenty-two. The prefrontal cortex — the region associated with long-term thinking, impulse control, and weighing consequences — is not fully developed until around age 25. This is not an excuse; it is a fact about biology. Courts in the United States have recognized it: in 2012, in Miller v. Alabama, the Supreme Court ruled that mandatory life sentences for juveniles were unconstitutional specifically because of brain development research. But the digital record does not care about neuroscience. What you post at twelve is permanently associated with your name and face in searchable databases, regardless of who you become.

Section 2 — AI Systems That Read Your Past

In 2022, a team of researchers at MIT and Harvard published a study in Nature Human Behaviour examining whether AI systems trained on social media data could predict future behaviors and outcomes — including academic performance, mental health crises, and legal trouble. Their finding: with sufficient historical data, predictive accuracy was significant enough to be commercially valuable. Companies in the background-check and "predictive analytics" industries had already been selling versions of these tools to employers and landlords for several years.

In 2023, investigative journalists at the Markup identified a company called Skopenow that offered AI-powered background checks to employers and law enforcement that included analysis of a subject's historical social media activity, going back as far as the platform's data allowed. The tool flagged content involving weapons, drug references, protests, and a list of other categories that the company determined to be relevant to "risk assessment." The tool had no mechanism for context: a photo of someone at a shooting range legally was treated identically to a photo of someone with an illegally obtained weapon. A post about a protest was treated identically regardless of the cause. The AI saw pattern matches, not meaning.

Predictive profiling Using AI analysis of past behavior — including social media history — to make predictions about a person's future behavior, often for hiring, lending, or law enforcement purposes.

You are currently inside the data collection window for these systems. The posts, likes, group memberships, and behavioral signals you generate between ages 12 and 18 are being captured and archived. Some of that data will be analyzed by AI systems making decisions about you in the future. Those decisions may include whether you are offered a job, admitted to a program, approved for housing, or flagged for additional investigation. This is not speculation. It is the current state of commercially available tools, in operation right now.

The Ethical Tension

Predictive profiling tools that use childhood social media data to assess adults face a fundamental tension: people change. A twelve-year-old who posted offensive content and genuinely grew out of that worldview by eighteen is not the same person. But the record treats them as identical. Should there be a mandatory expiration date on the use of data collected about minors? Who decides how long is long enough? And if a person has genuinely changed, does their past digital self have any claim to privacy?

Section 3 — Intentional Identity Construction

In April 2014, a seventeen-year-old in Houston named Zach Wolk began what he called a "digital footprint project." He systematically documented the difference between what he had posted online without thinking versus what he would want a future employer, college admissions officer, or romantic partner to see. He wrote about the project for his school newspaper. The article, picked up by a few education blogs, generated significant discussion among school counselors and college advisors because it articulated something most adults had not thought to say to teenagers: your digital footprint is not an accident. It is a record. You can be intentional about what kind of record you build.

The concept of intentional identity construction — deliberately shaping your digital presence rather than letting it accumulate passively — has been discussed by digital literacy educators since the early 2010s. By 2024, it was a formal topic in several national school curricula. The core idea is straightforward: the same properties (scalability, persistence, searchability) that make digital mistakes damaging also make deliberate positive contributions durable. A well-written post, a thoughtful comment, documented creative work, or consistent participation in a recognized community accumulates the same way an embarrassing post does — except it serves you rather than haunts you.

This is not about performing a fake persona for the internet. It is about understanding that the digital record is real, that it will be read, and that you have more agency over it than most people exercise. The specific actions are not complicated: posting things you would be comfortable explaining in any context; not posting things that require being anonymous to post safely; using privacy settings not as a permission slip for things you would regret but as a way to have genuinely private conversations; being thoughtful about what you share about other people, not just about yourself.

Knowing This Changes How You See Your Own Timeline

Every post you have ever made is part of a data set. Future AI systems will analyze that data set in ways that are not fully predictable right now. Understanding that your digital activity is both a record and a data source — not just a stream of moments — changes how you relate to the question "should I post this?" It is not about fear. It is about seeing the actual stakes clearly, which most people your age are not yet doing.

Section 4 — Where This Course Leaves You

Over four lessons, you have looked at four dimensions of digital identity and AI: how content spreads and persists (Lesson 1), how algorithmic systems build models of you from behavioral data (Lesson 2), how AI can be used to create content involving your identity without your participation (Lesson 3), and how the long digital record is already being analyzed by systems making real decisions about real people (Lesson 4).

The picture is not comfortable. But it is accurate. And accuracy is more useful than comfort when navigating a system that will affect your life whether you understand it or not.

There are three things worth carrying out of this course. First: the mechanisms. Scalability, persistence, generativity, behavioral modeling, inference, data repurposing, temporal persistence, predictive profiling. These are not vocabulary words for a test. They are the names of forces that are operating on you right now, and knowing their names means you can see them operating rather than being surprised by effects you cannot explain.

Second: the questions. For any situation involving digital identity and AI, the questions are: Who has this data? What can be inferred from it beyond what I shared? Who else can access those inferences? What decisions could be made based on this? And: is there a consent layer missing here — did the person whose data this is actually agree to this use? Those questions do not always have good answers. But asking them is what separates someone navigating this landscape deliberately from someone being carried by it.

Third: the accountability you owe other people. The easiest part of this topic to miss is that you are not only a potential victim of the mechanisms described. You are also a potential actor within them. Every decision to forward a post, create a fake account, share a screenshot, or use an AI tool involving someone else's identity puts you on the other side of the equation. The same analysis applies: scalable, persistent, potentially generative. The same question applies: did they consent? The same answer applies: once sent, you cannot take it back.
