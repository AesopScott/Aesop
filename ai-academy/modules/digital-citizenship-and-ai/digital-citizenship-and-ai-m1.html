Who Are You Online With AI? · Introduction

Your Digital Identity Is Being Read Before You Speak

AI systems are forming opinions about you from your posts, your searches, and your silence — this course is about understanding what they see.

In October 2022, a 14-year-old in New Jersey discovered that the AI-powered content recommendation system on her TikTok account had, over six weeks, quietly shifted her entire feed from dance videos to content about eating disorders — without her ever searching for it. She hadn't asked for it. The algorithm had inferred from tiny behavioral signals — how long she paused on certain videos, which ones she rewatched for two seconds — that she was a good candidate for that content. Her parents found out when she mentioned it to a friend on a call. The Wall Street Journal had already documented this exact pattern in September 2021, using test accounts, watching the same drift happen in under an hour. The algorithm wasn't broken. It was working exactly as designed.

This is the world you're already navigating. AI systems — on every platform you use, in the search results you trust, in the suggested friends and videos and news stories that shape what you think is normal — are constructing a model of who you are, and then using that model to decide what you see next. Most adults don't fully understand how this works. After this course, you will.

This course won't make you paranoid or tell you to delete everything. It will give you a framework for thinking clearly about what information you share, how AI systems interpret it, and what you actually control. Four lessons. Real cases. No easy answers — but sharper questions than you had before.

Who Are You Online With AI? · Lesson 1

The Post Nobody Meant to Go Viral

What happens when something you share reaches people you never knew were watching — and how AI changed the scale of that forever.
When does a private moment become a public document?

On the morning of April 6, 2013, Justine Sacco was a PR executive at IAC with 170 Twitter followers. She was boarding a flight from London to Cape Town, South Africa. Before takeoff, she sent a tweet that she thought was a sardonic, self-aware joke about privilege — the kind of thing she might say to a friend. It read: "Going to Africa. Hope I don't get AIDS. Just kidding. I'm white!" She put her phone in airplane mode and fell asleep for eleven hours.

While she slept, her tweet was screenshot and reshared. A journalist saw it. Then a reporter at Gawker posted it. Then the hashtag #HasJustineLandedYet began trending on Twitter — in the United States, United Kingdom, and South Africa simultaneously. By the time her plane touched down, she had over 10,000 mentions. Strangers had tracked her flight on FlightRadar24. Someone was waiting at the airport to photograph her face when she turned on her phone and read the replies. She was fired before she got through customs. The entire collapse of her professional life had unfolded while she was literally unreachable — unconscious at 35,000 feet.

Sacco later said she had no idea the tweet would leave her small circle. She hadn't built a public platform. She had 170 followers. But a single reshare by the right person inserted her post into an entirely different context — one with millions of readers, no shared history with her, and no reason to give her the benefit of the doubt. The internet had always had this potential. But by 2013, the AI-powered recommendation systems at Twitter, Facebook, and Google had made it instantaneous and global. What used to take days to spread now took minutes.

Context Collapse: The Concept Behind the Story

What happened to Justine Sacco has a name. Researcher danah boyd identified the phenomenon in 2010 and called it context collapse. The idea is straightforward: when you speak to a friend, you're in a specific context. You know who's listening. You know the shared jokes, the tone, the history. Your friend knows you didn't mean something literally when you said it. Context is the invisible layer that makes communication work.

Online, that layer gets stripped away. When a post can be seen by your grandmother, your school principal, a journalist in London, and a stranger who hates everything you represent — all at the same time — you're no longer speaking to one context. You're speaking to all contexts simultaneously. What reads as sarcasm to your friends reads as cruelty to a stranger. What reads as a personal update to your followers reads as evidence to someone building a case against you.

Context collapse When a message designed for one specific audience is seen by many different audiences at once, often stripping away the context that made it make sense.

This has always been theoretically possible with writing. If someone photographed a letter and mailed it to everyone you knew, context collapse would happen. But until social media and algorithmic amplification, the friction was so high it almost never occurred. Today the friction is essentially zero. A recommendation algorithm can surface a post from someone with 170 followers to millions of people if the engagement signals look interesting — and it doesn't know or care about your intent.

Stop and Think

Think about the last thing you posted publicly. Who did you imagine was reading it? Now: who actually could have been reading it? If those two answers are different, you've just experienced context collapse — even if nothing bad happened.

How AI Changed the Equation

In the years before algorithmic feeds — roughly before 2009, when Facebook launched its newsfeed and Twitter switched to algorithmic ranking — posts spread mostly through deliberate sharing. Someone had to actively decide to repost something. That friction wasn't nothing. It meant accidental virality was rare.

Starting around 2010 and accelerating sharply through 2015–2018, every major platform began using machine learning models to rank and recommend content. The models weren't designed to spread kindness or truth. They were optimized for a metric called engagement — meaning clicks, shares, time spent, replies. The systems learned quickly that outrage, shock, and moral violation drove more engagement than neutral content. Academic research published in 2017 by MIT's Sinan Aral and colleagues confirmed what engineers already suspected: false information spreads six times faster than true information on Twitter, largely because it's more emotionally activating.

This is the critical thing to understand: when a post goes viral, it isn't usually because lots of people independently chose to share it. It's because an AI model decided the post was good engagement bait and showed it to people who were likely to react strongly. The algorithm is a silent amplifier that most people don't know is there.

Engagement optimization When an AI system is trained to maximize reactions (clicks, shares, time-on-screen) rather than accuracy or user wellbeing. A side effect is that emotionally provocative content gets amplified over neutral content.

This means the Justine Sacco story isn't just about one bad tweet. It's about a system that was specifically designed to find posts that would trigger strong emotional reactions — and then show those posts to millions of people who weren't in the original conversation. Sacco's tweet didn't earn its reach. The algorithm granted it.

Permanence: The Persistence of Digital Records

There's a second force at work beyond virality: permanence. In 2010, a high school student named Kyle Doyle in Australia called in sick to work and then posted a Facebook status that said he wasn't going in and it was "worth it." His manager saw the post. He was disciplined. In 2013, a candidate for a position at a major US law firm lost his offer after the hiring committee found a tweet from his sophomore year of college making a racist joke he had forgotten he'd ever sent. In 2018, director James Gunn was fired by Disney after a coordinated effort surfaced decade-old tweets that had been sitting, publicly indexed, for years.

The Wayback Machine — an internet archive run by the non-profit Internet Archive — has been archiving web pages since 1996. Google's cache stores copies of pages even after the originals are deleted. Third-party archiving tools let anyone save a screenshot of a post before it's removed. From a practical standpoint, most things you post publicly should be considered permanent.

You Can Now See What Most People Miss

When people say "just delete it," they're assuming the only copy is the one you control. But anything that was publicly visible for even a few seconds could have been archived, screenshot, or indexed. Deletion removes your copy. It doesn't remove other people's.

AI makes this more intense in a specific way: large language models and image-recognition systems can now be trained on historical internet data, meaning content you posted years ago might influence how an AI system characterizes your behavior — even if you deleted it from the original platform. Several researchers have documented cases of AI-generated background checks surfacing information that had been "deleted" by users years earlier.

The Ethical Question That Doesn't Have a Clean Answer

Here is where it gets genuinely complicated. Justine Sacco's tweet was, by most readings, racially insensitive — regardless of her intent. The people who were angry at it had real reasons for their anger. They weren't wrong to be upset. And the systems that amplified the post to millions of people were designed by engineers who were trying to build something useful.

But consider: the punishment she received — global humiliation, job loss, death threats, years of anxiety — was not handed down by any court or ethical process. No one weighed her tweet against her entire character. No one gave her a chance to explain before the verdict. The scale of the response was not proportional to the harm of the original act. It was proportional to how much engagement the outrage generated.

Ethical Tension — No Clean Answer

If an AI system amplifies real harm (a racist tweet reaches millions and causes more damage), is the system doing something wrong? Or is it just revealing what was always there? And if a platform could reduce that amplification, should it be required to — even if doing so means deciding which speech counts as "harmful enough" to suppress? Who gets to make that call?

This question is being actively debated by legislators in the EU, US Congress, and the UK right now — in 2024 and 2025. The EU's Digital Services Act, which took full effect in 2024, actually requires large platforms to assess the systemic risks their recommendation algorithms create. This is new. It's happening because of exactly the dynamics you've just been reading about. Knowing this means you're reading current events with more context than most adults have.

Who Are You Online With AI? · Lesson 2

The Profile You Didn't Write

AI systems build detailed models of who you are from your behavior — not just what you say, but how you move, pause, and scroll.
If you never told a system anything about yourself, how much does it already know?

In September 2012, a statistician named Andrew Pole at Target Corporation revealed — in a New York Times interview with journalist Charles Duhigg — that the retailer had built a pregnancy prediction model. The model had been running since at least 2010. It worked like this: Target assigned every customer a unique ID tied to their credit card. The model tracked purchases over time and identified 25 products whose buying patterns changed in a specific sequence when a woman was pregnant — things like unscented lotion, large bags of cotton balls, certain vitamins. By analyzing these patterns, Target could estimate not just whether someone was pregnant, but how far along they were, accurate enough to send coupons timed to match specific trimesters.

The case that made national news: a man in Minnesota received Target mailers addressed to his teenage daughter, advertising baby furniture and maternity clothing. He went to the store and complained. The manager apologized. A few days later, the father called back. His daughter, it turned out, was pregnant. Target's model had inferred it before her family knew. The model was never told she was pregnant. It never asked. It read the pattern in her purchasing behavior — items she had bought without any awareness that they were being analyzed — and made a probabilistic inference that turned out to be correct.

This was 2010. The same basic logic — behavioral signal inference, trained on large datasets — underpins every major AI recommendation and profiling system you interact with today. It has only become more sophisticated, faster, and more pervasive in the years since. The question isn't whether systems are doing this to you right now. They are. The question is: what do they think they know about you, and how wrong can a model be while still being used to make decisions about your life?

Behavioral Data: What Gets Collected Without Asking

There's a category distinction most people don't make. Declarative data is what you tell a system directly: your name, your age, your interests when you fill out a profile. Behavioral data is everything the system observes about how you act: how long you pause on a video before scrolling past, which links you click but don't share, what time of day you're active, how fast you type when you're upset versus calm.

Behavioral data Information collected from observing how a person acts — movement patterns, timing, sequences of actions — rather than from what they explicitly say about themselves.

A 2018 study by researchers at Cambridge University found that Facebook "likes" alone — just the things you clicked a thumbs-up on — could predict your personality traits (openness, conscientiousness, extraversion, agreeableness, neuroticism) more accurately than your friends could, and in some cases more accurately than your family. Not because the likes were direct reports of your personality. Because the pattern of what you engaged with was a behavioral signal that correlated with personality measures in the training data.

By 2016, Cambridge Analytica — a political consulting firm — claimed it had built detailed psychological profiles on 230 million American voters using exactly this approach, having harvested Facebook data from a personality quiz app used by 270,000 people that also scraped the social graph of everyone connected to those users. The ICO (UK Information Commissioner's Office) later confirmed that the data harvesting had occurred. Whether the targeting actually changed votes remains contested. That the profiling happened does not.

Inference Engines: How Gaps Get Filled

One of the most important things to understand about AI profiling is that the model doesn't need complete information. It needs enough data points to make a prediction with sufficient confidence. The gaps in your data get filled by inference — statistical reasoning based on what other users with similar patterns turned out to be like.

In 2022, an investigation by The Markup found that Facebook's advertising system was allowing advertisers to target users based on inferred characteristics — including sensitive health conditions, political views, and income brackets — even though users had never directly reported these traits. The data was inferred from behavioral signals and then sold as a targeting category. You could be categorized as having diabetes, not because you said you did, but because your browsing patterns resembled the patterns of people who had disclosed a diabetes diagnosis.

Stop and Think

You don't have to have done anything wrong for an AI profile to affect you. If the system infers incorrectly — that you're a certain income bracket, health status, or political affiliation — those inferences still get used for decisions about what ads, loans, jobs, or content you see. Being incorrectly categorized is a harm even without any malicious intent.

This means the question of "I have nothing to hide" is missing the point. It's not about what you're hiding. It's about what the model thinks it has inferred, how confident the model is, and what decisions it or downstream systems make based on those inferences — often without any human reviewing the logic.

You Can Now See What Most People Miss

When someone says "AI just shows you ads," that's describing the output. The input is a detailed behavioral profile that gets built whether you opt in or not, is often inaccurate in ways you'll never know about, and is used for decisions far beyond advertising — including hiring, credit scoring, insurance pricing, and content access in some jurisdictions.

The Ethical Question: Inference Without Consent

Here's the tension that doesn't have a clean answer. The Target pregnancy model genuinely helped some customers — people who needed baby products got coupons for them. Behavioral inference can power medical screening tools that catch diseases before patients report symptoms. The same inference logic that felt invasive in 2012 is now used in early Alzheimer's detection tools, where subtle changes in typing and browsing patterns precede diagnosis by years.

But in the Target case: the daughter hadn't told her family yet. The model had effectively removed her ability to choose when and how to disclose her pregnancy — a decision that belongs to her, not to a retailer's inference engine. The question isn't whether the model was accurate. It was. The question is whether accuracy gives a system the right to act on an inference about something a person hasn't chosen to share.

Ethical Tension — No Clean Answer

If an AI model can accurately infer something private about you — a pregnancy, a health condition, a sexuality — does accuracy justify acting on that inference? Is there a difference between a human making the same correct guess versus a corporation's automated system? And who owns the inference — you, the system that made it, or the company that deployed it?

This question is currently in front of courts in the EU, US, and UK simultaneously. The EU's GDPR (General Data Protection Regulation), in force since 2018, includes provisions specifically about automated decision-making and inferred sensitive data. But the law is moving much slower than the technology. For now, the answer to "who owns the inference about you" remains genuinely unsettled.

Who Are You Online With AI? · Lesson 3

When the Filter Decides What's Real

Personalization algorithms don't just reflect your interests — they amplify them, narrow them, and gradually replace your view of the world with a curated version of itself.
If everyone sees a different internet, do we share any common reality at all?

In January 2011, internet activist Eli Pariser noticed something. He was a liberal who had been deliberately following conservative friends on Facebook to get a broader range of perspectives. But over a few weeks, their posts had quietly stopped appearing in his feed. No notification. No opt-in. Facebook's algorithm had silently inferred that he wasn't engaging with those posts — he wasn't liking or commenting — and had decided to stop showing them. The system was optimizing for engagement, which meant showing him more of what he'd reacted to before, and less of what he'd ignored. He published a book about this in 2011 called The Filter Bubble, and the term stuck.

But what Pariser documented in 2011 was mild compared to what the systems evolved into. By 2016, Facebook's News Feed algorithm had been refined through thousands of A/B tests and was processing over 100,000 signals per post per user to decide what to show. Internal Facebook research, leaked to the Wall Street Journal in September 2021 as part of the "Facebook Files," showed that the company's own researchers had found by 2016 that 64% of people who joined extremist groups on Facebook did so because the algorithm recommended them. Users who followed political content were often shown increasingly extreme versions of it — not because they sought it out, but because extreme content generated higher engagement, and the model optimized for engagement.

This is a more unsettling version of the problem than most people consider. It's not just that you see what you already believe — it's that the algorithm can actively push you toward more extreme versions of what you believe, because extreme versions are more engaging. The filter bubble doesn't just reflect your worldview. It pressurizes it.

Filter Bubbles and Echo Chambers: Two Different Problems

These terms get used interchangeably, but they're actually describing different things — and understanding the difference matters.

Filter bubble When an algorithm restricts what information you see based on your past behavior — without you choosing this restriction and often without your awareness. The narrowing is done by the system, not by you.
Echo chamber When a social environment — a group, community, or platform — reinforces existing beliefs because dissenting views are rare or unwelcome. This can happen with or without algorithms, through human social dynamics.

A filter bubble is something done to you by a system. An echo chamber is something that can emerge from human behavior — though algorithms accelerate and deepen both. A community that bans dissenting views is an echo chamber without any AI involved. A personalization algorithm that stops showing you content you don't engage with creates a filter bubble even if you never chose to exclude that content.

The significance is that filter bubbles are much harder to opt out of, because you often don't know they're happening. By 2019, researchers at Princeton (Guess, Nyhan, and Reifler) had published data suggesting that the algorithmic filter bubble effect was real but smaller than previously assumed for general news consumption — but much larger for politically engaged users who followed a lot of political content. The more you engaged with one type of content, the more severely the algorithm narrowed what you saw.

Radicalization Pathways: The YouTube Case

In 2017, journalist Zeynep Tufekci wrote in the New York Times about what she called YouTube's "radicalization rabbit hole." She had been researching political videos and noticed that YouTube's recommendation algorithm — the system that decides what to play next — consistently recommended progressively more extreme content. Watch a mainstream political speech; the recommendation panel fills with more aggressive commentary. Watch aggressive commentary; the recommendations shift toward fringe content. She documented this across political orientations: it happened with content from both left and right, from fitness videos into eating disorder content, from conspiracy-adjacent content into full conspiracy content.

Internal YouTube data, revealed in a 2019 Wall Street Journal investigation, showed that YouTube's own engineers had found that the recommendation system drove users to increasingly radical content. Engineers internally called it the "rabbit hole" problem. The system had been optimized for watch time — how long you stayed on the platform — and extreme, provocative content kept people watching longer. By 2019, YouTube said it had changed the algorithm. But the core incentive structure — maximize watch time — remained unchanged.

Stop and Think

This isn't about people being weak-minded or easily manipulated. A system optimized to keep you watching will find whatever content keeps your particular brain most activated. For most people, that's content that validates existing beliefs and intensifies emotions. You don't have to be unusually susceptible. You just have to use the platform.

By 2021, a coalition of researchers published findings in the journal Nature suggesting the radicalization effect was real but geographically uneven — more severe in countries where local-language content was sparser and global extremist content filled the recommendation vacuum. This is an institutional-level problem: YouTube has different effects in different societies based on the local content ecosystem, not just individual user behavior.

The Ethical Question: Personalization vs. Common Reality

Personalization isn't inherently malicious. Showing you things you're interested in is genuinely useful. The question is what it costs.

In 2022, philosopher C. Thi Nguyen published an influential paper describing what he called "epistemic cowardice in algorithms" — the tendency for systems to show you content that confirms what you already believe because that generates engagement, while avoiding challenging information that might cause you to leave the platform. He argued this creates what he called "value capture" — your actual, nuanced values get simplified and amplified into an exaggerated caricature of themselves, because the caricature is more algorithmically legible.

Knowing This Changes How You Read Every Headline About AI

When you read that "social media is polarizing society," the specific mechanism is this: personalization algorithms, optimized for engagement, systematically amplify extreme content because extreme content generates stronger engagement signals. The polarization isn't incidental. It's a predictable output of the optimization target. If you changed the optimization target — from engagement to, say, informed perspective-taking — the output would be different. That's a policy and design choice, not an inevitable feature of AI.

The ethical question: if a platform could reduce political polarization by showing users more diverse content — but doing so would reduce engagement and therefore revenue — should it be required to do so? This is currently before regulators in several countries. There is no consensus answer. But people who understand this problem are the ones who get to shape the answer.

Ethical Tension — No Clean Answer

Does a platform have an obligation to show you things you don't already believe? Does personalization become harmful when it systematically limits your exposure to disconfirming evidence? And if you genuinely prefer personalized content — if it makes the product more useful for you personally — does the social cost of collective polarization override your individual preference?

Who You Are When the System Is Watching

Understanding the forces doesn't make you immune — but it changes what choices you actually have.
Knowing how AI shapes your digital identity, what — if anything — can you actually do about it?

In March 2018, a 20-year-old computer science student named Dylan Curran in Ireland decided to download every piece of data Google and Facebook had collected on him. He posted the results on Twitter. Google had 5.5 gigabytes of data on him — roughly three million Word documents. It included every search he'd ever made, every location he'd been to while carrying his phone (including which floor of a building), every ad he'd clicked, every app he'd used and for how long, the full list of every person he'd ever called and for how many seconds, and a detailed log of every website he'd ever visited. Facebook had a further 600 megabytes: every message he'd ever sent, every person he'd ever searched for, every event he'd been invited to, every device he'd ever used to log in.

His tweet thread went viral — not because the data collection was secret, exactly, but because most people had agreed to it without ever imagining the scale. The terms of service had said data was collected. Nobody pictured five gigabytes. Curran's thread — viewed over 30 million times — made abstract data collection suddenly concrete. Journalists, teachers, parents, and teenagers shared it because seeing the list made something real that had previously just been a policy document nobody read.

This is where this module ends: not with alarm, but with clarity. You now understand context collapse, behavioral inference, engagement optimization, filter bubbles, and radicalization pathways. The question this final lesson addresses is the practical one: given all of this, what does an informed person actually do differently? And what are the genuine limits of individual action when the systems are this large?

Data Minimization: What You Can Actually Control

There's a concept in data privacy law called data minimization — the principle that systems should collect only the data they actually need. For individuals, the equivalent is a practical habit: share less by default. Not because you have something to hide, but because less behavioral data means less material for inference engines to work with.

Data minimization The practice of limiting what data you generate or share to what's actually necessary — leaving less material for profiling systems to analyze.

This is more granular than "delete your accounts." Specific practices make a measurable difference. Using a browser with tracker-blocking (Firefox with uBlock Origin, Brave) reduces the behavioral data that advertising networks collect from your web browsing. Turning off location history on your phone stops the floor-level tracking Curran documented. Using private browsing for sensitive searches removes them from your profile history. These aren't paranoid behaviors. They're the equivalent of closing the blinds — normal privacy maintenance that most people in the physical world take for granted.

But here's the honest limit: if you use a platform at all, the platform collects data about that use. You cannot fully opt out of behavioral data collection and still use the service. The question is how much you minimize within that constraint — and whether the services that respect your data are good enough alternatives to the ones that don't.

Intentional Friction: Slowing Down the Algorithm

In 2019, researchers at Duke University published a study in which they paid Facebook users to deactivate their accounts for four weeks before the 2018 US midterm elections. The results — published in the journal Science in 2020 — found that deactivated users were significantly less politically polarized, consumed less partisan news, and reported higher subjective wellbeing. When they returned to Facebook, some of the effects persisted for several weeks before fading.

You're not going to deactivate everything. But the study points at something useful: the algorithm's effect is not instantaneous and permanent. It works through accumulated exposure over time. Which means deliberate interruption — even partial, even temporary — changes the effect. Some researchers and digital wellness practitioners describe this as intentional friction: deliberately inserting pauses or obstacles between you and algorithmic content consumption.

Intentional friction Deliberately slowing down your consumption of algorithmic content — putting your phone in another room, setting time limits, turning off autoplay — to give your own judgment a chance to operate before the algorithm's next recommendation.

Specific, practical versions of this: turning off autoplay on YouTube forces you to actively choose the next video rather than passively follow the recommendation chain. Using a timer for social media sessions interrupts the infinite scroll that sustains engagement. Reading an article to the end before sharing it — which sounds obvious but most sharing happens without the sharer having read the full piece — introduces deliberation into the process.

Stop and Think

None of these changes require you to opt out of technology. They require noticing that the default settings on every platform are designed to maximize the platform's engagement, not your wellbeing or accuracy of information. Changing a default is a small act with asymmetric consequences.

The Limits of Individual Action

Here's the honest part. Everything in the previous two sections is real and worth doing. And it is also insufficient at the systemic level. The problems described in this module — context collapse, behavioral inference, engagement-driven radicalization — are not primarily problems of individual behavior. They're problems of system design. A person can minimize their data footprint, practice intentional friction, and still live in a society whose information environment is shaped by the same dynamics.

This is why the EU's Digital Services Act (2024), the UK's Online Safety Act (2023), and ongoing US Congressional debates about platform liability matter. These are attempts to change the incentive structures that create these dynamics at the design level — not by asking individuals to behave differently, but by requiring platforms to build systems differently.

In 2023, several US state attorneys general filed lawsuits against Meta, alleging that Instagram's design — specifically its engagement-optimization features — caused measurable psychological harm to minors. The lawsuits cited internal Meta research, including research the company commissioned in 2019 that found Instagram worsened body image issues in 13-to-17-year-old girls. The litigation is ongoing as of 2025. The outcome will affect the design of systems used by hundreds of millions of people.

You Can Now See What Most People Miss

When you read about technology regulation — platform liability, algorithmic transparency laws, data protection rules — you now have the framework to evaluate what the policy is actually trying to do and whether it addresses the right mechanism. Most people read these stories as "government vs. tech companies." You can read them as "which optimization target should these systems use, and who gets to decide?"

The final ethical question this module leaves you with: individual choices and systemic change are not in competition. You can minimize your own behavioral data footprint while also forming opinions about which regulations you support when they come to a vote. These are the same issue at different scales. People who understand how the systems actually work are the ones whose opinions about them will be most informed — and most useful.

Ethical Tension — No Clean Answer

If a platform's default settings cause measurable harm — to polarization, to mental health, to the accuracy of shared information — does choosing to use the platform make you partly responsible for that harm? Or is responsibility located entirely at the design and policy level? And does understanding a system change your moral relationship to it — are you more responsible once you know?
