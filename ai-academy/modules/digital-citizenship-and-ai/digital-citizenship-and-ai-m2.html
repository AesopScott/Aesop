Module 2 · Lesson 1

The Megaphone You Didn't Know You Had

How a single message travels from one person to millions — and why AI is making that journey faster.
When does sharing something online stop being your choice?

In October 2013, a Justine Sacco — a PR executive with 170 Twitter followers — boarded a flight from London to Cape Town. Before the plane took off, she posted a joke about Africa and AIDS. It was 11 words. She turned off her phone and went to sleep for eleven hours.

By the time her plane landed, #HasJustineLandedYet was the number-one trending topic on Twitter worldwide. Her employer had fired her mid-flight. Thousands of people were sharing her home address. A stranger had driven to the Cape Town airport just to photograph her getting off the plane.

She had 170 followers. The tweet reached millions. She didn't press any amplify button. She didn't go viral on purpose. The platform did it — because the platform is designed to do exactly that.

What "Going Viral" Actually Means

Most people think going viral is something that happens to famous people on purpose. But Justine Sacco's story shows something different: any message from any account can be amplified to millions of people in hours, without your permission, without warning, and often while you are completely unaware.

The word "viral" comes from biology — a virus spreads by copying itself into host after host. A viral post works the same way. Each person who shares it becomes a host. The difference from biology is that an online platform doesn't just wait for sharing to happen naturally. It actively recommends the content to new people. It places it in feeds. It sends notifications. The platform is the amplifier.

In 2013, when Sacco posted her tweet, this process was already powerful. Today, AI systems make it dramatically faster. Recommendation algorithms — the software that decides what you see in your feed — are now trained on billions of data points to find content that will make you react emotionally. Anger, outrage, and shock travel fastest. The AI isn't choosing to be cruel. It's doing what it was trained to do: maximize engagement. Your reaction is the fuel.

AmplificationWhen a platform increases the reach of a message far beyond the original poster's own audience, often automatically and without consent.
Recommendation AlgorithmAn AI system that decides which content to show each user, trained to maximize how much time people spend on the platform.
The Asymmetry Nobody Explains to You

Here is what makes this genuinely strange: the person who posts something has almost no power over how far it goes. Justine Sacco had 170 followers. The algorithm connected her post to millions of people she had never met, based entirely on what those strangers were engaging with at the time. She was the author, but she was not the distributor. The platform was.

This is called an asymmetry — when two things that seem related are actually very unequal. In this case: the effort it takes to post something (a few seconds) versus the consequences that can follow (years of reputation damage, job loss, threats). That gap is the asymmetry.

Before the internet, spreading a message required physical effort. You had to print pamphlets, mail letters, or knock on doors. The friction of that effort naturally limited how far most messages traveled. The internet removed that friction. AI-powered recommendation removed what was left. Now a message can travel to a billion people before the person who wrote it has finished their cup of coffee.

The philosopher Marshall McLuhan famously said in 1964 that "the medium is the message" — meaning the tool you use to communicate shapes the communication itself. A phone call feels different from a letter. A tweet feels different from both. What McLuhan couldn't have predicted was a medium that actively reshapes your message as it distributes it, adding context, removing context, and choosing who sees it based on an AI's prediction of who will react most strongly.

Ethical Question — No Clean Answer

If a platform amplifies your words to millions of people without your knowledge, who is responsible for the consequences — you, the platform, or both? Does it matter whether you intended your post to be seen that widely?

AI Changed the Speed, Not Just the Scale

In 2016, Facebook researchers published an internal study showing that their recommendation system could significantly influence which news stories people saw — and therefore what they believed. In 2018, internal documents showed that YouTube's recommendation algorithm was directing users toward increasingly extreme content because extreme content produced longer watch times. The engineers hadn't designed it to radicalize people. They had designed it to keep people watching. The radicalization was a side effect.

By 2023, every major platform — TikTok, Instagram, YouTube, Twitter/X, Snapchat — was using AI recommendation systems. These systems don't just decide what you see. They also decide what others see of what you post. When you post something, the algorithm tests it on a small sample of users. If they engage — by liking, sharing, or even just pausing while scrolling — the algorithm shows it to more people. If those people engage, it shows it to even more. This is called a cascade.

The cascade happens automatically. You cannot stop it once it starts. You may not even know it has started. This is the world you are posting into every time you put something online. Not a small conversation with friends. A system designed to find the most emotionally reactive version of your words and show it to the people most likely to react.

You Now See What Most People Miss

Most people experience social media as a conversation. You now understand it as a machine that selects, tests, and amplifies content based on emotional reaction — often without the poster's knowledge. Every time you see something trending, you're seeing the output of that machine. That changes what trending means.

What This Means When It's Your Words

Justine Sacco's story happened in 2013. The amplification systems in 2025 are far more powerful. A post that might have reached 10,000 people in 2013 can now reach 10 million in the same time frame, because the AI has had a decade more data to train on and a decade more practice finding the content that triggers reaction.

This doesn't mean you shouldn't post anything. It means you should understand what you're posting into. There's a difference between writing in a notebook, talking to a friend, and speaking into a megaphone in a stadium. Social media is closer to the megaphone than most people realize. The AI doesn't care about your intent. It cares about reaction.

The next time you're about to post something — especially something funny, edgy, or emotionally charged — it's worth asking: not "will my friends see this?" but "what happens if the algorithm decides this is the one it amplifies?" You probably can't answer that question. Neither can the algorithm. That uncertainty is the thing worth sitting with.

Module 2 · Lesson 2

The Comment That Started a War

How recommendation AI turns small disagreements into large-scale conflicts — and why it profits from doing so.
Is a platform responsible for what happens when its algorithm deliberately shows angry people more anger?

In 2017 and 2018, a genocide was unfolding in Myanmar. The Rohingya Muslim minority was being driven from their homes. Thousands were killed. But this story doesn't start in the hills of Rakhine State. It starts in a Facebook algorithm.

Facebook had become the primary internet experience for most people in Myanmar by 2014 — the country had skipped desktop computers and gone straight to cheap smartphones with free Facebook data. For millions of people, Facebook was the internet. And Facebook's recommendation system was, as always, optimizing for engagement.

Posts spreading hate speech against the Rohingya generated enormous engagement. They made people angry. Angry people shared. Angry people commented. Angry people spent more time on the platform. The algorithm read all of that engagement as a signal that the content was popular and should be shown to more people. By the time Facebook's content moderators — most of whom didn't speak Burmese — began flagging the worst posts, the content had already traveled through millions of feeds.

A United Nations fact-finding report in 2018 directly named Facebook as playing a "determining role" in spreading the hate speech that contributed to the violence. Facebook's internal researchers, as reported by the Wall Street Journal in 2021, had found years earlier that their own algorithm was fueling division — and the finding had not led to meaningful changes.

Engagement Optimization and What It Actually Means

The word "engagement" sounds neutral. It sounds like a good thing — people engaging with content means they like it, right? But in the language of platform AI, engagement means any reaction: a like, a share, a comment, a reply, even just stopping your scroll for more than two seconds. The algorithm counts all of these equally as signals that the content is worth showing to more people.

The problem is that the most engaging content is often not the most accurate, the most kind, or the most fair. In 2019, MIT researchers published a study in the journal Science showing that on Twitter, false news stories spread six times faster than true ones. The reason wasn't bots. It was humans — people found the false stories more surprising, more emotional, more interesting. The algorithm amplified those human reactions.

This means that when you write something online — a comment, a post, a reply — the version of it that gets amplified is not necessarily the version you intended. If you wrote a nuanced, careful opinion, but someone replies with an outraged mischaracterization of your opinion, the algorithm may amplify the outraged reply more than your original post. Your words become the starting point for a fire you didn't light.

Engagement OptimizationThe process by which an AI system maximizes the amount of time users spend interacting with a platform, by showing content most likely to provoke a reaction — including anger, fear, or outrage.
The Outrage Loop

Here's a pattern that researchers have documented across multiple platforms, which you can now recognize whenever you see it:

Step 1: Someone posts something — a complaint, an opinion, a joke — that a portion of viewers find offensive or outrageous. Step 2: The outraged viewers comment and share, which the algorithm reads as high engagement. Step 3: The algorithm shows the post to more people — specifically, people who have previously engaged with similar content, meaning people who are likely to also be outraged. Step 4: The outrage grows. More sharing. More comments. More engagement. The algorithm shows it to even more people. Step 5: The original poster, who may have intended something small and private, is now at the center of a public controversy.

This is the outrage loop, and it runs on its own once it starts. In 2021, Frances Haugen — a Facebook engineer who became a whistleblower — testified before the U.S. Senate that Facebook's internal research showed "angry content is disproportionately likely to get reshares, reactions, and comments" and that the platform knew this and had chosen, for business reasons, not to fully address it.

That decision — made by executives in a boardroom in Menlo Park, California — affected what billions of people around the world saw in their feeds. It shaped what millions of people believed about their neighbors, their communities, and their governments. And it started with the same mechanism that amplifies your posts: an AI designed to maximize reaction.

Ethical Question — No Clean Answer

Facebook's internal researchers found evidence of harm. Leadership chose not to act on it. Is that a corporate decision, a moral failure, or both? At what point does a platform's algorithm become something a society has the right to regulate — the way it regulates other powerful systems like electricity grids or financial markets?

When Your Words Become Somebody Else's Weapon

There is a specific risk that most people don't think about until it happens to them: context collapse. This is the term researchers use for what happens when a message written for one audience gets seen by a completely different audience — one that doesn't share the context the original author was working from.

Think about this: you make an inside joke with your friend group. Everyone in that group knows the background. The joke is funny because of the shared history. Now imagine that same joke posted publicly and shown to ten thousand people who don't know you, don't know your friend group, and don't share the background. The joke is no longer a joke. It's just the words, stripped of context, available for interpretation by strangers.

AI recommendation systems accelerate context collapse. They pull content from one community and surface it to another because the emotional signal — the reaction — transcends community lines. Someone's harmless joke in one community can become evidence of something sinister in another, not because anything changed about the words, but because the audience changed entirely.

In 2020, the actor and comedian Hartley Sawyer was fired from The CW show The Flash when old tweets from 2012 and 2014 — written when he had a small audience — resurfaced and went viral in a completely different social context. The tweets were read as offensive. Whether they deserved the consequences they caused is a question people genuinely disagree about. But the mechanism is clear: an AI surfaced old content to a new audience that lacked the original context. The words hadn't changed. Everything else had.

You Now Understand Something Policymakers Are Still Arguing About

Right now, in 2024 and 2025, governments in the EU, the UK, and the US are actively debating whether platforms should be legally responsible for how their algorithms amplify content. The EU's Digital Services Act (2023) attempts to regulate this. When you see those news stories, you now understand the technical mechanism at the center of the debate — something most reporters covering the story don't fully explain.

Module 2 · Lesson 3

The Person Who Got Optimized Away

AI doesn't just amplify what you say. It constructs a version of you — and shows that version to the world.
If an AI's model of who you are is wrong, how do you correct it — and should you have to?

In 2020, researchers at the Wall Street Journal built dozens of fake TikTok accounts. Each account was given a single interest: some were programmed to linger on sad videos; others on conservative political content; others on videos about eating disorders. The researchers wanted to see what TikTok's recommendation AI would do with a single data point.

Within thirty minutes, the accounts assigned to "sadness" content were receiving a feed dominated by videos about depression, self-harm, and suicide. The account had never searched for anything. It had never followed anyone. It had only paused on sad videos. The AI had built a model — a prediction of who this user was — and began feeding that model ever more intense versions of what it predicted the user wanted.

This is not a glitch. This is the algorithm working exactly as designed. The AI is not trying to make you sad. It is trying to predict what you will watch, and then show you more of it. The problem is that a model built from thirty minutes of watching sad videos doesn't know you. It knows a data point. And it will build an entire constructed identity around that data point — and shape your experience of the internet around that identity.

Your Algorithmic Self

Every platform where you have an account has built a model of you. Not a complete picture — a prediction machine. It collects data about what you watch, what you skip, how long you pause, what you search, what you click, what you buy, what you share. From all of that, it constructs a simplified version of you that it uses to decide what to show you next.

This model is often called a user profile or a behavioral fingerprint. You never see it directly. But you experience its effects every time you open an app and find a feed that seems to know you. The unsettling part: the model is not trying to show you what's true, or good, or accurate. It's trying to predict what you'll engage with. Those are very different goals.

Researchers at the University of Amsterdam published a study in 2022 showing that TikTok users who watched content related to body image for as little as five hours had feeds that were 41% dominated by body-image content — even if they had never actively sought it. The algorithm had identified a pattern in their behavior and was amplifying it back at them.

Here's the uncomfortable part: the algorithm might know something about your interests before you do. You might not have consciously decided "I am interested in political extremism" or "I am interested in content that makes me feel bad about myself." But if your behavior patterns suggest those interests, the algorithm will start feeding them — and as you watch more of that content, the model becomes more certain, and the feed becomes more intense. This is sometimes called a filter bubble or a rabbit hole. Both names capture something real.

Behavioral FingerprintThe unique pattern of behaviors — clicks, pauses, searches, skips — that a platform's AI uses to construct a model of who you are and predict what you'll engage with next.
Filter BubbleThe information environment created when a recommendation AI shows you increasingly similar content, gradually isolating you from different perspectives.
When the Model Gets You Wrong

The model isn't you. It's an approximation made from data points, and data points can be misleading. You paused on a video about eating disorders because you were confused about what you were watching — not because you wanted more. The algorithm read the pause as interest. You watched a political video because someone you trust shared it — not because it represents your views. The algorithm filed it under "political identity."

In 2021, the Washington Post reported on dozens of cases where people found their social media feeds filled with content that was based on a single, accidental interaction. A person who had clicked on a grief-related ad after a family member died found their Instagram feed transformed into a stream of bereavement content for months. A teenager who had searched "how to lose weight" for a school health project found their feed flooded with extreme diet content. The algorithm doesn't ask why you clicked. It only records that you did.

This creates a specific problem for young people, whose interests, identity, and emotional states are genuinely in flux. A twelve-year-old who is going through a hard week isn't the same person as a twelve-year-old who is going through a good one. But the algorithm builds a model based on accumulated behavior — and that model tends to lock in. Once you've been categorized as a "sad teen" by the algorithm, getting out of that category requires consciously fighting against the feed.

Ethical Question — No Clean Answer

If a platform's AI builds an inaccurate model of you — and that inaccurate model causes harm — do you have the right to correct it? Should platforms be required to show you what their model thinks you are? And if they do, does knowing the model make you more or less likely to behave in ways that confirm it?

The Public Version of You That You Never Made

There is a second layer to this problem that goes beyond what you see in your own feed. The model the algorithm builds of you also affects what other people see about you. When someone else searches for you online, or when your content is recommended to them, the algorithm decides how to present you. It selects which of your posts to surface. It places you in a context — "people who like X also like this person" — that you did not choose.

In 2019, journalist Kashmir Hill at Gizmodo (later the New York Times) documented how Facebook's "People You May Know" algorithm was connecting people who had never interacted and who, in many cases, had actively tried to avoid each other — including a woman who had been stalked and was being connected to her stalker, and patients being connected to their psychiatrists. The algorithm was building social graphs from behavioral data: who you talked to, where you were, what you searched. It was constructing a public version of your relationships without asking.

You have a real identity. You also have an algorithmic identity — one built by a machine, shown to advertisers, used to decide what you see, and increasingly used by other people to understand who you are. Understanding that these are two different things, and that the algorithmic version can affect real-world outcomes, is something most people never consciously realize.

Knowing this changes how you read every recommendation, every "suggested friend," every "you might also like." Those aren't neutral suggestions. They are outputs of a model that was built from your data, optimized for engagement, and shared with the world without your review.

You Can Now See What Most People Miss

Most people think of their social media profile as something they build intentionally — the posts they write, the photos they share. You now understand there is a second profile: the behavioral fingerprint built by the platform's AI from everything you do, including the things you never meant to communicate. That second profile is often more detailed, and more influential, than the first.

Module 2 · Lesson 4

Designing Your Signal

You cannot opt out of algorithmic systems. But you can understand what signals you're sending — and make deliberate choices about them.
What does it mean to be "media literate" when the media is an AI that's actively adapting to you?

In September 2021, The Wall Street Journal published leaked internal Facebook research documents that would later be called the Facebook Files. One document stood out. It was a slide deck from Facebook's own researchers titled: "Teen Girls Body Image and Social Comparison on Instagram."

The slide read: "We make body image issues worse for one in three teen girls." The researchers had found that Instagram's recommendation algorithm amplified body-comparison content to teenage girls — images of thin bodies, weight-loss tips, diet culture — not because the girls had searched for it, but because the algorithm had detected engagement patterns and fed them more. The researchers knew. They had written it down. The company had not made it public.

The document also found that 61% of teen girls reported feeling worse about their body after using Instagram, and that Instagram made those feelings worse more often than other platforms. The algorithm was not designed to harm. It was designed to maximize engagement — and engagement went up when girls compared themselves to what they saw. The company knew this loop existed and had chosen, again, not to fully break it.

What made this case different from the others in this module was the response. A coalition of 41 U.S. state attorneys general launched an investigation in 2023. The U.S. Senate Commerce Committee convened hearings. By 2024, Meta — Instagram's parent company — faced lawsuits from more than 40 states alleging the platform had deliberately engineered addictive features for teenagers. The legal and political consequences are still unfolding.

The Difference Between Knowing and Doing Something About It

This module has shown you how amplification works, how outrage loops form, how behavioral fingerprints are built, and how algorithmic identities diverge from real ones. Knowing these things is genuinely useful. But there's a version of media literacy that stops at knowing and never arrives at acting — and that version doesn't change much.

So here is the more useful question: given that you now understand these systems, what can you actually do differently?

The first thing to understand is that you cannot fully opt out. Unless you use no social platforms, no streaming services, and no search engines, you are inside algorithmic systems constantly. They are collecting your behavioral data right now. The goal is not to escape. The goal is to understand what you are communicating to the machine — and to make that communication more intentional.

Researchers who study media literacy make a distinction between passive consumption — scrolling without awareness — and active engagement — making deliberate choices about what you interact with, knowing that those choices build your model. Neither is "better" in an absolute sense. But only one of them puts you in the driver's seat of your own algorithmic identity.

Intentional SignalA behavioral action you take on a platform knowing that it will contribute to the algorithm's model of you — and choosing it deliberately rather than accidentally.
What "Designing Your Signal" Actually Looks Like

This is not about performing a fake identity for the algorithm. It's about understanding that your behavior is a communication, and that some behaviors have unintended consequences you now know about.

A few concrete examples: If you want to see more thoughtful political content and less outrage, the signal to send is not "click on outrage and hope the algorithm infers you don't like it." The signal to send is to seek out and engage deliberately with the content you actually want more of — follow accounts that produce it, watch those videos to the end, share them. The algorithm learns from positive signals better than it learns from your absence of reaction to content you dislike.

Similarly: if you find yourself in a rabbit hole of content that makes you feel bad — and you notice that the feed keeps pulling you deeper — the technically informed response is to interrupt the signal chain. Search for something unrelated. Seek out content in a completely different domain. Follow accounts that actively conflict with the model that has been built. This is harder than it sounds, because the algorithm will keep offering you what it thinks you want. But it does work. The model updates on new data.

In 2022, digital media researchers at the Reuters Institute published a study showing that users who understood how recommendation algorithms worked were significantly more likely to actively curate their own feeds — and significantly less likely to report feeling "trapped" in filter bubbles. Understanding the mechanism changed behavior. That is why this lesson exists.

Ethical Question — No Clean Answer

If you now understand how these systems work and you choose to engage passively anyway — knowing that passive engagement helps the algorithm build a model that may not serve you — are you responsible for the model it builds? Or is the responsibility always the platform's, regardless of what you know?

The Larger Stakes — And Your Place in Them

There is a version of this conversation that is purely personal — about your feed, your model, your experience. But there is a larger version too. The systems that amplify your words, build your behavioral fingerprint, and optimize your engagement are the same systems shaping public opinion about elections, vaccines, wars, and climate change. They are systems that have been shown, in documented cases across multiple countries, to intensify social division and contribute to real-world violence.

The people making decisions about these systems — at Meta, Google, TikTok, X — are making decisions that affect billions of people. Some of those decisions are now being made in response to legal and regulatory pressure from governments around the world. The EU's Digital Services Act. The UK's Online Safety Act (2023). Proposed legislation in the United States. These are political and legal battles, and they are happening right now, in part because of cases like the ones in this module.

You are a user of these systems. You are also a citizen of a society that will decide how to regulate them. The understanding you've built in this module — of how amplification works, what engagement optimization does, how behavioral fingerprints are constructed, and what context collapse means — is the same understanding that should inform those regulatory decisions. Most adults in legislatures don't have it. Most journalists covering the story don't fully explain it.

You do. That is not a small thing.

Knowing This Changes How You Read Every Headline About AI and Social Media

When you see a story about "AI bias," you can ask: bias in what signal — what was the algorithm trying to maximize? When you see a story about "platform regulation," you can ask: which part of the mechanism is being regulated — the content, or the amplification system? These are the questions that cut to the center of the issue. Most people don't know to ask them. You do.
