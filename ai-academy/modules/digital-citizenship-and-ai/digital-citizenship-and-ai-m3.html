Module 3 · Lesson 1

When the Algorithm Decides

How automated systems make life-altering decisions — and who is responsible when they get it wrong.
If a machine made a mistake that ruined someone's life, who is actually to blame?

In the spring of 2017, a teacher named Melanie Mills received a letter that changed everything. After eleven years in the classroom — years of strong evaluations, devoted students, parents who wrote letters praising her — she was being fired. Not by her principal. Not by the school board. By a number.

Houston's school district had adopted a system called EVAAS — the Education Value-Added Assessment System. It used a statistical algorithm to calculate how much each teacher supposedly contributed to student test scores. Melanie's number came out low. The district said the algorithm's output was final. She asked to see how the score was calculated. The company that owned the algorithm said that information was proprietary — a trade secret. She couldn't challenge a number she couldn't see.

Melanie and several colleagues sued. In 2017, a federal judge ruled that the district's process violated their constitutional right to due process — meaning: you can't have a decision made against you by a system you're not allowed to examine. The judge called it a "black box." The teachers had been judged by something no one — not even the district administrators — fully understood.

What Is an Algorithm, Actually?

An algorithm is just a set of instructions. When you follow a recipe — "add two eggs, mix, bake at 350 degrees" — you're following an algorithm. Algorithms themselves are neutral. But when we talk about the algorithms shaping the digital world, we usually mean something more complex: decision-making systems that take in data about you and produce an output that affects your life.

Melanie's EVAAS score was an output. A loan application approval or denial is an output. Whether your social media feed shows you a news story or buries it — that's an output. Whether a parole board software recommends you stay in prison or go free — that is an output with enormous stakes.

The key insight is this: algorithms aren't magic. They were built by people. Those people made choices about what data to use, how much weight to give different factors, and what outcome to optimize for. Every one of those choices reflects a set of values — and sometimes, those values are wrong, incomplete, or biased.

Algorithmic accountability The idea that whoever builds or deploys an algorithm bears responsibility for what it does — including harm it causes.
Black box A system whose internal workings are hidden — you can see what goes in and what comes out, but not how the decision was made.
The COMPAS Case: An Algorithm That Predicted Race

The same year Melanie filed her lawsuit, a journalism investigation called Machine Bias — published by ProPublica in 2016 — exposed a different algorithm being used across American courtrooms. It was called COMPAS (Correctional Offender Management Profiling for Alternative Sanctions). Judges used it to help decide whether a defendant should be kept in jail before trial, or released. It gave each person a "recidivism risk score" — a number predicting how likely they were to commit another crime.

ProPublica analyzed thousands of cases in Broward County, Florida. Their finding: Black defendants were nearly twice as likely as white defendants to be falsely flagged as high-risk. White defendants who later reoffended were more often labeled low-risk. The algorithm was making racially skewed predictions — and judges were using those predictions in real sentencing decisions.

The company that made COMPAS disputed the findings. Researchers argued about the statistics. But one fact was undeniable: a person named Bernard Parker — a Black 18-year-old who had no prior offenses and was caught trying to steal a bicycle — received a higher risk score than a white man named Vernon Prater, who had previously been convicted of armed robbery. Parker never reoffended. Prater did. The algorithm had it exactly backwards.

The Ethical Question

If an algorithm is wrong on average but right more than a human judge would be, is it acceptable to use it? What if the errors fall disproportionately on one group of people? There is no clean answer. Sit with that.

Why "The Computer Did It" Is Not an Answer

Here's a pattern you'll see again and again. Something goes wrong. A person is harmed. And someone in authority says: "We just followed what the system recommended." Or: "The algorithm made that call, not us." This is sometimes called automation bias — the tendency to trust an automated system's output more than human judgment, and to use it as cover when things go badly.

But think about it clearly: someone chose to use that algorithm. Someone decided its output would carry weight in real decisions. Someone decided not to build in an appeals process. Someone decided to protect it as a trade secret rather than open it to scrutiny. Each of those decisions was made by a human being, at a company or a government office. The algorithm didn't choose to exist. The algorithm didn't choose to be used.

You now understand something that most people — including many adults in positions of authority — still haven't worked out: when an algorithm harms someone, there is always a human chain of decisions behind it. Finding who is responsible means tracing that chain. That skill — asking "who built this, who deployed this, who benefits from this, who gets hurt by this" — is one of the most valuable things you can learn about the world you're growing up in.

You Can Now See What Most People Miss

Most people treat algorithm-based decisions as if they were facts of nature — like weather. You now know they're choices made by people with specific interests. Every "the system says so" is actually "someone decided to build a system that says so." That's a fundamentally different thing.

Module 3 · Lesson 2

The Feed Is Not a Mirror

How recommendation algorithms shape what you believe — and how that power has been tested at massive scale.
If the platform decides what you see, can you trust what you think you know?

In January 2012, Facebook's data science team secretly ran an experiment on 689,003 users. For one week, without telling anyone, they altered what showed up in those users' News Feeds. Some users saw more positive posts. Others saw more negative ones. Then the researchers measured whether the users' own posts became more positive or more negative in response.

The result: they did. People who saw more negative content wrote more negative posts. People who saw more positive content wrote more positive ones. The conclusion, published in the journal PNAS in June 2014: emotional states can be transferred to others via emotional contagion through social networks — without any direct interaction, just by controlling what people saw.

When the study became public, the reaction was intense. Users were furious. Researchers debated whether the experiment was ethical — participants had not consented to being part of a psychological experiment. Facebook COO Sheryl Sandberg called it "poorly communicated." The lead researcher, Adam Kramer, posted an apology. But the experiment itself was never retracted. And its core finding — that the platform's algorithm could shift users' emotional states — was never disputed. The company had proved it could do this. The question was what they would do with that knowledge.

What a Recommendation Algorithm Actually Optimizes For

Every platform that shows you content — YouTube, TikTok, Instagram, Spotify, Netflix — has a recommendation algorithm. These algorithms learn your behavior: what you click, how long you watch, what you skip, what makes you stop scrolling. Then they use that data to predict what you'll engage with next.

Engagement is the key word. Engagement means clicks, watch time, shares, comments, reactions. Platforms make money from advertisers, and advertisers pay for attention. So the algorithm's job — the thing it is literally optimized to maximize — is to keep you on the platform as long as possible.

Here's the uncomfortable finding that has come out of internal research at multiple platforms: content that triggers strong emotions tends to generate more engagement than content that is calm, accurate, or nuanced. Outrage, fear, and disgust drive more clicks than contentment or mild interest. This isn't a bug. It's what happens when you optimize for engagement without constraints.

In 2021, a whistleblower named Frances Haugen — a former Facebook data scientist — leaked internal company documents to the Wall Street Journal and testified before the U.S. Senate. She said Facebook's own research showed that its Instagram algorithm was worsening mental health in teenage girls, and that the algorithm's amplification of emotionally extreme content was making political polarization worse. She had the internal documents to prove the company knew. The research had been done. The decisions had been made not to act on it.

Engagement optimization Designing an algorithm to maximize the time and attention users spend on a platform — often by surfacing content that triggers strong emotional responses.
Filter bubble A state where an algorithm shows you only content that matches your existing views, gradually narrowing what information you're exposed to.
The YouTube Radicalization Pipeline

In 2018, researcher Guillaume Chaslot — who had worked on YouTube's recommendation algorithm before being fired — published data showing that YouTube's algorithm had a consistent pattern: it would steer users from mainstream political content toward increasingly extreme content. Start watching a moderate political video, and the "up next" queue would gradually lead you toward more radical versions of the same position.

Chaslot's explanation: extreme content keeps people watching longer. Longer watch time means more ad revenue. The algorithm wasn't "trying" to radicalize anyone — it was doing exactly what it was built to do, which was maximize watch time. Radicalization was a side effect of optimization.

YouTube disputed the specific findings but acknowledged the problem existed and began making changes in 2019 — reducing recommendations of what it called "borderline content." But by 2019, more than two billion people were using YouTube monthly. The scale matters. When an algorithm shapes what two billion people see, even small nudges toward extreme content have real-world consequences.

The Ethical Question

A platform can legally show you whatever it wants in your feed. It isn't lying to you — it's just choosing what to show. Is that manipulation? Is there a difference between a platform that promotes extreme content because it profits from engagement, and a newspaper editor who chooses which stories to put on the front page? Where does curation end and manipulation begin?

Your Feed Is a Product — You Are the Raw Material

Here is the shift in perspective that changes how you use every platform: the content isn't the product. You are the product. Your attention is being sold to advertisers. Your emotional state is the raw material the algorithm works with. The more it can make you feel something — anger, desire, fear, belonging — the more effectively it can monetize your time.

This doesn't mean every platform is evil or that you should delete every app. It means you should be a different kind of user — one who understands the incentive structure you're operating inside. When you find yourself feeling outraged by something you just scrolled past, ask: did this happen because it's genuinely important, or because an algorithm that profits from my outrage decided to show it to me?

Knowing this doesn't make you immune. The Facebook experiment showed that emotional contagion works even on people who aren't paying attention to it. But it does change your relationship to the feed. You're not passively discovering what's happening in the world. You're receiving a curated selection of reality, optimized for someone else's revenue. That's a different thing. And you now know the difference.

Knowing This Changes How You Read Every Headline About AI

Every story about AI shaping public opinion, spreading misinformation, or influencing elections traces back to this mechanism — recommendation algorithms optimizing for engagement. Understanding engagement optimization is the foundation for understanding almost every major AI-and-society debate of the last decade.

Misinformation at Machine Speed

How AI changes the scale, speed, and believability of false information — and what that demands of you as a reader.
When you can no longer trust your own eyes, what do you trust?

On March 25, 2023, an image began spreading across Twitter, Reddit, and Facebook. It showed Pope Francis wearing a large white puffer jacket — the kind you might see a rapper or a street-fashion influencer wearing. The image was striking, surprising, and immediately shareable. Millions of people saw it. Many shared it. News outlets wrote about the Vatican's apparent style evolution.

It was completely fake. A Chicago construction worker named Pablo Xavier had generated the image using Midjourney, an AI image tool, while recovering from a dental procedure. He uploaded it to Reddit as an experiment. Within hours it had escaped into the broader internet, detached from any context, treated as a real photograph. Xavier had not intended to deceive anyone. The image simply spread faster than the truth could follow.

This was not the most dangerous case of AI-generated misinformation in 2023 — not even close. But it was the most widely noticed, because it was funny and harmless and gave people a clear moment to realize: I believed something that didn't exist. For those who shared it, that was a useful lesson. For those who never found out it was fake, the Pope still wears that coat.

Three Ways AI Has Changed Misinformation

1. Speed and scale. Before AI generation tools, creating a convincing fake image required skill and time. Creating a fake video of a real person required a professional production team. Now a single person with access to free tools can generate hundreds of convincing fake images, videos, or news articles in hours. The bottleneck used to be production. Now it's distribution — and recommendation algorithms have already solved that problem.

2. Believability. The classic advice was "look for the blurry edges" — early AI images had telltale artifacts. That advice is already outdated. In 2023, researchers at MIT found that people could only correctly identify AI-generated faces 48.2% of the time — slightly worse than flipping a coin. For realistic still images, human detection has essentially failed. Video is harder to fake convincingly, but that gap is narrowing.

3. Personalization. AI can now generate misinformation targeted at specific individuals. Scam calls that use AI voice cloning to sound like a family member asking for help are already documented. In 2023, a family in Arizona received a phone call that sounded exactly like their teenage daughter, saying she had been kidnapped and asking for ransom. The FBI confirmed it was an AI-generated voice clone. The daughter was safe at home. The call was constructed from audio scraped from her public social media.

Deepfake AI-generated media — usually video or audio — that realistically depicts a real person saying or doing something they never said or did.
Synthetic media Any image, video, audio, or text generated or significantly altered by AI — not captured from the real world.
The Liar's Dividend

Legal scholars Bobby Chesney and Danielle Citron coined a term in 2019 that has become one of the most important concepts for understanding AI and truth: the liar's dividend. Here is what it means.

Once people know that convincing fake videos can be created, real videos can be dismissed as fake. If a politician is recorded saying something genuinely awful, they can claim the recording is AI-generated — and a portion of the audience will believe them. The existence of deepfake technology doesn't just enable new lies. It provides cover for real events to be denied. The liar's dividend is the benefit bad actors get from a world where nothing can be verified at a glance.

This is already happening. In 2023, multiple political figures facing video evidence of misconduct claimed the footage was AI-generated. Courts are beginning to grapple with how to authenticate video evidence. Journalists have to spend significant resources verifying footage before publishing it. The mere possibility of fakes has undermined the evidentiary value of real recordings.

The Ethical Question

Some researchers argue that AI detection tools — systems that can identify whether an image was AI-generated — are the solution. Others point out that detection tools are always slightly behind generation tools: by the time you can detect yesterday's fakes, today's are already undetectable. If we can't reliably detect synthetic media, should there be laws requiring AI-generated content to be labeled? Who enforces that? What happens when the label is removed? There is no clean answer. The tools that create the problem and the tools that solve it are being built by the same people.

What Responsible Information Consumption Looks Like Now

The old rules — "check if the website looks professional," "see how many people are sharing it," "look for grammatical errors" — are all now obsolete or nearly so. AI-generated text has near-perfect grammar. AI-generated news sites look identical to real ones. Something being widely shared proves only that it's good at triggering engagement, not that it's true.

What works now is a different set of habits. Lateral reading — opening a new tab and searching for what other sources say about the same thing, rather than reading the original source more carefully — is what professional fact-checkers actually do. Source tracing — finding where a piece of media first appeared before it was shared — can often reveal whether it was produced in good faith or not. And perhaps most importantly: slowing down before sharing. The moment something triggers a strong emotional reaction is precisely the moment to slow down, not speed up. That emotional reaction may be the result of good content design, not genuine importance.

You now understand why this matters at a level most people never reach: the AI tools that generate synthetic media and the recommendation algorithms that distribute it are operating in the same ecosystem, optimizing for the same thing — your engagement. They are extraordinarily good at what they do. Knowing that is the beginning of being a reader who can operate in this environment without being used by it.

You Can Now See What Most People Miss

The liar's dividend means that the problem of AI-generated misinformation isn't only about fake things being believed. It's also about real things being doubted. Understanding both directions of that problem — and why they're caused by the same technology — puts you significantly ahead of most adults discussing this topic publicly.

Module 3 · Lesson 4

Who Gets to Make the Rules?

How AI governance actually works — who makes the decisions, who is excluded from them, and why that matters for everyone.
If the people building powerful technology are also the people writing the rules for it, is that a problem?

In December 2023, negotiators from the European Parliament and EU member states met in Brussels for three days of intense, closed-door negotiations. They were finalizing the EU AI Act — the world's first comprehensive law specifically governing artificial intelligence. For months, the draft had largely addressed technical AI applications like credit scoring and medical diagnostics. Then, at the last minute, something new demanded to be addressed: systems like ChatGPT and image generators — what negotiators were calling "general-purpose AI."

The problem was urgent. These systems were already deployed to hundreds of millions of users. They could generate text, images, code, voice — anything. The law had to decide: how do you regulate something that can do almost anything? The negotiations nearly collapsed twice. France, Germany, and Italy — home to major AI startups — pushed hard to exempt powerful AI models from heavy oversight, arguing that strict rules would hurt European competitiveness against American and Chinese companies.

The final agreement was reached on December 9, 2023. It imposed transparency requirements on the most powerful AI systems and banned certain applications entirely — like real-time facial recognition in public spaces by law enforcement, with exceptions. But the exact line between "powerful enough to regulate heavily" and "not powerful enough" was drawn in ways that critics said conveniently excluded several existing commercial systems from the toughest rules.

Who was in the room? Elected officials, government lawyers, and industry lobbyists. Who was not in the room? Users. Civil society groups that had advocated for stronger protections said they had been largely sidelined. The law that would govern AI for hundreds of millions of people was written in negotiations they could not attend.

Three Models of AI Governance

When societies have to govern a new, powerful technology, they basically have three options. Understanding these options lets you evaluate any governance proposal you encounter.

1. Government regulation. Elected legislatures write laws; regulatory agencies enforce them. This is slow — laws take years to pass and longer to update. It's also democratic in principle: elected officials are accountable to voters. The risk is that technical experts who work at AI companies often know far more about how the technology works than the lawmakers writing rules about it. This is sometimes called the regulatory lag problem.

2. Industry self-regulation. Companies set their own guidelines and enforce them internally. This is fast and technically sophisticated. It's also subject to obvious conflicts of interest: the company setting the rules is also the company that profits from how those rules are drawn. When Meta, Google, and OpenAI publish "responsible AI principles," they are self-regulating. There is no independent enforcement mechanism unless governments create one.

3. Technical standards and professional norms. Engineers, researchers, and professional bodies agree on shared standards — similar to how doctors have medical ethics standards, or how engineers have safety codes. This can be effective in specific technical domains but struggles with questions of values and power, which aren't technical problems.

In practice, AI governance today is a mixture of all three — with industry self-regulation currently doing the most work, government regulation slowly catching up, and technical standards providing a foundation. The EU AI Act is significant precisely because it's an attempt to make government regulation meaningful at a time when industry self-regulation has faced serious credibility challenges.

Regulatory lag The gap between when a technology becomes powerful and when effective laws governing it are in place — during which the technology operates largely under its creators' own rules.
AI governance The set of rules, laws, standards, and norms that determine how AI systems are built, deployed, audited, and held accountable for their effects.
The Representation Problem

In 2022, a research team led by Abeba Birhane — an AI ethics researcher at Trinity College Dublin — published an analysis of who was making AI ethics decisions at major institutions: standards bodies, company ethics boards, government advisory panels, and research institutions. The findings were stark. These bodies were overwhelmingly composed of people from wealthy countries, working at well-funded Western institutions, with academic training in computer science or law — and they were making decisions about how AI systems would work for billions of people in contexts they had limited direct experience with.

This matters because the values built into an AI system reflect the values of the people who built it. An AI system trained primarily on English-language internet data, optimized for engagement patterns common among American users, and governed by ethics frameworks written by European and American academics will produce different outputs — and different harms — when deployed in different cultural contexts.

At an institutional level, this is why calls for "diverse AI governance" are not just about representation for its own sake. They're about the quality of the rules themselves. A governing body that includes people who will actually be affected by a system's errors — not just people who profit from its deployment — will write better rules. Whether that happens depends on who has power in the governance process.

The Ethical Question

Some AI researchers argue that AI development is moving too fast for effective democratic governance — that by the time a law passes, the technology it regulates has been superseded. They suggest that a small group of technically sophisticated experts should have authority to make rapid governance decisions, even without democratic accountability. Others say that is exactly the kind of technocratic power concentration that democratic institutions were built to prevent. Both positions have serious arguments behind them. Neither resolves cleanly.

What You Can Actually Do

This is the lesson in a module called "Responsibility in a World of Algorithms" — so it's worth being direct about what responsibility looks like from where you're standing.

You are not powerless. The governance structures being built right now — the laws, the standards, the professional norms — are mostly new. Many of the people writing them are figuring it out as they go. The fields of AI ethics, AI policy, and algorithmic accountability are young enough that entering them is genuinely possible for someone who starts now. The EU AI Act that passed in December 2023 will require significant revision within a few years. The frameworks being used to evaluate AI systems are contested. The field is open.

More immediately: being an informed, critical user of AI systems matters at scale. When enough people understand how recommendation algorithms work and push back against the emotional manipulation they rely on, platforms have commercial incentives to change. When enough people understand algorithmic accountability and demand transparency, political pressure for regulation builds. When young people articulate why these issues matter — in school, online, eventually in professional contexts — they shape the conversation.

You now understand the full picture: how algorithms make consequential decisions, how recommendation systems shape belief, how synthetic media undermines truth, and how the rules governing all of this are made by a small group of people with their own interests. That understanding puts you in a different position than someone who just uses these tools. You're not just a user. You Can Now See What Most People Miss

The governance of AI is not a technical question — it's a political one. Questions about who gets to use AI, how it can be deployed, what harms are acceptable and to whom, who bears the cost of errors — these are not questions engineers can answer alone. They are questions about values, power, and democratic accountability. Understanding that is what it means to be an informed participant in the world these systems are reshaping.

Who Gets to Make the Rules? · 5 questions
1. What was the key controversy in the EU AI Act negotiations of December 2023?
Correct. The last-minute controversy was how to handle general-purpose AI (like large language models), with France, Germany, and Italy pushing for exemptions that would protect their domestic AI industries — nearly collapsing the negotiations twice.
The core controversy was how to regulate powerful general-purpose AI systems, with major EU economies pushing for lighter regulation to stay competitive with US and Chinese AI companies.
2. What is "regulatory lag" and why does it matter for AI governance?
Correct. Regulatory lag describes the period — often years — during which powerful technology operates before effective governance exists. During that gap, the companies building the technology effectively set their own rules, which creates conflicts of interest.
Regulatory lag is the gap in time between when technology becomes powerful and when laws catching up to it take effect. During that gap, self-regulation by the industry fills the space — with all the conflicts of interest that implies.
3. Why did researcher Abeba Birhane's 2022 analysis argue that AI ethics bodies lacked diversity — and why does that matter for the quality of AI governance?
Correct. The representation problem isn't about optics — it's about the quality of the rules. People who haven't experienced the harms a system can cause will write different (typically weaker) protections against those harms than people who have.
The argument is that the values built into governance frameworks reflect the experience of the people writing them. When those people are overwhelmingly from wealthy Western contexts, the frameworks will have blind spots about harms in other contexts — making the governance worse, not just less representative.
4. A government is deciding whether to regulate a new AI system. The company that makes it argues: "Our system is too complex for legislators to understand, so regulation should be left to technical experts inside the industry." What is the strongest counter-argument to this position?
Correct. They require democratic accountability precisely because the industry has a conflict of interest.
The strongest counter-argument is that AI governance questions are fundamentally about values — who is harmed, whose interests count, what trade-offs are acceptable — and those questions require democratic accountability, not just technical expertise.
5. Which of the following scenarios best illustrates "industry self-regulation" as a form of AI governance?
Correct. Industry self-regulation is when companies set and enforce their own rules. The critical feature — and the critical weakness — is that there is no independent enforcement mechanism. Companies can write principles that are technically complied with while the underlying harms continue.
Industry self-regulation means companies write the rules for themselves. The key feature is the absence of external enforcement — the company sets the standard and decides whether it's being met.

1. What did the federal judge's ruling in the 2017 Houston EVAAS case establish?
Correct. The ruling established that being fired based on a system whose workings you can't examine is a violation of due process — a foundational case in algorithmic accountability law.
The ruling was specifically about due process — the constitutional requirement that you be able to examine and challenge decisions made against you. A hidden algorithm fails that standard.
2. In the COMPAS recidivism scoring case, ProPublica found the algorithm most significantly failed by:
Correct. The racial disparity in false-positive rates — Black defendants falsely labeled high-risk at roughly twice the rate of white defendants — was the central finding of ProPublica's 2016 Machine Bias investigation.
The key problem was a racial disparity in false positives: Black defendants who did not reoffend were labeled high-risk at nearly twice the rate of white defendants who did not reoffend.
3. "Automation bias" best describes:
Correct. Automation bias is about human behavior — the tendency to defer to automated systems and to say "the computer decided" to avoid responsibility for outcomes.
Automation bias describes how humans over-trust and hide behind automated outputs. It's about the psychology of decision-makers, not the mathematics of algorithms.
4. Facebook's 2012 emotional contagion experiment was ethically controversial primarily because:
Correct. The core ethical objection was the lack of informed consent. Standard research ethics require that people know they are participating in a study. Facebook ran a psychological experiment on nearly 700,000 people without telling any of them.
The primary ethical problem was that 689,003 real users were enrolled in a psychological experiment without their knowledge or consent — a fundamental violation of research ethics principles.
5. Guillaume Chaslot's research found that YouTube's recommendation algorithm escalated content toward extremism as a byproduct of:
Correct. The radicalization was an unintended consequence of watch-time optimization. The algorithm wasn't trying to radicalize anyone — it just learned that increasingly intense content produced the longer viewing sessions it was rewarded for producing.
Watch-time optimization was the cause. Extreme content kept people watching longer, so the algorithm — rewarded for watch time — gradually learned to recommend it. Radicalization was an unintended side effect of the objective function, not a design goal.
6. What is a "filter bubble" in the context of recommendation algorithms?
Correct. Filter bubbles form when algorithms consistently feed you content matching your existing preferences and beliefs, gradually reducing your exposure to different viewpoints — a problem for both individual understanding and social cohesion.
A filter bubble is the narrowing effect of recommendation algorithms — showing you increasingly similar content until the range of perspectives you encounter becomes very small.

Correct. The image was created without intent to deceive, and yet spread to millions of viewers as an apparently genuine photograph. The mechanism — virality detaching content from context — is the same whether the creator intended deception or not.
The significance is the spread mechanism: synthetic media detaches from context and travels as apparent reality, regardless of the creator's intent. Most people who saw the image never saw the correction.
8. According to the lesson, what is the "liar's dividend" — and which of these is an example of it?
Correct. The liar's dividend is specifically about real evidence being denied — the existence of deepfakes creates a credible denial strategy that can be applied to genuine recordings.
The liar's dividend is when a real recording or piece of evidence can be dismissed as AI-generated precisely because AI generation is now possible. It's about denial of truth, not creation of falsehood.
9. A fact-checker using "lateral reading" to verify a viral claim would:
Correct. Lateral reading means leaving the original source and going wide — finding what other sources say. It's what professional fact-checkers actually do, and research shows it's significantly more effective than careful reading of a single source.
Lateral reading means going out to other sources immediately, rather than spending more time with the original. More time on the original doesn't help if the original is designed to be convincing.

Correct. Self-regulation means the company setting the standard is also the company deciding whether it's being met — with no external enforcement. That's the structural conflict of interest that makes self-regulation insufficient on its own for high-stakes AI systems.
Self-regulation means no external enforcement — the company writes the rules and judges its own compliance. The conflict of interest is structural and inherent to the model.

The representation argument is about the quality of the rules. Governance bodies that don't include people who will be harmed by AI systems' errors will consistently underprotect those people — because they don't have direct experience of the risks.
