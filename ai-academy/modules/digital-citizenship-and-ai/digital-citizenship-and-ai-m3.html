Module 3 · Lesson 1

The Feed That Shaped an Election

How recommendation algorithms became the most powerful editors in history — and why you're now their subject
When a machine decides what you see, who is responsible for what you believe?

In 2017, a UN investigator named Marzuki Darusman sat in front of a screen and scrolled. He was reading Facebook posts from Myanmar — a country in Southeast Asia where almost no one had owned a smartphone five years earlier. When affordable phones arrived, millions of people got online for the first time. For most of them, Facebook was the internet. It was where they read news, talked to family, and found out what was happening in the world.

What Darusman found made his team sick. The platform's algorithm — the automated system that decided what content to show each user — had been amplifying posts that called for violence against the Rohingya, a Muslim ethnic minority. The algorithm wasn't doing this because it was evil. It was doing it because those posts generated the most engagement: shares, comments, reactions. Outrage travels faster than calm. The system had learned that, and it optimized for it.

By 2018, the UN concluded that Facebook's algorithm had played a "determining role" in spreading content that contributed to what the agency called genocide — the systematic killing of over 10,000 Rohingya people and the displacement of more than 700,000. Facebook later admitted it had been too slow to address the problem. The algorithm had run largely unsupervised for years.

A machine made choices. People died. And almost nobody outside of Myanmar had noticed it was happening.

What Is a Recommendation Algorithm, Exactly?

An algorithm is just a set of instructions — a recipe a computer follows to produce a result. A recommendation algorithm is a specific type: it watches what you do (what you click, how long you pause, what you share), and uses that data to predict what you'll engage with next. Then it shows you more of that.

Think of it like this: you're at a party and there's a very attentive host. Every time you laugh at a joke about soccer, they introduce you to another person who talks about soccer. Every time you look uncomfortable when someone mentions a sad topic, they steer you away from those people. Within an hour, the host has built you a custom bubble — a group of people chosen entirely to keep you comfortable and entertained.

That sounds nice, but here's the problem: the host's goal isn't your wellbeing. It's to keep you at the party as long as possible. Because longer time at the party means more ads shown to you, which means more money for the company running the event.

Every major platform you use — YouTube, TikTok, Instagram, Facebook, Spotify, even Google Search — uses algorithms like this. They are making thousands of decisions about what reality looks like to you, every single day, without asking permission.

AlgorithmA set of step-by-step instructions that a computer follows to complete a task — like deciding which video to show you next.
EngagementAny action a user takes: clicking, liking, commenting, sharing, or even just pausing to watch. Platforms measure this obsessively because more engagement means more ad revenue.
OptimizationWhen a system is designed to maximize one specific goal — like watch time — even if doing so creates problems elsewhere.
The Engagement Trap: Why Outrage Spreads Faster Than Truth

In 2018, a MIT Media Lab researcher named Sinan Aral led a team that analyzed 126,000 news stories shared on Twitter between 2006 and 2017. The finding was stark: false news spread six times faster than true news. And it wasn't bots doing most of the spreading. It was real people.

Why? Because false stories tend to be novel and surprising. They trigger strong emotions — shock, anger, disgust, fear. And those are exactly the emotions that make us hit share before we think. The algorithm learns this pattern quickly. Content that makes people feel strongly gets shown to more people. Content that makes people feel strongly often turns out to be distorted, misleading, or entirely invented.

This is the engagement trap: the system that decides what you see isn't trying to make you informed. It's trying to make you react. And it's been remarkably successful at its actual goal, even as it's failed completely at the side effect of keeping you accurately informed.

You now understand something that most adults using these platforms every day have never fully confronted: the feed you scroll is not neutral. It is a machine running an optimization experiment, and you are the variable being adjusted.

The Ethical Question

If a platform's algorithm causes real harm — contributing to violence, spreading dangerous health misinformation, pushing teenagers toward eating disorders — but the company claims it didn't intend any of that, are they still responsible? At what point does "we didn't mean to" stop being an acceptable answer?

Platform Responsibility: What Does It Actually Mean?

In October 2021, a whistleblower named Frances Haugen walked into the U.S. Senate with thousands of internal Facebook documents. She had worked as a product manager inside the company, and what she described to senators was a pattern: Facebook repeatedly discovered, through its own research, that its products were causing harm — particularly to teenage girls' mental health — and repeatedly chose not to fix it because fixing it would reduce engagement.

This is the institutional-level version of the problem. It's not just that algorithms are imperfect tools. It's that the companies building them often know what the tools are doing, measure the harm, and then weigh it against profit. The decisions that shape what a billion people see every day are being made by relatively small groups of engineers and executives, largely without democratic oversight.

In the European Union, the Digital Services Act came into force in 2023, requiring large platforms to assess and reduce the risks their recommendation systems create. It's one of the first serious attempts by a government to hold platforms accountable for their algorithms. In the U.S., no equivalent law exists yet. The debate is active right now, in legislatures and courtrooms, about who should be responsible and how.

These are decisions being made while you are growing up. The version of the internet that exists when you're 25 will depend partly on what societies decide in the next few years — and understanding what's actually at stake is how you participate in that decision.

You Can Now See What Most People Miss

When someone says "the algorithm showed it to me," they're describing a machine that was built to maximize your time and reaction — not your understanding. Every headline, video recommendation, and trending topic you encounter from this point forward carries that context. You're not just a viewer anymore. You're someone who understands the mechanism behind the curtain.

When AI Gets It Wrong — and Someone Pays

Automated systems make consequential mistakes. Understanding who is harmed — and who answers for it — is the defining accountability question of the AI era.
If a system trained on biased data produces a biased decision, who is responsible: the data, the system, or the people who deployed it?

In 2016, a journalist named Julia Angwin and her team at ProPublica published an investigation called "Machine Bias." They had obtained the risk scores assigned to over 7,000 people arrested in Broward County, Florida, by a software tool called COMPAS — Correctional Offender Management Profiling for Alternative Sanctions.

COMPAS was being used by judges and parole boards to help decide how long someone should stay in prison or whether they should be released. It produced a score from 1 to 10 predicting a person's likelihood of reoffending. The higher the score, the longer they might stay locked up. Judges weren't required to follow it, but in practice the scores were treated as authoritative — an objective machine verdict.

What Angwin's team found: Black defendants were nearly twice as likely as white defendants to be falsely flagged as high-risk — meaning the algorithm predicted they would reoffend when they didn't. White defendants were more often incorrectly rated low-risk when they later did reoffend. The algorithm wasn't deliberately racist. But it had been trained on historical criminal justice data — data that reflected decades of racially unequal policing and sentencing. It learned the patterns in that data, and then reproduced them at scale, inside courtrooms, wrapped in the authority of a computer score.

A Black man named Bernard Parker and a white man named Dylan Fugett had both been arrested for minor drug offenses at similar ages, with similar records. COMPAS rated Parker a 10 — maximum risk. Fugett: a 3. Parker had no subsequent violent offenses. Fugett was later arrested for an armed robbery.

What Is Algorithmic Bias?

A bias in everyday language means a leaning or prejudice — when someone has a bias, they tend to favor one thing over another unfairly. Algorithmic bias works similarly but has a specific origin: it comes from the data used to train the system, or from the choices made in designing what the system is trying to predict.

Here's a simplified version of how it happens: An AI system for hiring gets trained on 10 years of a company's successful employees. Those employees, historically, happened to be mostly men. The system learns that "successful employee" looks like: male, certain university background, certain extracurricular activities. When it evaluates new applicants, it scores women lower — not because it was told to, but because the pattern it extracted from the data happened to encode that correlation.

This exact scenario happened at Amazon in 2018. The company had built an AI recruiting tool that was eventually shut down because it was systematically downgrading resumes that included the word "women's" — as in "women's chess club" — because the training data reflected a male-dominated workforce.

The system didn't hate women. It was doing exactly what it was trained to do: reproduce the patterns in the data. The problem is that the data contained a historical inequality, and the system learned to project that inequality into the future.

Algorithmic biasWhen an automated system produces results that systematically favor or disadvantage certain groups, usually because of patterns in the training data or the design choices made by the people building the system.
Training dataThe examples an AI system learns from. If those examples contain biases or imbalances from the real world, the AI is likely to learn and repeat those biases.
The High-Stakes Domains: Where Bias Has Real Consequences

Algorithmic bias would be a minor annoyance if AI systems were only recommending movies. The problem is that these systems are now embedded in decisions that determine the shape of people's lives.

In healthcare, researchers at UC Berkeley published a study in 2019 showing that an algorithm used by major U.S. hospitals to identify which patients needed additional care was less likely to flag Black patients even when they were actually sicker. The algorithm had been trained to use healthcare spending as a proxy for health needs — but because Black patients had historically received less care (and therefore spent less), the system underestimated how sick they were. An estimated 28% of patients who should have been flagged were missed, and the disparity fell disproportionately on Black patients.

In facial recognition, a 2018 study by MIT researcher Joy Buolamwini — published as part of her "Gender Shades" project — showed that commercial facial recognition systems from major tech companies misidentified the gender of darker-skinned women up to 34.7% of the time, while misidentifying lighter-skinned men only 0.8% of the time. Police departments across the U.S. were using these systems. In 2020, a Black man named Robert Williams in Detroit was wrongfully arrested because a facial recognition system incorrectly matched his face to surveillance footage. He was held in police custody for 30 hours before the error was caught.

Each of these cases involves the same structure: a system trained on imperfect data, deployed in a high-stakes context, producing a result that someone had to live with.

The Ethical Question

If an AI system is more accurate on average — getting decisions right for, say, 90% of people — but is consistently wrong for a specific group (say, wrong 40% of the time for Black defendants, 10% for white defendants), is it ethical to use it? "More accurate on average" sounds good, but averages can hide who bears the cost of the errors.

Who Is Responsible When an Algorithm Causes Harm?

This is the hardest question, and it doesn't have a clean answer yet — legally or ethically. Here are the layers:

The data collectors gathered historical records that encoded existing inequalities. Are they responsible for the patterns their data contains?

The engineers built the system and chose what it would optimize for. Did they test it adequately for disparate impact before deployment?

The company sold or licensed the tool to courts, hospitals, and employers. Did they disclose its limitations? Did they profit from its use while others paid the price of its errors?

The institutions — judges, hospital administrators, HR departments — chose to use the system and in many cases treated its outputs as authoritative rather than as one input among many. Did they abdicate responsibility by deferring to a machine?

The COMPAS company, Northpointe, argued that their tool was never meant to be the sole basis for decisions — it was a supplementary tool. Many of the judges using it didn't fully understand how it worked. The people scoring the highest on it had no way to challenge the score or even know it was being used. This is the accountability gap at the center of algorithmic systems: when something goes wrong, responsibility gets so distributed across layers of people and systems that no single actor is clearly at fault — and the person harmed is left with no one to hold responsible.

Knowing This Changes How You Read Every Story About AI

Whenever you see a headline like "AI system to replace human judges" or "AI hiring tool improves efficiency," you now have a question that most readers don't think to ask: What is this system trained on? Who was tested in the validation data? And who bears the cost when it gets something wrong? That's not skepticism for its own sake — it's the minimum reasonable question for any system making consequential decisions about human lives.

The Data You Give Away Without Knowing

Every tap, scroll, pause, and purchase generates a profile. Understanding what that profile contains — and who uses it — is not optional knowledge anymore.
If a company knows more about your habits than your closest friend does, what does that mean for your ability to make genuinely free choices?

In 2014, a Facebook researcher named Aleksandr Kogan built a personality quiz app called "thisisyourdigitallife." About 270,000 people downloaded it and agreed to share their Facebook data with Kogan's research. Standard enough. But Kogan's app also harvested data from those users' friends — people who had never downloaded the app, never agreed to anything, and had no idea their information was being collected. Through this method, he accumulated data on 87 million Facebook users.

Kogan then sold that data to a political consulting firm called Cambridge Analytica. The firm used it to build detailed psychological profiles — dividing people into categories based on traits like openness, conscientiousness, and anxiety — and then delivered targeted political advertising for the 2016 U.S. presidential election and the Brexit referendum in the UK. The ads weren't shown randomly. They were aimed at specific psychological types in specific swing districts, with messaging tuned to match the fears and values the profiles predicted.

In 2018, a whistleblower named Christopher Wylie — who had worked inside Cambridge Analytica — went public with the details. His description: "We exploited Facebook to harvest millions of people's profiles and built models to exploit what we knew about them and target their inner demons."

Facebook was fined $5 billion by the U.S. Federal Trade Commission in 2019 — the largest fine the FTC had ever issued. It barely covered 10% of Facebook's annual revenue that year.

What Data Is Actually Being Collected About You?

Most people think of their data as: what they post online. Photos, status updates, comments. That's the tiny visible part. The vast majority of the data collected about you is behavioral — it's the trace your actions leave, not the statements you choose to make.

Here is a partial list of what a typical smartphone app can collect, often with a single permission click: your location history (a precise record of everywhere you've been); your contacts (who you know); your microphone data; your browsing history; how long you look at each item on a screen (dwell time); which notifications you respond to and which you ignore; when you wake up and when you go to sleep based on phone activity; your heart rate if you use a fitness app; and your spending habits if a payment app is present.

A company like Google processes this kind of data across billions of users. What it produces isn't just ads — it's a predictive model of human behavior that is, in many respects, more accurate than what people know about themselves. In one famous example, the retailer Target was able to predict which customers were pregnant, and approximately how far along, based on shifts in their purchasing patterns — before those customers had told anyone.

The term for this is the data economy: a system in which personal behavioral data is the commodity that makes platforms profitable. You don't pay money for most apps and platforms. You pay with your data — and most people have no detailed idea what they've paid, to whom, or what it's being used for.

Behavioral dataInformation about what you do — when you click, how long you look, where you go — as distinct from information about what you say or share intentionally.
Data brokerA company that buys and sells personal data, often combining information from many sources to build detailed profiles on individuals — usually without those individuals' knowledge.
Psychographic targetingDelivering specific messages to people based on inferred psychological traits — like their values, fears, or personality — rather than just their demographics like age or location.
Consent, Manipulation, and the Fine Print Nobody Reads

In theory, you consent to data collection when you click "I agree" on a terms of service document. In practice, those documents are deliberately designed to be unreadable. A 2019 study found that reading every privacy policy for every app and website the average American uses would take approximately 76 work days per year. The documents are written in legal language by lawyers whose job is to grant companies maximum latitude while creating legal cover for the word "consent."

A Norwegian consumer rights organization called Forbrukerrådet produced a report in 2020 called "Out of Control," documenting how 10 popular apps shared personal data with 135 different companies — including names, locations, and "sexual interests" — often with no clear indication to users. Grindr, a dating app, was found to be sharing users' HIV status with two advertising technology companies.

The question of whether this constitutes genuine consent — or sophisticated coercion dressed up as a checkbox — is genuinely contested. At an institutional level, the EU's General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR), enacted in 2018, tried to address this by requiring that consent be "freely given, specific, informed, and unambiguous." Companies responded with a wave of cookie consent banners that technically comply but are designed to make it as easy as possible to accept everything and as annoying as possible to decline. The problem of real versus nominal consent remains unsolved.

The Ethical Question

If companies design consent processes that are technically legal but practically impossible for ordinary people to navigate meaningfully — is the consent real? And if a 12-year-old clicks "I agree" on a terms of service they haven't read and couldn't fully understand, what moral obligation does the company have?

The Power Asymmetry and What You Can Do With It

The data economy rests on a profound imbalance: companies know an enormous amount about you, and you know almost nothing about what they know. This isn't an accident — it's the design. Information asymmetry is where the profit lives.

Understanding this doesn't mean you need to delete all your apps or live off the grid. What it means is that you can start making more deliberate choices. Reviewing what permissions each app actually needs. Understanding that "free" apps are products where you are the commodity. Recognizing when a piece of content feels uncannily targeted and asking why. Using browsers and settings that limit tracking where it's practical.

At the policy level, data rights are one of the most consequential debates happening in legislatures worldwide right now. The California Consumer Privacy Act (2020) gives California residents the right to know what data companies hold about them and request its deletion. The EU's GDPR goes further. The U.S. federal government has no comprehensive equivalent yet. These debates are about power — specifically, who has the right to build detailed models of your behavior and what they're allowed to do with those models.

You are, right now, one of the most data-rich demographics in history: a young person who has grown up online, who carries a smartphone, who uses apps every single day. The profile that exists on you somewhere is extensive. Understanding that that profile exists, and who benefits from it, is no longer a privacy-nerd topic. It's foundational digital citizenship.

You Can Now See What Most People Miss

Most people experience targeted ads or eerily accurate recommendations as a kind of technological magic — spooky but vague. You now know the mechanism: behavioral data, aggregated into profiles, sold across a market of data brokers, and used to predict and influence your choices. "Free" is a price disguised as no price. Every app you use is a transaction. You're just not the one being paid.

2. A 2019 study found that reading all privacy policies for apps the average American uses would take 76 work days per year. What is the most significant implication of this fact?
Precisely. The legal framework for data collection leans heavily on "you agreed." But if the agreement is designed to be unreadable, in documents that would take months to process, by people who have no real alternative (if you want the app, you must agree), then "consent" is functioning as a legal fig leaf rather than a genuine expression of informed choice.

3. Target was able to predict customer pregnancies before those customers had told anyone. Why is this capability significant beyond just being an effective advertising tool?
Right. The significance is the breach of informational autonomy — the idea that you have the right to decide what personal information you share and when. But Target found out anyway, from watching you.
The key issue is what data inference means for privacy. If companies can infer deeply personal information from patterns in public behavior, then "I didn't post it" is no longer sufficient protection. 4. A classmate says: "I don't care if apps track me because I have nothing to hide." What is the most substantive problem with this reasoning?
Strong answer. "Nothing to hide" conflates privacy with criminality — as if the only reason for privacy is to conceal wrongdoing. But privacy is about autonomy: the right to control your own narrative, to not have your words taken out of context, to not have your location or health data available to parties who might use it to harm you in ways you haven't anticipated. "Nothing to hide" now can become "something to hide" when the laws or the people in power change.
The "nothing to hide" argument is a common but flawed position. It assumes the data will only be used by trustworthy actors for benign purposes — but data can be stolen, subpoenaed, resold, or used in contexts that didn't exist when it was collected. Privacy isn't about secrets; it's about who gets to decide what information about you flows where and for what purpose.
5. Grindr was found to be sharing users' HIV status with two advertising companies. What specific type of harm does this represent that goes beyond ordinary privacy intrusion?
Exactly. This is what privacy scholars call a "contextual integrity violation" — information shared in one context (a health/dating app) flowing to an entirely different context (advertisers) in ways the user could not have anticipated and would not have consented to if asked. HIV status carries specific stigma and discrimination risk in employment, insurance, and housing. The harm isn't theoretical; it's about real power that can now be exercised over someone's life.
The harm here is concrete. HIV status is among the most sensitive categories of personal information — it affects employment opportunities, insurance eligibility, and personal relationships in documented ways due to stigma and discrimination. Sharing it with advertisers without consent isn't just an abstract privacy violation; it hands third parties leverage over someone's life that person never agreed to give.

Module 3 · Lesson 4

Who Builds the Rules — and Who Gets Left Out

The people designing AI systems are not a neutral cross-section of humanity. The values encoded in these systems reflect whose voices were and weren't in the room.
When a small group of people builds systems that affect billions, what obligations do they have to the people who were never asked?

In November 2018, thousands of Google employees walked out of their offices in cities around the world — Tokyo, London, Berlin, Singapore, New York, San Francisco. They were protesting how the company had handled sexual harassment allegations against senior executives. But embedded in their demands was something broader: they wanted a say in what Google builds, not just how it treats its employees.

The following year, a Google AI ethics researcher named Timnit Gebru co-authored a paper warning about potential harms in large language models — the type of AI that would eventually power products like ChatGPT and Google's own Bard. In December 2020, she was fired. Google claimed she had violated company policy. Gebru said she was pushed out for raising concerns about AI systems that could amplify bias and be used to surveil marginalized communities.

The story made headlines globally — not just as a workplace dispute, but as a symptom of a structural problem: the people most likely to raise concerns about AI's impact on vulnerable communities are often the people with the least institutional power within the companies building those systems. Gebru, a Black woman who had done pioneering research on facial recognition bias (as Joy Buolamwini's collaborator on the Gender Shades project), was also one of the few researchers with that specific lived perspective inside a major AI lab.

After her departure, over 2,600 Google employees and 4,300 academic researchers signed an open letter supporting her. In April 2021, another prominent Google AI ethics researcher, Margaret Mitchell, was also fired. Google then disbanded the unit they had both worked in.

The Homogeneity Problem in AI Development

Who actually builds AI systems? A 2021 survey by the AI Now Institute found that women make up approximately 15% of AI research staff at major tech companies, and Black and Hispanic engineers remain dramatically underrepresented. A 2018 survey of AI researchers globally found that 80% were men, and the vast majority came from North America, Europe, or East Asia.

This matters because the people building a system make choices — about what problem to solve, what data to use, what failure modes to worry about, what counts as "working correctly." Those choices reflect the assumptions, experiences, and blind spots of the people making them. A team that has never experienced housing discrimination may not think to test whether their mortgage-approval AI discriminates. A team that doesn't include people who've experienced aggressive policing may not recognize the specific risks of a predictive policing tool they're building.

This isn't an accusation of bad intent. It's a structural observation: homogeneous teams have systematic blind spots that diverse teams are more likely to catch. The absence of certain voices in the design process doesn't just create representation problems — it creates technical problems, because it means certain failure modes go untested and certain harms go unrecognized until after deployment.

Value alignmentThe challenge of building AI systems whose goals and behaviors match the values of the people they affect — a task complicated enormously by the fact that people have different values and not all of them are represented in the design process.
Structural biasBias that emerges not from individual prejudice but from the way institutions, systems, and processes are organized — often invisibly replicating inequalities even when no individual actor intends it.
Values in the Code: What Gets Baked In

In 2019, Apple launched the Apple Card, a credit card in partnership with Goldman Sachs, with approval decisions made by an algorithm. Almost immediately, people noticed a pattern: some women were receiving credit limits dramatically lower than their husbands, even in cases where the wife had a higher credit score and more assets. The algorithm had been trained on historical credit data — which reflected decades of women having less access to credit — and had imported those patterns into its decisions.

The Apple Card example illustrates something precise: an AI system is never value-neutral. Every choice made in building it encodes assumptions. What counts as a "good" credit applicant? What historical data is treated as authoritative? What patterns are interpreted as risk signals? Each of these questions is answered by people with particular perspectives, working within institutions with particular interests.

The philosopher Langdon Winner wrote in 1980 that "artifacts have politics" — that designed objects encode the values of their creators and enforce those values on users. A bridge built too low for buses to pass under (as Winner described about some New York parkways) is a political decision made permanent in concrete. An algorithm trained on data that undervalues women's creditworthiness is a political decision made permanent in code.

AI systems make choices about language, too. When a translation system defaults to male pronouns when translating gender-neutral words, or when a content moderation system is less effective at flagging harassment in languages other than English, these are value-laden design choices that appear to be purely technical.

The Ethical Question

When a company builds an AI system that will affect millions of people — in healthcare, criminal justice, housing, education — do they have an obligation to include those people in the design process? If the answer is yes, what would that actually look like in practice? And if the answer is no — who gave the company the right to make those decisions unilaterally?

Your Place in This Story

The decisions about how AI systems are built, governed, and deployed are being made right now — not in the abstract future. The companies involved are lobbying governments. The governments are drafting laws. Researchers are publishing papers arguing for different standards. Community organizations are suing platforms. Individual engineers are deciding whether to raise concerns or stay quiet.

Every one of those actors was once in a classroom learning to think about these problems — or wasn't, and is making decisions without that framework. The people who will shape the next decade of AI are somewhere in school right now. The question of whether they approach those decisions with a sophisticated understanding of power, bias, consent, and accountability — or whether they treat AI as a purely technical domain where ethics is someone else's department — is being answered in classrooms and modules exactly like this one.

You don't have to become an engineer or a lawyer or a policy researcher. But you do live in a world organized by systems that were designed by people, to serve particular goals, with particular assumptions baked in. Understanding that those systems are designed — not natural, not inevitable, not neutral — is the minimum required to be a fully aware participant in the world they're building around you.

That is what digital citizenship means in the era of AI. Not just "be kind online." Not just "don't share your password." But: understand the architecture of the systems you live inside, ask who built them and for whose benefit, and know that the shape of those systems is not fixed.

You Can Now See What Most People Miss

When someone says AI is just a tool — neutral, objective, mathematical — you now know what's missing from that claim. Every tool was designed. Every design encodes choices. Those choices reflect the perspectives, interests, and blind spots of the people who made them. The most consequential question in AI isn't "what can it do?" It's "who decided what it should do, and who wasn't in the room when that decision was made?"

3. The Apple Card algorithm gave some women lower credit limits than their husbands despite higher credit scores. What specific design choice most likely caused this?
Precisely. Historical data carries historical inequality. If women historically received less credit — due to discriminatory practices that were legal until the 1970s in the U.S. — an algorithm trained on that data learns to treat "female" behavioral and financial patterns as indicators of higher risk, because the training data makes it look that way. The algorithm isn't wrong about the pattern — it's wrong to treat a pattern created by discrimination as a neutral risk signal.
The most supported explanation is training data composition. Women in the U.S. were legally denied equal access to credit for most of the 20th century — they couldn't get credit cards in their own names without a husband's co-signature until 1974. An algorithm trained on credit history that predates or spans those restrictions learns patterns that were created by discrimination, not by actual creditworthiness differences.
4. A classmate argues: "Tech companies should be free to build whatever AI they want — if people don't like it, they just won't use it." What is the strongest counter-argument to this position?
Strong reasoning. "Just don't use it" assumes a free market in which alternatives exist and participation is voluntary. But when AI systems determine your parole conditions, your mortgage approval, your healthcare diagnosis, or your job prospects, you are subject to them whether you chose to be or not. The "opt out" response works for apps you download by choice; it fails completely when the system is embedded in institutions that have power over your life.
The opt-out argument only works when participation is genuinely voluntary. AI systems embedded in criminal justice, healthcare, credit, and employment aren't products you choose to use — they're systems you're processed through. People have no meaningful ability to opt out of an algorithm that a judge uses to determine their sentence, or that an insurer uses to set their premiums.
5. An AI translation app defaults to male pronouns when translating gender-neutral sentences from Finnish (which has no gendered pronouns) into English. Is this a technical problem, a values problem, or both?
Exactly. This is the technical-values entanglement at the heart of AI design. The training data made male defaults statistically common — that's a technical fact. But deciding what to do about that statistical pattern — accepting it, correcting for it, using a gender-neutral default, flagging the ambiguity — requires a values choice. The people who built the app made that choice. Even "do nothing and use the statistical default" is a choice, not a neutral outcome.
Technical and values problems are harder to separate than they look. Training data skews toward male defaults — that's a technical condition. But "therefore the app defaults to male" is a decision, not an inevitability. Someone chose not to correct for it, add alternatives, or flag the ambiguity. That choice encodes an assumption about gender norms, even if no one explicitly decided to be sexist.
