Module 4 · Lesson 1

The Machine That Couldn't See Dark Skin

What a camera in a soap dispenser revealed about who AI is built for — and who gets left out.
If an AI system works perfectly for some people and fails for others, is that a bug, or something deeper?

In the autumn of 2019, a software engineer named Chukwuemeka Afigbo posted a short video to Twitter. The clip showed two people standing at an office soap dispenser — the kind that uses a sensor to detect your hand and release soap automatically. When a white hand reached under the nozzle, the sensor triggered immediately. When a Black hand reached under, the sensor did nothing. Nothing. The hand had to be covered with a white paper towel before the machine would respond.

The video went viral. Millions of people watched it. The company that made the dispenser said the sensor wasn't designed to detect skin tone — it used infrared light, and darker skin reflects less infrared than lighter skin. It was never tested on a wide enough range of people. The gap wasn't intentional hatred. It was something subtler and in some ways harder to fix: the people who built it simply hadn't thought carefully about everyone who would use it.

This wasn't a one-off glitch. The same year, a ProPublica investigation had already documented that facial recognition software built by major tech companies misidentified darker-skinned women at error rates up to 34% — compared to less than 1% for lighter-skinned men. MIT researcher Joy Buolamwini had documented this in her 2018 paper "Gender Shades." She had to wear a white mask for the camera to detect her own face.

Why AI Systems Learn to Ignore Some People

AI systems learn from data. Feed an AI a million photos to learn what a face looks like, and it will get very good at recognizing faces — but only the kinds of faces it saw most. If the training data was mostly photos of lighter-skinned men (which early facial recognition datasets were), then the AI becomes an expert at recognizing lighter-skinned men and a poor guesser for everyone else.

This is called representation bias — when the data used to train an AI doesn't actually represent the full range of people who will encounter it. It's one of the most common and consequential forms of AI bias. The AI isn't malicious. It learned from what it was shown. But the result can be just as harmful as deliberate discrimination.

Representation bias When training data over-represents some groups and under-represents others, causing an AI to perform worse for the under-represented groups.

Here's what makes this hard to spot: the AI might report very high accuracy — say, 96%. That sounds excellent. But if the 4% of errors are concentrated almost entirely on one demographic group, that group experiences a deeply unreliable system. Averages hide whose experience is being averaged away.

The Pipeline: Where Bias Enters

Bias doesn't just enter through training data. It can enter at every step in how an AI is built. Who decides what problem to solve? If the engineers are mostly from one demographic group, they may not even notice that certain people's needs aren't being considered. Who labels the data? If workers classifying images of people all share similar backgrounds, their categories will reflect their assumptions. Who tests the finished system? If testers don't include the full range of eventual users, problems only show up after the system is deployed — in the real world, on real people.

Joy Buolamwini called this the "coded gaze" — the idea that AI systems encode the perspective and blind spots of the people who built them. The technology isn't neutral. It carries the assumptions, priorities, and oversights of its creators.

Ethical Question

If a company builds an AI system that works well for 90% of users but consistently fails for a specific minority group, are they responsible for fixing it even if it's expensive? What if fixing it for that group makes the system slightly worse for the majority? There's no clean answer here. Sit with that tension.

What You Can See Now

Most people encounter an AI system and ask: "Does it work?" You can now ask something sharper: "Does it work equally well for everyone — and if not, who pays the price for the gap?" That's a fundamentally different question, and most users never think to ask it.

When a photo app applies beauty filters, when a speech recognition system mishears an accent, when a hiring algorithm screens out certain names — these aren't random malfunctions. They are systematic patterns tied to whose experience was centered when the AI was designed. Knowing this changes how you read every headline about AI performance and every statistic about AI accuracy.

You now see what most people miss

A high accuracy score for an AI system is almost meaningless without knowing how that accuracy is distributed. An AI that's 98% accurate but fails almost entirely on one group isn't a good AI — it's a tool that excludes people while looking excellent on paper. You now have the vocabulary to say exactly that.

The soap dispenser story ended without a satisfying resolution. Most affected products were updated only partially. Facial recognition systems with documented disparities continued to be sold to police departments across the United States. Understanding why requires looking at who has power in the communities where AI gets built — and that's where this module goes next.

The Algorithm That Decided Who Got the News

How Facebook's feed algorithm in 2016 didn't just show content — it chose which communities got amplified and which got buried.
When an algorithm decides what millions of people see, is that a technical decision or a political one?

In the months before the 2016 U.S. presidential election, Facebook's News Feed algorithm was making approximately one billion decisions per day about which posts to show to which users. These weren't random choices. The algorithm had been trained to maximize "engagement" — clicks, likes, shares, and time spent on the platform. It was extremely good at its job.

What it discovered, across millions of user experiments, was that outrage drove engagement better than almost anything else. Angry posts got shared more. Frightening headlines got more clicks. Content that confirmed what someone already believed made them stay on the platform longer. So the algorithm fed people more of what made them angry and afraid — not because anyone at Facebook decided to do that, but because that's what the data said worked.

By 2018, Facebook's own internal research — later leaked to the Wall Street Journal in 2021 — showed that the company's researchers had found that the algorithm was actively making political polarization worse. One internal memo from 2016 read: "Our algorithms exploit the human brain's attraction to divisiveness." A proposed fix was ready. Facebook's leadership decided not to implement it because it would reduce overall engagement metrics.

Filter Bubbles: When Algorithms Become Walls

What happened to Facebook's users is an example of what researcher Eli Pariser named the "filter bubble" in 2011 — though the problem had existed before he named it. A filter bubble forms when a recommendation algorithm becomes so good at showing you content you already agree with that it effectively walls you off from encountering different perspectives.

Filter bubble A state where an algorithm shows you only content that matches your existing views, gradually cutting you off from information or perspectives that might challenge you.

This matters for communities because the effect isn't uniform. Research published in 2019 by NYU's Center for Social Media and Politics found that users who got their news primarily through social media algorithms were significantly less likely to encounter factual corrections to false claims — not because they chose to avoid them, but because the algorithm had optimized them out of their feed. The bubble wasn't chosen. It was constructed.

For AI systems specifically, this creates a reinforcing loop: the algorithm learns what you engage with, shows you more of it, you engage more because it's familiar, the algorithm learns your engagement is rising, shows you even more. Within weeks, two people with initially similar views can end up in completely different information ecosystems — seeing different "realities" about the same events.

Who Gets Amplified — And Who Gets Silenced

Filter bubbles are one side of the coin. The other is amplification asymmetry — the pattern where algorithms systematically boost certain voices and certain types of content while others effectively disappear. This isn't random.

A 2021 study by Twitter's own machine learning team analyzed the platform's recommendation algorithm and found that content from right-leaning political sources was amplified significantly more than content from left-leaning sources in six of the seven countries they studied. Twitter published this research themselves — a rare act of algorithmic transparency. What they couldn't fully explain was why. The algorithm hadn't been told to favor any side. It had learned that certain posting styles, certain emotional registers, certain types of content generated more interaction. And those patterns correlated with political lean.

The deeper pattern: algorithms optimize for what spreads, and what spreads is not always accurate, important, or representative of what a community actually thinks. A loud, angry minority can dominate an algorithmic feed while a thoughtful, quieter majority barely registers.

Ethical Question

If a platform's algorithm is making political polarization worse — and the company knows this from its own research — but fixing it would reduce profits, what obligation does the company have? Is a private company responsible for the health of public political discourse? And who gets to decide what "healthy" discourse even means?

Reading the Feed With New Eyes

You now possess something most adults scrolling social media don't: an understanding of the machinery running beneath the surface. Every post you see on a platform with a recommendation algorithm has been selected. Not by a human editor who thought it was important — by a system trained to maximize your engagement. The question "why am I seeing this?" has a specific, answerable structure even when you don't know the exact answer.

You now see what most people miss

Next time you're on any social platform, you can ask: Is this the most important news right now, or the most engaging? Those are different things. The algorithm doesn't know the difference — and it doesn't care. You do. That distinction is the beginning of algorithmic literacy.

At the institutional level, this is now a policy debate happening in real time. The European Union's Digital Services Act, passed in 2022, requires large platforms to audit their recommendation algorithms and give users the option to see content in chronological order rather than algorithmic order. Understanding why that law exists — and what it's trying to fix — is exactly the kind of knowledge that shapes the policy decisions being made right now.

The Content Moderator's Impossible Job

In 2021, Frances Haugen revealed documents showing Facebook knew which communities its moderation systems ignored. What happens to the people in those communities?
If an AI system that decides what speech is allowed works far better in English than in any other language, who bears the cost of that gap?

Between 2017 and 2018, Myanmar experienced one of the most rapid campaigns of violence against an ethnic minority in modern history. The Rohingya people — a Muslim minority group in a predominantly Buddhist country — were targeted by military forces and civilian mobs. The UN described it as a genocide. More than 700,000 people fled the country. Villages were burned. Thousands were killed.

Investigators found that Facebook had been the primary communications platform in Myanmar — many people used "Facebook" as a synonym for "the internet." And on that platform, coordinated hate campaigns against the Rohingya had been spreading for years. Researchers and local organizations had been reporting this content to Facebook since 2013. Facebook had almost no Burmese-speaking content moderators. Its AI content moderation systems had been trained overwhelmingly on English.

In 2018, Facebook's CEO Mark Zuckerberg testified before the U.S. Senate. When asked about Myanmar, he said the company was "working hard" on the issue and was "investing heavily in Burmese language" detection. A 2021 lawsuit filed by Rohingya refugees in the UK and U.S. alleged Facebook owed $150 billion in damages for its role in the violence. Frances Haugen, who leaked internal Facebook documents in October 2021, revealed that the company's own researchers had documented serious moderation gaps in dozens of languages — and that resources were concentrated almost entirely on English-language content affecting American users.

The Language Gap in AI Moderation

Content moderation — the process of deciding what speech is allowed on a platform — increasingly uses AI systems to flag and remove harmful content. These systems need to understand language: they need to know that a sentence is threatening, or that a phrase is a slur in a particular culture. That requires training data — enormous amounts of text labeled to indicate what's acceptable and what isn't.

Almost all of the largest and most accurate labeled datasets for this kind of AI training are in English. Building comparably accurate systems for other languages requires comparable amounts of quality training data — which takes years and significant resources to develop. The result: AI content moderation is dramatically less accurate in most of the world's languages.

Moderation gap The difference in how effectively harmful content is caught and removed across different languages and communities — typically far worse for non-English content on major platforms.

A 2022 report by Global Witness tested the major platforms' ability to catch violent political speech in English versus in Arabic, Tigrinya (spoken in Ethiopia/Eritrea), and Rohingya. The AI systems approved harmful content in non-English languages at rates 5 to 10 times higher than equivalent content in English. This meant that communities already facing real-world violence had the least protection online.

Who Is the Community, Really?

The Myanmar case forces a confrontation with a question that sounds philosophical but has life-or-death implications: when a platform says "our community standards," whose community do they mean?

Facebook in 2017 had roughly 2 billion users. Most of the people designing its systems, writing its content policies, and training its moderation AI were American. American cultural context, American legal frameworks, and American political concerns shaped what got flagged, what got removed, and what was left to spread. A user in Myanmar, Ethiopia, or Brazil was interacting with a system whose implicit model of "the community" was centered thousands of miles away.

This isn't unique to Facebook. It's a structural feature of how the global technology industry is organized. The people building AI systems are not representative of the people who will use them — demographically, linguistically, or culturally. And unlike a bug in code, this kind of gap is very hard to patch.

Ethical Question

Facebook was a private company, not a government. It didn't have an obligation to police speech under any country's law. But when a private platform becomes the dominant communications infrastructure for a country — where almost everyone gets their news, talks to family, and organizes politically — does the company acquire obligations it didn't choose? Who should decide that? And how would you enforce it?

A Framework for Asking Better Questions

When you encounter any AI system that moderates, recommends, or filters speech — whether it's a platform's content policy, a school's email filter, or a game's chat moderation — you now have a framework for analysis. Ask: What language or community was this system primarily trained on? Who benefits most from its protections? Who bears most of its failures?

These aren't just abstract questions for academics. They determine whether communities get to speak safely online, whether violence gets amplified or stopped, and whose version of "normal" the entire digital world defaults to.

You now see what most people miss

Every platform you use has a "default community" baked into its design — usually English-speaking, usually Western, usually reflecting whoever the engineers were. Most users never see this. You can. And that means when you read about AI moderation failing somewhere in the world, you can explain exactly why — not with vague language about "bias," but with specific structural reasons. That's a rare kind of clarity.

Who Gets to Fix It?

COMPAS scored defendants in American courts. It was wrong more often for Black defendants. The question of who should fix that — and how — is still unanswered.
When an AI system produces biased outcomes and the people who built it, the people who deploy it, and the courts using it all have different interests — who is actually responsible for change?

In 2013, a man named Vernon Prater was arrested for shoplifting in Broward County, Florida. He was white, and he had a serious prior criminal record. A risk assessment tool called COMPAS — Correctional Offender Management Profiling for Alternative Sanctions — scored him low-risk. He was released. He was later convicted of armed robbery.

That same year, a woman named Brisha Borden was arrested for riding a scooter that turned out to be stolen. She was Black, eighteen years old, and had no adult criminal record. COMPAS scored her high-risk. She spent a longer period awaiting trial than she might have otherwise. She committed no further crimes.

In 2016, ProPublica analyzed COMPAS scores for more than 7,000 defendants in Broward County. They found a striking pattern: Black defendants were nearly twice as likely as white defendants to be falsely flagged as high-risk when they were actually going to commit no future crime. White defendants were more likely to be falsely flagged low-risk when they actually went on to reoffend. COMPAS wasn't just sometimes wrong — it was wrong in systematically different ways depending on race.

The company that made COMPAS, Northpointe (later Equivant), disputed the analysis. They said their tool was "accurate" — the same percentage of high-risk scores reoffended across racial groups. ProPublica said that missed the point entirely. Both were using the word "fair" to mean different things.

The Definitions of Fairness Disagree With Each Other

The COMPAS controversy revealed something genuinely surprising: mathematical fairness can be defined in multiple ways — and some of those definitions are mathematically incompatible. You cannot always have them all at once.

Northpointe's definition of fairness: if you score 7/10 for risk, you should have roughly the same probability of reoffending whether you're Black or white. This is called calibration — the scores mean the same thing across groups.

ProPublica's definition: false positives (being labeled high-risk when you're actually low-risk) should be equally distributed across racial groups. This is called equal false positive rates.

In 2016, researchers at Cornell University and Carnegie Mellon University published mathematical proofs showing that — in the presence of underlying group differences in base rates — you cannot achieve both perfect calibration and equal false positive rates simultaneously. You have to choose. The algorithm can't do both. This means every fairness choice involves a value judgment about whose errors matter more — and there's no neutral technical answer.

Algorithmic fairness A property of an AI system that produces equitable outcomes — but there are multiple, conflicting mathematical definitions of what "equitable" means, and they cannot all be satisfied at once.
The Power Structure Around Broken Systems

Even when a problem is clearly documented, who actually has the power to fix it? In the COMPAS case, this question became very uncomfortable. The company that built COMPAS had financial incentive to defend their product. The courts using it had invested in the system and often lacked the technical expertise to audit it. Defendants had limited legal ability to challenge algorithmic scores — in many cases, courts ruled that the algorithm's inner workings were proprietary trade secrets that defendants couldn't examine, let alone contest.

A Wisconsin case, State v. Loomis (2016), went to the state Supreme Court. Eric Loomis argued that using COMPAS in his sentencing violated his due process rights because he couldn't challenge an algorithm he couldn't see. The court ruled against him, saying the judge had considered COMPAS as just one of many factors. Civil liberties organizations argued the ruling set a dangerous precedent: secretive AI influencing liberty decisions, insulated from scrutiny.

This structure — where a flawed AI is used in high-stakes decisions, its workings are secret, and affected people have almost no recourse — repeats across many domains. Credit scoring. Insurance pricing. Benefits eligibility. Healthcare triage. The people most likely to be harmed by biased AI are often the people with the least power to challenge it.

Ethical Question

If an AI tool influencing legal decisions is a trade secret, should defendants have the right to see how it works — even if that means the company loses competitive advantage? Is algorithmic transparency more important than commercial privacy? And who should be the judge of that tradeoff — courts, legislatures, or regulators who may not understand the technology?

What Accountability Actually Looks Like

The COMPAS story doesn't have a clean ending. The tool is still used in courts. The debate about its fairness continues. But it generated something important: a public record of what algorithmic harm looks like, documented with specific names, specific numbers, and specific methodology. That record has become the foundation for legislative efforts in multiple states to restrict algorithmic decision-making in criminal justice.

Real accountability for AI bias tends to come from three places: investigative journalism (ProPublica's reporting changed what was politically possible), independent academic research (the Cornell and CMU papers reframed the entire policy debate), and organized advocacy (civil liberties organizations filing legal challenges and lobbying for disclosure requirements).

You now see what most people miss

The COMPAS case is not just about one tool. It's a template for how AI bias enters systems of power, how it hides behind technical complexity and trade secret claims, and how it eventually gets challenged. You now understand all the layers of that process — from the mathematics of incompatible fairness definitions to the political economy of who can fix a system and who can't. That's not a beginner's understanding of AI and society. That's what the actual debate looks like.

You are entering a world where these debates — about who AI hears, who it ignores, who it harms, and who has the power to change it — will shape policy, law, and daily life for decades. You are not too young to have a position on them. In fact, the people making these decisions right now are working with frameworks developed by researchers who, in many cases, were exactly your age when these systems were first being built and no one was asking these questions yet.

Communities, Bias, and Who Gets Heard · 15 questions · Pass = 80%
1. Chukwuemeka Afigbo's 2019 video showed a soap dispenser failing to detect dark skin. What underlying technical issue caused this?
Correct — a reflectance gap that wasn't caught during inadequate testing.
The sensor used infrared reflectance — and darker skin absorbs more infrared. This went uncaught because testing wasn't broad enough.
2. Joy Buolamwini found she needed to wear a white mask for facial recognition systems to detect her face. This directly illustrates:
Correct — representation bias from unrepresentative training data.
This is representation bias: the training data skewed toward lighter-skinned faces, so the system learned those faces much better than others.
3. An AI medical tool reports 96% overall accuracy but 85% accuracy for elderly patients and 98% accuracy for everyone else. What does this reveal?
Correct — averages obscure whose experience is being averaged away.
The aggregate hides inequity. Elderly patients experience significantly worse outcomes, which is invisible if you only look at the overall number.
4. Facebook's 2016 News Feed algorithm favored outrage-inducing content primarily because:
Correct — the algorithm found what worked and optimized for it, without anyone explicitly deciding to favor outrage.
The algorithm was optimizing for engagement metrics. It learned — not from any explicit decision — that outrage and fear drove more engagement than calm or accurate content.
5. A filter bubble forms when:
Correct — a filter bubble is algorithmically constructed, not deliberately chosen by the user.
A filter bubble is built by an algorithm around you — not a conscious choice you make. The algorithm keeps serving what you engage with until alternative views are effectively absent.
6. Twitter's 2021 internal algorithm study was unusual because:
Correct — companies almost never voluntarily publish research documenting their own platform's political asymmetries.
Twitter voluntarily published research documenting political amplification asymmetry in their own algorithm — in six of seven countries studied, right-leaning content was amplified more.
7. The EU's Digital Services Act (2022) addresses algorithmic amplification concerns by:
Correct — the DSA targets transparency and user control, not outright prohibition.
The DSA requires algorithm audits and gives users chronological feed options. It doesn't ban algorithms or mandate political balance.
8. The primary reason Facebook's AI moderation failed to stop anti-Rohingya content in Myanmar was:
Correct — a language and resource gap with catastrophic real-world consequences.
The failure was a moderation gap: English-dominant AI training data and virtually no Burmese-language human moderators meant harmful content spread unchecked.
9. The 2022 Global Witness report found that violent political speech in non-English languages was approved by AI moderation at rates:
Correct — a 5–10x gap, not a marginal difference.
The Global Witness report found non-English harmful content was approved at 5–10 times the rate of equivalent English content — a dramatic gap, not a minor one.
10. Frances Haugen's 2021 document leak revealed that Facebook's moderation resource allocation:
Correct — Haugen's documents showed the company knew about the gaps and concentrated resources elsewhere anyway.
Haugen's leaked documents showed resources heavily concentrated on English/American content, with documented non-English gaps that the company's own researchers had identified but not adequately addressed.
11. ProPublica's 2016 COMPAS analysis covered approximately how many defendants in Broward County, Florida?
Correct — 7,000+ defendants gave the analysis statistical weight to identify systematic patterns.
ProPublica analyzed more than 7,000 defendants — large enough to identify systematic bias patterns, not just isolated incidents.
12. Northpointe (COMPAS's maker) and ProPublica reached different conclusions about COMPAS's fairness because they:
Correct — incompatible fairness definitions, not bad faith from either side.
Both used real data. They used different fairness definitions: Northpointe used calibration; ProPublica used equal false positive rates. These definitions are mathematically incompatible under real-world conditions.
13. In State v. Loomis (2016), the Wisconsin Supreme Court ruled that using COMPAS in sentencing was acceptable even though the defendant couldn't inspect the algorithm. Civil liberties organizations objected because:
Correct — the concern was algorithmic secrecy combined with high-stakes decision-making, insulated from challenge.
The objection was procedural and fundamental: a trade-secret algorithm was influencing someone's freedom, and they had no legal mechanism to inspect or contest it. That principle matters beyond this one case.
14. The Cornell/CMU mathematical proofs about algorithmic fairness showed that choosing a fairness definition is ultimately:
Correct — the impossibility result transforms AI fairness from a technical problem into a values problem. There is no neutral technical answer.
The proofs showed that fairness definitions are sometimes mathematically incompatible. That means any system must choose — and choosing whose errors matter more is a values judgment, not a technical optimization.
15. Across all four lessons in this module, what is the most accurate description of how AI bias typically enters and persists in systems?
Correct. This is the module's through-line: bias enters through structural gaps (data, optimization, testing) and persists through structural forces (business incentives, legal protection of proprietary systems, resource concentration). Understanding both halves is what makes the analysis complete.
The module documented a consistent pattern: bias enters through unrepresentative data, engagement-focused optimization, and inadequate testing — and stays because fixing it is costly, companies have incentives to protect existing systems, and affected communities often have limited recourse.
