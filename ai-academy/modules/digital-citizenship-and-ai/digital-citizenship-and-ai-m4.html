Module 4 · Lesson 1

What the Algorithm Amplifies

How online communities form, and why AI makes some voices louder than others.
When AI decides what you see, who decides what AI shows?

In March 2018, a researcher named Renée DiResta testified before the U.S. Senate Intelligence Committee about something she had spent two years mapping: how platforms like Facebook, YouTube, and Twitter had been algorithmically amplifying fringe content — not because anyone told them to, but because their recommendation systems were optimized for one thing above all others: engagement.

She described what she called "rabbit holes." A user watches one video about vaccine side effects. The algorithm, noticing that conspiracy content drives more watch time than mainstream content, serves up something slightly more extreme. Then more extreme again. Within seven clicks, the same user is watching content claiming governments are hiding cancer cures. Nobody chose this path. The algorithm chose it for them.

YouTube's own internal research, leaked in 2019, confirmed what DiResta had been saying: the recommendation engine was consistently pushing users toward more radical content because radical content kept people on the platform longer. More time on platform meant more ads. The algorithm wasn't evil. It was just optimizing for something that happened to reward outrage and extremism.

Why Algorithms Build Communities — Whether You Ask Them To or Not

Here is something most people don't realize: you don't have to join a community online. The algorithm will place you in one.

Every time you watch, click, like, pause, or share, you are teaching the algorithm something about you. The algorithm groups you with millions of other users who behaved similarly. It then shows you what those other users engaged with next. This is called collaborative filtering — the same system Netflix uses to recommend shows, the same system Spotify uses to recommend songs. It's not inherently sinister. It's just pattern matching at enormous scale.

But the pattern has a problem. Most recommendation algorithms are trained on engagement data — clicks, views, watch time. They are not trained on "what content is accurate" or "what content is good for this person." They are trained on what made previous users stay on the platform. And it turns out that content triggering strong emotions — anger, fear, tribal belonging — keeps people engaged longer than calm, nuanced content.

Engagement baitContent designed specifically to trigger emotional reactions (outrage, fear, excitement) because algorithms reward high-engagement content with wider distribution.
Filter bubbleA state where your feed contains only content that confirms what you already believe, because the algorithm learned that agreement keeps you clicking longer than challenge.

The result is that people with similar initial views get fed increasingly similar — and often increasingly extreme — content, and gradually form what feel like communities, but are actually algorithmically sorted groups. They didn't find each other. The algorithm found them for each other.

What Facebook's Own Data Said

In 2021, the Wall Street Journal published a series called "The Facebook Files" based on internal documents leaked by a former employee named Frances Haugen. One document in particular was striking: a 2018 internal presentation titled "Our Algorithms Exploit the Human Brain's Attraction to Divisiveness."

Facebook's own researchers had found that when they adjusted the algorithm to reduce "reshares" of content — meaning they made it slightly harder for outrage-bait to spread — user engagement dropped by about 0.19%. That's a tiny number. But 0.19% of Facebook's user base in 2018 was roughly three million people. The company decided the business cost was too high and did not implement the change.

This is the decision you don't see when you scroll your feed: somewhere, someone calculated the dollar value of your outrage and decided it was worth keeping.

Ethical Question — No Clean Answer

If a company builds a system that makes people angrier and more divided — not because they intended that outcome, but because they chose not to fix it once they knew — how responsible are they for what happens next? Is "we didn't mean for it to happen" a valid defense when you had the data and made a choice?

How This Shapes Who Gets Heard

Amplification isn't random. Algorithms don't treat all voices equally — they treat all engagement signals equally. And not all communities generate the same kind of engagement.

Research from 2019 by the Reuters Institute at Oxford found that political content from conservative sources on Facebook received, on average, significantly higher engagement than equivalent content from centrist or liberal sources. This wasn't because more people agreed with it. It was because content designed to provoke tribal reactions — us-versus-them framing, threat narratives, emotional urgency — happened to align with what certain political communicators had learned to produce. The algorithm rewarded the rhetoric, not the politics.

The same mechanism works in every community, not just political ones. Gaming communities that produce outrage about changes to games. Sports communities that amplify the most hostile takes. Fan communities where the loudest voices drown out the quieter ones. Whoever learns to trigger the algorithm gets amplified. Everyone else gets buried.

This creates a profound distortion in what you hear online: you are not hearing a representative sample of what people think. You are hearing the voices that the algorithm decided were most engaging. Those voices may have nothing to do with what most people actually believe.

You Can Now See What Most People Miss

When you read comments under any post — any post, on any platform — you are not reading "what people think." You are reading what the engagement algorithm decided to show at the top. The calm, thoughtful comment with 3 likes is below the fold. The furious reply with 847 reactions is at the top. That ranking was made by software. Understanding this changes how you read every feed you ever open.

AI's New Role in This System

The systems DiResta described in 2018 were primarily rule-based and statistical. The algorithms that run feeds today are more sophisticated — many are built on the same foundational technology as large language models. They understand context, predict what you will want to see next, and continuously update their model of you based on each interaction.

This makes them more powerful, but it doesn't change the fundamental dynamic. An AI recommendation system trained on engagement data still learns that outrage works. It just learns it faster, more precisely, and at a scale that older systems couldn't reach.

What's new is what happens when AI generates content too, not just distributes it. When AI can write posts, comments, and articles that are indistinguishable from human writing, the question of "who is actually in this community" becomes much harder to answer. A community might be 30% real people and 70% AI-generated content designed to keep those 30% engaged — and no one in the community would know. This isn't science fiction. It was already happening on Twitter, Facebook, and Reddit by 2020, through coordinated networks of automated accounts called bot farms.

Bot farmA network of fake automated accounts designed to artificially inflate engagement, spread specific messages, or manufacture the impression of grassroots community support.

The question for you — living inside these systems — is: how do you know what's real? And more urgently: does it change anything for you if some of the voices that shaped your views turned out not to be human?

Training Data and Who Gets Left Out

When AI learns from the internet, it learns the internet's biases — including who was never there.
If an AI was trained mostly on data from one group of people, what happens when it meets everyone else?

In July 2015, a software engineer named Jacky Alcine opened Google Photos on his phone to look through his recent pictures. The app had automatically sorted his images into labeled albums — "Dogs," "Cars," "Flowers." Then he found one that stopped him cold. Google's AI had placed photos of him and his friend — both Black — into an album it had automatically labeled "Gorillas."

He posted the screenshot to Twitter. Google's response was fast: within hours, executives apologized and said they were "appalled and genuinely sorry." They fixed it — but not by fixing the AI. They fixed it by removing the gorilla label entirely from the system. For years afterward, Google Photos could not identify gorillas, chimpanzees, or similar primates because the company had no good solution to the underlying problem.

The underlying problem was this: Google's image recognition AI had been trained on a dataset that contained far fewer images of Black faces than white faces. When the model encountered a dark-skinned face, it had less data to work from, made worse predictions, and in this case, made a catastrophically wrong one. The AI wasn't racist the way a person can be racist. It was ignorant in a way that produced racist outcomes — because the data it learned from didn't represent everyone equally.

What "Training Data" Actually Means

Every AI system that recognizes, generates, or predicts something learned to do so by studying examples. Those examples are the training data. A face recognition AI studied millions of labeled photos. A language AI like the kind that writes text studied hundreds of billions of words from books, websites, and other sources. A music recommendation AI studied what millions of listeners played next.

The critical thing to understand: the AI can only learn from what's in the data. It cannot know about what's missing.

Think about what was on the internet in 2010, when many large AI training datasets were first assembled. English-language content vastly outnumbered all other languages. Content created by people in the United States, Europe, and East Asia vastly outnumbered content from Africa, South Asia, and Latin America. Content written by college-educated adults vastly outnumbered content from children, elderly people, or people with limited literacy. Content from men outnumbered content from women on many technical platforms.

Training dataThe collection of examples an AI model learns from. The model's capabilities, blind spots, and biases are largely determined by what's in — and what's missing from — this data.
Representation biasA type of AI bias that occurs when certain groups of people are underrepresented in training data, causing the AI to perform worse for those groups than for well-represented groups.

An AI trained on this uneven data learns an uneven picture of the world. It becomes very good at the things well-represented in the data, and it becomes worse — sometimes dangerously worse — at things involving people or situations that weren't well-represented.

The Medical AI That Missed Dark Skin

In 2019, a research team led by Ziad Obermeyer at UC Berkeley published a study in the journal Science about a widely-used healthcare algorithm. This algorithm was deployed at hospitals across the United States to identify patients who needed extra medical care. It had been trained on historical healthcare data — meaning it learned from records of who hospitals had previously treated most intensively.

The problem: historically, Black patients had received less healthcare than white patients with identical medical needs, because of systemic inequities in the healthcare system. The algorithm learned from this history. It therefore predicted that Black patients needed less care than white patients with the same actual health problems, because that's what the historical data showed.

Obermeyer's team estimated the algorithm was affecting roughly 200 million patients per year across the U.S. healthcare system. Because of the bias in the training data, Black patients were being systematically under-referred for care they actually needed.

This is what researchers call historical bias: when training data reflects past injustices, the AI learns to reproduce those injustices, and then gets deployed at scale — affecting millions of people — before anyone checks whether the pattern it learned was one worth reproducing.

Ethical Question — No Clean Answer

The hospital algorithm was trained on real historical data. It was, in a sense, being perfectly accurate to what had actually happened. If AI reflects the world as it was, and the world as it was was deeply unfair, should AI be corrected to reflect a fairer world — or is that adding a kind of distortion? Who gets to decide which historical patterns are worth learning from?

Language Models and Whose Language They Know

The same dynamic appears in language AI. GPT-3, released by OpenAI in 2020, was trained on approximately 570 gigabytes of text. An enormous amount of that text came from websites indexed by Google in English-speaking countries. The model's understanding of the world was therefore shaped heavily by a specific slice of humanity: mostly English-speaking, mostly Western, mostly educated, mostly connected to the internet before 2019.

When researchers tested these models on knowledge questions about African history, Indigenous cultures, rural life in developing countries, or languages spoken by millions but with little internet presence, performance dropped dramatically compared to questions about American or European topics. The models weren't stupid — they were incomplete in ways that followed the contours of who had been loudest and most present on the internet.

This matters for the communities these models shape. When an AI writing assistant helps students in Kenya write essays, is it giving them the same quality of help it gives students in California? When an AI health chatbot advises people in rural India, does it draw on medical knowledge that reflects their context? These aren't hypothetical fairness questions. They are live policy questions being decided right now by companies and governments.

You Now Understand Something That Shapes Policy Decisions

When you hear that a government is deploying AI to help with education, healthcare, or hiring — you now know the first question to ask: who was in the training data? That question determines who the system will work well for and who it will fail. Most people don't know to ask it. You do.

Whose Values Does AI Learn?

The choices baked into every AI system — and why "neutral" is not actually a thing.
When engineers decide how an AI behaves, whose idea of "correct" are they using?

In 2019, a company called Sama — a subcontractor working for Meta (then Facebook) — was employing hundreds of workers in Nairobi, Kenya, to label AI training data. Their job was to review thousands of images and videos per day: beheadings, child abuse, suicide recordings, graphic violence. They were being paid roughly $1.50 per hour to decide what Facebook's AI moderation system should classify as harmful.

Their decisions mattered enormously. When a content moderator in Nairobi labeled an image as "graphic violence," that label went into a training dataset used to teach Meta's AI to recognize and remove similar content globally. The moderators' cultural context, their definitions of what was shocking versus what was normal, their sense of what constituted hate speech in which context — all of it was being encoded into decisions that would affect billions of users worldwide.

In 2023, several former Sama moderators sued Meta, alleging they had developed severe PTSD from the work and had been dismissed without adequate mental health support. A Kenyan court allowed the case to proceed. The trial exposed something that most people who use Facebook never think about: the values encoded in AI content moderation are not abstract or universal. They were decided, in part, by a specific group of people in a specific place — working under significant stress, for very little money, with very little power over the systems they were training.

Value Alignment: What It Is and Why It's Hard

Every AI system that makes a judgment call — this content is harmful, this loan applicant is risky, this candidate is a good fit, this image is appropriate for children — is expressing a value. Someone decided what counts as harmful, risky, appropriate, or suitable. Those decisions are called value alignment: making the AI's judgments align with human values.

The problem is: whose human values?

Take something that seems simple: the question of whether a photo is "appropriate for children." Different families have different answers. Different cultures have wildly different answers. A photo of a woman breastfeeding might be considered natural and appropriate in one context and inappropriate in another. A photo of a hunting kill might be normal family documentation in one community and disturbing content in another. Representations of religious imagery vary enormously in their cultural meaning.

Value alignmentThe process of making an AI system's decisions and behaviors match human values — complicated by the fact that humans don't all share the same values.
RLHF (Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback)A technique used to train AI models by having human raters evaluate responses. The AI learns to produce outputs that humans rate highly — but the humans doing the rating represent a specific demographic with specific values.

The teams deciding these questions at the largest AI companies are not a representative sample of humanity. A 2023 study found that AI research teams at major tech companies were predominantly male (around 80%), heavily concentrated in the United States and Western Europe, and skewed toward people with advanced degrees from a small set of elite universities. When these teams make default decisions about what AI considers polite, harmful, appropriate, or correct — they are making those decisions from inside a particular cultural bubble.

When "Safe" Means "Safe for Whom"

In 2021, researchers Abeba Birhane and Vinay Uday Prabhu published an analysis of LAION-400M, one of the largest image-text datasets used to train image AI systems. They found the dataset contained a significant number of images that were racist, pornographic, or depicted non-consensual scenarios. The AI systems trained on this data were then used in tools deployed to millions of users worldwide.

The same year, Timnit Gebru — a renowned AI ethics researcher who had been co-leading Google's Ethical AI team — was fired after publishing a paper arguing that large language models posed risks the industry wasn't taking seriously. Her firing sparked a significant public debate about whether AI companies were genuinely committed to the kind of critical examination her work represented, or whether safety talk was primarily a public relations effort.

These events matter together because they illustrate the same dynamic: the people most affected by AI systems' values are rarely the people making the decisions, and the people inside the system who try to surface those problems are not always protected for doing so.

Ethical Question — No Clean Answer

If the people labeling training data and setting content policies come from a specific demographic — and that demographic's values shape what billions of people see and don't see — is that a technical problem with a technical fix, or is it a democratic problem? Should decisions about AI values be made by companies, governments, or communities? And what happens when those three groups want different things?

The Invisible Choices in Every AI Tool You Use

Every AI tool you use has made hundreds of invisible value decisions before you ever open it. An AI writing assistant decides what tone is "professional." An AI image generator decides what a "beautiful" person looks like. An AI content filter decides what counts as "hate speech." An AI translation tool decides which dialect of a language is "standard." None of these decisions are neutral. All of them reflect choices made by specific people with specific backgrounds.

Researchers have documented some of these effects concretely. Studies of AI image generators showed that prompts like "a doctor" produced images of men far more frequently than women; prompts like "a criminal" produced images disproportionately of darker-skinned people. These outputs reflected the distribution of images in the training data — which in turn reflected real-world stereotypes that exist in media, stock photography, and the internet.

When AI amplifies existing stereotypes at scale — generating millions of images, answering millions of questions, making millions of hiring or credit decisions — those stereotypes get deeper grooves worn into them. The AI doesn't create bias from nothing. It learns bias from humans and then broadcasts it back to humans at a scale that would have been impossible before.

You Can Now See What Most People Miss

The next time an AI tool produces an output that seems strange — a weird translation, an odd recommendation, a response that doesn't quite fit your context — you can ask the right question: "What values were built in here, and whose were they?" That question is more useful than "why is the AI broken?" Because the AI isn't broken. It's working exactly as it was trained to work. The question is what it was trained to value.

What You Can Do About It

From understanding the problem to becoming someone who changes it.
When you know how these systems work, what does that make you responsible for?

In August 2020, a seventeen-year-old student in the United Kingdom named Ofqual controversy became one of millions of students affected by an algorithmic grading scandal. Because COVID-19 had cancelled traditional A-level exams, the British government used an algorithm developed by Ofqual — the UK's exam regulator — to predict what grades students would have received. The algorithm's predictions were based heavily on the historical performance of each school, not the individual student.

Students from lower-performing schools had their predicted grades downgraded automatically — regardless of what their teachers had assessed them to be capable of. Students from expensive private schools had their predicted grades boosted. A student whose teacher predicted an A could receive a C from the algorithm simply because their school had historically sent fewer students to university. Around 39% of predicted A-level grades were downgraded by the algorithm.

Students protested in the streets. They organized online. They filed legal challenges. Within eight days, the UK government reversed the decision and announced that all students would receive their teacher-assessed grades instead. The algorithm was abandoned. The people most affected by the algorithmic decision changed it — not because they had institutional power, but because they understood what had happened, named it clearly, and refused to accept it.

The Skill of Recognizing Algorithmic Harm

The UK students won because they could articulate what had happened. They weren't just angry — they could explain the mechanism. "The algorithm used school history instead of individual assessment, which punished students for where they happened to live." That specific, accurate description of the problem was more powerful than general outrage.

This is the first practical skill: being able to recognize when an AI system has made a decision, understand what inputs it used, and identify whether those inputs were appropriate for the decision being made.

Ask yourself these questions when AI affects something important to you:

What was the input?What data did the AI use to make this decision? Was that data actually a valid proxy for what was being decided?
Who designed the objective?What was the AI trying to maximize or optimize? Whose goal was that, and does it align with the goal of the people being affected?
Who is accountable?When an algorithm makes a wrong decision, who is responsible for correcting it? Is there a process for appeal? Does it work?

These questions are not technical. You don't need to understand how a neural network works to ask them. They're questions about power and accountability — and anyone can ask them.

How Communities Have Successfully Pushed Back

The UK grading case is not isolated. It is part of a pattern of communities identifying algorithmic harm and successfully demanding change.

In 2019, the city of San Francisco became the first major city in the United States to ban government use of facial recognition technology. The campaign was led by a coalition that included civil liberties organizations, community activists, and technology researchers. Their argument was not "facial recognition is always wrong." It was specific: "Current systems have documented higher error rates for Black and brown faces, and the government should not use systems this inaccurate to make decisions about people's liberty." San Jose, Boston, and Portland followed with similar bans within two years.

In 2020, a coalition of researchers and activists forced IBM, Amazon, and Microsoft to pause or halt the sale of facial recognition technology to police departments — not through legislation, but through a combination of published research documenting bias, public pressure campaigns, and direct corporate engagement. IBM announced it was exiting the facial recognition market entirely.

In each case, the people driving change were not primarily powerful insiders. They were researchers who published findings publicly, advocates who translated technical problems into human stories, and communities who organized around concrete demands. Understanding the technical problem was the prerequisite. Organizing around it was the actual lever.

Ethical Question — No Clean Answer

When communities successfully force a change in AI policy — like banning facial recognition — they solve one problem while possibly creating others. A city that bans facial recognition may rely more heavily on human officers making identification decisions, which have their own well-documented biases. Is a biased algorithm worse than a biased person? Does your answer change depending on whether an appeal process exists?

Your Actual Levers — At Your Age, Right Now

This isn't abstract future-you content. These are things that are available to you as a person who now understands how these systems work.

1. Name it when you see it. When a recommendation algorithm pushes something extreme, when a content filter removes something unfairly, when an AI tool performs badly for a certain group — the ability to name the mechanism matters. "The algorithm is optimizing for engagement, not accuracy" is more powerful than "this platform is bad."

Follow people whose experience differs from yours. Use search terms that take you outside your usual algorithmic cluster. You can partially undo the filter bubble by being intentional about it.

3. Ask about accountability structures. When a school, government, or company announces an AI tool, the questions worth asking are: Who is accountable when it's wrong? Is there an appeal process? Has it been tested for bias? Who had input into its values? These questions are increasingly legitimate and increasingly expected. Institutions that can't answer them have a problem.

4. Notice your own amplification. When you share, like, or repost content — you are part of the amplification engine. You are not just a consumer of algorithmic choices. You are a participant in them. Content you amplify travels further because of your participation. That's a small form of power and a small form of responsibility.
