Module 5 · Lesson 1

The Moment the Algorithm Got You Wrong

AI systems make errors that follow real people into the real world. What happens when you're the error?
If a machine decides something false about you, do you have the right to fight back — and does that right actually work?

In January 2020, a man named Robert Williams was standing in his driveway in Farmington Hills, Michigan, when two Detroit Police Department detectives pulled up and told him he was under arrest. His wife watched. His daughters watched. He was handcuffed, put in a patrol car, and driven to a detention facility, where he spent the night.

He had no idea what he had supposedly done. The charge, it turned out, was shoplifting — five watches worth about $3,800 from a Shinola store in 2018. Williams had never been to that store. A facial recognition algorithm, reviewing grainy surveillance footage, had matched his face to the suspect's face. The match was wrong.

When investigators showed a witness a photo lineup that included Williams' driver's license photo, the witness said only that it "looked like" him. That was enough. Williams was the first documented American arrested solely on the basis of a false facial recognition match — a fact confirmed by the American Civil Liberties Union, which took his case in 2021 and sued the City of Detroit.

He wasn't the last.

Why Algorithms Make These Kinds of Mistakes

Facial recognition systems are trained on photographs. They learn what faces look like by studying millions of images and finding patterns — the distance between your eyes, the shape of your jaw, the slope of your nose. When shown a new face, the system compares it to everything it has learned and says: this is my best match.

The problem is that "best match" is not the same as "correct match." And the training data matters enormously. Studies published in 2018 by MIT researcher Joy Buolamwini — part of what became the Gender Shades project — found that commercial facial recognition systems misidentified darker-skinned women up to 34.7% of the time, compared to error rates below 1% for lighter-skinned men. The reason: the training datasets contained far fewer images of darker-skinned faces, so the AI had less to learn from.

This isn't a flaw in one bad system. It's a structural consequence of who builds these tools, what data they use, and who reviews the results. Robert Williams is Black. So are the other known wrongful arrest cases involving facial recognition: Michael Oliver in Detroit in 2019, Nijeer Parks in New Jersey in 2019, and Porcha Woodruff in Detroit in 2023 — who was eight months pregnant when she was arrested.

You now understand something that most adults driving past that Farmington Hills driveway do not: these are not random glitches. The errors follow a pattern. And knowing that pattern changes how you read every story about AI "helping" law enforcement.

Key Term

Algorithmic error — when an AI system produces an output that is factually wrong, often because its training data was incomplete, biased, or poorly representative of the real world. The algorithm itself "worked" — it did what it was designed to do. The error came from what it was taught.

What "Standing Up" Actually Looks Like

When Robert Williams was handed the surveillance photo and the photo of himself side by side in the interrogation room, he held them up and said: "I hope you don't think all Black men look alike." The detective later admitted the images didn't match. Williams was released, but the charge wasn't dropped for several months.

Standing up against an AI error is not a single dramatic moment. It involves multiple steps, and each one has a different person responsible for it:

Step 1: Identify what the AI did. Was the error in the data? The algorithm? How the output was used? Williams' case involved all three — bad training data that produced a bad match that a human investigator accepted without sufficient skepticism.

Step 2: Document everything. Williams' case became legally actionable because the ACLU was able to show, in writing, the exact chain of events: algorithm flags image → officer reviews match → officer files charge. Documentation creates accountability.

Step 3: Find the decision point where a human could have stopped it. AI systems rarely act alone. There was a human who looked at that match and said "good enough." That human is the intervention point. Challenging the AI means finding those humans and making them answer for their decisions.

The Ethical Tension

The Detroit Police Department argued that facial recognition was just a "lead" — an investigative tool, not a verdict. Detectives were still supposed to verify the match before making an arrest. Does that argument hold? If a human approved the arrest, is the AI still to blame? Or does giving police a flawed tool make the tool-maker responsible? There is no clean answer here. Sit with it.

Step 4: Use institutions that exist for exactly this purpose. The ACLU, the Innocence Project, local public defenders, and — increasingly — state legislatures have taken up algorithmic accountability. In 2021, Detroit limited how facial recognition could be used following Williams' case. San Francisco banned it entirely for city agencies in 2019. Individual cases, fought publicly, change policy.

The Asymmetry of Power

Here is something hard to hold: the organizations that deploy AI systems — police departments, banks, hospitals, schools — have teams of lawyers, contracts with technology companies, and institutional authority. A person who has been wronged by one of these systems often has none of those things.

This is called an asymmetry of power — one side has far more resources and influence than the other. It's why individual cases matter so much when they are documented and publicized. The Williams case was covered by the New York Times, the Washington Post, and the MIT Technology Review. That coverage created pressure. Pressure created policy change. The algorithm didn't change — the rules around who could use it and how did.

You are probably not going to get arrested by a facial recognition system. But you might be screened out of a job application by a hiring algorithm, or flagged as a credit risk by a loan-scoring model, or have your social media account suspended by a content moderation AI. The structure is the same: a system made a decision about you, that decision has consequences, and the people who built the system are not in the room with you when it happens.

What those situations have in common with Williams' case is the solution: document it, name it, find the human who approved it, and use whatever institutional lever is available to you — complaint forms, regulatory bodies, media attention, legal aid, or simply telling other people what happened so they can protect themselves.

Asymmetry of power — A situation where one side in a dispute has significantly more resources, information, or authority than the other. When an individual challenges a corporation or government agency over an AI error, they are almost always the weaker side.
Your Position Right Now

Most people who encounter an AI error never find out it was an error. They think they were rejected by a person. They think the system was neutral. They don't know that the algorithm made a judgment, that the judgment was encoded in training data, and that the training data reflected the choices and blind spots of the people who built it.

You now know that. You can spot the structure of an AI error — biased data, unchecked output, human approval of a machine's verdict — before anyone names it for you. That's not a small thing. Most of the adults in Robert Williams' story didn't know it either, until someone made them see it.

Module 5 · Lesson 2

When Correcting the Record Isn't Enough

AI doesn't just make errors once — it can lock those errors in, spread them, and make them nearly impossible to undo.
What do you do when the truth doesn't catch up to the mistake?

In 2016, Tammie Hedges had never heard of a predictive algorithm called COMPAS. But COMPAS — Correctional Offender Management Profiling for Alternative Sanctions — had effectively heard of her. The software, used in courts across the United States, assigned criminal defendants a "risk score" predicting how likely they were to reoffend. Those scores influenced bail decisions, sentencing, and parole.

In 2016, the investigative outlet ProPublica analyzed COMPAS scores in Broward County, Florida and found something stark: the algorithm was nearly twice as likely to falsely flag Black defendants as high risk compared to white defendants, and nearly twice as likely to falsely flag white defendants as low risk. A Black defendant who did not go on to reoffend was labeled high-risk 44.9% of the time. A white defendant who did not reoffend was labeled high-risk 23.5% of the time.

The company that made COMPAS, Northpointe, disputed ProPublica's methodology. Researchers split into camps. Meanwhile, judges kept using the scores. Courts kept referencing them in sentencing documents. The error — or the dispute about whether it was an error — continued influencing real people's real lives while the argument about statistics played out in academic journals.

This is what happens when an algorithmic error doesn't just affect one person but gets baked into the system that processes thousands of people.

The Problem With "We Fixed It"

When a human makes a factual error — a journalist misquotes someone, a doctor misreads a scan — there is a relatively clear path to correction. Issue a correction. Read the scan again. The damage may linger, but the error can be identified and stopped.

Algorithmic errors work differently for several reasons:

1. They are invisible at the point of impact. When a judge uses a COMPAS score to set bail, neither the defendant nor their lawyer typically knows what inputs generated that score, why those inputs were weighted the way they were, or whether the score has been independently verified. The error happens inside a black box. (A black box is a system where you can see the inputs and the outputs but not what happens in between.)

2. They repeat automatically. A biased training dataset doesn't just cause one error — it causes errors on every single prediction the system makes, until someone changes the data or the model. COMPAS wasn't producing one wrong score per year. It was processing hundreds of defendants per county, per year, across multiple states.

3. Corrections don't reach everyone the error reached. A person who received a high-risk COMPAS score, served extra time, and was released has already lost those years. A correction issued in 2018 to the model doesn't give that time back. The error's footprint is asymmetric — it spreads fast, corrects slowly.

Key Term

Black box — an AI system whose internal decision-making process cannot be observed or explained, even by its creators in some cases. You can see what goes in (a defendant's criminal history, age, neighborhood) and what comes out (a risk score), but not why those inputs produced that output.

The Right to Explanation

In 2018, the European Union passed the General Data Protection Regulation — usually called the GDPR. One of its most significant provisions gives individuals the right to a meaningful explanation when an automated system makes a decision that significantly affects them. You have the right to know: what data was used, what logic was applied, and how to challenge the result.

The United States does not have an equivalent federal law. Individual states have begun to move — Illinois has strong biometric privacy rules; California has broad data rights under the CCPA. But for most Americans, if an algorithm decides something about you, you have no guaranteed legal right to know what it considered or why.

This is a live policy debate happening right now, in legislatures, courtrooms, and corporate boardrooms. The question being argued: should "algorithmic transparency" be a legal right the way freedom of speech or due process is? People who build AI systems often argue that their algorithms are proprietary — trade secrets, essentially — and that forcing companies to reveal them would destroy competitive advantage. People who are affected by those algorithms argue that you cannot challenge a decision you cannot see.

You now understand why this debate exists and what's actually at stake in it. That puts you ahead of most participants in these hearings, who are either technology lobbyists or policy generalists who've never thought about what a risk score actually is.

The Ethical Tension

A company spent years and millions of dollars developing a predictive model. Revealing how it works might allow competitors to copy it — or allow defendants to game the system by appearing lower-risk. But defendants can't challenge scores they can't see. Who owns an explanation of a decision made about you? There is no consensus answer to this question. The tension is real.

What "Standing Up" Looks Like at Scale

ProPublica's investigation didn't end COMPAS. But it changed the conversation. After 2016, courts in several states began requiring that defendants be given access to the factors used in their risk assessments. The Wisconsin Supreme Court ruled in 2016 — in State v. Loomis — that COMPAS scores could be used in sentencing, but judges had to explicitly acknowledge they were doing so and explain their reasoning beyond the score. It wasn't a ban. It was a requirement for human accountability on top of machine output.

That's what structural correction looks like. Not one person winning one appeal, but a documented investigation creating enough pressure that institutions are forced to add human checkpoints to automated decisions. The lever is always the same: make the error visible, name the mechanism, identify the human responsible, create a record.

For someone your age, this matters because the systems being built right now — the hiring tools, the admissions algorithms, the content moderation systems — will be making decisions about you within the next few years. The debates happening in courts and legislatures right now are setting the rules for those decisions. Your generation will either inherit those rules or change them.

AI Hallucinations and the People They Hurt

When AI invents facts — and those facts are about real people — the consequences can be severe and fast.
If an AI confidently states something false about you, who is responsible for the damage?

In February 2023, a law professor at Leah Harber's firm ran a search on ChatGPT, looking for examples of legal cases involving sexual harassment by lawyers on law school trips. The AI produced a detailed, confident response. It cited specific case names, quoted court documents, named dates, and — most importantly — named a specific lawyer as a defendant in one of the cases.

That lawyer was Mark Walters, a radio host in Georgia. The cases ChatGPT described did not exist. The documents it quoted were fabricated. The incidents it described never happened. ChatGPT had hallucinated — a term used when a language model generates information that is plausible-sounding but entirely invented. Walters sued the developer responsible for the search, Eugene Volokh, for defamation.

Two months later, in May 2023, a New York attorney named Steven Schwartz submitted a legal brief to a federal court in which he cited six cases he had found using ChatGPT. When the opposing attorneys couldn't find those cases, they asked for the citations. The cases didn't exist. ChatGPT had invented them, complete with fake quotes and fake holding statements. Judge P. Kevin Castel fined Schwartz and his firm $5,000 and called ChatGPT's outputs "legal gibberish."

These two cases happened within months of each other. They are not isolated. They are part of a pattern.

What Hallucination Actually Is

A large language model like ChatGPT or Claude doesn't "know" things the way a database knows things. It doesn't store facts and look them up. Instead, it predicts what text should come next based on patterns in billions of examples it was trained on. That process is extraordinarily powerful for generating fluent, coherent language — but it has a specific failure mode: when the model doesn't have the information needed to answer accurately, it doesn't say "I don't know." It generates what would be a plausible answer — and that plausible answer might be entirely fabricated.

The word "hallucination" is actually a somewhat misleading metaphor. An AI isn't confused or dreaming. It's doing exactly what it was designed to do — generating probable text — and that design produces fiction in contexts where facts are required.

Here's the critical element: hallucinated text often sounds more confident and authoritative than accurate text. The fake legal cases in the Schwartz brief were formatted exactly like real legal citations. Walters' name appeared in a fabricated narrative with the same syntactic certainty as a verified court record. The system produces conviction without truth.

Key Term

Hallucination — when a language model generates text that sounds accurate and is presented with apparent confidence, but is partially or entirely fabricated. The model isn't "lying" in a human sense — it has no intent. But the output can cause real harm to real people.

The Defamation Problem

When a hallucinated AI output harms someone's reputation — as it did with Mark Walters — we enter legal territory that courts have not fully mapped yet. Defamation is the publication of a false statement that harms someone's reputation. Traditionally, defamation requires a person or organization that made the false statement to have done so with some degree of fault — either knowing it was false, or not caring whether it was true.

AI hallucinations complicate this. The AI didn't "know" the statement was false — it doesn't know anything, in the traditional sense. The company that built the AI didn't specifically intend to defame Walters. The user who prompted the AI wasn't trying to damage his reputation. And yet a false, damaging statement about a real, named person was generated and circulated.

Legal scholars are currently debating whether the companies that build and deploy large language models can be held liable for defamatory outputs. Section 230 of the Communications Decency Act has historically protected internet platforms from liability for user-generated content — but AI-generated content isn't user-generated. It's machine-generated, and the machine is the platform's product.

Australia and the UK have moved faster than the US on AI defamation cases. In 2023, an Australian mayor, Brian Hood, threatened to sue OpenAI after ChatGPT falsely stated he had been convicted of bribery — in reality, Hood had been the whistleblower who exposed the bribery scheme. OpenAI corrected the error. Whether the correction repaired the reputational damage is a different question entirely.

The Ethical Tension

If you use an AI tool and it generates a false statement about a real person, and you share that statement without checking it, are you responsible? The AI produced the words. You didn't invent them. But you chose to share them. At what point does the human in the loop become the responsible party — and does that responsibility diminish if the AI's confidence made the statement seem credible? There is no settled answer here.

How to Stand Up Against Hallucination Harm

If you are the person harmed by a hallucination — your name, your work, your reputation described falsely — you have several options, all of which require the same core action: create a documented record of the false statement before trying to have it corrected, because once corrected, the evidence of the original harm may disappear.

Screenshot and timestamp first. Before you contact anyone, document what the AI said, when, and in what context. This is evidence. Once you report it to the company, the output may be altered or deleted.

Use the company's reporting mechanism. Every major AI company has a way to report harmful outputs. This is not just about getting your specific case fixed — every report goes into a dataset that companies use to identify patterns. Your individual report contributes to systemic change.

Contact the person or platform who shared the output. If someone used an AI-generated hallucination about you and shared it, that person may have a correction obligation under platform terms of service, and in some jurisdictions, under defamation law.

If the harm is significant, consult a lawyer about defamation. The law here is unsettled, but that cuts both ways — it also means there is room to make new legal arguments. The Brian Hood case changed OpenAI's behavior without a lawsuit ever being filed. The threat of litigation was enough.

You now understand something that the attorney in the Schwartz case apparently did not: AI outputs require verification before they are used as facts in any consequential context. That verification habit is not optional — it is what separates competent use of these tools from dangerous use.

Module 5 · Lesson 4

From Noticing to Acting: The Architecture of Pushback

You've learned to see AI errors. Now: what does it actually take to do something about them?
Knowing something is wrong and having the tools to change it are two different things. What tools do you actually have?

In 2020, a Dutch court ordered the government of the Netherlands to shut down an automated welfare fraud detection system called SyRI — System Risk Indication. SyRI had been used since 2014 to combine data from multiple government databases — tax records, employment records, utility payments, housing — to generate a risk score predicting whether a household was committing benefit fraud.

The system had been deployed primarily in lower-income neighborhoods with higher proportions of immigrant residents. It never explained to anyone why they had been flagged. People who were investigated often didn't know SyRI existed. The court found that SyRI violated the European Convention on Human Rights — specifically Article 8, the right to private life — because its opacity made it impossible for citizens to understand or challenge decisions made about them.

A civil society organization called the Dutch Foundation for Privacy Rights brought the case. Not a famous lawyer. Not a government whistleblower. A nonprofit staffed largely by volunteers and digital rights advocates. They filed in 2019. They won in 2020. The government did not appeal. SyRI was shut down.

This is what organized, documented, institutional pushback looks like when it works.

The Four Levers of Pushback

Throughout this module you've seen several cases where AI errors were challenged. Looking across all of them — Williams, COMPAS, SyRI, Hood, Schwartz — a pattern emerges. Effective pushback uses one or more of four levers:

Lever 1: Legal action. Courts can order AI systems shut down (SyRI), require companies to correct outputs (Hood), and impose penalties for misuse (Schwartz). Legal action requires either a lawyer or a legal aid organization, and it requires a documented harm. It's slow and expensive, but it sets precedents that apply to everyone, not just you.

Lever 2: Regulatory bodies. In the EU, data protection authorities can investigate AI systems and fine companies under the GDPR. In the US, the Federal Trade Commission has begun investigating AI companies for deceptive practices. In 2023, the FTC opened an investigation into OpenAI's data handling practices. You can file a complaint with a regulatory body without a lawyer. Those complaints build a record that investigators use to decide where to focus attention.

Lever 3: Public accountability — journalism and documentation. ProPublica's COMPAS investigation didn't produce a court order, but it produced policy change. Williams' case was covered nationally because the ACLU published a detailed account of it. When individual harms are documented and made visible, they become collective — other people recognize their own experience in yours, and collective recognition creates political pressure. A documented, public case of AI error is much harder to ignore than a private complaint.

Lever 4: Institutional pressure — procurement, voting, organizing. The organizations that deploy AI systems — schools, hospitals, banks, police departments, government agencies — answer to someone. Schools answer to school boards. Police departments answer to city councils. Banks answer to regulators and shareholders. Identifying who controls the organization that deployed the AI that harmed you is the first step in applying pressure to the right place. At an institutional level, decisions about which AI tools get purchased and deployed are made by a small number of administrators. Those decisions can be influenced.

What People Your Age Actually Did

This is not hypothetical. Young people have been central to several AI accountability campaigns in recent years.

In 2020, students at Akdeniz University in Turkey organized to challenge an AI-based exam monitoring system that penalized students for eye movements, background noise, and unusual typing patterns during online exams — disproportionately affecting students in noisy households or with disabilities. The organizing was primarily student-led and resulted in the university revising how the system's outputs were used.

In the United Kingdom, A-level students in 2020 received algorithmically downgraded exam scores after their physical exams were cancelled due to COVID-19. The algorithm weighted historical school performance data heavily, meaning students from lower-performing schools received systematically lower scores regardless of their individual work. Students, parents, and teachers organized. Within days, the government reversed the results — the largest reversal of AI-driven academic decisions in British history.

In both cases, the pattern was the same: individual cases were connected to show a pattern; the pattern was made public; institutional decision-makers felt enough pressure to change course. No one needed a law degree. They needed evidence, coordination, and persistence.

You Now Know This

You can see the structure of an AI error, identify where the human accountability point is, and name which lever of pushback is most likely to work in a given situation. Most adults who encounter AI errors don't have this framework. They don't know it's an option to push back at all. You do — and knowing what's possible is the first requirement for actually doing it.

The Hard Question This Module Can't Answer for You

Knowing how to push back and deciding to push back are different things. Pushing back has costs. It takes time. It can put you in conflict with institutions that have authority over you — your school, your employer, your government. It can feel isolating if the people around you don't see what you see. And it often doesn't work the first time, or the fifth.

The cases in this module that worked — SyRI, the UK A-levels, Williams' case — took months or years. The people involved were told they were wrong, told the system was fine, told they didn't understand the technology. Some of them had the support of organizations with resources. Some didn't.

The question this module can't answer for you is: when is the harm serious enough to be worth the cost of fighting? That's a judgment call. It depends on the severity of the harm, the resources you have, the institutional context you're in, and what you believe about whether systems can be changed. These are real variables, not rhetorical ones.

What you can do right now, before any specific harm occurs, is build three habits: document consistently — keep records of algorithmic decisions that affect you; name the mechanism — when something seems wrong, identify whether a machine made the decision and on what basis; and know the lever — for any institution you interact with, know who it answers to. These habits cost almost nothing before you need them, and they are worth an enormous amount if you ever do.

The Ethical Tension — Final

AI systems are also genuinely useful. They detect cancer earlier than human radiologists in some studies. They help students with dyslexia read faster. They translate language in real time across barriers that used to take years to cross. Pushing back against AI errors doesn't mean rejecting these tools. It means demanding that the organizations that deploy them take responsibility for the harms they cause. How you hold both truths — genuine benefit and genuine harm — without collapsing into either "AI is always good" or "AI is always bad" is the actual skill this course is trying to build.
