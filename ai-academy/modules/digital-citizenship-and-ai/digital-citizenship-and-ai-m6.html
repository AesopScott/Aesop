Module 6 · Lesson 1

What a Pledge Actually Does

From a student's deleted post in 2013 to the document you'll write today — promises about online behavior have real consequences.
Why would anyone need to write down what kind of person they want to be online — and who is that document really for?

In September 2010, Tyler Clementi, an 18-year-old freshman at Rutgers University in New Jersey, jumped from the George Washington Bridge after his roommate secretly streamed video of him on the internet. The roommate, Dharun Ravi, had used a webcam to broadcast Tyler's private moments to people online. Tyler had done nothing wrong. Ravi was later convicted on bias intimidation charges.

What followed wasn't just a legal case. Schools, companies, governments, and advocacy organizations all asked the same question: Could a different kind of culture online have prevented this? Could people who saw the broadcast have done something? Could Ravi himself, at some earlier moment, have paused and thought about what kind of person he wanted to be online?

In 2011, the organization It Gets Better Project and dozens of school districts began rolling out digital citizenship pledges — written commitments students signed about how they would treat others online. Not because a signature stops cruelty. But because the act of writing down who you want to be forces you to think about it before a hard moment arrives.

A Pledge Is Not a Rule

Here's the first thing to understand: a digital citizenship pledge is not a rule someone else made for you. Rules say "you must" or "you cannot." A pledge says "I will" — it comes from you. That's a completely different relationship to an idea.

Rules are enforced from the outside. If nobody's watching, rules disappear. A pledge is enforced from the inside, by your own sense of who you are. That's why pledges matter even when — especially when — no adult is around and no algorithm is logging what you do.

This is also why pledges can feel uncomfortable to write. If you write down "I will not share someone else's private information without their consent" and then you do exactly that six months later, you have to deal with the fact that you broke your own word to yourself. That tension is the whole point. It's supposed to mean something.

Ethical Question — No Clean Answer

If a student signs a digital citizenship pledge and then watches someone get publicly humiliated in a comment thread without saying anything — did they violate the pledge? Does staying silent count as participating?

What AI Changes About This Conversation

Digital citizenship pledges have existed since roughly 2007, when educators first started using the term in schools. But those early pledges were written for a world without AI in daily use. They focused on things like cyberbullying, password safety, and not sharing personal information with strangers.

Now, in 2024, most teenagers interact with AI tools every week — for homework help, for creative projects, for talking through problems, for entertainment. This changes what a pledge needs to say. You're not just deciding how to treat other humans online. You're deciding how to use tools that can write for you, think alongside you, and make it very easy to deceive others at scale.

Consider what happened in January 2023 at Sequoia Union High School District in California. Students began submitting essays that were clearly AI-generated. Teachers noticed because the writing was technically fluent but weirdly hollow — no personal voice, no real mistakes. The district scrambled to write policies. But the students who hadn't used AI felt cheated. The students who had used it felt they'd done nothing wrong. Nobody had ever asked them to think about it before it happened.

A pledge written before you face that situation is worth a hundred policies written after.

You Can Now See What Most People Miss

Most people think digital citizenship is about protecting yourself from bad things online. You now understand it's actually about deciding, in advance, who you want to be — before you're in a situation where the easy choice and the right choice are different things.

The Four Domains Your Pledge Will Cover

Over this module's four lessons, you'll draft a pledge organized around four domains — four areas of online life where decisions actually happen. Here's a preview:

Identity & Honesty

How you present yourself and how you use AI to speak or create — without pretending to be something you're not.

Privacy & Consent

How you handle information about others, including what you share, forward, or ask AI to find.

What you do when you witness harm online — because watching and doing nothing is also a choice.

How you decide what to believe, what to verify, and how you use AI without letting it think for you entirely.

Each lesson in this module focuses on one domain. By Lesson 4, you'll have a complete draft pledge with real, specific commitments — not vague promises to "be kind," but precise statements about how you'll actually behave.

Module 6 · Lesson 2

Identity & Honesty — Who's Speaking When AI Helps?

The first domain of your pledge: what you owe people when you let AI speak in your voice.
If a piece of writing sounds like you, uses your name, and represents your position — but an AI wrote most of it — is that honest? Does it depend on the situation?

In December 2022, Senator Chris Murphy of Connecticut wrote a Twitter thread that went briefly viral — not for what it said, but for why he wrote it. Murphy had asked ChatGPT to write a speech for him, then published the result alongside his own version, asking his followers to compare them. The AI's version was polished, grammatically perfect, and completely indistinguishable from a real political statement. Murphy called it "genuinely frightening."

Around the same time, researchers at Cornell University published a study showing that people could not reliably tell the difference between AI-generated and human-written persuasive essays. The AI essays were actually rated as more credible in some trials. This created a direct question about political speech, job applications, school essays, social media posts — any context where the whole point is that a real human being is communicating their real views.

The question wasn't whether AI writing was good. It was: when you send something with your name on it, what are you actually claiming? That you wrote it? That you believe it? That the ideas are yours? When AI helps, which of those claims are still true — and which become a kind of quiet lie?

The Spectrum of AI Assistance

Here's something worth mapping out carefully: AI assistance isn't binary. There's a huge difference between "I asked AI to check my grammar" and "I asked AI to write this from scratch." Most of the genuinely hard questions live in between.

Think of it as a spectrum. On one end: using AI as a spell-checker or thesaurus — almost everyone agrees that's fine, because you're still doing all the thinking. On the other end: copying AI output word for word and presenting it as your own original work — almost everyone agrees that crosses a line. The middle is where it gets complicated.

What about asking AI to help you structure an argument you've already developed? What about asking it to rewrite a paragraph you wrote but felt was weak? What about generating several versions of an idea and choosing the best one? None of those are obviously wrong — but all of them mean the final product is partly AI and partly you, and it might not be clear to the person reading it.

The core honesty question is not "did AI help?" — it's "am I accurately representing what kind of work this is?"

Ethical Question — No Clean Answer

What "Your Voice" Actually Is

One of the underrated things about your voice as a writer — even if you don't think of yourself as a writer — is that it carries information about who you are. Your sentence length. Your word choices. Your tendency to go on a tangent and come back. Your specific way of making a point. These things developed over years of thinking and speaking and reading and living.

When AI writes in your name, it draws on patterns from millions of other people's writing. The result can sound competent, even eloquent. But it doesn't sound like you — because it isn't. And the person reading it might think they're learning something about who you are, when really they're just reading a statistical average of everyone who ever wrote something similar.

In 2023, a teacher named Erin Tripp at a high school in Oregon described asking her students to write about a memory from childhood. Several submitted AI-generated responses. She said reading them felt "hollow — like shaking hands with a glove." The personal essay, she argued, exists specifically to put a real person in the room. AI-written personal essays defeat their own purpose.

This doesn't mean AI writing is wrong in every context. It means the context matters — and that honesty requires you to know which contexts demand your real voice and to deliver it.

Knowing This Changes Everything

You can now see something most people treat as a gray area and call it clearly: AI assistance isn't about the tool — it's about whether the person receiving your work has an accurate understanding of what kind of work it is. That's the standard your pledge should encode.

Drafting Pledge Domain 1: Identity & Honesty

Your pledge commitment for this domain should be specific enough that you'd know — in an actual situation — whether you were honoring it or not. "I will be honest online" is too vague. Consider instead building a commitment around the key principle from this lesson: the person receiving your work should have an accurate picture of what kind of work it is.

Starter Language for Domain 1

When I submit work with my name on it, I will be clear — to myself and when relevant to others — about how much of the thinking, writing, and structuring was mine.
I will not use AI to speak my personal views or represent my personal experiences as if I wrote them from scratch, in contexts where that distinction matters to the person reading.
When AI assists my work, I will make sure the ideas and positions represented are actually ones I hold — not just outputs I found convincing in the moment.

Module 6 · Lesson 3

Privacy, Consent & the Bystander Problem

Domains 2 and 3 of your pledge: what you owe people when you handle their information — and when you watch something go wrong.
When you forward a screenshot, share a location, or scroll past someone being humiliated online — what exactly are you responsible for?

In August 2012, a 16-year-old girl was sexually assaulted by two members of the Steubenville High School football team after a party. What made the case nationally significant was not just the assault — it was what happened on social media in the hours and days after. Photos and videos were posted and shared. Text messages joked about what had happened. At least one video showed bystanders laughing. Dozens of people saw the posts. Very few said anything.

In 2013, Ma'lik Richmond and Trent Mays were found guilty by a juvenile court judge. Two additional students faced charges related to failing to report the assault. But what struck legal scholars and educators was the role of the sharers — the students who forwarded content, who liked posts, who chose to engage with material that documented someone being harmed.

None of those students had assaulted anyone. But they had made a series of choices: to share, to forward, to watch, to stay silent. Every single one of those was a choice. And together, those choices created an environment where the harm felt normal, documented, even entertaining.

Privacy Isn't Just Your Own Information

Most conversations about privacy online focus on protecting your own data — don't share your password, don't post your home address, use two-factor authentication. That's all real. But there's another privacy domain that gets far less attention: your responsibility for other people's information.

When you screenshot a conversation and send it to someone not in that conversation, you've made a privacy decision on behalf of everyone who said something in it. They didn't consent. You decided for them.

When you tag someone in a photo they didn't know was being taken, or share their location without asking, or forward a message someone sent you privately — you're making a unilateral decision about information that isn't only yours to share.

AI makes this more complicated, not simpler. In 2023, a teenager in the UK used an AI image generator to create realistic-looking fake photos of a classmate and shared them in a group chat. The classmate had not consented to having their likeness used. The images were humiliating. The AI's role was to make the harm easier and faster to produce — but the decision to produce it was entirely human.

Consent — meaning whether the person whose information is involved agreed to that use of their information — is the standard your pledge should apply.

Consent In the context of privacy, consent means the person whose information is being shared or used has actually agreed to that use — not just "they didn't tell me not to."
Ethical Question — No Clean Answer

Your best friend tells you something deeply personal in a private conversation. Later, you're worried about them and you screenshot the conversation and show it to a trusted adult. You broke their privacy. You may have helped them. Were you right to do it?

The Bystander Effect, Online and With AI

In 1964, a New York woman named Kitty Genovese was attacked outside her apartment building. Reports at the time claimed 38 neighbors heard or saw the attack and did nothing. Later investigations disputed some details of that original account, but the story launched decades of research into what psychologists call the bystander effect: the more people are present when something bad happens, the less likely any individual is to intervene, because everyone assumes someone else will act.

Online, the bystander effect is supercharged. A cruel post might be seen by 500 people. Each of those 500 people sees 499 others who could respond. The diffusion of responsibility is enormous. And unlike a physical space, where your presence is visible, online you can observe without anyone knowing you were there.

This creates a specific moral situation that your pledge needs to address directly: what do you commit to doing when you see harm happening and you have the ability to do something? Not always. Not perfectly. But specifically — do you have a commitment to act, or do you have permission to scroll past?

In 2022, a study by the Cybersmile Foundation found that 70% of young people aged 12–25 had witnessed online bullying, but only 20% had intervened. The gap between witnessing and acting is not a knowledge gap — most people know they should do something. It's a commitment gap. They never decided, in advance, what they would do.

You Can Now See What Most People Miss

Most conversations about online safety focus on what's done to you. You now understand the harder question: what you do when something is done to someone else. That's where your pledge needs to be most specific — because it's the decision most likely to arrive in a moment when doing nothing is the easiest path.

Drafting Pledge Domains 2 & 3

These two domains are closely related — privacy violations often create the conditions for bystander situations. Your pledge commitments for both should be specific about what you will actually do, not just what you won't do.

Starter Language for Domain 2: Privacy & Consent

Before sharing, forwarding, or saving information about another person — including screenshots, locations, photos, or messages — I will ask whether that person would consent to this use if I asked them.
I will not use AI to generate, alter, or distribute content involving another person's likeness, voice, or private information without their explicit agreement.
I will treat information shared with me in private as private — not as content available for wider distribution unless the person who shared it agrees.

Starter Language for Domain 3: Impact & Bystander Responsibility

When I witness someone being harmed, humiliated, or targeted online, I will not treat silence as neutral — I will do something, even if that something is only a private message to the person being targeted.
I will not share, like, or engage with content that I know exists to humiliate or harm a specific person, even if I find it funny in the moment.
I acknowledge that forwarding harmful content is participation in harm, regardless of whether I created the content or added commentary to it.

Module 6 · Lesson 4

Critical Trust — Believing Well in an AI World

Domain 4 of your pledge: what you owe yourself — and the people around you — when AI can generate convincing misinformation in seconds.
If AI can produce false information that looks completely real, what does it actually mean to be a responsible person online — and what does your pledge need to say about it?

Three days before Slovakia's parliamentary election in September 2023, an audio recording circulated on Facebook. In it, a voice that sounded exactly like Michal Šimečka — leader of the Progressive Slovakia party — appeared to discuss plans to buy votes and raise beer prices. The recording spread rapidly.

It was fake. It had been generated using AI voice-cloning technology. Independent researchers at the EU DisinfoLab confirmed this within hours. But by then, the recording had already reached hundreds of thousands of people. And critically, Facebook's policies required a 72-hour pause before fact-checking content during an election period — meaning the correction arrived after the damage was done.

Progressive Slovakia lost the election by a narrow margin. It's impossible to prove the audio recording changed the outcome. But it's also impossible to prove it didn't. A two-minute audio clip, generated by an AI in minutes, may have influenced which government came to power in a European nation. This is not a hypothetical. This happened in 2023.

The Verification Obligation

Before AI voice-cloning and image generation became widely available, there was a rough heuristic most people used: if something sounded authentic, looked authentic, and came from what seemed like a real source — it was probably real. That heuristic was already imperfect, but it had some connection to reality.

Now it has almost none. A sufficiently capable AI can generate audio that sounds like any specific person saying anything. It can generate photos of events that never occurred. It can write convincing first-person accounts of experiences it invented. The technical barrier between "real" and "fabricated" has essentially collapsed for anyone with access to consumer AI tools.

This means that every person who shares information online now has a verification obligation that didn't exist ten years ago. Not to be a fact-checker for every single thing they read — that's not realistic. But to have a personal standard for what you require before passing something on to others, especially when the stakes are high.

At an institutional level, this is now a policy problem. In 2024, the European Union's AI Act included specific requirements for labeling AI-generated content. Major platforms began rolling out detection tools. But no law or tool removes the responsibility from the individual who shares false content to their own network. Institutional solutions don't replace personal ones.

Ethical Question — No Clean Answer

You see a video clip that confirms something you already believe is true about a politician you dislike. It looks real. You share it with your followers. Three hours later, you learn it was AI-generated and entirely false. You delete it. But thousands of people already saw it. Are you responsible for the harm the clip caused — even though you didn't create it and you deleted it as soon as you knew?

Letting AI Think For You vs. Thinking With AI

There's a second dimension to critical trust that's less about misinformation and more about your own thinking. When you use AI to help you understand something — form an opinion, research an issue, decide what to believe — there's a risk that you're not actually thinking. You're outsourcing your thinking and then adopting the conclusions as your own.

This matters because AI systems have biases embedded in their training. They don't always surface uncertainty honestly. They can present one reasonable-sounding interpretation of a contested question while not mentioning the other interpretations that exist. And they have a tendency — documented by researchers at the MIT Media Lab — to produce confident-sounding answers even when the evidence is genuinely ambiguous.

In 2023, a New York attorney named Steven Schwartz submitted a legal brief that cited six court cases as precedents. All six were invented by ChatGPT. Schwartz had not checked whether they were real — he had trusted the AI. He was sanctioned by the judge. The error was completely preventable, but it required a specific habit: treating AI output as a starting point to be verified, not a conclusion to be accepted.

That habit is what the Critical Trust domain of your pledge is about. Using AI to help you think is powerful. Letting AI think for you and reporting the results is a kind of intellectual abdication.

You Can Now See What Most People Miss

Most people treat AI outputs as either reliable facts or unreliable garbage — a binary. You now understand the more precise truth: AI can be a powerful tool for expanding your thinking, but only if you remain the person who evaluates, verifies, and ultimately decides what to believe. That's not a limitation of AI. It's a responsibility that belongs to you.

Completing Your Pledge — Domain 4 and the Final Document

This is the final domain — and the one that makes your pledge complete. With all four domains, you'll have a document that addresses every major category of online decision-making that involves AI.

Starter Language for Domain 4: Critical Trust

Before sharing content that makes a factual claim — especially about a specific person or event — I will apply at least one verification step: checking the source, looking for a corroborating report, or acknowledging to myself that I haven't verified it if I share it anyway.
I will use AI as a tool to expand and challenge my thinking, not as a replacement for my own reasoning — meaning I will question AI outputs rather than simply accepting them as conclusions.
When I change my mind because of AI-generated or AI-curated content, I will be able to say why — not just that the content was convincing, but what specific evidence or reasoning it provided.
I will not share content I suspect might be AI-fabricated simply because it supports a view I already hold.

Your completed pledge now covers all four domains. In the final lab, you'll assemble the full document and test it against a cumulative scenario — one that touches all four domains at once, as real situations usually do.

A pledge isn't a destination. It's a reference point — something you can return to when a decision feels hard, and ask: who did I say I wanted to be? That question, in the right moment, is worth more than any rule.
