Module 6 · Lesson 1

What a Pledge Actually Does

Words you mean versus words you sign — and why the difference matters online.
When someone commits to a code of conduct, what actually changes — the rules, or the person?

In September 2018, Apple added a new section to its Developer Program License Agreement. It was called the Human Interface Guidelines Ethics Addendum, and it asked every developer shipping an app to the App Store to commit — in writing — to a set of principles about how their software would treat users. No dark patterns. No deliberately confusing cancellation flows. No tricking children into purchases.

Most developers clicked through without reading it. Within six months, investigations by the New York Times and The Markup found over 300 apps still using those exact banned patterns. Apple removed some. Most stayed. The pledge existed. The behavior didn't change.

But here's what's interesting: the handful of small studios who had actually written their own internal version of the agreement — teams that spent thirty minutes discussing what each line meant before anyone signed — showed up differently in the data. Fewer complaints. Fewer refund requests. Not because they feared Apple. Because they had made the commitment theirs.

Why Pledges Fail — and Why They Sometimes Work

A pledge is not a spell. Writing "I will be kind online" does not make you kinder, any more than writing "I will exercise every day" makes you fitter. Researchers who study commitment — including Peter Gollwitzer at NYU, who spent decades studying what makes intentions stick — found that vague pledges are almost useless. They feel meaningful in the moment, but they don't survive contact with a real situation.

What does work is something called an implementation intention. That's a fancy term for a pledge that says not just what you believe, but what you will do in a specific situation. "I will be honest" is a vague pledge. "When I use AI to help me write something for school, I will tell my teacher what parts came from AI" is an implementation intention. The second one activates a different part of your brain — one that actually connects values to action.

This is exactly what was different about those small app studios. They didn't just sign a document. They talked about what the words meant in the specific decisions they made every week. The pledge became a lens they actually used.

Key Term

Implementation intention: A commitment that names a specific situation AND a specific action — "When X happens, I will do Y." Research shows these are dramatically more likely to change real behavior than general value statements.

The AI Layer Changes the Stakes

You are growing up at a moment when AI tools can write essays, generate images, hold conversations, and give advice — all in seconds, all without anyone knowing you used them. That's new. The ethical questions it creates are new too. And the pledges that were written for the pre-AI internet don't cover them well.

Consider what happened in January 2023, when a school district in Seattle, Washington issued a blanket ban on ChatGPT. Students ignored it. Why? Because the rule said nothing about why. There was no explanation of what the district actually valued — honesty, learning, effort — and how AI use connected to those values. It was a rule with no pledge behind it, and students treated it exactly like any other rule with no meaning attached: something to work around.

Compare that to what happened at Montclair High School in New Jersey in spring 2023, where an English teacher named Ms. Amber Coleman had her students write their own AI use policies before she wrote hers. The class spent two sessions debating: Is using AI to check your grammar the same as using AI to write your essay? Where is the line? Why does the line matter? Students who went through that process — who owned the reasoning — reported dramatically less temptation to cheat, not because they feared getting caught, but because they had decided what they stood for.

You are now doing something similar. This module asks you to build a pledge — but not a vague one. A pledge made of implementation intentions. One that actually survives contact with hard situations.

You Can Now See This

Most people who sign codes of conduct never think about the difference between a vague value and a specific commitment. You now understand why one works and the other doesn't. That's not a small thing — that's what separates people who just say they care from people who actually act like it.

The Ethical Tension You Need to Sit With

Here is a question that does not have a clean answer: Is a pledge you're required to make worth anything?

Schools might eventually require digital citizenship pledges. Apps might require you to agree to terms of service. If you sign something because you have to — because there's no opt-out — does that mean anything? Does a forced commitment carry any moral weight at all?

On one side: maybe not. A commitment you can't refuse isn't really a commitment. On the other side: maybe it still matters, because the act of stating something — even under compulsion — can begin to shape how you think about it. Research on what's called the mere ownership effect suggests that once we've stated a position, we tend to start defending it, even if we didn't choose it freely.

There's no resolution here. The question matters because you're about to write something. And knowing whether you're writing it freely or because a module requires it — and whether that distinction matters — is worth sitting with before you start.

Module 6 · Lesson 2

What You're Actually Promising When You Use AI

Honesty online used to mean don't lie. Now it means something harder.
If AI writes something you agree with, and you post it, did you lie?

In March 2023, Steven Schwartz, a lawyer at a New York law firm called Levidow, Levidow & Oberman, filed a legal brief in a federal court case. The brief cited six prior court decisions as precedents — real cases that supported his client's position. The problem was discovered by the opposing lawyers: none of the six cases existed. Schwartz had used ChatGPT to help research the brief. The AI had fabricated the cases, complete with realistic-sounding names, dates, and legal reasoning. Schwartz had not checked them against any official court database.

When the judge, P. Kevin Castel, demanded an explanation, Schwartz said he had trusted the AI's output without verifying it. He was sanctioned — formally punished by the court — and the case became a landmark example of what happens when someone lets AI speak for them without checking what it said.

Schwartz didn't intend to deceive the court. He was not a bad person trying to win dishonestly. He was a person who had made an implicit promise — a promise that attorneys automatically make when they file a document — that everything in it was real. He used a tool that broke that promise without him realizing it. And he was still held responsible.

The Implicit Promises We Forget We're Making

When you post something online — a tweet, a comment, a school essay — you are making a series of promises that you probably never think about. You are promising, implicitly, that the words came from you, that you believe them, that the facts you cite are real, and that you're the one who should be held responsible for them. These aren't rules anyone wrote down. They're the basic assumptions that make communication possible.

AI breaks several of these assumptions at once, and it does so invisibly. When you use an AI chatbot to draft something, who is speaking? The words came from a model trained on other people's text. The claims may or may not be accurate. The tone may or may not reflect how you actually think. But the post carries your name and your credibility.

This is not an argument against using AI. It is an argument for being precise about what you promise when you do. Transparency — making your use of AI visible to people who need to know — is the core commitment that everything else in a digital citizenship pledge depends on.

Implicit promise: A commitment you make automatically by doing something, without ever saying the words. Filing a legal brief implicitly promises the citations are real. Submitting an essay implicitly promises you wrote it.
Transparency: Making your process visible to the people who have a legitimate reason to know it. Not the same as announcing everything to everyone — context matters.
The Spectrum of AI Use — and Where Honesty Applies

Not all AI use is the same. Using AI to check your spelling is different from using AI to generate an argument you didn't think of. Using AI to brainstorm ideas is different from using AI to write your conclusion. The question isn't whether you used AI — it's whether the implicit promises you made are still being kept.

Think about it this way. If a teacher assigns an essay to understand what you think about a topic, and AI generates the essay's argument, the teacher has been given false information about your thinking. That's a broken promise, even if every word is technically accurate. But if you're submitting a cover letter to a job and you use AI to help polish your phrasing, most readers would not say you broke a promise — nobody reads a cover letter as a test of your word-choice abilities.

Context determines what the implicit promises are. A pledge about honesty and AI has to grapple with this. It can't just say "don't use AI to cheat" — because the line between use and misuse depends on what was implicitly promised in each specific situation.

In October 2023, the International Baccalaureate — one of the most rigorous academic programs in the world, with students in over 150 countries — released its official policy on AI use. Rather than banning it, they said students must be able to explain their reasoning process in a conversation with a teacher. If you can explain it, the thinking is yours, regardless of what tools helped you get there. That's a real line — one based on what the implicit promise of an IB assessment actually is.

Ethical Question — No Clean Answer

If you use AI to help you write something you fully agree with and would have written yourself if you had more time — is that dishonest? You're not deceiving anyone about your views. But you are getting an advantage others might not have. Does the implicit promise include the effort, or just the conclusion?

Building Your Honesty Commitment

A strong pledge around honesty and AI has three components. First, verification: you commit to checking AI output for accuracy before you present it as fact, the way a journalist checks a source before publishing. Second, attribution: when it matters — and you get to develop judgment about when it matters — you make your AI use visible. Third, ownership: you only allow AI-assisted work to speak for your thinking when it actually does.

These aren't rules someone imposed on you. They are what honesty has always meant, translated into an era with a new kind of tool. Steven Schwartz didn't face sanctions because he used AI. He faced sanctions because he allowed AI to speak for him in a context where he had implicitly promised otherwise.

You now understand something that most people — including many adults — still haven't worked out clearly: the problem with AI and honesty isn't the AI. It's the gap between what the tool does and what the person using it has promised.

You Can Now See This

When you see someone post AI-generated content without disclosure, you can now name exactly what kind of promise is potentially being broken — and whether context makes it matter. That's a sharper ethical lens than most people have.

Module 6 · Lesson 3

Who Gets Hurt When You Get It Wrong

A pledge that doesn't name who it protects is just a performance.
When AI-assisted content goes wrong, who actually bears the cost — and are they the people with any power to prevent it?

In October 2022, a 14-year-old girl in Almendralejo, Spain started receiving messages from classmates showing sexually explicit images that appeared to be of her. She had never taken those photos. The images were generated by an AI tool called Clothoff, which had been used by a group of boys at her school to create fake nude images of girls they knew — using real photos from their classmates' social media profiles as source material. By the time the story became public in September 2023, investigators had identified at least eleven girls targeted, some as young as 11 years old.

The boys who did this were using a publicly available app. Many of them, according to their parents and teachers, had not thought carefully about what they were doing. The app was free. It took seconds. The harm to the girls — the terror, the shame, the months of anxiety about who had seen the images — was permanent and serious. The girls had done nothing. The harm came entirely from someone else's choice to use a tool without thinking about who would bear the cost.

This case triggered legal changes in Spain, the UK, and eventually the United States. But the legal response came after. The harm was already done. No law prevented the first image. Only a prior commitment — a real one, with named victims in mind — could have done that.

Harm Has a Direction

The most important thing about the Almendralejo case isn't the technology. It's the structure of harm: the people who used the tool experienced no cost; the people who suffered the harm had no say. This pattern — where risk and harm flow toward people who had no power in the decision — is called asymmetric harm. It shows up constantly in digital life.

When someone shares a rumor online, the person spreading it often faces no consequences. The person the rumor is about may lose friendships, opportunities, or safety. When someone uses AI to write fake reviews, the business owner — who never knew it was happening — faces real financial harm. When deepfake videos spread, the person depicted carries the cost of the association, even if every viewer eventually learns it was fake.

A pledge that only talks about what you will do — without naming who your choices affect — misses the most important question. The question isn't just "am I acting ethically?" It's "who bears the cost if I get this wrong?"

Asymmetric harm: A situation where the person making a choice experiences little or no cost, while someone else — often someone with no power in the situation — bears the full consequence.
The Bystander Layer

In the Almendralejo case, the boys who created the images were not the only people who made choices. There were students who saw the images and didn't report them. There were students who passed them on. There were people who found out and said nothing.

Digital citizenship pledges almost always focus on the person taking the action — the creator, the poster, the sender. They rarely address the bystander. But research on online harm consistently shows that the bystander role is where most of the actual prevention happens. A single person saying "this isn't okay" — in a group chat, in a comment, in a conversation — shifts what feels normal.

In 2019, researchers at Rutgers University studying cyberbullying found that when even one peer said the behavior was wrong, incidents were de-escalated in roughly 57% of cases. The person doing harm usually wasn't thinking about harm. They were thinking about status, or jokes, or fitting in. The bystander who named the harm interrupted that logic.

Your pledge needs a bystander commitment — not because you plan to witness harm, but because you probably will. Knowing in advance what you'll do when you see something wrong is the difference between acting and freezing.

Ethical Question — No Clean Answer

If you see a classmate using an AI tool in a way that seems harmful — generating fake images, spreading AI-written rumors — and you report it, you might damage a friendship, be accused of overreacting, or trigger consequences you didn't intend. Is the obligation to act still there? Does the cost to you change the moral weight of inaction?

Naming Your Protected People

One of the most powerful things a pledge can do is name who it is for. Not "I will be kind to others" — but: I will think about the specific person on the receiving end of my action before I post, share, or generate something. This is called other-centered thinking, and it's practiced deliberately by some of the most careful communicators online.

Journalists are trained to ask: "If the person I'm writing about reads this, what is their experience?" Content moderators are trained to ask: "Who is the most vulnerable person who might encounter this?" These aren't abstract ethics — they are practical habits of mind that prevent the specific category of harm where the creator never thought about the receiver at all.

At an institutional level — and this matters as policy — the European Union's AI Act, passed in March 2024, specifically identifies "vulnerable groups" who deserve additional protection when AI systems affect them. The law is saying formally what good individual practice says personally: some people have less power to protect themselves from AI-generated harm, and that asymmetry creates an obligation for those who have more power in the situation.

Your pledge should include at least one commitment that names who you're protecting — not in the abstract, but as a real category of person: someone younger, someone being talked about, someone who didn't consent to be part of the interaction. Making the protected person concrete makes the commitment real.

You Can Now See This

When you read a digital citizenship pledge that only talks about what the person signing it will do — but never names who it protects — you can now identify exactly what's missing. A pledge without named beneficiaries is a performance. A pledge with them is a commitment to a specific person who can actually be harmed.

Module 6 · Lesson 4

Writing the Pledge That's Actually Yours

Everything you've learned in this course narrows to this: what do you actually stand for?
What makes a pledge yours — and what would it take for you to break it?

In May 2024, Anthropic — the AI safety company that makes Claude — published its Acceptable Use Policy, a document listing what its AI would and wouldn't be used for. Unlike most corporate policies, it included something unusual: a section called "Core Views," which didn't just state rules, but explained the reasoning behind them. Why certain uses were refused. What values were being protected. What the company believed about the relationship between AI and human wellbeing.

The document was written by a team that included both engineers and ethicists. It went through eleven drafts. Early drafts, according to internal accounts later published in Wired magazine, read like legal contracts — precise and cold. Later drafts read more like commitments made by actual people who had thought carefully about what they stood for.

A researcher at Stanford's Center for Human-Centered AI noted something striking when she compared Anthropic's final policy to those of other major AI companies: it was the only one that included language about what the company would do differently if it turned out to be wrong. Most policies read like rules that apply to everyone else. This one read like a commitment made by someone who knew they might fail and wanted to account for that possibility.

That's what a serious pledge sounds like. Not "I will never do anything wrong." But "here is what I stand for, here is what I'll do when things get hard, and here is how I'll know if I've failed."

The Anatomy of a Pledge That Lasts

Everything in this module — and in this entire course — has been building toward this: the moment when you decide what you actually stand for online and with AI. Not what a school told you. Not what looks good on paper. What you, right now, actually believe and are willing to commit to.

A pledge that lasts has four components. Each one addresses a way that pledges typically fail. First, it has specific commitments — not values, but implementation intentions with named situations and named actions. Second, it has named beneficiaries — real categories of people the pledge is designed to protect. Third, it has a failure clause — an honest account of how you'd know if you broke it, and what you'd do next. Fourth, it has a revision process — an acknowledgment that you'll update it as you learn more.

That last one surprises people. Why would a pledge include plans for revision? Because a pledge that can't be updated is a pledge that will become irrelevant the moment reality changes — and in the world of AI, reality changes every few months. The pledge isn't the destination. It's a living document for a person in the middle of figuring things out.

What Goes Into Each Section

Specific commitments look like this: "When I use AI to help me write or research something, I will verify any factual claim before I present it as true." "When I see someone using AI to target or harm another person, I will say something — to the person, to a trusted adult, or to a platform report system." These name a situation, name an action, and name a standard. They can be evaluated after the fact.

Named beneficiaries might include: "people who appear in content I share without choosing to be there," or "younger kids who might encounter what I post before they have the context to evaluate it," or "classmates whose reputations could be affected by what I say or generate." The specific names don't matter. The habit of mind does: before you act, you ask who is affected.

A failure clause sounds like: "If I share something AI-generated without checking it first, I will correct the record as quickly and visibly as I can." Or: "If I stay silent when I should have spoken up, I'll acknowledge that to myself and ask why — not to punish myself, but to understand what made it hard." This isn't weakness. This is how serious professionals handle commitments. It's what separated Anthropic's policy from everyone else's.

A revision process is as simple as: "I'll revisit this pledge when something major changes — a new AI tool I'm using, a situation where my current commitments don't cover what happened, or once a year to see if I still believe what I wrote." Most people don't do this. Most people who do this find that their pledge becomes more useful over time, not less.

The Pledge Builder — Your Draft Framework

What my digital citizenship pledge covers:

Honesty commitments: When I use AI, I will verify, attribute, and only let it speak for thinking I actually hold.
Named beneficiaries: The people my commitments are designed to protect — not just "others," but specific categories of people who could be harmed.
Bystander commitments: What I will do when I see someone else causing harm with AI or online tools.
Failure clause: How I'll know if I broke a commitment and what I'll do next.
Revision note: When I'll revisit this pledge and under what circumstances I'll update it.
The Hardest Question at the End

Here is the question this entire module has been building toward. Not a quiz question. A real one: What would it actually take for you to break the pledge you're about to write?

Think about it honestly. Social pressure? Fear of being left out? A situation where the harm seems small or distant? The answer to this question is not something to be ashamed of — it's the most useful information you have about where your commitment is vulnerable. The developers who signed Apple's agreement and then ignored it weren't bad people. They were people who hadn't thought about the specific pressures that would make their commitment feel optional.

Knowing your vulnerabilities is how you write commitments that survive them. If you know you're likely to stay silent in group situations, your bystander commitment should name what you'll do when it's a group, not just when you're alone. If you know you're likely to share things quickly when you're excited, your verification commitment should name what the pause looks like — ten seconds, one source check, one question to yourself about who might be affected.

This course has given you a vocabulary, a set of cases, and a framework. The pledge is where it becomes personal. It's where you stop learning about digital citizenship and start practicing it.

Ethical Question — No Clean Answer
