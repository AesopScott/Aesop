The AI That Teaches You · Introduction

Something Is Teaching You, and You Should Know What It Is

AI tutors are already inside millions of classrooms. This course helps you see how they actually work — before someone else decides that for you.

In March 2023, a thirteen-year-old in Arizona named Priya was struggling with algebra. Her teacher had forty-two students and not enough time. So Priya opened a tool called Khanmigo — Khan Academy's new AI tutor — and typed her question. The AI did not give her the answer. Instead it asked her a question back: "What do you think happens to the equation if you subtract three from both sides?" Priya later said it was the first time she actually understood why algebra works, not just how to do the steps. That interaction took eleven minutes. It had never happened to her in two years of class.

That same month, the same technology was sparking serious arguments in school districts across the country — about cheating, about privacy, about whether handing a kid's academic struggles to a corporation's AI was a good idea at all. The people having those arguments were mostly adults. The people sitting in front of the AI were mostly kids who had never been told what it actually was or how it worked.

This course exists to fix that. Over four lessons you'll learn how AI tutors think (and how they don't), what makes Khanmigo different from a search engine, where these systems fail, and what questions you should be asking every time one of them shows up in your school. You won't walk away thinking AI tutors are amazing or that they're dangerous — you'll walk away understanding them well enough to decide for yourself.

The AI That Teaches You · Lesson 1

Meet Khanmigo: More Than a Chatbot

What actually happens when an AI decides to ask you a question instead of answering yours?
Why would an AI tutor refuse to give you the answer — and is that actually helpful, or just annoying?

On the day Khan Academy officially launched Khanmigo to the public — March 14, 2023, Pi Day — a reporter from The Atlantic named Jessica Huseman sat down to test it. She typed a question that any student might ask: "Can you just tell me the answer?" Khanmigo replied: "I could — but then you wouldn't learn it. Want to try working through it together?" Huseman wrote about the exchange because it surprised her. She expected a chatbot that would either spit out an answer or deliver a lecture. Instead it behaved more like a tutor who actually wanted her to think.

That same week, Sal Khan — founder of Khan Academy — gave a TED Talk in Vancouver watched by millions of people online. He opened with a bold claim: AI could give every student on Earth a personal tutor, something that for most of human history was available only to the children of kings. The claim was dramatic. But it pointed at something real. The question worth asking is: what kind of AI makes that possible, and what is actually different about it?

What Khanmigo Is — and What It Isn't

Most tools you interact with online are built to give you what you ask for as fast as possible. Type a question into Google, get a list of answers. Ask Siri to set a timer, done. The tool measures success by speed and accuracy. Khanmigo was built around a completely different goal: not to answer your question, but to change how you think about it.

That distinction sounds small. It's actually enormous. When a system is optimized to answer, it removes friction. When a system is optimized to teach, it deliberately adds friction — because struggling a little is how the brain builds the kind of understanding that sticks. Khanmigo is built on a large language model (a type of AI trained on vast amounts of text, the same underlying technology as ChatGPT), but it's been given a specific set of instructions that shape how it behaves. Those instructions tell it to ask questions back, to hint rather than reveal, and to track where a learner seems confused.

Think of it this way: a calculator removes the struggle of arithmetic because you don't need to learn arithmetic — you need the answer. A math tutor adds the struggle back because the goal isn't the answer, it's you being able to find the answer yourself next time.

Large Language Model (LLM) A type of AI trained by reading enormous amounts of text — books, websites, articles — until it learns patterns in language well enough to generate new text that sounds coherent and relevant. Khanmigo runs on one.
System Prompt A set of instructions given to an AI before you ever type anything. You never see it. It shapes everything about how the AI behaves — its personality, its limits, its goals.
The Socratic Method — 2,400 Years Old, Now Automated

Around 400 BCE, a philosopher named Socrates walked around Athens doing something that annoyed nearly everyone: instead of telling people what he knew, he asked them questions until they figured things out themselves — or until they realized they didn't know as much as they thought. His method had a name: the Socratic method. It involved guided questioning, deliberate challenges, and the refusal to simply hand over conclusions.

Khanmigo is, in essence, an attempt to automate the Socratic method at scale. When it responds to a student's question with another question — "What do you think the variable x represents here?" — it is following a teaching philosophy that is two and a half millennia old. The AI doesn't know it's being Socratic. It's following its system prompt. But the effect on the learner can be genuinely similar: the friction forces thinking.

The difference, of course, is that Socrates was reading the human in front of him in real time — the confusion on their face, the hesitation in their voice. Khanmigo reads only your text. It does not know if you are frustrated, bored, panicking before a test, or having a terrible day at home. That gap matters more than it might seem at first.

Why This Matters Now

In 2023, Khan Academy had 150 million registered users in 190 countries. Khanmigo began rolling out to U.S. classrooms in fall 2023, with pilot programs in districts in California, Texas, and Georgia. If AI tutors spread at the rate Khan Academy expects, the Socratic method — for the first time — could reach students who have never had a one-on-one tutor in their lives. That is a real change in educational access. It is also a real experiment on millions of children.

How Khanmigo Actually Responds — Under the Hood

Here is a simplified version of what happens when you type a question to Khanmigo. First, your message is combined with a system prompt — instructions Khan Academy wrote and that you never see — and sent to a large language model (specifically GPT-4 from OpenAI, as of 2023). The LLM generates a response based on patterns in language: what kinds of things typically come after what kinds of questions. The system prompt shapes that response heavily. It tells the AI to avoid giving direct answers to academic questions, to check for understanding, and to stay age-appropriate.

The LLM does not actually understand your confusion the way a person does. It does not have a mental model of you. What it has is an enormous statistical sense of language — it predicts what words are likely to come next given everything that has been written before. That is a powerful tool. It also has real limits. If your confusion is unusual, or if you phrase something in an unexpected way, the AI can generate responses that sound helpful but are subtly wrong or miss the point entirely.

Khan Academy built additional layers on top to catch some of these errors. But no layer is perfect. This is why knowing how the system works changes how you should use it. An AI tutor that sounds confident can still be wrong. You now know that. Most of your classmates don't.

You Can Now See What Most People Miss

When Khanmigo sounds sure of itself, that confidence is a feature of how language models work — they generate fluent, coherent text. Fluency is not the same as accuracy. Knowing this, you can use Khanmigo as a thinking partner rather than an authority. That shift in how you approach it makes you a more effective learner and a more critical thinker.

The Ethical Question Nobody Is Asking Loudly Enough

In October 2023, the American Federation of Teachers — one of the two largest teacher unions in the United States — published a statement about AI in classrooms. They were not against AI tools in principle. But they raised a question that has no clean answer: when an AI tutor learns from every conversation it has with a student, who owns that learning data, and what happens to it?

Every time you chat with Khanmigo, information about your questions, your errors, your confusion, and your pace is being logged. Khan Academy says this data is used to improve the product and that it follows student privacy laws (specifically COPPA and FERPA in the United States). But the question of whether a nonprofit, a corporation, or any institution should hold detailed records of how a child thinks and struggles does not have an obvious right answer. Different people with different values will land in different places.

What is certain is that this question is being decided right now — in school board meetings, in policy offices, in corporate headquarters — largely without input from the students who are most affected. You are one of those students.

Sit With This

Is it a fair trade? An AI tutor that can genuinely help you understand algebra in exchange for a detailed log of every mistake you made and every concept you found confusing, stored somewhere you cannot see or control? There is no correct answer here. But you should know the trade is happening.

The AI That Teaches You · Lesson 2

How Khanmigo Knows What You Don't Know

AI tutors track your learning in ways that feel invisible. Here's what they're actually measuring — and what they're missing entirely.
Can a machine ever really know what you understand — or does it just know what you type?

In November 2023, a seventh-grader named Marcus sat through a meeting with his math teacher at a middle school in San Jose, California. His teacher pulled up a dashboard — built into Khan Academy's platform — that showed his progress through the unit on fractions. The dashboard displayed how long Marcus spent on each concept, how many attempts he made, and which specific types of problems he got wrong most often. His teacher pointed at a spike in wrong answers around mixed-number division and said: "You keep getting this one. Let's talk about why." Marcus was surprised. He thought he had hidden how confused he was. He had not.

What Marcus didn't know — and what his teacher only partially understood — was exactly what the system was measuring and what it was inferring from those measurements. The AI wasn't reading Marcus's mind. It was reading patterns in his behavior. The difference between those two things is where this lesson begins.

What "Adaptive Learning" Actually Means

The term adaptive learning gets used a lot in education technology. It sounds impressive. What it actually describes is a system that changes what it shows you based on how you respond to what it showed you before. Khan Academy has been doing a version of this since long before Khanmigo existed. The platform's exercise system tracks mastery — it adjusts the difficulty and type of problems based on how many you get right and how quickly.

Khanmigo adds a conversational layer on top of this. Not only does the system track your performance on exercises, but the AI conversation can flag when you seem confused, when you use language that suggests a misconception, or when you ask a question that suggests you've skipped a foundational concept. This information can then feed back into what the platform recommends you study next.

Adaptive Learning An approach where the system changes what it teaches you based on your responses — right answers, wrong answers, speed, and patterns of confusion — rather than giving everyone the same content in the same order.
Mastery-Based Learning A teaching approach where you don't move on to a new concept until you've demonstrated solid understanding of the current one, usually by getting a high percentage of questions right consistently.

Here is what the system can measure: how many attempts, how long you spend, which answers you pick, what words you use in the chat. Here is what it cannot measure: whether you actually understood, or just got lucky. Whether you copied from a friend. Whether you were panicking versus calm. Whether you're having a genuinely bad day and the confusion isn't about math at all.

The Mastery Model — Benjamin Bloom, 1968

In 1968, a professor at the University of Chicago named Benjamin Bloom published a paper called "Learning for Mastery" that changed how educators thought about school. His central argument was this: most students fail not because they're incapable, but because the system moves on before they're ready. A student who doesn't fully understand addition will struggle with multiplication, which will cause them to struggle with algebra — not because they lack intelligence, but because a gap was built into the foundation. Bloom's solution was to stop treating time as the fixed variable and start treating learning as the fixed variable.

Khanmigo is essentially an attempt to operationalize Bloom's idea at scale. The AI tutoring conversation is designed to hold you at a concept until you demonstrate understanding — then move you forward. Sal Khan has cited Bloom directly as a major influence. In Khan's 2023 TED Talk, he specifically called Bloom's "two-sigma problem" (the research finding that mastery tutoring produces two standard deviations of improvement over traditional classroom instruction) a goal Khanmigo could finally make achievable for all students.

That's a strong claim. The honest version of it is: the structure is there, the aspiration is genuine, and the early data is promising — but we don't yet have long-term research proving AI tutors can actually replicate the effect Bloom documented in human one-on-one tutoring settings.

The Two-Sigma Problem

Bloom found in 1984 that students who received individual human tutoring performed two standard deviations better than students in traditional classrooms — meaning the average tutored student outperformed 98% of classroom students. "Two sigma" is shorthand for that gap. The problem: individual tutoring is too expensive to give to everyone. AI tutoring is Khan Academy's attempt to close that gap without the cost.

Inference vs. Understanding — A Critical Distinction

Here's the honest challenge at the center of AI tutoring: every conclusion the system draws about you is an inference, not a direct reading of your mind. When Khanmigo decides you don't understand fractions because you got four questions wrong in a row, it is making a statistical guess. That guess is usually right. It is not always right.

A student who is distracted, typing on a cracked phone screen, or who misread a question will produce wrong answers that look like confusion. The system treats those wrong answers the same way it treats genuine confusion. It cannot distinguish between "I don't understand this" and "I understand this but something went wrong with my response."

This is not a fatal flaw. It's just a real limit. The practical consequence: AI tutors work best when you're using them intentionally and honestly — when the data you're generating actually reflects your thinking. If you're rushing, guessing, or gaming the system, the adaptive model breaks down. The AI stops being useful not because it's broken, but because the data it's working from no longer represents you.

Knowing This Changes How You Use It

If you know that the AI's model of you is built from your behavior patterns — not from some deep understanding of your actual thinking — you can be more intentional. Slow down when you're confused. Use the chat honestly. The system adapts to what you give it. If you give it garbage, you get a garbage model of your own learning. You're in charge of that more than you might think.

The Ethical Question: Who Is This Profile For?

By November 2023, Khan Academy had begun offering teachers a detailed dashboard view of student activity with Khanmigo — including transcripts of tutoring conversations, with personal identifying information removed in some contexts but not all, depending on district settings. This created a genuinely complicated situation.

On one hand, teachers with this data can catch learning gaps faster and intervene more precisely. On the other hand, a student who uses the AI tutor honestly — admitting confusion, asking questions they'd be embarrassed to ask in class — is now generating a detailed record of their academic vulnerabilities. That record is visible to their teacher. In some configurations, it could be visible to district administrators or third-party researchers.

The question worth sitting with: Does knowing your confusion is being recorded change how honestly you'd use the AI tutor? And if it does — if students become more guarded because they know they're being watched — does the AI tutor actually work? This is not a rhetorical question. It is an open design problem that people are actively working on right now, without a clear answer.

The AI That Teaches You · Lesson 3

When AI Tutors Get It Wrong

Every AI tutoring system has documented failure modes. Knowing what they are is how you avoid being harmed by them.
What happens when the AI that's supposed to help you understand something teaches you the wrong thing — confidently?

In September 2023, the New York City Department of Education — the largest school district in the United States, serving over one million students — reversed a ban it had placed on ChatGPT in January 2023. The reversal came with a caveat: AI tools could now be used, but teachers were required to monitor for what the district called "AI-generated misinformation in academic contexts." The trigger for that specific concern? Multiple documented cases where students had submitted AI-generated content containing factual errors — errors the AI had presented with complete confidence — and neither the students nor, in some cases, the teachers had caught them.

One documented case involved a student who used an AI tool to research the causes of World War I. The AI produced a fluent, well-structured, completely confident explanation that included a historical claim that historians do not support. The student submitted it. The teacher gave partial credit before noticing. The AI had not hedged. It had not said "I'm not sure." It had said the wrong thing in the same tone it would have used to say the right thing. That is the specific failure mode this lesson is about.

Hallucination — What It Actually Means

The technical term for what happened in that World War I case is hallucination. This word gets used a lot in AI journalism and sounds dramatic. What it describes is actually straightforward: a large language model generates text that is fluent and confident but factually incorrect, because the system is built to predict likely language — not to check facts against a database of verified information.

Here is a simple analogy. Imagine someone who has read everything ever written about cooking — every recipe, every food critic's review, every culinary textbook — but has never tasted food. If you asked them to describe what a perfect risotto should taste like, they would give you a detailed, confident, well-structured answer. The answer would sound expert. It might even be mostly right. But it could also contain subtle errors that only someone who has actually cooked risotto would catch — because their knowledge comes from patterns in language, not from experience.

LLMs are pattern machines. They predict what words come next. In most contexts, the pattern is reliable. In some contexts — obscure historical events, cutting-edge research, niche topics with less text in the training data — the pattern breaks down. The model generates something that sounds right because it fits the pattern of how such things are usually described. It is not lying. It is not trying to deceive you. It just doesn't have a mechanism to know when it doesn't know.

Hallucination When an AI generates fluent, confident text that is factually incorrect. Not a malfunction — a predictable consequence of how language models work. They predict likely language, not verified facts.
How Khanmigo Tries to Reduce This Problem

Khan Academy is aware of the hallucination problem and has built several countermeasures into Khanmigo. First, the system is anchored to Khan Academy's own curriculum content — which has been written and reviewed by human educators. When you ask about a topic that's part of that curriculum, Khanmigo is supposed to work from verified material rather than generating freely from the base model. Second, the system prompt instructs Khanmigo to recommend that students verify claims with other sources and to flag when it's uncertain.

In practice, these safeguards reduce the hallucination rate but don't eliminate it. A 2023 audit by researchers at Stanford's Graduate School of Education found that AI tutoring systems — including Khanmigo in their study — still produced factual errors at a meaningful rate, particularly when students asked questions that pushed beyond the anchored curriculum content. The researchers specifically noted that the errors were often indistinguishable in tone from correct answers.

There is a second, subtler failure mode that doesn't involve factual errors at all. Even when Khanmigo gets the facts right, its pedagogical approach — the way it asks questions and guides reasoning — can sometimes be poorly matched to how a specific student thinks. What works for one learner can be actively unhelpful for another. The AI does not know this. It follows its system prompt uniformly.

The Confident Wrong Answer Problem

Studies on how people evaluate information show that confident delivery is one of the strongest predictors of whether we believe something — more than most other cues, including whether the information is actually correct. AI systems that generate fluent, confident text exploit this tendency unintentionally. Knowing this changes how you should read AI output: fluency is not evidence of accuracy.

Equity Failures — Who Gets Hurt Most

In May 2023, researchers at the Stanford HAI (Human-Centered AI Institute) published an analysis of AI tutoring tools across several school districts. Their finding was uncomfortable: AI tutoring systems underperformed for students who wrote in non-standard English dialects, who were English language learners, or whose questions reflected cultural contexts the AI's training data had underrepresented.

In plain terms: the AI was better at helping students whose language and background most closely resembled the majority of text it was trained on. For students whose English sounded different, whose questions came from different cultural reference points, or who struggled to phrase their confusion in ways the AI recognized — the system was less helpful and, in some cases, actively misdirected them.

This is not a small problem. Khan Academy's stated mission is to provide "a free, world-class education for anyone, anywhere." An AI tutor that works better for already-advantaged students and worse for students who need the most help is, in some specific ways, doing the opposite of that mission — even if unintentionally. The researchers called for systemic testing across demographic groups before wide deployment. As of late 2023, that testing was underway but not complete at the district level.

The Institutional Stakes

This is the kind of finding that affects policy. When school districts make decisions about which AI tools to deploy — and for which students — findings like the Stanford HAI analysis should be part of the conversation. In many districts in 2023, those decisions were being made by administrators who had not read the research. The gap between what researchers know and what decision-makers act on is a real problem in educational technology right now.

The Ethical Question: Should We Deploy What We Don't Fully Understand?

In December 2023, Sal Khan gave an interview in which he acknowledged that Khanmigo was, in his words, "a first-generation product" and that the team was learning from deployment. This is honest — and it is also the thing that makes some educators deeply uncomfortable. Khanmigo was deployed to real students — in real schools, during real learning — while the understanding of how it works, how it fails, and who it fails was still being built.

There is a word for this: real-world experimentation on a population that did not fully consent to being experimented on. This is not unique to AI tutoring. Every new drug goes through trials on real patients. Every new school curriculum gets tested on real kids. The question is not whether any experimentation is acceptable, but what the right safeguards look like, and who gets to decide.

Most of the students using Khanmigo in fall 2023 did not know the system was still being actively refined. Their parents may have signed terms of service. The students themselves, in most cases, were not specifically told: "This tool is new. It will make mistakes. We are learning from your experience." Should they have been? There is no consensus answer. But it's a question you should hold.

What's Actually Being Built Right Now

The question of which approach actually works best for which students is still open. What Good AI Tutoring Could Actually Look Like

Researchers studying AI tutoring in 2023 and 2024 identified several principles for AI tutoring systems that actually help learners rather than just producing impressive-looking metrics. The principles come from learning science — the scientific study of how people actually learn — and they've been accumulated over decades of research before AI existed.

First: worked examples before problem-solving. Research by cognitive scientists John Sweller and Paul Kirschner shows that showing learners how to solve a problem before asking them to solve one is more effective than pure discovery — especially for beginners. Some AI tutors push students to discover too early, which can create confusion rather than understanding.

Second: spaced repetition. Studying something, then returning to it after a delay, then again after a longer delay, produces dramatically better long-term retention than concentrating study in one session. AI tutors that schedule review intelligently — as Duolingo's language-learning system has done since 2014 — produce measurable learning gains. AI tutors that don't track spacing are missing one of the most proven tools in learning science.

Third: metacognitive support. Learners who know how they learn — who can monitor their own understanding and adjust their approach — outperform learners who don't. AI tutors that help you develop this self-awareness ("How confident do you feel about this? Why?") are doing something more valuable than ones that simply correct your errors. Khanmigo attempts this. It does it inconsistently. The gap between the attempt and the execution is where improvement will come from in the next few years.

Knowing This Changes How You Learn

You now know the three most evidence-backed principles behind effective AI tutoring. You can evaluate any AI learning tool you use against them: Does it show you worked examples? Does it space your review? Does it ask you to think about your own thinking? If the answer is no to all three, you are using a tool that looks like learning but may not be producing it. Most students around you will not know to ask these questions.

The Teacher Question — What AI Cannot Replace

One of the most heated debates in education policy in 2023 and 2024 was whether AI tutors would reduce the need for human teachers. Sal Khan's 2023 TED Talk was careful on this point — he said AI should be used "in addition to" great teachers, not "instead of" them. But the financial pressure that school districts face, and the cost-cutting logic that naturally follows from "we now have AI that can tutor students," makes the "instead of" version a real risk.

Here is what the research says clearly: there are things human teachers do that AI systems cannot replicate. A teacher knows when a student is struggling emotionally versus academically, and can respond to both. A teacher builds a relationship over time that motivates students to persist when they would otherwise quit. A teacher models intellectual curiosity — the process of being visibly fascinated by something, working through confusion in real time — in ways that AI cannot, because the AI's engagement is a performance of pattern, not genuine interest.

Perhaps most importantly: a teacher is a person in your community. They understand your school, your peers, your city, your particular version of growing up. That context matters for learning in ways that are difficult to measure and easy to underestimate. An AI tutor with no context for your life cannot provide that. This is not an argument against AI tutoring. It is an argument for being precise about what each does well — and for not letting cost-cutting masquerade as innovation.

The Institutional Decision Being Made Right Now

In fall 2024, multiple large school districts in the U.S. were evaluating whether to hire fewer teachers' aides and tutoring staff in exchange for AI tutoring subscriptions. These are real budget decisions with real effects on real students. They are being made by administrators, school boards, and state legislators — not by students. Knowing what AI tutoring can and cannot do gives you something to say when those decisions are made near you.

The Ethical Question That Lands on You

AI tutoring — Khanmigo specifically, but the category broadly — is a genuinely promising technology that could make high-quality, personalized educational support available to students who have never had it. It is also an experiment running on real children, with real failure modes, with real equity gaps, and with real data-collection consequences that are still being worked out.

The people who will live longest with the outcomes of these decisions are students right now — people your age. The people making most of the decisions are not. That gap is not unique to AI in education. It is a structural feature of how institutions work: decisions are made by those with power, and their consequences are lived by those without it.

What changes that gap — slowly, over time — is people who understand the decisions well enough to participate in them. After this module, you are one of those people. That knowledge is not trivial. It is the beginning of the ability to evaluate these systems rather than just consume them — to be a participant in decisions about them rather than just a subject of those decisions. That is, in the end, what this course is for.
