Module 4 · Lesson 1

Khan Academy's Khanmigo vs. Duolingo Max

Two companies built AI tutors in the same year. They made almost every different choice possible.
When a tool claims to "personalize" your learning — what is it actually doing, and what does it cost you to use it?

In March 2023, two of the most recognized names in education technology released AI products within weeks of each other. They were both using the same underlying model — GPT-4, made by OpenAI. They were both aimed at students. They were both described in press releases as "personalized learning assistants." And they were built almost completely differently.

Khan Academy launched Khanmigo on March 14, 2023 — the same day OpenAI released GPT-4 to the public. Sal Khan, the founder, had been working with OpenAI for months. Khanmigo was designed to never give students direct answers. If you asked it "what is 7 × 8?" it would ask you back: "What do you think? What strategy could you use?" The whole design was built around a single belief: making a student think is more valuable than giving them information.

Two weeks later, on March 28, 2023, Duolingo announced Duolingo Max, a subscription tier powered by GPT-4. Its flagship feature was called "Explain My Answer" — meaning if you got a question wrong, the AI would explain exactly why. The second feature was "Roleplay," where you could practice French or Spanish in a simulated conversation. Duolingo's design philosophy was the opposite of Khan's: give the student exactly what they asked for, immediately, every time.

Same model. Same year. Completely different philosophies about how humans learn. One of those philosophies will work better for you. The question is — which one, and how would you know?

The Socratic Method vs. The Direct Answer

Khanmigo was explicitly designed around something called the Socratic method — named after the ancient Greek philosopher Socrates, who never told students anything directly but instead kept asking questions until students figured things out themselves. The idea is that when you struggle to reach an answer, the answer sticks in your memory more firmly than if someone just handed it to you.

This isn't just philosophy — there's research behind it. In 2011, researchers at Washington University in St. Louis published a study showing that students who were tested on material before being taught it learned significantly more than students who just studied it. The effort of trying — even failing — primed the brain to retain what came next. Khanmigo was designed to create that kind of productive struggle.

Duolingo Max took the opposite approach, and also had research behind it. Duolingo's internal data team, which tracks hundreds of millions of learners worldwide, found that immediate, specific feedback accelerates the early stages of language learning faster than any other method. When you make a mistake in Spanish, not knowing why for ten minutes doesn't help you — knowing why right now does.

ScaffoldingThe way a teacher or tool gives you just enough help to keep moving without giving you so much help that you stop thinking. A good scaffold gets removed as you get stronger.
Direct instructionTelling a student exactly what they need to know, clearly and immediately. Faster in the short term, but can reduce how deeply a concept is understood.

Here is what makes this genuinely complicated: both methods work — but for different things and different moments. The question isn't which philosophy is right. The question is: when you open a learning tool, do you know which philosophy it's using on you?

What "Personalization" Actually Means in Each Tool

Both tools advertise personalization. But they mean almost completely different things by it.

Khanmigo's personalization works at the conversation level. It adapts how it talks to you based on what you say. If you seem confused, it tries a different analogy. If you're frustrated, it acknowledges that. The personalization is in the dialogue — it responds to what you actually type, in real time. However, Khanmigo does not, as of its 2023 launch, automatically adjust which exercises you see next based on your performance history. A teacher or the student themselves has to make those choices.

Duolingo's personalization works at the curriculum level. Before Duolingo Max even existed, Duolingo had spent years building a system that tracks every answer you give and uses a statistical model to decide which words you're about to forget (based on a concept called the spacing effect — your brain needs to re-encounter information at specific intervals to retain it long-term). Duolingo Max adds AI conversation on top of that already-personalized curriculum.

Ethical Question

Duolingo's personalization works because the company has detailed data on hundreds of millions of learners. Your mistakes, your speed, your forgetting curve — all of it is stored and analyzed. That data made Duolingo better at teaching. But it also means a private company knows exactly where your gaps are, in what language, on what days. Does the benefit justify that? Who else might that data be useful to?

The word "personalization" sounds unambiguously good. You now know enough to ask the harder question: personalized how, based on what data, and who else can see it?

The Cost Structure Nobody Talks About

Khanmigo launched in 2023 as a paid product — $4 per month for students, with proceeds going directly to support Khan Academy's nonprofit mission. For teachers, it was free. The explicit logic was that teacher use should not be paywalled because access to good teaching already has severe equity problems in the US education system.

Duolingo Max launched at $13.99/month (or $167.99/year), on top of the existing Duolingo Plus subscription. It was available first only in English-speaking markets learning Spanish or French. The "Explain My Answer" feature and Roleplay were locked entirely behind that tier. Learners using the free version got none of it.

This matters more than it might seem. In the US alone, approximately 50 million K-12 students are enrolled in public schools. About 40% of them qualify for free or reduced-price lunch — a rough proxy for economic disadvantage. AI tutoring tools that cost $14 per month create a new kind of educational inequality: not just "which school you go to" but "which AI-enhanced experience your family can afford."

That is not a hypothetical future concern. It is a design decision that was already made, in 2023, by engineers and product managers at specific companies. You now understand the design well enough to evaluate it. Most adults reading headlines about "AI in education" do not have this level of understanding. You do.

What These Tools Can't Do

Both Khanmigo and Duolingo Max share a significant limitation that neither company advertises prominently: neither can detect why you're struggling — only that you are. If you keep getting math wrong because you misunderstood fractions in 4th grade, Khanmigo might catch that through conversation. But if you're struggling because you haven't slept, because something hard happened at home, because you're bored and distracted — neither tool knows. Neither can know. They see text inputs, not people.

This is called the context problem in AI-assisted learning, and it's one of the most actively researched areas in educational technology. Human teachers read posture, tone of voice, facial expression, and classroom energy. They adjust not just content but emotional register — offering encouragement, backing off, joking, getting serious. AI tools, as of 2023, operate entirely in the domain of language. They are powerful within that domain. But the domain has edges.

You Can Now See This

When you read a headline that says "AI tutor matches performance of human teachers," you now know to ask: matched on what measure, in what context, with which students, over what time period? The headline leaves all of that out. You know enough now to know what was left out.

Module 4 · Lesson 2

Intelligent Tutoring Systems: Carnegie Learning's MATHia

Before ChatGPT, before Khanmigo, one company spent 25 years building the most researched AI math tutor in history — and still got something wrong.
If a tool has been studied by hundreds of researchers and proven to work — what does it mean when students still don't like using it?

In 1998, researchers at Carnegie Mellon University spun out a company called Carnegie Learning. It was built on 15 years of cognitive science research from CMU's PACT Center (Pittsburgh Advanced Cognitive Tutor). The lead researchers — John Anderson, Kenneth Koedinger, and their colleagues — had spent more than a decade studying exactly how humans learn mathematics, step by step, mistake by mistake.

Their software was called Cognitive Tutor, later renamed MATHia. The underlying architecture was called a Bayesian Knowledge Tracing model — which means the software tracked, for each student, the probability that they actually understood each specific mathematical skill, updating that probability with every problem they solved. It didn't just mark answers right or wrong. It estimated how firmly each concept was lodged in your mind, and it waited until it was confident you actually knew something before moving you forward.

By 2023, MATHia had been used by more than one million students per year across the United States. It had been evaluated in over 50 peer-reviewed studies. A major 2007 study funded by the US Department of Education found that students using Cognitive Tutor outperformed students in traditional classrooms by a statistically significant margin. This was not marketing — it was evidence gathered by independent researchers who had no financial stake in the outcome.

And yet, surveys of students using MATHia consistently found that many of them found it tedious, frustrating, and cold. A 12-year-old in Pittsburgh in 2019 described it this way: "It knows what I don't know better than I do. But it doesn't care."

How Bayesian Knowledge Tracing Works

The phrase "Bayesian knowledge tracing" sounds complicated, but the idea behind it is something you already understand intuitively.

Imagine a friend is learning to ride a bike. They fall three times. You're not sure if they understand balance yet. Then they ride successfully for ten seconds. Your confidence that they "get it" goes up — but only a little, because ten seconds is short. Then they ride for a full minute with no wobble. Now your confidence is high. Then they fall again. Your confidence drops a bit. This constant updating of your belief based on new evidence is the core of Bayesian reasoning.

MATHia does this for every mathematical skill separately. "Adding fractions with unlike denominators" is tracked independently from "finding common denominators." The system has a probability estimate for each one, updated with every answer. When a skill's probability crosses a threshold — meaning the system is confident you really know it — it marks that skill as mastered and moves on.

Knowledge componentA specific, identifiable piece of understanding — like "convert a mixed number to an improper fraction" — that a tutor system tracks separately from all other skills.
Mastery thresholdThe point at which a system's confidence that you know a skill is high enough to stop drilling you on it. Too low, and you'll forget it. Too high, and you'll be stuck forever on things you actually understand.

This architecture is why MATHia could be so effective in studies: it was genuinely tracking understanding, not just performance. A student who gets three problems right by guessing is treated differently from a student who gets three right by demonstrating a pattern of reliable thinking. The system could tell the difference.

The Problem: Effectiveness Is Not the Same as Experience

Here is where the MATHia story gets genuinely interesting — and uncomfortable for anyone who wants AI tutoring to have a simple answer.

The research evidence for MATHia's effectiveness is real and substantial. Students using it in studies did learn more math. The Bayesian models were accurate. The mastery thresholds were calibrated carefully over years of data. By nearly every objective measure, MATHia was doing what it was designed to do.

But "doing what it was designed to do" is not the same as "being something students want to use." A significant body of research on MATHia and similar intelligent tutoring systems found that students experienced higher levels of boredom and frustration than in traditional instruction — even when they were learning more. Researchers gave this phenomenon a name: gaming the system — students learned to click through problems quickly, making strategic guesses rather than actually thinking, because the interaction felt like something to finish rather than something to engage with.

The lesson is not that MATHia failed. The lesson is that a tool can be technically excellent and experientially poor at the same time. These two things can coexist, and they often do.

Ethical Question

If a tool demonstrably increases learning outcomes but students dislike using it and often disengage, should schools still mandate it? Who gets to decide — researchers who can prove effectiveness, students who experience it, teachers who observe the classroom, or parents who want results? And if the answer depends on which group you trust most — why do you trust them?

What MATHia Teaches Us About Comparing AI Tools

When you compare AI tutoring tools, most people look at two things: does it work, and what does it cost? MATHia teaches you that there is a third dimension that matters at least as much: what is it like to be a student using it, and does that experience support or undermine actual engagement over time?

A tool you stop using after two weeks is less effective than a slightly worse tool you use every day for a year. Engagement is not separate from effectiveness — it is part of it. Carnegie Learning understood this well enough that by 2020 they had invested significantly in adding more conversational, narrative, and character-based elements to MATHia. They were trying to preserve the mathematical rigor of the Bayesian core while making the experience feel less like a machine grading your homework and more like something a person cared about.

Whether they succeeded is a question researchers are still studying. What they were grappling with was real: the hardest problem in AI-assisted learning is not the AI. It is the human.

You Can Now See This

When a school district announces it's adopting an AI tutoring platform because "studies show it works," you now know to ask: what did the studies measure, over what time period, and what did students say about the experience? Effectiveness data and engagement data are different things, and you need both.

Module 4 · Lesson 3

Synthesis Tools: How ChatGPT Changed the Game

In November 2022, a tool launched that wasn't designed for education at all — and became the most widely used learning tool on earth within months.
When a general-purpose AI becomes a learning tool, who is responsible for what it teaches you?

On November 30, 2022, OpenAI released ChatGPT to the public as a free research preview. The company expected perhaps a few thousand users to try it over the first few weeks. What actually happened was that one million users signed up in the first five days. Within two months, it had reached 100 million — the fastest adoption of any consumer application in recorded history, surpassing TikTok, Instagram, and every other platform before it.

Almost immediately, a significant portion of those users were students. Not using it the way its designers intended — which was primarily for business writing, coding, and creative tasks — but using it to understand concepts they were confused about, to check their homework reasoning, to ask "wait, but why does that work?" at 11 pm when no teacher was available. Sam Altman, OpenAI's CEO, told The Atlantic in January 2023 that he had not expected students to become one of the primary user groups so quickly.

But ChatGPT was not designed to teach. It was designed to be helpful, harmless, and honest — a general-purpose assistant. This meant that when a student asked it a question, it would answer — completely, confidently, and sometimes incorrectly. It had no mechanism for withholding answers to promote struggle. It had no Bayesian model tracking which skills you were missing. It had no mastery threshold before moving you forward. It was extraordinarily fluent and occasionally wrong in ways that were very hard to detect.

A high school chemistry teacher in Boston named Sarah Bessen described what she observed in her classroom in early 2023: "Kids were using it to understand why reactions happen, and that was genuinely good — better than a textbook. But they were also trusting it on specific numbers, and it was confidently giving them the wrong ones. They didn't know how to tell the difference."

General-Purpose vs. Purpose-Built: What's the Actual Difference?

Khanmigo and MATHia are purpose-built tools. Every design decision — what they say, what they withhold, how they respond — was made with a specific educational goal in mind, by people who spent years thinking about how learning works. Their limitations are intentional. Khanmigo won't give you an answer because giving answers was determined to be counterproductive.

ChatGPT is a general-purpose tool. Its design goal was to be as useful as possible to as many people as possible. It was optimized to produce responses that feel correct, complete, and confident. That optimization makes it extraordinary for many tasks. But in educational settings, it creates a specific problem: there is no built-in distinction between "what this student needs right now" and "the most complete answer to this question."

HallucinationWhen an AI generates a response that sounds confident and fluent but is factually wrong. The AI isn't lying — it has no concept of lying. It's completing a pattern, and sometimes the pattern leads somewhere incorrect.
Fluency-accuracy gapThe difference between how convincing a response sounds and how correct it actually is. In AI tools, these two qualities can come apart — a very fluent, well-written answer can be wrong.

This is not a criticism of ChatGPT. It is a description of what it is. Understanding the difference between a general-purpose AI and a purpose-built educational AI is now a fundamental piece of information literacy. You have it. Most people reading about AI in education do not.

The Responsibility Question

In 2023, school districts across the United States responded to ChatGPT's rise with a range of policies. The New York City Department of Education — the largest school district in the US — initially blocked ChatGPT on school networks in January 2023. By May 2023, they had reversed the policy, issuing a statement saying that teaching students to use AI tools responsibly was more important than blocking them.

This reversal captures something real: when a general-purpose AI becomes a de facto learning tool for millions of students, the question of who is responsible for how it's used becomes genuinely difficult.

Is it OpenAI's responsibility to build explicit educational safeguards into ChatGPT — even though it was never designed as a tutoring tool? Is it schools' responsibility to teach students when to trust it and when to question it? Is it students' responsibility to verify what they learn? Is it parents'? Is it the governments that fund education?

Ethical Question

ChatGPT is free and available to anyone with an internet connection. Purpose-built tools like MATHia and Khanmigo cost money and require institutional adoption. This means the most sophisticated, pedagogically designed AI tutoring tools are largely available to students whose schools can afford them — while the free, general-purpose, occasionally-incorrect AI is the one most students actually use. Who is responsible for that gap, and what would closing it require?

These are not rhetorical questions. They are live policy debates happening in legislatures, school boards, and technology companies right now. You are old enough to follow these debates, understand them, and eventually participate in them. That starts with understanding exactly what is at stake — which you now do.

What ChatGPT Actually Gets Right as a Learning Tool

It would be inaccurate to frame ChatGPT only as a risk. Students who learned to use it well in 2022 and 2023 reported something that purpose-built tools often failed to provide: the feeling of having a conversation with someone who wasn't judging them.

A student who is embarrassed to ask a question in class, or who doesn't want to reveal that they still don't understand something from three chapters ago, can ask ChatGPT the same question fifteen different ways at any hour, with no social cost. This accessibility is significant. For students who feel behind, or who learn differently, or who simply have questions at 11pm — a patient, available, non-judgmental explainer has real value.

Researchers at Stanford and MIT have studied this phenomenon and found that students with high anxiety about appearing "dumb" in class showed measurably higher engagement with AI tutoring tools than with classroom instruction — not because the AI was better, but because the absence of social stakes changed what they were willing to try.

A tool being used by hundreds of millions of students, regardless of whether it was designed for that purpose, is shaping learning at a scale that no purpose-built tool has ever achieved. The question is not whether that's good or bad. The question is how to be a student who benefits from what it does well and stays alert to where it falls short.

You Can Now See This

The next time someone says "ChatGPT is bad for students," or "ChatGPT is great for students," you now know both claims are incomplete. You can describe specifically what it does well (accessibility, patience, concept explanation, non-judgmental space), what it does poorly (no mastery tracking, hallucinations, no Socratic scaffolding), and why the two can't be collapsed into a single verdict.

Choosing Wisely: A Framework for Evaluating Any AI Tool

Three tools. Three completely different architectures. One set of questions that cuts through any headline about AI in education.
Now that you understand what Khanmigo, MATHia, and ChatGPT actually do — how do you make a real decision about which one to use, and when?

In August 2023, the US Department of Education released a 64-page report titled "Artificial Intelligence and the Future of Teaching and Learning." It was the first major federal policy document to grapple seriously with AI tools in K-12 education, and it was read carefully by school administrators, technology directors, and education researchers across the country.

The report mentioned Khanmigo, referenced the history of intelligent tutoring systems, and discussed the ChatGPT disruption at length. But what made it notable was one specific recommendation buried on page 38: "Education institutions should develop systematic frameworks for evaluating AI tools before adoption — not based solely on vendor claims, but on pedagogical fit, data practices, equity implications, and evidence of effectiveness."

It was a reasonable recommendation. It was also one that most school districts, as of 2023, had no idea how to actually follow. They didn't have the vocabulary. They didn't have the comparison frameworks. They were being asked to evaluate tools whose underlying architectures they hadn't been trained to understand.

You have now spent four lessons in this module building exactly the understanding that report was asking for. You have looked at a Socratic conversational AI, a Bayesian mastery-tracking intelligent tutor, and a general-purpose language model used for learning. You have seen what each does, what each costs, and where each fails. You are now — genuinely — better equipped to evaluate AI learning tools than most of the adults making decisions about them in institutional settings.

The Five Questions That Cut Through Any AI Claim

When you encounter any new AI learning tool — in school, advertised to your parents, or used by a friend — these five questions will tell you most of what you need to know.

Question Why It Matters What Khanmigo / MATHia / ChatGPT Each Answer
1. Does it withhold or deliver? Withholding (Socratic) builds deeper understanding; delivering (direct) builds speed and reduces confusion. Neither is always right. Khanmigo: withholds. MATHia: delivers with tracking. ChatGPT: delivers fully, no tracking.
2. Does it track what you know? Tracking allows the tool to adapt over time. Without it, every session starts from scratch and you may drill things you already know. Khanmigo: session-level only. MATHia: deep multi-skill tracking. ChatGPT: no persistent memory (default).
3. What data does it collect, and who sees it? Personalization requires data. Data has value beyond the app. This is not paranoia — it is how these businesses work. Khanmigo: nonprofit, data used internally. MATHia: district-level data, subject to contracts. ChatGPT: conversations used for training (varies by settings).
4. What is the evidence, and who collected it? Vendor-produced studies are less reliable than independent research. Ask who funded the study and whether it was peer-reviewed. Khanmigo: limited (2023 launch). MATHia: extensive peer-reviewed research. ChatGPT: growing, mixed, ongoing.
5. Can you afford it, and what is it free at the expense of? Free tools often monetize data or attention. Paid tools create equity gaps. Both models have costs. Khanmigo: $4/month (students), free for teachers. MATHia: institutional pricing, significant. ChatGPT: free basic; paid for advanced features.
Matching the Tool to the Task

The most important practical insight from comparing these three tools is that they are not competing to be "best." They are good at different things. Choosing between them should be like choosing between a screwdriver and a wrench — the question isn't which is better, it's what you're trying to do.

Use a Socratic tool like Khanmigo when: You want to actually understand something — not just get through an assignment. You want to develop the ability to reason through a problem type rather than just get this particular answer. You have some patience. This is the tool for building durable understanding.

Use a mastery-tracking tool like MATHia when: You have a specific skill gap that needs systematic work. You want evidence that you've actually learned something, not just feel like you have. You're working on foundational skills where precision matters. This is the tool for drilling and verification.

Use a general tool like ChatGPT when: You need a concept explained differently than your textbook explained it. You want to explore "but why?" questions without a time limit. You're embarrassed to ask the same question repeatedly in class. You need something explained at 11pm. This is the tool for exploration and accessibility — but verify the specifics elsewhere.

Ethical Question

This framework assumes you have access to all three types of tools and the understanding to choose between them. Most students don't. They use whatever their school provides, or whatever is free, with no guidance about what each tool is actually doing. At an institutional level: should AI literacy — understanding what these tools are and how to use each appropriately — be a required part of K-12 curriculum? If not, who benefits most from students staying in the dark?

The View from Here

In 2023 and 2024, governments in the European Union began drafting what became the EU AI Act — the first comprehensive legal framework regulating AI systems, including educational ones. It introduced categories of risk: AI systems used in education were classified as "high risk," meaning they are subject to stricter transparency and accountability requirements than most other AI applications. The EU's reasoning was explicit: educational AI shapes how people think, what they believe they know, and what opportunities they have access to. That power requires accountability.

The same institutions that built Khanmigo, MATHia, and ChatGPT will be among the entities that regulation is written for. The same questions you now know to ask — about pedagogy, data, evidence, equity, and cost — are the questions regulators are trying to encode into law. This is not an abstract policy space. These are decisions about the infrastructure of learning for every person your age and younger.

You are old enough to follow these decisions as they're made. You understand enough to evaluate them. And someday — probably sooner than feels real right now — you will be among the people making them.
