Module 6 · Lesson 1

The Prompt Is the Blueprint

How the instructions you give an AI tutor shape everything it does — and what happens when those instructions are bad
If you could rewrite an AI's instructions from scratch, what would you change first?

In the spring of 2023, Khan Academy launched an AI tutor called Khanmigo — one of the first classroom AI tools deployed to real students at scale. Within weeks, teachers and students started noticing something strange. When students asked Khanmigo to explain a hard math problem, it would often refuse to show the full solution. It would dance around the answer, ask Socratic questions, then stop mid-explanation and say: "What do you think the next step is?"

Some students loved it. Many others were furious. "Just tell me the answer," posted one eighth-grader in a Reddit thread about the tool. "I already tried. I just need to see it done once."

The students weren't wrong. And neither was the design. Khanmigo was behaving exactly as it was instructed. Its designers had written a system prompt — a hidden set of instructions — telling it never to give direct answers, only to guide. The prompt was built around a real pedagogical theory. But that theory doesn't work for every student, every subject, or every moment in a learning session. The instructions were the product. And the instructions had a flaw.

What Is a System Prompt, and Why Does It Run Everything?

Every AI tutor — Khanmigo, Duolingo's Lily, the chatbot on your school's homework help site — runs on top of a large language model. That model, by itself, doesn't know it's a tutor. It doesn't know it's supposed to be patient, or age-appropriate, or avoid giving direct answers. Someone had to tell it those things. That telling happens through a system prompt: a block of instructions written by a human designer that shapes how the AI behaves before any student ever types a word.

Think of it this way. The AI model is like a theater actor with incredible range — it can play any role. The system prompt is the script handed to that actor before the show begins. It says: you are a patient math tutor, you speak to middle schoolers, you never give direct answers, you always ask a follow-up question. The actor follows those instructions on every single interaction, with every single student, every time.

That's powerful. And it's dangerous. Because if the script has a bad line in it, every performance is slightly broken.

System Prompt
Hidden instructions given to an AI before a conversation starts. Students usually can't see them. They control tone, behavior, limits, and teaching style.
Pedagogical Theory
A researched idea about how people learn best. "Don't give answers; ask guiding questions" is one theory. "Show the full worked example first" is another. Neither is right 100% of the time.
The Real Design Decisions Inside Khanmigo

In interviews published after the launch, Khan Academy's team explained their reasoning. They had based Khanmigo's system prompt on Socratic tutoring — a technique where a teacher guides students to discover answers through questions rather than simply stating them. Research from the 1980s and 1990s showed this approach could be highly effective. Nobel Prize–winning physicist Richard Feynman famously used it. It's a real and respected method.

But Socratic tutoring was designed for one-on-one human conversations, where a skilled teacher can read a student's face, notice when they're frustrated, and decide in that moment to just explain the thing. An AI prompt can't do that. A prompt is a fixed rule. And a fixed rule applied to millions of students in millions of different situations will always fail some of them.

This is the hidden challenge of designing AI tutors: you're not designing for one student. You're writing a policy. Every word in your system prompt is a rule that will be applied to every single student who uses the system — whether they're breezing through a topic or desperately stuck at 11 PM the night before a test.

The Design Tension

If your prompt says "always guide, never tell," you protect students from lazy thinking — but you also strand students who genuinely need to see something modeled first. If your prompt says "always explain fully," you help students who are stuck — but you may enable students to skip the hard thinking that actually builds understanding. There is no prompt that perfectly serves both needs at the same time.

What a Better Prompt Looks Like

After feedback from students and teachers, Khan Academy refined Khanmigo's prompt over the months that followed. They introduced what designers call conditional logic in natural language — instructions like: "If a student has tried more than two times and is showing signs of frustration, provide a worked example." This is still a rule. But it's a more nuanced rule. It reads context before applying behavior.

You can do this too. The best AI tutor prompts don't just declare a personality. They define situations and responses. They ask: what should this AI do when a student is confident? When a student is confused? When a student asks the same question three times? When a student tries to get the AI to just do their homework?

Every one of those situations needs a thought-out answer baked into the prompt. And every one of those answers reflects a real choice about what education is for.

You Now See What Most People Miss

When you open an AI tutor app and it feels frustrating or helpful, most people think: "this AI is smart" or "this AI is dumb." You now know that what you're experiencing is the design of a prompt — a human decision about what the AI should do. That means it can be redesigned. And knowing that, you can do it yourself.

The Ethical Question You Should Sit With

Khan Academy's Khanmigo was built by a nonprofit with genuine educational goals. Its Socratic approach was based on real research. But it still frustrated students who needed direct help — and those students were disproportionately the ones who didn't have human tutors at home to fill the gap the AI created.

Here's the question: when an AI tutoring design is based on good research but still fails certain students, who is responsible for fixing it? The designers? The schools that deployed it? The students' parents? The researchers whose theory was applied incorrectly? And what if the students who are most harmed by the bad design are also the least likely to have anyone advocating for them?

There's no clean answer. But you need to hold this question as you design anything. A prompt is never just a technical document. It's a choice about whose needs get centered.

Module 6 · Lesson 2

Feedback That Actually Teaches

The difference between "that's wrong" and feedback that creates understanding — and why most AI tutors still get this wrong
What makes a piece of feedback actually change how someone thinks — rather than just telling them they failed?

In 2001, researchers at Carnegie Mellon University in Pittsburgh, Pennsylvania, had spent nearly a decade building something called the Cognitive Tutor — an AI tutoring system for algebra. It ran in real classrooms, not just labs. And by 2010, a massive controlled study found something striking: students using Cognitive Tutor scored significantly better on algebra tests than students in traditional classrooms. It worked.

But not because of what most people assumed. When researchers interviewed students, they found the students didn't think the system was smarter or friendlier than a teacher. What they appreciated was something very specific: when they got a problem step wrong, Cognitive Tutor didn't just say "incorrect." It identified exactly which concept they'd misapplied — not just the answer, but the thinking error underneath it. It would say something like: "You subtracted before dividing. What does the order of operations tell you to do here?"

That precise, targeted feedback was the engine. The researchers called it error-specific remediation. Most AI tutors in 2023 still don't do it consistently. Most just say: "That's not quite right. Try again!" And students try again with exactly the same wrong mental model they had before.

Why "Try Again" Teaches Almost Nothing

When you get something wrong and someone says "nope, try again," two things can happen. Either you figure out why you were wrong — which means you already had the mental resources to catch your own error — or you guess. You eliminate options. You work backwards from the answer you know you're supposed to reach. You learn to navigate the system without actually understanding the concept.

This is called gaming the tutor. It's documented in the educational technology research from at least the mid-1990s. Students are extremely good at it. Given enough attempts and the knowledge that the system will eventually accept the right answer, many students will simply try every option until something works. They walk away having produced the correct answer without forming the correct understanding.

The fix isn't to give fewer attempts. The fix is to change what feedback does. Feedback, to actually work, needs to address the specific misconception the student holds — not just confirm that they produced the wrong output.

Error-Specific Remediation
Feedback that identifies the exact wrong thinking behind a mistake — not just the fact that the answer was wrong. "You applied the rule for area when this question asked for perimeter" is error-specific. "That's incorrect, try again" is not.
Gaming the Tutor
When a student figures out how to get the AI to accept a correct answer without actually understanding the material — by eliminating options, memorizing patterns, or exploiting retry logic.
What Cognitive Tutor Actually Did Differently

Carnegie Mellon's team built their system on a framework called Knowledge Component Analysis. Instead of modeling "algebra" as one big thing, they broke it into hundreds of tiny concepts — like "distribute a negative sign across parentheses" or "recognizing when to use the zero product property." Each problem step was tagged to specific knowledge components. When a student got step three of a problem wrong, the system knew which knowledge component that step tested — and could deliver feedback targeted to exactly that component.

This is expensive to build. It took years of expert analysis for a single subject. It required deep collaboration between cognitive scientists, subject matter experts, and engineers. You couldn't just write a single sentence in a prompt and get this behavior. It was a full knowledge architecture underneath the interface.

But the principle can be applied more simply. Even if you can't build a full knowledge component map, you can write a prompt that instructs an AI tutor to: ask the student what rule they applied before telling them they're wrong; name the concept that seems to have been misunderstood; and offer one specific corrective explanation rather than a generic encouragement to retry.

The Feedback Design Checklist

Good feedback in an AI tutor does four things: (1) Acknowledges what the student got right, if anything. (2) Identifies the specific error or misconception, not just the wrong answer. (3) Connects the error to a concrete principle or rule. (4) Gives the student one clear next step — not a vague "think harder."

The Ethical Dimension: Who Gets the Good Feedback?

Here's something uncomfortable. Cognitive Tutor worked remarkably well — but it was expensive, it required licensed software, and it was primarily adopted by schools in wealthier districts who could afford the implementation costs. Research showed it was especially effective for students from lower-income backgrounds when they did get access, because those students often had less access to human tutors who could catch misconceptions after school.

So the students who most needed the precise, targeted feedback were exactly the students least likely to get it. Better AI tutoring tools were deployed where there was already more tutoring support — not where the gap was greatest.

When you design a better AI tutor, you're making an implicit choice about access. A tool that runs in a premium subscription app, or requires expensive setup, or is only adopted by schools with tech budgets, will widen educational gaps — even if the tool itself is excellent. The design of the feedback engine and the design of who gets to use it are not separate questions. They're the same question.

You Now See What Most People Miss

Every time an AI tutor says "not quite right, try again!" a designer made a choice — to skip the hard work of building specific feedback. You now know what specific feedback looks like, how it was built, and what it costs to skip it. That knowledge puts you ahead of most people building these tools right now.

Module 6 · Lesson 3

Pacing, Patience, and the Student Who Already Knows

Why AI tutors waste everyone's time — and how to design adaptive pacing that respects what a student already knows
How should an AI tutor know when to slow down and when to get out of the way?

When Duolingo launched in 2012, its co-founder Luis von Ahn had a clear goal: make language learning accessible to anyone, anywhere, for free. By 2018, Duolingo had over 300 million registered users — more people than were learning a second language in any school system on Earth. It was the most downloaded education app in the world.

And it had a problem that its own internal researchers documented: advanced learners were quitting at alarming rates. The dropout data showed a pattern. New learners stuck around. But users who already had some background in a language — who'd studied Spanish in school for two years, for example — were hitting the early Duolingo lessons and leaving. Fast.

The lessons weren't wrong. They weren't badly designed. But the system didn't know what the student already knew. A 17-year-old who'd taken three years of French was being walked through "this is a dog, this is a cat" — slowly, repetitively, cheerfully. The AI treated her exactly like a true beginner. She left. Duolingo's response, developed between 2015 and 2018, was a system they called placement testing combined with what they internally called the placement jump — a mechanism that could skip a student past material they'd already mastered. It was a direct response to a pacing failure.

The Problem of Assumed Ignorance

Most AI tutors are built with a clean slate assumption: every student who opens the app knows nothing yet. This is a safe assumption if you're building for true beginners. It is a deeply insulting assumption if the student is not a beginner. And most students, in most educational contexts, are not beginners. They bring prior knowledge, prior misconceptions, and prior experience into every session. Ignoring that prior knowledge doesn't just waste time — it signals to the student that the system doesn't see them.

Research in education calls this prior knowledge activation. Before any new instruction begins, good teaching surfaces what the learner already knows — both to build on it and to correct misconceptions before they get reinforced. A human tutor does this naturally in the first five minutes of a session: "What have you already covered? What felt shaky? Where do you want to start?"

An AI tutor can do this too. But it requires designing for it explicitly. If your system prompt doesn't include any instruction to probe prior knowledge, the AI will behave as if everyone is starting from zero.

Prior Knowledge Activation
The practice of surfacing what a learner already knows before teaching new material — to build on accurate knowledge and correct misconceptions before they get worse.
Adaptive Pacing
Adjusting the speed and depth of instruction based on what the learner already knows and how quickly they're demonstrating mastery. The opposite of one-size-fits-all pacing.
How to Design for Adaptive Pacing in a Prompt

You don't need Duolingo's engineering team to build adaptive pacing into an AI tutor. You need good prompt design. Here's what that actually looks like in practice.

First, your prompt should instruct the AI to ask about prior knowledge before beginning instruction: "Before explaining a new concept, ask the student what they already know or have tried." This is a single instruction. It costs nothing. And it completely changes the first two minutes of every session.

Second, your prompt should tell the AI to calibrate explanation depth based on what it learns: "If the student demonstrates familiarity with foundational concepts, skip them and proceed to more advanced material. If the student shows uncertainty, slow down and use concrete examples." Again — this is language, not engineering. It's in the prompt.

Third, and most importantly: your prompt should tell the AI to check comprehension before moving on, not just after a student gets a right answer. A right answer doesn't always mean understanding. A student can guess correctly, or remember a pattern without knowing why it works. Comprehension checking means asking: "Can you tell me in your own words why that answer is correct?" before the AI moves forward.

The Pacing Design Principle

A well-paced AI tutor has three gears: fast (skip material the student clearly knows), normal (teach at the expected level), and slow (re-teach using different explanations and more examples). Your prompt needs to tell the AI when to shift between them — and crucially, how to recognize which gear is needed right now.

When Patience Becomes Condescension

There's another side to this. AI tutors are often designed to be very patient — to never express frustration, always encourage, always find something positive to say. That sounds good. But taken to an extreme, it creates a different problem: the AI becomes condescending.

When a capable student gets something right and the AI responds with "Great job! You're doing amazing!" — that's not encouragement. That's noise. The student knows it's not "amazing" to get a basic multiplication fact correct. Hollow praise is one of the fastest ways to lose a learner's trust and attention.

This was documented in a 2020 study at Stanford's Graduate School of Education, where researchers found that students aged 11–14 were significantly less engaged with AI tutors that gave frequent generic positive feedback compared to systems that acknowledged correct answers briefly and immediately moved forward. The students described the overpraising systems as "fake" and "like talking to a baby."

Respect, in tutoring, sometimes looks like getting out of the way. Moving fast when a student is ready. Not making them sit through explanations they don't need. That is also a form of caring.

You Now See What Most People Miss

The next time an AI tutor walks you through something you already know, or praises you for something trivial, you're experiencing a design failure — not a feature. Knowing that pacing is designable, and that hollow praise is documented and avoidable, means you can build something that actually respects its students.

Ethical Question — No Clean Answer

Adaptive pacing means students move through material at different speeds. In a classroom, this is often invisible. In a school-issued AI tutor app, it might be visible — to teachers, to administrators, maybe to other students. If one student's app is showing "Unit 7" and another's is showing "Unit 2" at the same time, what does that do to how those students see themselves? Should AI tutors make learning pace visible, or protect it? Who has a right to see that data?

From Draft to Deployed: Testing Your Design

How AI tutor designers test what they build — what breaks in the real world, and how to think like an auditor before your design ever reaches a student
How do you know if an AI tutor you designed is actually working — for every student, not just the ones it was built for?

In 2017, a company called Cerego — which built an AI-powered memory and learning platform — partnered with researchers at MIT to test how well its algorithm predicted what students needed to review. Cerego's system used a model of human memory called spaced repetition to decide when to show each student each piece of content. The AI's job was to show you something just before you were likely to forget it, keeping it in memory more efficiently than random review.

The MIT researchers ran a careful evaluation across thousands of students. What they found was troubling: the algorithm worked beautifully for students who had consistent daily engagement — students who opened the app regularly and for roughly similar amounts of time each session. For those students, the memory model was accurate and the review scheduling was genuinely better than random.

But for students who had irregular engagement — who missed days, then came back with a long session, then missed more — the algorithm's predictions were significantly worse. Sometimes worse than no scheduling at all. The model had been trained and tested primarily on consistent users. When it encountered inconsistent ones, it failed. And inconsistent users, the researchers noted, tended to be students with more chaotic home environments — students who were also, in many cases, the most educationally vulnerable.

The lesson was stark: testing a design on your best-case users doesn't tell you if it works for your worst-case users. And your worst-case users are usually the ones who need it most.

What "Testing" Actually Means for an AI Tutor

When engineers test software, they typically check for bugs: does the button work, does the page load, does the code crash? That's important. But for an AI tutor, those functional tests miss the deeper question: is this design teaching effectively, fairly, and honestly?

There are three distinct types of testing that AI tutor designers should do — and almost none of them involve code.

Adversarial testing means deliberately trying to break your design. You give the tutor a student who lies about their level, a student who gives nonsense answers, a student who tries to get the AI to do their homework for them, a student who tests every edge case. You see what the prompt allows and what it breaks against. You look for the holes.

Equity testing means deliberately recruiting your least-likely users. You test on students with learning differences. Students who are significantly behind. Students who have irregular access. Students who are English language learners. If your design only works for the student it was implicitly designed for, it needs to be redesigned.

Drift testing means watching what the AI actually does in sessions over time — because AI language models can behave differently across long conversations than they do in the first few exchanges. A prompt that works for a 5-message session may produce unexpected behavior in a 40-message session. You have to test the long game, not just the opening move.

Adversarial Testing
Deliberately trying to make a system fail — giving it edge cases, bad actors, and unexpected inputs to find where the design breaks before real users encounter it.
Equity Testing
Specifically checking whether a design works for users who are different from the default assumptions built into it — users with different access patterns, abilities, or backgrounds.
Thinking Like an Auditor, Not Just a Builder

Right now, in 2024 and 2025, companies deploying AI tutors in schools are mostly doing so without independent audits. There are no standardized tests for "does this AI tutor produce equitable outcomes?" the way there are standardized tests for "does this food contain lead?" That gap is not a technical accident. It reflects a choice — that deployment speed matters more than verified outcomes. Students are the test environment.

But this is changing. Organizations like the AI Now Institute and researchers at institutions like MIT, Carnegie Mellon, and Stanford have begun publishing frameworks for auditing educational AI. Some school districts — including New York City, which in 2023 became one of the first large urban districts to publish guidelines for AI tool adoption — have started requiring vendors to provide evidence that their tools don't discriminate by race, language, or disability status before being adopted.

An auditor's mindset asks different questions than a designer's mindset. A designer asks: "What should this tool do?" An auditor asks: "What does it actually do, for whom, and what are the consequences for the students it works badly for?" You need both. And the best AI tutor designers carry both simultaneously.

The Auditor's Checklist

1. Who is this designed for? Who isn't in that description? 2. What happens when it's used by someone outside the intended user profile? 3. Where does the design fail silently — without the student or teacher knowing? 4. Who has access to the data this tool generates, and how might that data be used against students? 5. If this tool produces worse outcomes for a specific group, who notices — and who decides whether to fix it?

The Institutional Stakes — and Why You Matter Here

In 2023, the U.S. Department of Education published a report called "Artificial Intelligence and the Future of Teaching and Learning." It called for "human-centered AI" in classrooms and acknowledged that current tools were being deployed far ahead of the evidence base for their effectiveness. The report explicitly invited researchers, educators — and students — to help develop the frameworks that don't yet exist.

This is not a finished field. The rules for how AI tutors should behave, how they should be tested, what they should and shouldn't be allowed to do — most of those rules are still being written. And they're being written by people who are often not students, not teachers, and often not from the communities most affected by the tools they're deploying.

You, having now built and stress-tested an AI tutor design across four lessons, understand the mechanics of feedback, pacing, prompting, and equity testing better than most adults making decisions about these tools right now. That's not a metaphor. The knowledge gap between what's being decided and what's actually known is real — and you've just crossed to the side that knows.

You Now See What Most People Miss

When an AI tutoring company says their tool "works" and shows you data — you now know to ask: works for whom? Tested how? On which users? For how long? With what equity testing? Most people who hear those claims nod and move on. You now know what questions to ask, and those questions are exactly the ones the field needs most right now.

Correct. The algorithm was calibrated on consistent users. Irregular users — often from more chaotic home environments — got worse scheduling than random review would have given them.
Re-read the Cerego case. The problem emerged specifically with irregular users — those who didn't engage consistently. The algorithm had been trained on consistent users and generalized poorly.
2. Adversarial testing of an AI tutor involves:
Yes. Adversarial testing means probing for failure — sending in users who lie, behave unexpectedly, or try to break the system. The goal is to find what the prompt allows before real students encounter it.
Adversarial testing is specifically about finding failure modes, not confirming success on typical users or competing with other products. It means deliberately trying to break the design.
3. Why might an AI tutor that works perfectly in a 5-message session fail in a 40-message session?
Correct. Drift testing exists because model behavior in extended sessions can diverge from behavior in short ones. A prompt that holds through 5 exchanges may produce unexpected outputs at exchange 40.
This is about what the lesson calls "drift testing" — the fact that AI model behavior can shift over a long conversation in ways that short tests don't reveal. Prompts need to be tested over full session lengths.
4. An AI tutoring company presents data showing their tool improved test scores by 15% for students in their pilot program. What auditor question should you ask first?
Exactly the right question. The Cerego case demonstrates that "works on average" can mask "fails badly for the most vulnerable users." Who is in the data matters as much as what the data shows.
An auditor's first question is always about who the data represents. An average improvement can hide systematic failures for specific subgroups — which is exactly what the Cerego case illustrated.
5. The U.S. Department of Education's 2023 report noted that AI tools were being deployed "far ahead of the evidence base." This means:
Correct. Students are essentially in a live experiment — tools are deployed at scale without the kind of rigorous, independent, long-term testing that other high-stakes interventions require.
The report was raising a concern, not a legal issue or an endorsement. "Ahead of the evidence base" means the tools are in classrooms before we know for certain they work well and equitably for all students.

Design a Better AI Tutor Yourself — 15 questions · Pass at 80%
1. What is a system prompt in the context of an AI tutor?
Correct. System prompts are the behavioral blueprint — invisible to users, written by designers, controlling tone, approach, and limits.
System prompts are designer-written instructions, not student messages or AI summaries. They define the AI's role before any conversation begins.
2. The main design problem with Khanmigo's original Socratic approach was that:
Yes. The method itself is valid — the problem was applying it rigidly to every student in every situation, ignoring context.
The issue wasn't the theory's validity or the AI's intelligence — it was that a fixed prompt can't adapt to individual context the way a human teacher can.
3. "Gaming the tutor" describes a behavior where students:
Correct. Gaming means producing right outputs through process of elimination — not through understanding. The student has "won" without learning.
Gaming the tutor means navigating the retry logic to produce correct answers without the correct internal understanding — a real and well-documented phenomenon in ed-tech research.
4. Carnegie Mellon's Cognitive Tutor identified which specific knowledge component a student had misapplied. Why does this make feedback more effective?
Exactly. Error-specific feedback addresses the root cause of the wrong answer, not just the symptom — which is what creates actual learning rather than just correction.
The value of knowledge component mapping is precision in feedback — being able to say "you misapplied this specific concept" rather than just "that's wrong."
5. Which of these system prompt instructions best prevents a student from gaming the tutor?
Right. Requiring explanation of a correct answer makes it impossible to game through process-of-elimination — you can't explain why something is right if you only guessed it.
Limiting attempts or showing answers addresses the symptom, not the cause. Asking students to explain correct answers tests genuine understanding rather than correct answer production.
6. Duolingo introduced placement testing and placement jumps primarily to address:
Correct. Advanced users were quitting because the system didn't recognize their prior knowledge. Placement features allowed the system to adapt starting level to the individual learner.
The dropout problem was specifically about prior knowledge not being recognized. Placement features were the design solution to the clean slate assumption that was driving advanced users away.
7. Prior knowledge activation in an AI tutor is most important because:
Yes. Two goals in one: build on what's correct, and fix what's wrong before new content makes it worse.
Prior knowledge activation isn't about personalization theater or skipping difficulty. It's a diagnostic step that protects new learning from being built on faulty foundations.
8. A 2020 Stanford study found students aged 11–14 were less engaged with AI tutors that gave frequent generic praise. The students described those systems as:
Correct. "Fake" and "like talking to a baby" — both describe the same thing: hollow encouragement that signals the system doesn't actually see or respect the student.
The Stanford finding was specifically about hollow praise. Students recognized it as insincere and condescending, which reduced rather than supported engagement.
9. An AI tutor's "three gears" for pacing (fast, normal, slow) should be triggered by:
Yes. Adaptive pacing means the gear shifts in response to the student's demonstrated knowledge and comprehension — not by schedule, preference, or randomness.
Adaptive pacing requires reading the student's current state — what they demonstrate they know — not following a preset schedule or external preference.
10. The Cerego/MIT study's most important design lesson was:
Correct. The algorithm was calibrated on its best users. When it encountered its most vulnerable users — irregular engagers from unstable home situations — it failed without anyone initially noticing.
The key lesson wasn't about spaced repetition's effectiveness or who should use AI tutors. It was about testing: if your pilot only included consistent users, your "it works" conclusion is incomplete.
11. Equity testing of an AI tutor means:
Right. Equity testing means deliberately recruiting the users who are least like the assumed default — to find where the design fails for them before deployment.
Equity testing isn't about pricing, same content for all, or demographic balance alone. It's specifically about testing with users who are different from the default assumptions built into the design.
12. Drift testing an AI tutor addresses the risk that:
Correct. A prompt that works in short sessions may produce unexpected behavior when the conversation is 40 messages long. Drift testing checks for this.
Drift testing is about session length, not long-term memory or daily variation. It catches failure modes that only emerge after many exchanges within a single conversation.
13. NYC became one of the first large school districts in the U.S. to require what from AI vendors before adoption?
Yes. NYC's 2023 guidelines required equity evidence — proof that the tool works across different student groups — as a condition of adoption.
NYC's requirement was specifically about non-discrimination evidence — proof the tool doesn't produce inequitable outcomes by race, language, or disability status.
14. What's the difference between a designer's mindset and an auditor's mindset when evaluating an AI tutor?
Correct. The lesson frames this explicitly: designers build toward a vision; auditors investigate reality — especially for the users the vision didn't account for.
Designer and auditor mindsets are distinct. The lesson is explicit: designers ask "what should it do?" while auditors ask "what does it actually do, and who gets hurt when it fails?"
15. A company claims their AI tutor raises test scores by 12% on average across all students. As an auditor, your most important follow-up question is:
Exactly right. "Average improvement" can hide systematic failure for specific subgroups. The auditor's job is to disaggregate the data — who benefited, and who didn't?
An average is a starting point, not an answer. The auditor question is: does the average mask inequity? Which students got the 12% and which students got zero — or negative outcomes?
