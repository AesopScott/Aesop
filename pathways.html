AI Career Pathways

Where your skills point.

AI work splits into four lanes. Each one needs different skills, attracts different people, and is hiring for different reasons. This is what they look like and which AESOP courses build toward each.

Four lanes, not a ladder

Most "AI career" frameworks rank one role over another. This one doesn't. The four lanes are different kinds of work, not different levels of seniority. A senior Translator and a senior Builder both exist; neither is the goal of the other. A learner finds their lane by paying attention to which skills they keep wanting to deepen — not by following someone else's career plan.

These lanes also shift over time. Most working AI practitioners move between two of them over a decade. AESOP's transcript shows your current affinity, not your permanent identity.

🔨
Builders

Engineers who make AI systems exist.

Builders write the prompts, design the agents, fine-tune the models, and wire up the systems everyone else uses. The work is hands-on, technical, and produces software that ships.

Example roles

  • AI Engineer
  • ML Engineer
  • Prompt Engineer
  • AI Agent Developer
  • RAG Systems Engineer

AESOP courses that build toward this lane

  • Building with AI
  • Prompt Engineering for Developers
  • RAG Systems from Scratch
  • Building AI Agents I
  • Working with the Anthropic API
🔗
Integrators

Connectors who bring AI into existing work.

Integrators identify where AI fits, evaluate which tools to use, and weave AI into existing products, workflows, and teams. The work is part technical, part strategic, and produces leverage rather than software.

Example roles

  • AI Solutions Architect
  • AI Product Manager
  • AI Operations Lead
  • Workflow Automation Specialist
  • AI Implementation Consultant

AESOP courses that build toward this lane

  • AI Tools for Solo Founders
  • AI for Marketing & Growth
  • AI for Product Development
  • Build AI Workflows (No Code)
  • Applied AI Development
⚖️
Governors
Undersupplied globally

Stewards who keep AI accountable.

Governors audit, regulate, secure, and ethically constrain AI systems. They write the policies, identify the bias, run the red teams, and answer the hard questions. This lane is undersupplied globally — the demand is real and growing.

Example roles

  • AI Governance Lead
  • AI Risk & Compliance Officer
  • AI Ethics Specialist
  • AI Security Auditor
  • AI Red Team Operator

AESOP courses that build toward this lane

  • AI Governance
  • AI Ethics
  • AI Bias & Fairness
  • AI Security & Red Teaming
  • AI Safety & Alignment Basics
🗣️
Translators

Bridges who make AI legible to everyone else.

Translators explain AI to people who don't build it — leaders, parents, students, clients, citizens. They turn technical reality into clear understanding without dumbing it down. This is the lane the world needs most and trains for least.

Example roles

  • AI Educator
  • AI Literacy Trainer
  • AI Communications Specialist
  • AI Policy Translator
  • AI Change Manager

  • AI & Misinformation

How AESOP measures pathway affinity

Every AESOP skill maps to one or more lanes with a weight: primary (this skill is core to the lane), secondary (this skill helps in the lane), or foundational (this skill is needed in every lane). A learner's affinity score for a lane is the weighted sum of their proficiency in mapping skills, normalized against a theoretical maximum. Strong affinity in a lane suggests their current skill profile is already pointed there. Weak affinity is not a verdict — it's information about what would change with more learning.

Pathway affinity is shown on the AESOP transcript and updates each time a learner completes new courses or reassesses. The transcript is private to the learner; what they share, and with whom, is theirs.
